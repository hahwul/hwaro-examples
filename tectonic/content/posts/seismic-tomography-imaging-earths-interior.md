# Seismic Tomography: Imaging Earth's Hidden Interior

We cannot drill to the mantle. The deepest borehole ever created -- the Kola Superdeep Borehole in Russia -- reached only 12.2 km, barely scratching the upper crust. To study what lies beneath, geophysicists rely on seismic waves: natural signals generated by earthquakes that travel through the entire planet.

## The Principle

Seismic tomography works on the same principle as a medical CT scan. In a CT scan, X-rays pass through the body from many angles, and a computer reconstructs the internal structure. In seismic tomography, earthquake waves pass through the Earth from many source-receiver pairs distributed around the globe.

Variations in wave speed reveal variations in temperature, composition, and phase. Hot material slows seismic waves; cold material speeds them up. By combining thousands of measurements, geophysicists build three-dimensional maps of the planet's interior.

## Types of Seismic Waves

Two primary body wave types are used in tomography:

**P-waves (Primary waves)** -- Compressional waves that travel through solids and liquids. They are the fastest seismic waves, arriving first at recording stations. P-waves can traverse the entire Earth, including the liquid outer core.

**S-waves (Secondary waves)** -- Shear waves that travel only through solids. The fact that S-waves cannot pass through the outer core was the original evidence that this layer is liquid.

Surface waves (Love waves and Rayleigh waves) are also used, particularly for imaging the upper mantle and crust at high resolution.

## Major Discoveries

Seismic tomography has revealed several features that fundamentally changed our understanding of Earth's interior:

### Subducted Slabs

Cold, fast-velocity anomalies beneath convergent boundaries trace the paths of subducted oceanic plates descending into the mantle. Some slabs stall at the 670 km discontinuity; others punch through into the lower mantle. Beneath the western Pacific, tomographic images show slab material extending all the way to the core-mantle boundary.

### Mantle Plumes

Hot, slow-velocity anomalies rising from the deep mantle have been imaged beneath several hotspot volcanoes, including Hawaii and Iceland. The geometry and depth of origin of these plumes remain subjects of active debate. Some appear to originate at the core-mantle boundary; others may be shallower features of the upper mantle.

### Large Low-Shear-Velocity Provinces (LLSVPs)

Two continent-sized anomalies sit at the base of the mantle -- one beneath Africa and one beneath the Pacific. These LLSVPs are characterized by reduced shear wave velocities and appear to be compositionally distinct from the surrounding mantle. Their origin is unknown. Hypotheses include:

- Accumulated subducted oceanic crust
- Primordial material preserved since Earth's formation
- Iron-enriched zones created by interaction with the core

### Ultra-Low Velocity Zones (ULVZs)

Thin patches at the very base of the mantle show dramatically reduced wave speeds, suggesting the presence of partial melt. These zones are only 5-40 km thick but may play a role in the initiation of mantle plumes.

## Data and Methods

Modern seismic tomography relies on:

- **Global seismic networks** -- Thousands of broadband seismometers recording earthquakes continuously
- **Waveform inversion** -- Fitting the full waveform shape, not just arrival times, to extract more information
- **Adjoint methods** -- Computationally intensive techniques that iteratively refine 3D Earth models
- **Ambient noise tomography** -- Using background seismic noise (ocean waves, wind) rather than earthquakes

## Resolution and Limitations

The resolution of tomographic models depends on the distribution of earthquakes and recording stations. Coverage is excellent in well-instrumented regions (Japan, Europe, western North America) but sparse in oceanic areas and the Southern Hemisphere.

Typical resolution:

- Upper mantle: 50-100 km
- Lower mantle: 200-500 km
- Core-mantle boundary: 100-300 km

New deployments of ocean-bottom seismometers and dense temporary arrays are steadily improving coverage in previously undersampled regions.

## The Future

The next frontier in seismic tomography is full-waveform inversion at the global scale -- using the complete seismic wavefield to build Earth models with unprecedented detail. Combined with mineral physics experiments and geodynamic simulations, these images are bringing us closer to understanding the convective engine that drives plate tectonics from below.
