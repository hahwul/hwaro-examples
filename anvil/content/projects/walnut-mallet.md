A joiner's mallet shaped from a solid block of black walnut. No glue joints, no hardware -- just one piece of wood and a lot of shaping.

<!-- more -->

<table class="spec-table">
  <tr><th>Material</th><td>Black walnut block, 4" x 4" x 12"</td></tr>

## Process

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 01 -- Layout</div>
  Drew the profile directly onto the walnut block with a marking gauge and pencil. The head is 3.5 inches wide tapering to 3 inches, and the handle integrates directly from the head stock.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 02 -- Rough Shaping</div>
  Cut the rough profile on the bandsaw. Removed the bulk of the waste from the handle transition area. Left plenty of material -- it's easier to remove wood than to add it back.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 03 -- Handle Shaping</div>
  Used a drawknife to bring the handle down to an octagonal cross-section, then refined with a spokeshave to a comfortable oval. The transition from head to handle got careful attention with a rasp to avoid a stress riser.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 04 -- Head Refinement</div>
  Slightly crowned both striking faces with a rasp so the contact point is centered. Added a gentle chamfer to all edges of the head to prevent chipping.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 05 -- Finishing</div>
  Sanded through 80, 120, and 220 grits. Applied three coats of boiled linseed oil over three days, wiping off the excess after 20 minutes each time. The walnut grain came alive with the oil.
</div>

## Notes

The integral handle design means no wedges or joints to fail, but it requires a larger starting block and more careful grain orientation. The grain should run parallel to the striking faces.

Weight came out to about 18 ounces -- heavier than a typical mallet, which is fine for chisel work. The walnut has good shock absorption.
