A simple project with satisfying results. Four wall hooks forged from 3/8-inch round mild steel bar stock, each with a decorative twist in the shank.

<!-- more -->

<table class="spec-table">
  <tr><th>Material</th><td>3/8" round mild steel bar</td></tr>
  <tr><th>Tools</th><td>Coal forge, 2lb cross-peen hammer, anvil, twist wrench, wire brush</td></tr>

## Process

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 01 -- Stock Preparation</div>
  Cut four 8-inch lengths of round bar. Chamfered the cut ends on the grinder to remove burrs. Marked the twist section with soapstone at 2 inches and 5 inches from one end.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 02 -- Drawing the Point</div>
  Heated the tip to bright orange and drew it out to a tapered point over about 15 heats. The goal was a gradual taper, not a needle -- these need to look substantial on the wall.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 03 -- Twisting the Shank</div>
  Heated the marked section evenly and clamped one end in the vise. Used a twist wrench to put four full rotations in the 3-inch section. The key is even heat -- cold spots create uneven twists.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 04 -- Bending the Hook</div>
  Bent the tapered end over the horn of the anvil to form the hook curve. Then bent the opposite end at 90 degrees to create the wall mounting plate. Punched a screw hole through the flat.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 05 -- Finishing</div>
  Wire-brushed at black heat to remove scale. Applied beeswax while the steel was still warm enough to melt it on contact. The wax darkens the finish and provides basic corrosion resistance.
</div>

## Notes

The third hook has a slightly uneven twist -- the heat wasn't distributed well enough before I started turning. It's not obvious once mounted, but it's a reminder to be patient with the forge.

Beeswax finish works well for indoor pieces. For anything going outside, I'd switch to a clear lacquer or oil-based finish.
