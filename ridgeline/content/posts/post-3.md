## Overview

The Skyline Trail is the signature day hike at Mount Rainier National Park. Starting from the Paradise visitor area at 1,646 meters, the loop climbs to Panorama Point at 2,073 meters before returning through subalpine meadows that erupt with wildflowers in late July and August.

This is not a wilderness experience in the traditional sense. The trail is paved in sections, and you will share it with hundreds of other visitors on a summer weekend. But the views of Mount Rainier's glaciated south face are unmatched, and the alpine garden ecology is genuinely world-class.

## Trailhead

Paradise, Mount Rainier National Park. The parking lot fills by 10:00 AM on summer weekends. Arrive before 8:00 AM or after 3:00 PM to find a spot. A national park pass is required.

## Route Description

### Paradise to Panorama Point

From the Paradise parking area, follow signs for the Skyline Trail. The first kilometer climbs steadily through subalpine meadows on a paved path. Stay on the trail. The meadow ecology here is fragile, and social trails cause visible damage that takes decades to heal.

At the junction with the Golden Gate Trail, bear left to continue on the Skyline Trail. The grade steepens as the pavement gives way to packed dirt and rock. Marmots are everywhere in this zone, whistling from their boulder perches.

Panorama Point offers a commanding view of the Nisqually Glacier, the Tatoosh Range to the south, and on clear days, Mount Adams, Mount Hood, and Mount St. Helens.

### Panorama Point to the Pebble Creek Junction

Above Panorama Point, the trail traverses a rocky slope that often holds snow into August. When snow is present, follow the wands and boot track carefully. The exposure is mild but the footing can be treacherous on hard-packed snow without traction.

The high point of the loop reaches approximately 2,100 meters before beginning the descent toward Pebble Creek.

### Descent via Myrtle Falls

The return leg drops through the most spectacular meadow sections of the route. In peak bloom, the slopes are covered with lupine, paintbrush, aster, and dozens of other species. The colors against the backdrop of the mountain are extraordinary.

A short spur trail leads to a viewpoint above Myrtle Falls, a photogenic cascade that is worth the brief detour.

## Conditions and Timing

The trail is typically snow-free from mid-July through September. Earlier in the season, the upper sections may require microspikes. The Park Service posts current trail conditions at the Paradise ranger station.

Afternoon clouds often build against the mountain, so morning starts offer the best chance of clear summit views.

## Gear Notes

- Sturdy trail shoes (the rocky sections above Panorama Point are rough on lightweight footwear)
- Layers (temperatures at Panorama Point can be 10 to 15 degrees cooler than the parking lot)
- Microspikes (early season)
- Sun protection
- 1.5 to 2 liters of water

## Wildlife

Hoary marmots, Cascade golden-mantled ground squirrels, and Clark's nutcrackers are commonly seen along the trail. Do not feed the marmots. They have become habituated enough without further encouragement.
