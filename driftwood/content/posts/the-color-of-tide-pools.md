Tide pools are best photographed in the hour after the water recedes. The inhabitants have not yet retreated, and the pool surface is still enough to act as a mirror for whatever sky hangs above. On overcast mornings the reflection is a uniform grey-white, which turns the pool into a lightbox illuminating whatever lives below.

The palette is surprising. Coralline algae paints the rock in shades of dusty rose and lavender. Green anemones sit in clusters, their tentacles extended in water but contracted to dark knobs when exposed to air. Hermit crabs move across the bottom trailing tiny wakes. The water itself takes on a faint green cast from the algae, shifting toward amber in shallower sections where sand has settled.

A macro lens at close range transforms these basins into landscapes of their own. The sense of scale disappears entirely. A cluster of mussels becomes a mountain range; a ribbon of kelp, a river through a valley.

The challenge is stillness. Any disturbance to the water ruins the clarity. These frames required patience, a low tripod, and a willingness to wait for the surface to settle between exposures.
