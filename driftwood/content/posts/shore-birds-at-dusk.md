The shorebirds arrive at the surf line as the beach empties of people. Western sandpipers move in tight groups, running forward as each wave retreats and scattering back as the next arrives. Their legs are a blur of motion but their bodies remain remarkably level, as though mounted on invisible tracks.

Semipalmated plovers work individually, stopping abruptly to tilt their heads and listen for prey beneath the sand. Their stop-start rhythm is harder to anticipate than the sandpipers' continuous movement. A long lens and fast shutter are necessary, but the fading light pushes ISO higher than ideal.

The most successful frames from this session came when I stopped trying to freeze motion and let the birds blur slightly against a sharp background. At one-fifteenth of a second, the running sandpipers become streaks of warm grey above sharp reflections in the wet sand. The effect is closer to how the eye actually perceives them --- not as frozen specimens but as movement and pattern.

As the light dropped further the birds became pure silhouettes against the wet sand's mirror of the sky. These final frames reduce the scene to its simplest elements: dark shapes, reflected light, and the thin bright line where surf meets shore.
