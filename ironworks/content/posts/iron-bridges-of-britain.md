The Iron Bridge at Coalbrookdale, erected in 1779 over the River Severn in Shropshire, stands as one of the most significant structures in the history of engineering. Designed by Thomas Farnolls Pritchard and cast at Abraham Darby III's foundry, it was the first major bridge in the world to be constructed entirely of cast iron. Its single arch, spanning some thirty metres, demonstrated that iron could serve not merely as a material for tools and machinery but as a structural medium capable of bearing enormous loads.

The bridge's construction was itself a feat of ingenuity. The iron ribs, each cast in two halves, were assembled using techniques borrowed from carpentry and traditional timber-frame construction. Mortise-and-tenon joints, dovetails, and wedges held the structure together, as the builders had no precedent for joining iron members at such a scale. The result was a structure of remarkable elegance, its semicircular arch rising above the gorge with a lightness that belied the material's weight.

The success at Coalbrookdale inspired a generation of iron bridge builders. Thomas Telford's designs pushed the material further, culminating in ambitious projects across Wales, Scotland, and England. The Pontcysyllte Aqueduct, completed in 1805, carried the Llangollen Canal across the Dee Valley on slender iron troughs supported by stone piers rising over thirty-eight metres above the river. These structures proved that iron, properly understood and skillfully employed, could achieve what stone and timber could not, opening new possibilities for civil engineering that would culminate in the great railway viaducts of the Victorian era.
