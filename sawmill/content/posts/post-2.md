## Overview

An end-grain cutting board is one of the best beginner woodworking projects. It teaches fundamental skills -- accurate cutting, careful glue-ups, and patient finishing -- while producing something genuinely useful. This board measures 16 x 12 x 1.5 inches.

## Step 1: Choosing the Wood

Hard maple is the standard choice for cutting boards. It is dense, closed-grain, and food-safe. Avoid softwoods and open-pore species like oak, which can harbor bacteria.

For this board, I used a single 8/4 hard maple board, approximately 48 inches long and 6 inches wide.

## Step 2: Initial Cuts

Start by crosscutting the board into strips 1.75 inches wide. The extra quarter inch accounts for material lost to planing after the first glue-up.

After cutting, arrange the strips and look at the grain. Alternate the growth rings to help the board stay flat over time.

## Step 3: First Glue-Up

Apply a thin, even layer of food-safe wood glue (Titebond III) to each mating face. Clamp the strips together into a flat panel. Use cauls across the top to keep everything flush.

Let the glue cure for a full 24 hours before removing the clamps.

## Step 4: Creating End Grain

Once the first glue-up is flat and cured, plane or sand the top and bottom smooth. Then crosscut this panel into 1.75-inch strips again, perpendicular to the first cuts. This creates the end-grain pattern.

## Step 5: Second Glue-Up

Arrange the end-grain strips, rotating every other one 180 degrees to create a checkerboard pattern in the grain. Glue and clamp as before.

## Step 6: Flattening and Finishing

After the second glue-up cures, flatten both faces. A drum sander works well here, taking very light passes to avoid tear-out on the end grain. Sand through 220 grit.

Round over all edges with a router and a 1/8-inch roundover bit, then sand the edges smooth.

## Step 7: Oil Treatment

Apply a generous coat of food-grade mineral oil to all surfaces. Let it soak in for 20 minutes, then wipe off the excess. Repeat this three times over three days.

For ongoing maintenance, re-oil the board every few weeks or whenever the wood looks dry.

## Notes on End Grain

End-grain boards are superior for knife work because the blade slides between the wood fibers rather than cutting across them. This is easier on both the knife edge and the board surface. An end-grain board will show fewer cut marks and last significantly longer than a face-grain or edge-grain board.
