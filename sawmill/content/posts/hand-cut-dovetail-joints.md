## Planning

The dovetail joint is one of the strongest and most visually distinctive joints in woodworking. Its interlocking pins and tails create a mechanical bond that resists being pulled apart, making it ideal for drawer construction, box corners, and case work.

This guide covers through dovetails cut entirely by hand. No jigs, no router, no power tools. Just a saw, a chisel, a marking gauge, and patience.

### Tools

- Dovetail saw (Western or Japanese, either works)
- Marking gauge
- Bevel gauge set to a 1:8 ratio for hardwood (or 1:6 for softwood)
- Sharp chisels (1/4-inch and 1/2-inch)
- Marking knife
- Mallet
- Coping saw or fret saw for waste removal

### Materials for Practice

Cherry and poplar are recommended for learning. Cherry has a fine, consistent grain that takes clean cuts and shows layout lines well. Poplar is inexpensive and forgiving, making it ideal for the first dozen attempts before moving to finer stock.

Cut practice pieces to 3/4-inch thickness, 4 inches wide, and 6 inches long. This size is comfortable to hold and clamp while learning the motions.

## Cutting

### Step 1: Set the Marking Gauge

Set the marking gauge to the exact thickness of the mating board. Scribe a baseline around both ends of both boards. This line defines how deep the joint will be and must be precise. Every cut references this line.

### Step 2: Lay Out the Tails

The tails are cut first. Using the bevel gauge set to a 1:8 slope, mark the tail angles on the end grain of the tail board. For a 4-inch-wide board, three tails with two half-pins at the edges produce a proportional and strong joint.

Space the tails evenly. Mark the waste areas clearly so there is no confusion when cutting.

### Step 3: Cut the Tails

Clamp the tail board vertically in the vise. Using the dovetail saw, cut along each layout line down to the baseline. The saw should split the line on the waste side. Accuracy matters here, but small errors in angle are less critical than keeping the saw perpendicular to the face of the board.

After the angled cuts are complete, remove the bulk of the waste between the tails with a coping saw, staying about 1/16 inch from the baseline.

### Step 4: Chop to the Baseline

Place the board flat on a solid surface. Using a sharp chisel sized to fit between the tails, chop down to the baseline. Work from both faces, meeting in the middle. A sharp chisel and controlled mallet strikes are essential here. The baseline should be a clean, square shoulder.

### Step 5: Transfer and Cut the Pins

Place the tail board on end, aligned with the pin board. Using a marking knife, trace the tail shapes onto the end grain of the pin board. This transfer must be exact, as it determines how tightly the joint will fit.

Extend the lines down the face of the pin board using a square. Cut along these lines with the dovetail saw, again staying on the waste side. Remove the waste with a coping saw and chop to the baseline.

## Assembly

### Step 6: Test the Fit

The joint should go together with firm hand pressure or a few light taps from a mallet. If it requires heavy force, identify the tight spots and pare them carefully with a chisel.

A joint that is too loose can still be glued, but the mechanical strength is reduced. A joint that is too tight risks splitting the pin board. The ideal fit is snug -- it goes together with gentle persuasion and stays put without glue.

### Step 7: Glue-Up

Apply a thin layer of glue to all mating surfaces. Assemble the joint and clamp lightly. Check for square. Wipe away any squeeze-out with a damp cloth before it dries.

## Finish

Once the glue has cured, plane or sand the end grain flush with the face of the adjoining board. A sharp block plane works best here, taking very light passes across the end grain to avoid tear-out.

The finished joint should show clean, tight lines where the pins meet the tails. In cherry, the contrast between the end grain and face grain creates a subtle visual distinction that highlights the joinery.

## Notes on Practice

Dovetails are a skill that improves only through repetition. The first few joints will have gaps. The saw cuts will wander. The chisel work will be rough. This is normal and expected.

Cut a joint every day for two weeks and the improvement will be dramatic. The motions become automatic, the saw tracks straighter, and the chisel finds the baseline with less effort.

Keep the practice joints. Line them up in order. They are a record of progress, and looking back at joint number one after completing joint twenty is one of the more satisfying moments in learning this craft.
