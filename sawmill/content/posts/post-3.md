## Overview

This desk came from an estate sale in rough condition: loose joints, a damaged writing surface, missing hardware, and decades of grime. Underneath it all was solid quarter-sawn white oak with beautiful ray fleck patterns. The goal was to make the desk fully functional again while keeping its aged character intact.

## Step 1: Assessment

Before touching any tools, document the piece thoroughly. Note every joint, every crack, every bit of damage. Understanding how the piece was originally built tells you how to repair it properly.

This desk had:

- Four loose mortise-and-tenon leg joints
- A split in the writing surface running 14 inches
- Two missing drawer pulls
- A broken drawer runner
- Heavy finish buildup and surface grime

## Step 2: Disassembly

Take the desk apart carefully, working with the original joinery rather than against it. Use a rubber mallet and a block of softwood to persuade stubborn joints. Never force a joint -- if it will not move, check for hidden fasteners.

Label every part as it comes off. I use painter's tape with a numbering system and take photographs at each stage.

## Step 3: Cleaning

Strip the old finish using a card scraper rather than chemical stripper. A sharp scraper removes finish efficiently without damaging the wood surface or the patina beneath. Work with the grain, always.

For crevices and turnings, use steel wool dampened with denatured alcohol. This dissolves old shellac and varnish without raising the grain excessively.

## Step 4: Structural Repairs

**Loose joints:** Clean all old glue from the mortises and tenons using a chisel and warm water. Apply fresh hide glue and reassemble. Hide glue is reversible, period-appropriate, and bonds well even in joints that are slightly loose.

**Split writing surface:** Open the crack slightly, work hide glue into it using a thin palette knife, then clamp closed. Once cured, plane the surface flat. The repaired crack is nearly invisible along the grain line.

**Broken drawer runner:** Mill a new runner from matching white oak. Shape it to match the original and attach it the same way -- with screws through slotted holes to allow for seasonal wood movement.

## Step 5: Hardware

The two missing drawer pulls were replaced with period-appropriate brass bail pulls sourced from a restoration hardware supplier. When replacing hardware on antique pieces, matching the original style matters more than matching the exact manufacturer.

## Step 6: Finishing

Shellac is the traditional finish for furniture of this era, and it was the right choice here. It enhances the warmth of the oak and gives the ray fleck pattern depth.

Apply shellac in thin coats with a pad (French polishing technique), building up gradually. Four coats gave a subtle sheen without looking overly glossy. Between coats, rub lightly with 0000 steel wool.

## A Note on Restoration Philosophy

Restoration is not about making an old piece look new. It is about making it functional and stable while respecting its history. The small dents, the wear patterns on the drawer edges, the slight unevenness of a hand-planed surface -- these are marks of a well-lived life. Preserve them.
