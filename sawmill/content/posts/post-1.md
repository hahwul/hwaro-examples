## Overview

This project is a tall bookshelf built from solid American black walnut, designed to fit a specific wall in a home study. The finished piece stands 72 inches tall, 36 inches wide, and 12 inches deep, with five adjustable shelves.

## Step 1: Selecting the Lumber

Walnut is a hardwood with a fine, straight grain that machines well and takes finish beautifully. For this project, I selected boards that were at least 6/4 thickness to allow for milling down to a final dimension of one inch.

Key considerations when selecting walnut:

- Look for consistent color through the board
- Avoid boards with excessive sapwood unless you want the contrast
- Check for twist and cup before purchasing
- Buy 15-20% more than your cut list requires

## Step 2: Milling and Dimensioning

All boards were first jointed on one face and one edge, then planed to final thickness. After planing, the boards rested in the shop for 48 hours to acclimate before final dimensioning.

The cut list:

- 2 side panels: 72 x 12 x 1 inches
- 5 shelves: 34 x 11.5 x 1 inches
- 1 top panel: 36 x 12 x 1 inches
- 1 bottom panel: 36 x 12 x 1 inches
- 1 back panel: 72 x 36 x 0.25 inches (plywood)

## Step 3: Joinery

The shelves sit in dado grooves routed into the side panels. Each dado is 3/8 inch deep and cut to the exact width of the shelf stock. A router with a straight bit and an edge guide made this work repeatable and precise.

The top and bottom panels are joined to the sides with rabbet joints, reinforced with screws concealed by walnut plugs.

## Step 4: Assembly

Dry-fit everything first. Once satisfied with the fit, apply glue to the dados and assemble from the bottom up. Use plenty of clamps and check for square at every stage.

The back panel is attached with small brads and glue. It adds significant rigidity to the structure.

## Step 5: Finishing

After assembly, sand the entire piece through 220 grit. Apply three coats of Danish oil, sanding lightly between coats with 320 grit. Danish oil brings out the depth of walnut's color without building up a thick film on the surface.

## Lessons Learned

The most important lesson from this build: let your wood acclimate. Two of my initial shelf blanks developed a slight cup after milling because I rushed the process. Taking the extra day to let the wood settle saves rework later.
