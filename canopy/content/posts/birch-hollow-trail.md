There are trails you hike for the destination and trails you hike for the walk itself. Birch Hollow is the latter, particularly in the first two weeks of October when the hardwood forest turns and the light filtering through the canopy shifts from green to amber to copper.

## Lower Section

The trail begins along Birch Creek, following the water upstream through a mixed forest of paper birch, sugar maple, and yellow birch. The grade is gentle for the first two miles, gaining elevation so gradually that the climb barely registers. The creek is constant company -- audible even when the trail pulls away from the bank.

The forest floor in autumn is a study in decomposition and color. Fallen leaves layer in reds and golds over mossy rocks. Mushrooms appear in clusters on rotting birch stumps -- chanterelles if you know what to look for, though we leave them for those with more confident identification skills.

## The Climb

At mile 2.5, the trail turns away from the creek and begins climbing in earnest through a series of long switchbacks. The forest transitions from hardwood to spruce and fir as elevation increases. The understory simplifies -- fewer ferns, more rock and root.

The final half mile to the lookout is steep but short. The trail emerges from the trees onto a rocky clearing where the fire lookout stands.

## Fire Lookout

The Birch Hollow lookout is a restored 1940s fire tower, staffed seasonally by volunteer fire spotters. When occupied, visitors are often invited up the stairs for a 360-degree view of the surrounding valleys. On a clear October day, the view is a sea of color stretching to every horizon.

We sat on the lookout deck for the better part of an hour, watching cloud shadows move across the ridges. The wind carried the faint sweet smell of decaying leaves from far below.

## Return

The descent follows the same route. In autumn light, the downhill walk reveals details missed on the way up -- the way afternoon sun backlights individual birch leaves, the particular quality of shadow in a hardwood forest at four in the afternoon.
