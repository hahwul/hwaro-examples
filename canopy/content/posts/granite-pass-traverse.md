The Granite Pass Traverse is not a maintained trail in any conventional sense. It follows a series of use paths, cairned routes, and open cross-country travel through some of the most remote terrain in the southern Sierra. This is a route for experienced backcountry travelers who are comfortable with map and compass navigation.

## Day One: Approach

The first eight miles climb steadily through mixed conifer forest -- Jeffrey pine and red fir giving way to whitebark pine as elevation increases. The trail is well-defined here, following an old stock route used by packers decades ago. Camp at the upper meadow at 9,400 feet, where a spring-fed stream provides reliable water through August.

## Day Two: The Pass

Leave camp early. The route above the meadow enters a boulder field with intermittent cairns marking the way. Navigation requires attention -- in fog conditions, this section demands compass bearings at minimum.

The pass itself is a narrow notch in a granite wall at 11,340 feet. The final 200 vertical feet involve Class 3 scrambling on solid but steep rock. Hands are necessary. Helmets are prudent. The scramble is brief but exposed, with significant consequences for a fall.

### Descent

The west side of the pass drops steeply through loose talus before reaching a chain of alpine tarns. The walking here is delicate -- scree slopes require careful foot placement. Below the tarns, a faint trail re-emerges and leads through krummholz forest back to the western trailhead.

## Equipment Notes

- Helmet recommended for the summit scramble
- Carry detailed topographic maps. GPS is supplemental, not primary.
- Bear canister required for overnight camping in this area
- Ice axe and crampons necessary before mid-July
- Minimum 4 liters water capacity for the pass crossing
