The American desert in March is a landscape of contradictions -- bone-dry washes running between walls of stone laid down by ancient seas, and every flat surface suddenly covered in wildflowers that will be gone within three weeks. Desert Wash Canyon captures both of these realities in a single out-and-back route.

## The Wash

The trail, such as it is, follows a sandy wash downstream between walls of Navajo sandstone. The sand is deep in places, making for slow walking, but the canyon walls are worth the effort. Layers of geological time are readable in the rock -- tan, rust, cream, and deep red bands recording hundreds of millions of years of deposition.

In the narrower sections, the wash becomes a proper slot canyon. The walls close to arm's width and rise forty feet overhead. Light enters from above in shafts, illuminating the curved sandstone in warm tones. These sections are short -- a few hundred yards at most -- but they are the highlight of the route.

## Wildflower Bench

At mile four, the canyon opens onto a broad bench dotted with desert marigold, Indian paintbrush, and globe mallow. The bloom was at its peak during our visit, and the contrast of bright petals against red sand and blue sky was striking. We turned around here, though the wash continues for several more miles into increasingly remote terrain.

## Conditions

- This route should not be attempted if rain is forecast anywhere in the watershed. Flash flood risk in slot canyons is real and deadly.
- Carry all water. There are no reliable sources along the route.
- Spring (March-April) is the optimal season. Summer temperatures routinely exceed 100 degrees.
- No shade exists for the majority of the route. Sun protection is essential.
