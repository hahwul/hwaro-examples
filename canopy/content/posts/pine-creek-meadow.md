Pine Creek Meadow is the hike I recommend to anyone returning to the mountains after a winter away. It has enough elevation gain to test your lungs, enough distance to test your legs, and a destination beautiful enough to make both feel worthwhile.

## Through the Pines

The trail starts in a dense stand of ponderosa pine at 8,580 feet. The first three miles climb at a moderate grade through forest that opens and closes rhythmically -- dense stands alternating with small clearings where wildflowers have taken hold in the available light.

The ponderosa eventually yields to Engelmann spruce and subalpine fir. The air temperature drops. The trail surface changes from dry dirt to something softer, darker, more organic. At mile four, the first patches of lingering snow appear in shaded hollows.

## The Meadow

The forest parts at mile 4.7, and the meadow reveals itself all at once. It occupies a broad basin below a ring of unnamed peaks still carrying heavy snow in mid-June. The grass is intensely green -- that particular shade that only exists in mountain meadows fed by snowmelt.

In June, the meadow floor is carpeted with columbine, lupine, and marsh marigold. A braided stream crosses the meadow's center, its channels shifting annually with snowmelt patterns. We found a dry spot near the meadow's edge, unpacked lunch, and watched a pair of red-tailed hawks working the thermals above the ridge.

## Return Notes

The descent is straightforward -- retrace the approach. Afternoon thunderstorms build reliably above 10,000 feet from mid-June onward. Plan to be below treeline by early afternoon.

- Trekking poles helpful on the descent
- Trail is usually snow-free by mid-June, though early-season conditions vary by year
- Parking lot accommodates roughly twenty vehicles
