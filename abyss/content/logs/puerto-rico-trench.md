## Background

The Puerto Rico Trench is the deepest point in the Atlantic Ocean and the Caribbean Sea, situated at the boundary between the Caribbean Plate and the North American Plate. Maximum depth: approximately 8,376 m at the Milwaukee Deep. Tonight's target: 7,920 m, a site designated PRTx-7 from sonar bathymetry conducted during the 2021 Atlantic Hadal Survey.

The Atlantic hadal ecosystem is less studied than its Pacific counterparts. The Pacific hosts the majority of deep-sea research infrastructure. The Atlantic is deeper in relative terms per trench volume, and significantly more isolated — the trench is a contained basin, its biological communities potentially evolving in greater isolation than Pacific hadal fauna.

This is the first crewed descent to PRTx-7.

---

## Launch — 0520 Local

Weather: overcast, 12-knot wind from the northeast, sea state 3. The Atlantic Explorer holds position at the trench coordinates with dynamic positioning. The submersible — a modified LF model with extended sample collection capacity — is lowered to the surface at 0520 and releases from the crane at 0541.

Descent begins immediately. The ship's silhouette recedes on the upward cameras. By 0558 it is a faint shape against grey morning sky. By 0610 there is nothing above but water.

---

## 0–800 m — Epipelagic and Upper Mesopelagic

Atlantic surface fauna: a school of Atlantic bluefin tuna follows the vehicle for approximately 400 m before losing interest. Loggerhead sea turtle at 210 m, ascending to breathe. Portuguese man o' war at the surface — its pneumatophore visible from below as a translucent blue membrane against the brightening sky.

At 600 m: the scattering layer. Denser here than Pacific analogs, possibly because of the higher productivity of the North Atlantic. Lanternfish, myctophids, krill in enormous density. The sonar return is almost solid.

At 800 m: temperature has dropped to 7.2 C. Salinity has increased. Below is the North Atlantic Deep Water — a dense, cold water mass that forms in the Labrador and Nordic Seas and flows south and then west at depth. The vehicle enters this layer and the instruments register the transition immediately.

---

## 800–3,500 m — Bathypelagic

The mid-water of the Atlantic is sparse in the way that deep space is sparse: not empty, but the distances between things are vast enough to make each encounter feel isolated.

At 1,100 m: a deep-sea anglerfish — Melanocetus johnsonii, the humpback anglerfish. Female specimen, approximately 18 cm. The bioluminescent lure — a modified dorsal spine tipped with a bacterial light organ — pulses at 0.8 Hz. The fish is stationary, lure active, waiting. It does not respond to the vehicle's approach and is still visible on the aft camera at 60 metres distance before the vehicle's descent carries it out of range.

At 2,400 m: notable absence of visible organisms for a 40-minute period. The bathypelagic desert — a real phenomenon in certain sectors of the mid-Atlantic where downwelling suppresses biological productivity. The sonar shows nothing. The cameras show nothing. The only sound is the vehicle's life support system.

At 3,100 m: a large cephalopod mantle visible at the edge of camera range. Tentacles not visible. Estimated mantle length: 80–120 cm. No species identification possible at distance and lighting conditions. Logged as unidentified cephalopod, PRTx-7 waypoint 3.

---

## 3,500–6,000 m — Abyssal

The Puerto Rico Trench floor at abyssal depth (above the hadal threshold at approximately 6,000 m) is carpeted with a calcareous ooze — pale, fine-grained, the accumulated remains of planktonic foraminifera settling from the surface over millions of years.

At 4,800 m: the first of several unusual sediment formations. Circular depressions, 40–80 cm in diameter, arranged in loose clusters. These formations are consistent with bioturbation — the disturbance of sediment by burrowing organisms — but the scale and regularity of arrangement is atypical. Documented with stereo cameras and sonar mapping. No organisms visible at the disturbance sites.

At 5,700 m: a xenophyophore — a single-celled organism that can grow to over 20 cm, making it one of the largest individual cells known — rests on the sediment surface. It is translucent, almost colourless. Its pseudopodia extend outward into the sediment. A living feature of the landscape. At this scale, the cell contains thousands of nuclei, thousands of mitochondria, an architecture of complexity inside what appears structurally simple.

---

## 6,000–7,920 m — Hadal Zone

The trench walls become prominent on sonar at 6,200 m. The descent is no longer through open ocean but through a geological structure.

At 6,800 m: the sediment layer changes character. The calcareous ooze disappears — we are below the carbonate compensation depth, where calcium carbonate dissolves into solution. What remains is a siliceous clay, red-brown, rich in radiolarian and diatom frustules that survive where carbonates cannot. The sediment has been accumulating here since the Cretaceous.

At 7,200 m: the first amphipods. Consistent with the expected distribution pattern for Atlantic hadal fauna. However, the specimens appearing at the camera margins have a distinctly different body plan from Eurythenes gryllus, the expected dominant species at this depth in the Atlantic. Larger. The pereopod arrangement appears modified.

**7,920 m — Bottom contact: 1136 local.**

---

## Bottom Operations — 7,920 m

The sediment at PRTx-7 is unlike any documented Atlantic hadal site. A dense bacterial mat extends across approximately 15 square metres of the survey area, white-grey, with a texture that resembles marine snow compressed into a surface layer. The manipulator arm depresses the mat surface by approximately 3 cm before encountering resistance. Samples collected.

The amphipods.

Three bait traps were deployed at 7,920 m. Within 4 minutes, all three traps registered significant activity on the pressure sensors. When the trap cameras were reviewed, the amphipods swarming the traps were clearly anomalous.

Morphological characteristics inconsistent with any described Atlantic hadal amphipod species:

- Body length: 31–67 mm (large for Atlantic hadal fauna)
- Uropod III significantly elongated relative to body length
- Eye structure: compound, with a reflective tapetum-like layer (unexpected at depth where light is absent)
- Antennule modification: the first segment bears a cluster of what appear to be chemosensory setae in a configuration not documented in hadal amphipoda

Sixty-two specimens recovered alive. Seventeen preserved for morphological study. Remaining forty-five alive in chilled seawater containers for genetic analysis.

If the genetic sequencing confirms what the morphology suggests, this is an undescribed genus-level taxon. The reflective eye structure is particularly anomalous — this kind of adaptation implies either a recent descent from shallower depths, or the presence of a bioluminescent light source at depth sufficient to drive maintained visual investment.

Time at bottom: 3 hours 22 minutes. Pressure: 793 atmospheres. Temperature: 2.1 C.

---

## Ascent and Recovery

Standard ascent profile. At 3,400 m, sonar detected a large biological target — estimated displacement equivalent to a cetacean of 15–25 metres — moving northeast at approximately 8 knots. Depth consistent with sperm whale foraging. The acoustic modem registered a sequence of clicks that the onboard system identified as sperm whale biosonar, directed upward.

Surface recovery at 1736. All samples secured. Amphipod cultures maintained at 2 C for transfer to the molecular biology lab.

---

## Preliminary Conclusions

PRTx-7 is an active, complex hadal community with at least one potentially undescribed macrofaunal species. The bacterial mat community and the anomalous sediment formations require further investigation — the circular bioturbation structures may be related to the amphipod colony or to a larger, as-yet unobserved burrowing organism.

The Atlantic hadal zone is understudied relative to its scientific value. The isolation of the Puerto Rico Trench from Pacific hadal systems means that its biota has been evolving independently for geological time. The morphological anomalies in the amphipod samples support this: Atlantic hadal conditions have produced fauna that looks, at the level of body plan, like a parallel solution to similar evolutionary pressures.

Full genetic results expected in six to eight weeks. A return expedition to PRTx-7 is proposed for Q1 2025.
