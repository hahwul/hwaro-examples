Lapis lazuli, the stone of heaven, has been valued for over six thousand years. Ground to powder and purified through a painstaking process of kneading, washing, and filtering, it yielded *ultramarine* -- the most precious pigment in the painter's arsenal.

## The Afghan Mines

The finest lapis came from the remote mines of Sar-e-Sang in present-day Afghanistan. Merchants carried it along the Silk Road, through Persia, and into the workshops of Venice and Florence. By the time it reached a European painter's palette, its cost rivaled that of gold.

## The Master's Choice

Because of its extraordinary expense, ultramarine was reserved for the most sacred subjects. The Virgin Mary's robe, the vast heavens above a crucifixion -- only these warranted such a pigment. Lesser blues, such as azurite or smalt, filled the rest.

> The painter who uses ultramarine must be certain of his patron's purse, for there is no pigment so costly, and none so unforgiving of hesitation.

## Synthetic Rivals

In 1826, a French chemist named Jean-Baptiste Guimet produced a synthetic ultramarine, ending centuries of scarcity. Today the color is cheap and ubiquitous, yet the original stone retains its mystique. When you see that deep, resonant blue on a fifteenth-century altarpiece, you are looking at compressed time: millions of years of geological formation, months of overland trade, and hours of meticulous grinding -- all distilled into a single brushstroke.

### A Simple Palette Test

```
Pigment          | Source       | Cost (15th c.)
-----------------|--------------|---------------
Ultramarine      | Lapis lazuli | 8 ducats/oz
Vermilion        | Cinnabar     | 2 ducats/oz
Lead white       | Lead + acid  | 0.5 ducats/oz
```

The disparity speaks volumes about the hierarchy of color in the medieval workshop.
