Before the first stroke of color, the tempera painter prepares the *ground*. A wooden panel is sanded smooth, then coated with layer after layer of gesso -- a mixture of chalk or gypsum bound with rabbit-skin glue. Each coat is applied thin, allowed to dry, and sanded again.

## The Logic of Layers

A single panel may receive eight to twelve coats. The goal is a surface that is perfectly flat, blindingly white, and smooth as bone. This luminous ground is not merely structural; it is optical. Tempera paint is translucent. The light passes through the pigment, bounces off the gesso beneath, and returns to the viewer's eye carrying the color with it.

Without a proper ground, the colors look dull and lifeless. The gesso is the hidden actor in every great panel painting.

## Rabbit-Skin Glue

The traditional binder for gesso is made by soaking and heating rabbit skins. The resulting glue is strong, flexible, and has been used for centuries. Modern substitutes exist, but many conservators still prefer the original formula for its proven durability.

## Modern Parallels

In web design, the "ground" is the background. A pure white or a carefully chosen off-white serves the same purpose as gesso: it allows the foreground elements -- text, images, structural lines -- to appear vivid and intentional. A cluttered or poorly chosen background diminishes everything placed upon it.

The lesson of gesso is simple: **prepare the surface before you paint**.
