In tempera painting, there are no soft edges. The medium dries too quickly for blending. Every form is built up from thin, hatched strokes laid side by side -- a technique that naturally produces crisp, defined boundaries.

## Constraints as Virtue

Where oil painting permits the artist to blur one form into another, tempera demands clarity. Each shape must be planned. Each boundary must be deliberate. There is no room for ambiguity, and none for correction.

This is not a limitation; it is a discipline. The hard edge forces the painter to commit. The result is an image with extraordinary visual authority -- every element placed with precision and defended by a clear border.

## Borders in Design

The same principle applies to layout and typography. A card with a firm border reads as more intentional than one that fades into the page. A heading underscored by a solid rule carries more weight than one floating in whitespace.

> Borders are not decoration. They are structure. Remove them, and the composition dissolves.

Consider the difference:

- A box with a 4px solid border and a hard shadow announces itself.
- A box with a subtle gradient border and a diffused glow whispers.

Both are valid, but this theme chooses the former. It chooses the bold line, the definite boundary, the unapologetic frame.

## The Hatching Technique

Tempera painters build tone through *hatching*: parallel strokes of slightly varying color laid in overlapping layers. Up close, the individual strokes are visible. At a distance, they merge into smooth, luminous surfaces.

This is analogous to how a well-structured page works. The individual components -- headings, paragraphs, images, rules -- are distinct and visible. Together, they form a coherent whole greater than the sum of its parts.

### The Principles

1. Every element has a clear boundary.
2. No boundary is accidental.
3. Structure is visible, never hidden.
4. Flatness is honest; depth is earned.
