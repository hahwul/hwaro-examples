## Identification

Medium-sized shark with a broadly rounded snout and large, round eyes. The body is grey above and white below, with a distinctive dark trailing edge on the caudal (tail) fin. First dorsal fin is plain grey without markings -- this distinguishes it from the blacktip reef shark.

Typical length at maturity: 1.5-2.0 meters.

## Habitat and Behavior

Found primarily along outer reef slopes, channel entrances, and walls with strong current flow. They are most active during incoming tides when nutrient-rich water draws prey species into the reef zone.

Grey reef sharks are social and often patrol in loose groups of 5-20 individuals. They exhibit a well-documented **threat display** when feeling cornered: exaggerated side-to-side swimming, arched back, and lowered pectoral fins. If this display is observed, divers should increase distance slowly without rapid movement.

## Dive Encounters

Consistently sighted at Blue Corner (Palau) during drift dives. Best observation method is to hook into the reef at 20-25 meters and remain still. They will often approach to investigate stationary divers, passing within 3-5 meters.

Morning and late afternoon dives produce the highest encounter rates, correlating with known feeding activity windows.

## Conservation

Listed as Near Threatened (IUCN). Populations are stable in well-managed marine protected areas but declining in regions with unregulated fishing. Reef shark populations are a reliable indicator of overall reef ecosystem health.
