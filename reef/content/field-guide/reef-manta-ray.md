## Identification

Large ray with a diamond-shaped body and distinctive cephalic fins (horn-like structures flanking the mouth used to direct water flow during feeding). Dorsal surface is dark with lighter shoulder patches that vary between individuals. Ventral surface displays a unique pattern of dark spots and blotches -- these markings are used for individual identification, similar to human fingerprints.

Distinguished from the oceanic manta (*Mobula birostris*) by smaller maximum size and the absence of a caudal spine remnant.

## Habitat and Behavior

Reef mantas are found in coastal waters, frequently visiting specific cleaning stations where small wrasses and other cleaner fish remove parasites from their skin, gills, and mouth. They return to the same stations repeatedly, sometimes daily.

**Feeding behavior** involves barrel rolling at the surface or in mid-water when plankton concentrations are high. During feeding, the cephalic fins unfurl to create a funnel directing water into the mouth.

**Cleaning station protocol:** Mantas approach from downstream and hover over the cleaning bommie, often reducing their speed to near zero. Cleaner wrasses focus on the gill slits, mouth, and ventral surface.

## Dive Encounters

The most reliable encounters are at known cleaning stations during slack tide. Position 8-10 meters from the station at or below the manta's depth. Avoid bubbles rising directly into the manta's path -- approach from the side.

Mantas are curious and may approach divers closely if no sudden movements are made. Avoid touching or chasing -- this disrupts cleaning behavior and can cause the individual to leave the station.

## Conservation

Listed as Vulnerable (IUCN). Threats include targeted fishing for gill plates, bycatch, and habitat degradation. Many key sites are now within marine protected areas. Individual photo-ID databases help monitor population health and movement patterns.
