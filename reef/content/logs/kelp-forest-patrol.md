## Conditions

Overcast skies with filtered light penetrating the canopy. Surface temperature was 16C, dropping to 14C at depth. Visibility was 10 meters -- typical for this time of year. Mild swell from the northwest created gentle surge in the shallows but was unnoticeable below 8 meters.

7mm wetsuit with hood and gloves. Adequate for the duration but cold was noticeable in the last 10 minutes.

## The Dive

The kelp forest at this site is mature, with giant kelp (*Macrocystis pyrifera*) canopy reaching the surface from holdfasts at 18-20 meters. Moving through the stipes is like navigating a vertical forest -- shafts of filtered light create columns of visibility between the dense growth.

At 15 meters, a **harbor seal** appeared at the edge of visibility, circling twice at speed before approaching to within 3 meters. It maintained eye contact for approximately 30 seconds, then spiraled upward through the kelp and out of sight. A second seal was spotted at 10 meters depth 5 minutes later, resting in a gap in the canopy.

The rocky reef substrate supports heavy invertebrate coverage:

- **Purple sea urchins** in clusters of 20-40 in exposed areas
- **Bat stars** (orange and purple morphs) on vertical rock faces
- **Spanish shawl nudibranchs** -- counted 7 individuals on a single rock shelf
- **Spiny lobster** antennae visible in multiple crevices

Several **garibaldi** were actively defending territories on the reef top. Their bright orange coloration was conspicuous even in reduced visibility.

## Post-Dive Notes

Cold-water kelp diving rewards slow movement and close attention to substrate. The macro life density exceeds most tropical reefs. Recommend 5mm minimum with hood -- the cold limits bottom time more than air supply at this depth.
