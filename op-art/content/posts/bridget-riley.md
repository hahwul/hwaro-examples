Bridget Riley's career spans over six decades, but her most iconic works date from the early 1960s. Paintings like *Movement in Squares* (1961) and *Fall* (1963) demonstrated that pure abstraction — without any representational content — could produce a visceral physical response in the viewer.

## From Flat to Dynamic

Riley's early black-and-white works are exercises in precision. Every stripe, curve, and interval is calibrated to create maximum visual disruption. The canvases appear to ripple, bulge, and vibrate, despite being perfectly flat paint on perfectly flat surfaces.

Her method was systematic:

1. Begin with a simple geometric unit — a line, a circle, an ellipse
2. Repeat the unit across the canvas with calculated variation
3. Introduce gradual shifts in spacing, width, or curvature
4. Let the accumulated distortions create emergent visual effects

## The Color Period

In the late 1960s, Riley introduced color. Rather than diminishing the optical effects, color amplified them. Stripes of complementary hues placed side by side create a shimmer that monochrome cannot achieve.

Her stripe paintings are deceptively simple: vertical bands of color, edge to edge. But the careful selection and sequencing of hues produces an experience closer to music than painting — rhythm, harmony, and dissonance rendered in pigment.

## Legacy

Riley's work proved that abstraction does not require emotional ambiguity. Her paintings produce clear, measurable effects on the viewer's perception. They are experiments in visual science as much as works of art.
