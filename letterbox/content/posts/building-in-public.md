Most blogs present finished thoughts. Polished essays. Final products. But some of the most compelling writing on the internet comes from people who share their process -- the rough drafts, the dead ends, the things that didn't work.

## The Newsletter Advantage

The newsletter format encourages this kind of transparency. When you number your issues and publish on a regular cadence, you create an implicit contract with your readers: this is a journey, not a destination.

Issue 1 doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be honest.

## What Building in Public Looks Like

Here are a few patterns that work well in a newsletter format:

1. **Weekly updates** -- Share what you worked on, what you learned, and what's next
2. **Design decisions** -- Explain why you chose one approach over another
3. **Mistakes and corrections** -- When something breaks or changes, write about it
4. **Reader responses** -- Incorporate feedback from your audience

## The Archive as a Timeline

One advantage of Letterbox's archive-style layout is that it creates a natural timeline. Readers who discover you through Issue 47 can scroll back through every issue and watch your thinking evolve.

This is something social media can never provide. Posts disappear into feeds. Threads get buried. But a numbered archive? That's a body of work.

## Start Before You're Ready

The best time to start a newsletter was a year ago. The second best time is now. Pick a topic, set a cadence, and publish Issue 1. You can figure out the rest as you go.

That's the beauty of building in public. You don't need a plan. You need a habit.
