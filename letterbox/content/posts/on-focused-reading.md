There is a reason the best reading experiences share a common trait: constraint. Books have margins. Newspapers have columns. Email newsletters have a fixed width. The constraint is not a limitation -- it is a feature.

## The 60-Character Rule

Typographers have known for centuries that the optimal line length for body text is between 45 and 75 characters. Go wider, and the eye struggles to find the next line. Go narrower, and reading becomes choppy.

Letterbox uses a 660px container with serif typography at 17px. This lands comfortably in the sweet spot -- roughly 65 characters per line.

## Why Serif?

Source Serif 4 was chosen for body text because of its generous x-height and open letterforms. Serif fonts provide small visual anchors that guide the eye along each line, reducing fatigue during long reads.

> "The best typography is invisible. It lets the reader focus on what matters -- the words."

For UI elements like navigation, dates, and metadata, we switch to Inter. This creates a clear visual hierarchy: serif for content, sans-serif for interface.

## White Space is Not Empty Space

Notice the generous padding inside the letter container. The space between sections. The breathing room around headings. None of this is wasted. Every pixel of white space is working to reduce cognitive load and keep attention on the text.

## The Paper Metaphor

The white card on a warm background is intentional. It evokes a physical letter -- something you hold, something that has edges and weight. The subtle border reinforces this: your content is contained, precious, worth framing.

This is not minimalism for its own sake. It is minimalism in service of reading.
