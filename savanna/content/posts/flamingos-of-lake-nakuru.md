From a distance, Lake Nakuru appears to have a pink shoreline. Move closer and the color resolves into individual forms: hundreds of thousands of lesser flamingos standing in the shallow alkaline water, their heads bent downward as they filter-feed on the cyanobacteria and diatoms that thrive in this harsh, soda-rich environment. At peak concentrations, Lake Nakuru has hosted more than a million flamingos at once, creating one of the most visually striking wildlife spectacles anywhere on Earth.

The lesser flamingo is uniquely adapted to life on alkaline lakes. Its bill contains a specialized filtering apparatus -- rows of lamellae that strain microscopic food particles from the water while excluding salt and sediment. The bird feeds with its head inverted, pumping water through the bill with rapid movements of the tongue. This feeding method is so efficient that a single flamingo can process several liters of water per minute. The carotenoid pigments in their algal diet are responsible for the distinctive pink coloration of their feathers, a signal of health and feeding success that plays a role in mate selection.

Lake Nakuru sits in Kenya's Great Rift Valley, one of a chain of alkaline lakes that stretches from Tanzania to Ethiopia. These lakes are ecologically fragile systems, sensitive to changes in water chemistry, rainfall patterns, and human activity. Industrial pollution, agricultural runoff, and water diversion projects upstream have altered the chemical balance of several Rift Valley lakes in recent decades, sometimes causing dramatic crashes in the algal blooms that flamingos depend upon. When conditions at one lake deteriorate, the birds relocate to another, but suitable alternatives are limited and increasingly degraded.

The lesser flamingo is classified as Near Threatened by the International Union for Conservation of Nature. An estimated 2.2 to 3.2 million individuals survive, with the majority concentrated in East Africa. Their dependence on a small number of alkaline breeding sites -- most critically Lake Natron in Tanzania, where the majority of the species reproduces -- makes them acutely vulnerable to any disturbance at those locations. Protecting the integrity of these alkaline lake ecosystems is essential not only for flamingos but for the broader web of specialized organisms that have evolved to inhabit one of the planet's most extreme aquatic environments.
