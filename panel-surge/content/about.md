## A conference of collisions, not presentations

Panel Surge was founded in 2014 by a group of moderators who had spent fifteen years on the conference circuit and had come to the same conclusion from different directions: that the panel, as an event format, had been devoured by the keynote. Most &ldquo;panels&rdquo; had become a series of short presentations with a moderator who read out the speakers&rsquo; biographies and thanked everyone at the end.

We decided to put the collision back.

## Our format

Every session at Panel Surge is a panel. There are no keynotes, no fireside chats, no masterclasses, and no presentations. The only exception is the end-of-day reflection, which is a closed session for moderators.

A panel has three components:

1. **A question.** Every panel has exactly one question, set in advance, printed on the programme. The question is interrogative and has a yes/no answer in principle, though we expect the panel to disagree about what the answer is.
2. **Panelists who disagree.** We do not invite panels of people who agree with each other. Every panel has at least two panelists who will take opposing positions, named in advance.
3. **A moderator.** The moderator is a professional. All our moderators have been trained on our moderator programme, which runs eight weeks over the autumn before the conference.

## The audience vote

For every debate-format panel, the audience votes at the start and at the end. The panel &ldquo;wins&rdquo; if the vote has moved in the direction of the panel&rsquo;s stronger case.

No panel has won by more than eleven per cent in our twelve-year history. Most panels change the audience&rsquo;s mind by two or three per cent. The standing joke in the moderator room is that Panel Surge is the only conference whose purpose is to slightly inconvenience the audience&rsquo;s priors.

## Moderator rules

Our moderators follow a standing code:

- **Speak last.** The moderator&rsquo;s opinion is never the first thing said on any topic.
- **Cut off fairly.** A moderator cuts off every panelist at the same margin, not the ones they happen to disagree with.
- **Pass, do not seize.** The microphone is passed by the moderator. It is never seized by a panelist.
- **Questions after, not during.** Audience questions come after the moderator declares the panel closed. No exceptions.

## Panelist rules

- **Arrive prepared.** You were given the question. You were given the names of the other panelists. You had six weeks.
- **Answer the question.** Do not redirect to a question you prefer.
- **Disagree specifically.** &ldquo;I respectfully disagree&rdquo; is not a disagreement; it is a preamble. We want to hear the sentence that follows.
- **Yield when cut.** The moderator has the floor; when the microphone leaves you, it leaves.

## Rooms

- **Stage A** &mdash; main floor, 480 seats, used for the keynote panel and the end-of-day closing.
- **Stage B** &mdash; east wing, 220 seats, used for the paired panels and the rapid-fire format.
- **Stage C** &mdash; basement, 120 seats, the smallest room and, in our experience, the one in which panels go most wrong.
- **The Bridge** &mdash; a closed room used only for moderator-only sessions. No audience, no recording.

## How to be on a panel

We do not accept applications. Panelists are invited by the programme committee, which meets twice a year. If you would like to be considered for a future panel, the most useful thing you can do is to be on a bad panel elsewhere and disagree well.

If we have not invited you yet, it is because we have not yet found the right panel for you. Keep being interesting in public.
