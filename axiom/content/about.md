## Design Philosophy

Axiom is not merely a collection of components. It is a system governed by a single mathematical constant: phi, the golden ratio, approximately 1.618033988749.

Where most design systems rely on arbitrary spacing scales and typographic choices, Axiom derives every proportion from phi. The result is a visual language where each element relates to every other element through a shared mathematical ancestry.

## Why the Golden Ratio

The golden ratio appears throughout nature, architecture, and art. From the spiral of a nautilus shell to the proportions of the Parthenon, phi produces a sense of balance that humans perceive as inherently harmonious.

In practical terms, a phi-based system offers several advantages:

- **Consistent rhythm.** Spacing values at 1rem, 1.618rem, 2.618rem, and 4.236rem create a visual cadence that feels neither too tight nor too loose.
- **Scalable hierarchy.** Typography sized at phi intervals produces headings that are distinctly differentiated without being jarring.
- **Predictable relationships.** Any two adjacent values in the scale share the same ratio, making layout decisions systematic rather than subjective.

## The Grid

Axiom's background grid is not decorative. It serves as a persistent reference plane, a reminder that every element sits within a structured coordinate space. The 60-pixel grid module subdivides cleanly at phi intervals, providing anchor points for alignment.

## Geometric Primitives

Three shapes form the semantic foundation of Axiom:

**The Circle** represents continuity, cycles, and completeness. In interface terms, circles appear as avatars, status indicators, and progress rings. The circle has no beginning and no end, making it ideal for elements that represent ongoing states.

**The Triangle** represents direction, hierarchy, and decision. Triangles serve as navigation cues, dropdown indicators, and sorting arrows. Their inherent asymmetry creates visual tension that draws the eye.

**The Square** represents stability, containment, and structure. Squares and rectangles form the basis of cards, containers, and grid cells. Their regularity provides a foundation upon which other shapes can contrast.

## Color as Structure

Axiom's palette is intentionally restrained. The primary color is near-black (#212121), ensuring maximum readability. The accent gold (#C6A700) provides emphasis without competing for attention. Gray tones (#E0E0E0, #F5F5F5, #BDBDBD) define boundaries and surfaces.

This limited palette forces hierarchy to emerge from proportion and spacing rather than color alone, a more durable approach that remains coherent across contexts.

## Principles

1. **Derive, do not decide.** Every measurement should trace back to phi. If a value cannot be derived, question whether it belongs.
2. **Structure over decoration.** The grid, the shapes, and the palette exist to organize information, not to embellish it.
3. **Silence is a feature.** Whitespace governed by phi is as deliberate as any visible element. Do not fill space simply because it exists.
4. **Mathematics is universal.** A system built on mathematical constants requires no cultural translation. Proportion communicates across contexts.
