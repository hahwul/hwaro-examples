Before social media, before event apps, before digital marketing — there were flyers. Photocopied, wheat-pasted, handed out at record shops and after-hours clubs. These small pieces of paper carried the entire visual identity of rave culture.

## The Constraints

Rave flyer designers worked under extreme conditions:

- **Time** — events were announced days or hours before they happened
- **Budget** — most flyers were photocopied in black and white
- **Anonymity** — designers rarely signed their work
- **Legal risk** — many events were unlicensed, so flyers had to communicate without being too explicit

These constraints produced a distinctive aesthetic: dense, layered, energetic, and deliberately cryptic.

## Visual Language

Common elements of rave flyer design:

- **Smiley faces** — the universal symbol of acid house
- **Spiral patterns** — visual representations of altered states
- **Distorted type** — stretched, rotated, and overlapping text
- **Collage** — cut-and-paste imagery mixing found photos, illustrations, and abstract patterns
- **Information density** — lineups, dates, addresses, and prices packed into every available space

## Legacy

The anonymous designers of rave flyers created one of the most distinctive graphic traditions of the 20th century. Their work influenced a generation of graphic designers, from David Carson's deconstructed magazine layouts to the Y2K aesthetic that dominated early web design.

Today, acid graphics revivalists mine this archive for inspiration — not copying the style, but channeling its energy and disregard for convention.
