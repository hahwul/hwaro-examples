# The Calm Before the Storm: A Warning We Keep Ignoring

There is a peculiar silence that settles over a coastline in the hours before a major storm. The birds disappear. The air becomes heavy and still. The ocean, which will soon become an instrument of destruction, lies flat and glassy, almost apologetic.

We have come to know this silence well. Too well.

## The Pattern

Every decade, we rebuild. Every decade, the storms return. The insurance claims are filed, the federal aid is distributed, the foundations are poured again in the same locations, at the same elevations, with the same assumptions about what the ocean will and will not do.

This is not resilience. This is repetition.

## By the Numbers

The cost of coastal storm damage has increased at a rate that outpaces both inflation and population growth. The reasons are straightforward:

- Coastal populations have tripled since 1960
- Property values in flood-prone zones have increased faster than inland areas
- Infrastructure investment has favored development over protection
- Building codes have lagged behind updated risk assessments

## What the Data Shows

Atmospheric scientists have documented a measurable increase in the proportion of storms reaching the highest intensity categories. The physics behind this trend are well understood: warmer ocean surfaces provide more energy to developing storm systems.

The debate is no longer about whether this trend exists. It is about the rate of change and the timeline on which coastal communities must adapt.

## A Different Approach

Some jurisdictions have begun implementing managed retreat programs, offering buyouts to property owners in repeatedly flooded areas. These programs are politically difficult but economically rational. Every dollar spent on buyouts saves an estimated four to seven dollars in future disaster relief.

Others are investing in natural infrastructure: restored wetlands, living shorelines, and barrier island preservation. These approaches work with the coastal environment rather than against it.

## The Choice

The calm before the storm is not an absence of danger. It is a window of opportunity. How we use that window -- whether we prepare or proceed as if the silence will last -- defines the outcome of what follows.

The storms will come. The question is whether we will be ready.
