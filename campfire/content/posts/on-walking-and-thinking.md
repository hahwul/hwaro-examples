Yuki Ando has walked over twelve thousand kilometers across Japan, Korea, and parts of Europe. She is also a philosophy professor at Waseda University, specializing in phenomenology and embodied cognition. We spoke while walking a five-kilometer loop through a park in Tokyo.

<!-- more -->

**Campfire:** Why do you walk?

> **Yuki Ando:** Because thinking is a physical act. We pretend it happens in the head, but anyone who has tried to solve a problem while sitting in a chair knows that is not entirely true. When I walk, my thoughts synchronize with my steps. There is a rhythm to it. Ideas arrive that would never come to me at a desk.

**Campfire:** The walking-and-thinking connection has a long history. Aristotle, Kierkegaard, Thoreau.

> **Yuki Ando:** Nietzsche said he did not trust any thought that was not born in the open air. I think he was being dramatic, but he was also right. The history of philosophy is full of walkers, and I do not think that is a coincidence. There is something about forward motion that keeps the mind from getting stuck.

**Campfire:** Can you describe what happens to your thinking when you walk for days at a time?

> **Yuki Ando:** The first day is noise. Your mind is still full of whatever you left behind --- emails, obligations, arguments. By the second day, the noise starts to thin. By the third day, you are thinking differently. Not faster, but wider. You start to see connections between things that seemed unrelated. The landscape helps. A river does not care about your deadline. A mountain does not know your name. That indifference is freeing.

**Campfire:** You walk alone most of the time. Is that essential?

> **Yuki Ando:** For the kind of thinking I am describing, yes. Walking with a companion is wonderful, but it is a different activity. When you walk alone, the silence becomes your partner. You start a conversation with yourself that is more honest than any conversation you could have with another person, because there is no audience. No one to perform for.

**Campfire:** Is there a danger in romanticizing this?

> **Yuki Ando:** Of course. Walking is not magic. It will not fix your life or solve your problems. But it will give you the space to see them more clearly. And sometimes clarity is enough. Sometimes you do not need an answer. You just need to understand the question better.

**Campfire:** What is the longest you have walked without stopping?

> **Yuki Ando:** Fourteen hours, through the mountains in Kumano. I did not intend to walk that long. I simply forgot to stop. That is the best sign that you are doing something right --- when the doing disappears and you are just in it.
