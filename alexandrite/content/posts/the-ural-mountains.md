The Ural Mountains stretch like a spine across Russia, dividing Europe from Asia. It was here, in 1830, that miners working in an emerald deposit near the Tokovaya River discovered something unexpected: stones that changed color.

## A Royal Discovery

The gemstone was named after Tsar Alexander II, who was then heir to the Russian throne. The stone's colors — green and red — happened to mirror the colors of Imperial Russia, making it an instant sensation among the aristocracy.

## Mining History

The original Russian deposits were largely exhausted by the end of the 19th century. Today, alexandrite is found in:

- **Brazil** — the most significant modern source
- **Sri Lanka** — producing stones with subtle color change
- **East Africa** — Tanzania and Madagascar yield fine specimens
- **India** — occasional finds in the Andhra Pradesh region

## Legacy in Design

The Ural Mountains' harsh beauty — snow-covered peaks giving way to deep green valleys — mirrors the alexandrite's own dramatic contrasts. This duality of extremes is a core principle of the theme you're viewing: the tension between emerald tranquility and ruby intensity creates something more compelling than either alone.
