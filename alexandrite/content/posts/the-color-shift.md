Alexandrite is nature's own light show. Discovered in Russia's Ural Mountains in 1830, this remarkable chrysoberyl variety shifts from vivid emerald green in daylight to deep ruby red under incandescent light.

## The Science of Color Change

The phenomenon occurs because alexandrite absorbs light in a narrow band of the yellow spectrum. In daylight — which is rich in blue and green wavelengths — the stone appears green. Under tungsten light — which emphasizes red wavelengths — the same stone glows ruby.

> "Emerald by day, ruby by night." — A traditional description of alexandrite

## Rarity and Value

Natural alexandrite with strong color change is among the rarest gemstones on Earth. Fine specimens can command prices exceeding diamonds of equivalent size.

Key factors affecting value:

1. **Strength of color change** — the more dramatic the shift, the more valuable
2. **Saturation** — vivid greens and reds are preferred over muted tones
3. **Clarity** — fewer inclusions mean higher value
4. **Size** — stones over one carat with strong change are exceptionally rare
