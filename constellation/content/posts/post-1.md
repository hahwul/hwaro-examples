## Overview

Orion is among the most conspicuous and recognizable constellations in the night sky. Straddling the celestial equator, it is visible from virtually every inhabited location on Earth at some point during the year. Its brightest stars form a distinctive hourglass or bow-tie pattern that has been noted by observers for millennia.

## Principal Stars

### Betelgeuse (Alpha Orionis)

A red supergiant marking Orion's left shoulder, Betelgeuse is a semiregular variable star with an apparent magnitude that fluctuates between approximately 0.0 and 1.6. At roughly 700 light-years distant, it is one of the largest stars visible to the unaided eye, with an estimated diameter exceeding 1,000 solar radii.

### Rigel (Beta Orionis)

Rigel anchors the lower right of the figure. Despite its Beta designation, it is typically the brightest star in the constellation at magnitude 0.13. It is a blue-white supergiant at approximately 860 light-years, luminosity estimated at 120,000 times that of the Sun.

### The Belt Stars

The three belt stars -- Alnitak, Alnilam, and Mintaka -- form a nearly straight line spanning about 3 degrees. All three are hot, luminous blue supergiants at distances between 800 and 2,000 light-years.

## Deep-Sky Objects

### M42: The Great Orion Nebula

The most celebrated diffuse nebula in the sky, M42 is visible to the unaided eye as a fuzzy patch in Orion's sword. Through modest apertures, it reveals intricate structure: dark lanes, bright ridges, and the Trapezium Cluster at its heart. Distance: approximately 1,344 light-years.

### M43

An extension of the Orion Nebula complex, separated from M42 by a prominent dark lane. It surrounds the variable star NU Orionis.

### The Horsehead Nebula (Barnard 33)

A dark nebula silhouetted against the emission nebula IC 434, just south of Alnitak. Challenging visually, it is a rewarding astrophotography target.

## Observing Tips

Orion is best observed from November through March in the Northern Hemisphere. The constellation rises in the east shortly after sunset in December and is at its highest around local midnight in January.

- **Unaided eye**: trace the full figure, locate the belt, and spot M42 as a hazy patch in the sword
- **Binoculars**: resolve M42 into a cloud of nebulosity and split several double stars
- **Telescope (4-inch+)**: the Trapezium resolves into four stars; nebula detail becomes dramatic

---

Orion remains one of the finest entry points for anyone beginning a serious study of the night sky. Its combination of bright stars, contrasting spectral types, and accessible deep-sky objects makes it an inexhaustible subject of observation.
