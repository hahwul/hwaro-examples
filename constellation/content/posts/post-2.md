## Overview

The Pleiades (Messier 45) is the most prominent open star cluster in the sky and one of the nearest to Earth. Located in the constellation Taurus, it is visible to the unaided eye as a compact group of stars often likened to a tiny dipper. Under good conditions, most observers can distinguish six or seven members; keen-eyed observers under dark skies may count nine or more.

## Physical Characteristics

The cluster contains over 1,000 confirmed members spread across a volume roughly 43 light-years in diameter. The core is dominated by hot blue B-type stars, the brightest of which are named after figures from Greek mythology: Alcyone, Atlas, Electra, Maia, Merope, Taygeta, and Pleione.

## Reflection Nebulosity

Long-exposure photographs reveal the cluster is embedded in a faint blue reflection nebula. This material is interstellar dust through which the cluster is currently passing; it is not the remnant of the cloud from which the stars formed. The brightest patch of nebulosity surrounds Merope (NGC 1435) and can be glimpsed through large amateur telescopes under exceptional conditions.

## Age and Evolution

At approximately 100 million years old, the Pleiades is a relatively young cluster. Its brightest members have not yet exhausted their core hydrogen. Over the next several hundred million years, dynamical interactions will gradually disperse the cluster into the general galactic stellar population.

## Observing Notes

### Unaided Eye

Count the visible stars. Six are easy; seven require modest attention. Attempting a count under varying sky conditions is a useful gauge of transparency and limiting magnitude.

### Binoculars

A superb target in any binocular. A 7x50 or 10x50 pair reveals dozens of stars across a field roughly 1.5 degrees wide. The cluster's subtle blue tint may be apparent.

### Telescope

In telescopes of 8 inches or more, the reflection nebulosity around Merope becomes accessible. Use low magnification and a nebula filter if available. The cluster fills a wide field best appreciated at low power.

## Seasonal Visibility

The Pleiades is a hallmark of the autumn and winter sky in the Northern Hemisphere. It rises in the east in September evenings and remains well-placed for observation through March, reaching its highest altitude around midnight in November.

---

The Pleiades has served as a celestial calendar and navigational reference across cultures for thousands of years. Its compact brilliance and easy visibility ensure it remains a primary target for observers at every level of experience.
