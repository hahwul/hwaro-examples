## The Purpose

A trial balance is a list of every ledger account, with its current debit or credit balance,
struck at a moment in time. It is prepared as a check on the bookkeeping: if the entries
have been correctly posted in double-entry, the total of debits will exactly equal the total
of credits, and the list will balance. If the list does not balance, an error has been made
somewhere in the books, and the clerk must find it.

The trial balance does not, by itself, prove that the books are correct. An entry posted
twice to the wrong account will leave the trial balance in agreement while distorting the
underlying truth. But the trial balance will certainly catch an entry posted only once, or
an entry posted with unequal amounts on its two sides.

## The Reconciliation

> The trial balance is the first lie every bookkeeper tells themselves.

This observation, which the editor heard as an apprentice from an elderly Welsh auditor,
is less cynical than it sounds. The point is that the trial balance gives a false sense of
completion. It does what it claims &#8212; it proves that the books are arithmetically
consistent &#8212; but it says nothing about whether the transactions it records actually
happened.

The reconciliation proper &#8212; checking bank statements against ledger entries, invoices
against payments, stock counts against stock cards &#8212; comes after. A clerk who stops
at the trial balance has done half the job.

## On Discovering an Error

When the trial balance does not balance, there is a sequence of checks the careful
bookkeeper performs, in order:

1. Re-add the columns.
2. Look for an entry that has been transposed (e.g. 45.00 written as 54.00). Such errors always leave a difference divisible by 9.
3. Look for an entry that has been posted to the wrong side of an account. Such errors leave a difference equal to double the entry.
4. Look for an entry that has been omitted entirely. Such errors leave a difference equal to the entry itself.

If the difference is none of these, the error is probably more than one error in combination, and the clerk settles in for a long afternoon.

## What Balance Means

The value of the balance, in bookkeeping, is not philosophical but operational. A balanced
trial balance means the books may now be closed and the statements drawn up. An unbalanced
trial balance means they may not. This is the whole of the discipline &#8212; a disciplined
refusal to move forward until the arithmetic is clean.
