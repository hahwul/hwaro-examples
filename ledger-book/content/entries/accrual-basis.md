## Cash Basis

Under cash-basis accounting, a transaction is recognised when cash changes hands: income
when it is received, expense when it is paid. This is the bookkeeping of the small
household and the very small business; it is also the native form in which a bank statement
tells a story.

## Accrual Basis

Under accrual-basis accounting, a transaction is recognised when it is earned or incurred,
regardless of when the cash changes hands. A publication that ships a subscriber their
first issue in December, for an annual subscription paid in advance in October, recognises
one-twelfth of that revenue in each of October, November, December, and onward through
September of the following year. The cash was received in October; the revenue is earned
over twelve months.

## The Consequence

The accrual basis produces a more accurate picture of the activity of an enterprise at any
given date. It also, not coincidentally, makes it much easier to see when an enterprise is
lying to itself.

A bookshop on the cash basis, which receives a large advance payment for a bulk order of
textbooks, looks profitable in the month of the payment. The accrual basis correctly
recognises only the portion of that revenue which has actually been earned &#8212; which
is to say, only the portion for which the textbooks have actually been delivered. If none
have been delivered, none is revenue.

> What you have received is not what you have earned.

## The Moral

There is a small ethical gesture embedded in the accrual basis: it asks the bookkeeper to
distinguish between money in the hand and value honestly owed to the organisation. That
distinction is not always comfortable. An enterprise that looks prosperous under cash-basis
accounting may look thin under accruals, because half of its apparent income has not yet
been earned.

The cash basis is easier. The accrual basis is more honest.
