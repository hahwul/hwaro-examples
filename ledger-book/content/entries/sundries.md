## The Line

Near the foot of most ledgers, below the numbered accounts and above the closing totals,
there is a line marked *Sundries* or *Miscellaneous*. Into this line go the small
transactions that do not fit cleanly into any of the regular categories. A bookkeeper who
is being careful will use it sparingly; a bookkeeper who is being honest will use it at
all.

## The Honesty

A common failing among apprentice bookkeepers is the refusal to use Sundries. They believe,
reasonably enough, that every transaction ought to belong to a proper account. So they
create a new account each time one is needed, until the chart of accounts has two hundred
entries, most of which see a single posting per year, and the books become impossible to
navigate.

The more experienced clerk accepts that some small transactions do not merit their own
account and belongs to Sundries &#8212; not because they lack importance but because the
cost of creating a proper account for each would exceed the value of the classification.

## The Limit

Sundries is a line, not a refuge. A competent auditor will inspect it; a suspicious one
will inspect it closely. If, over the course of a fiscal year, Sundries has accumulated
more than a few percent of total expenditure, the audit will ask why, and the answer will
need to be better than &#8220;we didn&#39;t know where else to put it.&#8221;

> Sundries is the account where honesty ends and embarrassment begins.

## A Rule of Thumb

The editor recommends the following rule, which is not standard but works well in practice:
any single transaction exceeding one percent of quarterly turnover warrants its own account.
Any account attracting fewer than four postings per year, or whose total balance is less
than one-tenth of one percent of turnover, may be folded back into Sundries at the close of
the next reporting period.

The object is a chart of accounts that is lean enough to be read in full, and a Sundries
line that is small enough to be defended in an audit without a blush.
