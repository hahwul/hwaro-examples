The Vela Pulsar spins at approximately 11.2 rotations per second. That raw frequency sits well above the range of musical tempo, but when we apply harmonic subdivision and map it down through octave folding, something extraordinary emerges: a pulse remarkably close to 72 beats per minute.

## The Human Connection

Seventy-two BPM is not arbitrary. It is the average resting heart rate of a healthy adult. When we sit in the control room at the Parkes Observatory and listen to the Vela signal processed through our audio pipeline, the rhythm feels intimate. It feels alive.

The coincidence is, of course, just that -- a coincidence. The Vela Pulsar knows nothing of human hearts. But the resonance between a dying star's rotation and our own biological metronome speaks to something deeper about periodic phenomena in the universe.

## Processing the Signal

Our pipeline works in three stages:

1. **Capture** -- Raw radio frequency data collected at 1.4 GHz from the 64-meter dish.
2. **Dedispersion** -- Compensating for interstellar medium effects that smear the pulse across frequencies.
3. **Sonification** -- Mapping the cleaned pulse profile to an audio waveform with careful attention to timbre and envelope.

The result is a steady, warm thump -- like a kick drum recorded in a cathedral the size of a solar system.
