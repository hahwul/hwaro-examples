On April 28, 2020, magnetar SGR 1935+2154 produced a radio burst so powerful it was detected by multiple observatories simultaneously. The burst lasted roughly a millisecond, but within that sliver of time lies an architecture of waveforms rich enough to fuel an entire album of generative music.

## Anatomy of a Burst

A magnetar burst is not a simple spike. When you zoom into the time-frequency representation, you find:

- **A steep attack** with rise times under 100 microseconds
- **Complex substructure** showing multiple overlapping components
- **A spectral sweep** from higher to lower frequencies, not unlike a synthesizer filter envelope

This is, in essence, a natural frequency modulation event. The magnetar's twisted magnetic field lines reconnect and release energy across a broad spectrum, and the resulting waveform carries the signature of that violent geometry.

## From Data to Sound

We extracted the burst profile from the CHIME/FRB detection pipeline and imported it as a wavetable into a software synthesizer. The single-cycle waveform, derived from the burst envelope, produces a tone that is harsh, metallic, and strangely beautiful.

By modulating the wavetable position with an LFO synchronized to the original burst repetition rate, we created a patch that breathes with the rhythm of stellar catastrophe.

## Generative Possibilities

The real power of magnetar-derived waveforms is their complexity. Unlike synthesized oscillators built from simple mathematical functions, these waveforms carry the fingerprint of genuine physical processes. Each harmonic partial corresponds to a real phenomenon in the magnetosphere.

Feed these waveforms into a granular engine, scatter the grains across a reverb tail tuned to the interstellar medium's dispersion characteristics, and you have a sound that is simultaneously alien and deeply physical.
