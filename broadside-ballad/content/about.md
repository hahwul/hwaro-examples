In the age before newspapers and the penny post, it was the broadside ballad &mdash; a single, coarse sheet of verse, woodcut, and moralising commentary &mdash; that served the common reader as his chronicle, his theatre, and his song-book. Pasted upon the walls of taverns and tacked within the doors of country inns, these sheets were the true literature of the street.

The present publication revives the old form. Each page is set after the manner of the journeyman printers of Grub Street: the title in blackletter, the verse in roman, and all set between the firm double rules of the broadside. The woodcut, though drawn anew, observes the stark conventions of the old stock-cuts &mdash; black upon white, no half-tone, no shading but what the knife allowed.

We print what chapmen sold: lamentable ballads, admonitions, ditties, doleful complaints, and the occasional merry tale. No sheet is too humble; no topic too diurnal. The hangings at Tyburn, the prodigies at sea, the vagaries of love, and the follies of the great &mdash; all are fit subject for the ballad-monger.

The reader is advised: not every line is history. The ballad-maker is, by ancient custom, allowed a degree of embellishment. The curious may verify particulars against the records of the parish, the sessions, or the Stationers' Company. The incurious are invited merely to read, mark, and sing.

## The Printer's Hand

This sheet is composed in the following faces &mdash;

- **UnifrakturCook** and **UnifrakturMaguntia**, revival blackletter faces after the German precedents.
- **EB Garamond** for roman verse, a close cousin of the sixteenth-century punches of Claude Garamont.

The ornaments are drawn from the common stock of printer's flowers: quoins, lozenges, and the pilcrow. No photograph, nor any engraved plate, is employed &mdash; only the honest woodcut and the set letter.
