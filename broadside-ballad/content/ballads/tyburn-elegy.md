<div class="verse">
<span class="stanza-num">I.</span>
Ye gentle hearts that pity know,
Attend unto my song,
And hearken to a tale of woe,
That shall not tarry long.
</div>

<div class="verse">
<span class="stanza-num">II.</span>
At Tyburn Tree I yield my breath,
The crowd is gathered round,
And ere I meet the stroke of death,
My voice shall sweetly sound.
</div>

<div class="verse">
<span class="stanza-num">III.</span>
In Essex was I born and bred,
Of honest parents both,
Yet early from their path I fled,
And early broke my troth.
</div>

<div class="verse">
<span class="stanza-num">IV.</span>
On Hounslow Heath I took my stand,
With pistol cocked and bright,
And lightened every traveller's hand
Between the noon and night.
</div>

<div class="verse">
<span class="stanza-num">V.</span>
Yet God is just, and hath ordained
That wickedness shall end:
By Bow Street Runners was I chained,
And here my days I spend.
</div>

<div class="verse">
<span class="stanza-num">VI.</span>
So all ye prentices and youth,
Who hear this mournful strain,
Lay up within your hearts the truth:
That sin shall end in pain.
</div>

## A Note upon the Tune

The present ballad is fitted to the old air of *Chevy Chase*, as was the common practice. The reader is at liberty to sing, hum, or read it silently &mdash; though it is the opinion of this press that a ballad unsung is a ballad half-printed.

## Historical Notice

Thomas Faircloth, late of Colchester in the county of Essex, was apprehended upon Hounslow Heath in the year 17-- after a career of some four years upon the road. He was tried at the Old Bailey, found guilty of highway robbery, and executed in the usual manner. These verses purport to record his last words, though the present press makes no claim as to their strict accuracy.
