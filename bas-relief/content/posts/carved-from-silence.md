There is an old practice among stone carvers: before the first cut, you must learn to see the form already waiting inside the block. The sculptor does not add --- the sculptor reveals.

<!-- more -->

## The Weight of Absence

In bas-relief, what you leave untouched matters as much as what you carve away. The flat plane is not empty; it is the ground from which all meaning emerges. A figure pressed only millimeters above the surface can hold as much presence as a statue standing free in a courtyard.

This principle translates directly to visual design. The whitespace around a heading is not wasted space --- it is the surface against which the heading gains its authority. The margin between cards is not a gap --- it is the shared wall of stone that binds them into a composition.

## Depth Without Distance

What makes bas-relief remarkable is its economy. A coin, a medallion, a temple frieze --- each operates within the thinnest sliver of depth, yet each can tell a complete story. The viewer's eye reads shadow as dimension, interprets the gentlest undulation as landscape, figures, architecture.

In this theme, the same economy governs every element. A card does not float above the page by twenty pixels. It rises by four, and a carefully tuned shadow does the rest. The illusion of depth is more powerful than depth itself.

## Working with the Grain

Every material has a grain. Wood splits along it. Stone fractures around its inclusions. A good craftsperson reads the grain and works with it, not against it.

Digital surfaces have their own grain: the pixel grid, the viewport edge, the scroll direction. This theme respects that grain. Elements align to a consistent rhythm. Shadows fall in a single direction. Nothing fights the medium.
