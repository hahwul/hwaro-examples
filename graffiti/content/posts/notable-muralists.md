# Five Muralists Who Changed the Face of Cities

Muralism operates at a different scale than tagging or piecing. A mural can transform an entire block, shift the character of a neighborhood, and become a landmark that outlasts the buildings around it. Here are five artists whose walls deserve attention.

## 1. Diego Rivera

Before the modern street art movement existed, Rivera was painting monumental murals across Mexico and the United States. His work at the Detroit Institute of Arts and the National Palace in Mexico City demonstrated that walls could carry the weight of political and social narrative at a scale no canvas could match.

## 2. Lee Quinones

A pivotal figure in the New York graffiti scene of the late 1970s and early 1980s, Quinones moved from subway cars to handball courts to gallery walls. His Howard the Duck whole-car piece is one of the most iconic images in graffiti history. His transition to muralism helped legitimize street art as a fine art practice.

## 3. Os Gemeos

The Brazilian twin brothers Gustavo and Otavio Pandolfo brought the vivid colors and folk traditions of Sao Paulo to walls around the world. Their distinctive yellow-skinned characters and dreamlike scenes have appeared on buildings in New York, London, Lisbon, and dozens of other cities. Their work bridges graffiti, muralism, and fine art with a style that is immediately recognizable.

## 4. Swoon

Caledonia Curry, known as Swoon, works primarily with wheat-pasted portraits and large-scale installations. Her life-sized paper cutouts of everyday people -- installed on crumbling walls in cities from Brooklyn to Mumbai -- bring a human intimacy to the monumental scale of street art. Her work also extends to community-driven projects, including building floating rafts and rehabilitating houses in post-Katrina New Orleans.

## 5. JR

The French artist JR uses massive photographic prints pasted onto buildings, bridges, and train cars. His "Women Are Heroes" project in the favelas of Rio de Janeiro and his work on the US-Mexico border (a giant image of a toddler peering over the wall) demonstrate how street art can operate as both aesthetic intervention and political statement.

## The Common Thread

What connects these five artists is a shared belief that art should not be confined to private spaces. The wall -- public, exposed, impermanent -- is both their medium and their message.
