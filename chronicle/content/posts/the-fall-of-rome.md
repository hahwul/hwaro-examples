It is often taught that the Roman Empire fell in a singular catastrophic event, punctuated by the sacking of Rome in 410 AD by the Visigoths. However, modern historians increasingly view the decline as a gradual, systemic failure occurring over centuries, rather than a sudden collapse triggered solely by barbarian invasions.

The economic infrastructure of the empire had been severely strained long before Alaric's forces breached the city walls. Taxation was oppressive, inflation ran rampant, and the agricultural sector, the backbone of the Roman economy, was failing due to over-reliance on slave labor and soil depletion.

> The true collapse of Rome was not a singular roar of barbarian triumph, but the quiet crumbling of its economic foundation and civic institutions over countless generations.

Furthermore, the division of the empire into Western and Eastern halves exacerbated these vulnerabilities. The West, lacking the wealth and administrative resilience of the East, found itself unable to sustain its vast frontiers.

We must re-examine the traditional narrative and recognize the complex interplay of internal decay and external pressures that slowly unraveled the greatest empire of the ancient world.
