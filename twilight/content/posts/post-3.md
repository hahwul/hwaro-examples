The lake holds the light longer than the sky does. This is the first thing you notice if you stay past sunset. The sky has already moved on to deep blue, to near-black at the zenith, but the water is still carrying traces of the colors that were overhead twenty minutes ago. Peach. A warm, dusty rose. The faintest thread of gold along the western shore where the trees meet the waterline.

I did not plan to stay this late. The original intention was to photograph the sunset itself, the standard postcard shot of the sun dropping behind the ridge on the far side of the lake. I got that photograph. It is fine. It looks like every other sunset photograph ever taken.

But then I stayed, because the light on the water kept changing, and each change was more interesting than the last.

The surface of the lake is perfectly still. There has been no wind since late afternoon, and the water has settled into a state that is less like a liquid and more like a dark mirror laid flat across the valley. The reflection of the tree line on the far shore is so precise that if you turned the photograph upside down, you could not tell which half was real.

> Water does not create beauty. It borrows it from the sky and holds it after the original is gone.

At this hour, the distinction between the lake and the sky becomes difficult to maintain. They are the same color, the same texture. The horizon line, which was sharp and clear at sunset, has softened into a suggestion. The far shore is a dark band floating between two identical fields of blue-violet.

I switch to a longer exposure. Thirty seconds. A minute. At these durations, even the smallest movement in the water is smoothed away. The result is an image that looks less like a photograph and more like a painting where someone forgot to finish the edges.

The last useful light disappears around forty minutes after sunset. I know this not from any technical measurement but because that is when my camera starts producing images that are more noise than signal. The lake is still there, still holding some faint luminance that my eyes can detect but the sensor cannot.

I pack up by feel more than by sight. The trail back to the road is a pale line through dark grass. Behind me, the lake continues its work of holding the last light, a task it will perform for no audience at all, as it does every evening, long after the photographers have gone.
