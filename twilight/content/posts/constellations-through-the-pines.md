The pines frame the sky the way a window frames a view. Standing beneath them and looking up, the canopy creates an irregular border of black branches against a field of stars. The effect is to make the sky feel closer, more intimate, as though the universe has been reduced to the particular patch visible through this particular gap in these particular trees. It is a private sky. It belongs to this clearing and to whoever is standing in it.

I arrived at the trailhead an hour after sunset, when the last trace of blue had drained from the western horizon and the sky had achieved full darkness. The trail from the parking area to this clearing is short, perhaps ten minutes, but in the dark it felt longer. The ground underfoot was soft with fallen needles, and the smell of pine resin was strong, released by the warmth the trees had absorbed during the day and were now slowly giving back to the cooling air.

The clearing is small, perhaps thirty feet across. The pines surrounding it are tall, their trunks straight and evenly spaced in the way of trees that have been growing together for a long time. They have arranged themselves into something that feels deliberate, though of course it is not. There is no design in a forest. Only the accumulated result of each tree reaching for light over the course of decades.

> The night sky through trees is not diminished by the framing. It is focused. The branches do not block the stars. They organize them.

Overhead, the stars are sharp and still. There is no moon tonight, which is why I chose this evening. Moonlight is beautiful but it washes out the fainter stars, reducing the sky to its brightest points. Without the moon, the full depth of the sky is visible. Stars I have never noticed before appear in the gaps between the ones I recognize. The Milky Way is present as a faint band of lighter dark, not the dramatic river of light that long-exposure photographs suggest, but a subtle brightening, like a brushstroke of diluted white paint across a dark canvas.

I set up my camera on a tripod at the center of the clearing. The settings are slow: twenty-five second exposure, wide aperture, high sensitivity. At these settings the camera sees more than my eyes do. Stars that are invisible to me will appear in the photograph as small points of light, each one a sun at an incomprehensible distance, reduced by that distance to a pixel on a sensor.

Between exposures I turn off the camera's screen and let my eyes readjust. This takes time. Full dark adaptation requires twenty minutes or more, and any exposure to bright light resets the process. So I stand in the dark, patient, and wait for my vision to catch up with the night. Slowly the sky deepens. Stars that were invisible a moment ago appear, tentatively at first, then with increasing certainty.

The forest around the clearing is not silent. There are small sounds: the creak of a branch adjusting to the cooling temperature, the rustle of something moving through the undergrowth, the distant call of an owl that sounds once and then does not repeat. These sounds do not disturb the quiet. They are part of it, the way the spaces between notes are part of a piece of music.

I stay for three hours. In that time the sky rotates visibly overhead, the constellations shifting westward as the Earth turns. Orion, which was high in the south when I arrived, has moved toward the southwest and dropped closer to the tree line. New stars have risen in the east, appearing above the pines as if they have been waiting offstage for their moment.

The cold arrives gradually. It starts in the feet, then the hands, then settles into the core. The pines offer no warmth, but they do offer shelter from the slight breeze that moves through the forest at this hour. In the clearing the air is still, and the cold is the clean, dry cold of a clear night, not unpleasant but insistent.

When I finally pack up and walk back to the trailhead, the stars are still there, unchanged, continuing their slow rotation over the clearing. The pines stand in the dark, holding their frame steady around a sky that does not need an audience but seems, somehow, better for having one.
