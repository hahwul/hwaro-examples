There is a window of time -- no more than ten minutes, sometimes fewer -- when the city becomes something it is not during any other hour. The buildings, which spend most of the day as grey monoliths of glass and steel, suddenly catch fire. Not literally, of course, but the effect is the same. Every west-facing window becomes a mirror for the dying sun, and for those brief minutes the skyline burns with a color that has no proper name. It is not orange. It is not gold. It is something between the two, something the eye recognizes but the tongue cannot describe.

I have stood on rooftops waiting for this moment. I have cancelled plans for it. I have walked out of conversations mid-sentence because I saw, through a window, the light beginning to change.

The strange thing about those ten minutes is how they alter the geometry of the city. Shadows that pointed one direction all day suddenly stretch and bend. Alleyways that were dark at noon fill with warm, horizontal light. A fire escape that you have walked past a thousand times without noticing becomes, for a few minutes, a lattice of gold against a purple wall.

> The city does not know it is beautiful at this hour. That is part of what makes it beautiful.

People on the street change, too. They slow down. I have watched commuters stop in the middle of a crosswalk to look west. Taxi drivers pull over. A man holding a bag of groceries stands perfectly still on a corner, his face turned toward the light, and for a moment he is not a man with groceries. He is a figure in a painting that has not been painted yet.

And then it ends. Not gradually, but with a kind of quiet finality. The gold drains from the windows. The shadows go from warm to cool. The man with the groceries remembers that he is a man with groceries and continues walking. The city returns to what it was, except now it carries, for those who were paying attention, the memory of what it briefly became.

I never photograph the moment itself. Only the minutes just after, when the color is leaving and the sky is that particular shade of deep blue-violet that means the day is truly over. Those are the honest photographs. Not the spectacular ones, but the ones that show what twilight actually looks like: not a blaze of glory, but a slow, quiet withdrawal of light.
