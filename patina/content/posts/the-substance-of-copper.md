Copper begins its life as a warm, almost salmon-colored metal. Within hours of exposure to air, it starts to shift. First a dull brown, then darker, richer --- and eventually, over years, the unmistakable blue-green of verdigris.

## A Chemical Conversation

The patina on copper is not decay. It is a dialogue between the metal and its environment. Moisture, salt, sulfur compounds in the air --- each contributes to the final color. No two patinas are identical because no two environments are the same.

The Statue of Liberty took roughly twenty years to develop its current green surface. The copper roofs of Copenhagen's old stock exchange have been weathering since the seventeenth century. In both cases, the patina is now inseparable from the identity of the structure.

> "The green of old copper is not a color that was chosen. It is a color that was earned."

## Why It Matters

In a culture obsessed with newness, copper patina is a quiet argument for patience. The material does not need to be protected from time. It *uses* time as a collaborator.

<!-- more -->

Architects who specify copper know they are designing in four dimensions. The building they deliver is not the building their grandchildren will see. This is not a problem to solve but a feature to embrace.

Modern coatings can freeze copper in its bright state, and chemical treatments can accelerate patina to arrive in days rather than decades. Both miss the point. The value of genuine patina is that it cannot be shortcut.

### Working with Copper

Craftspeople who work with copper develop an intuition for how the metal will age in different contexts:

- **Coastal environments** produce green patina faster due to salt spray
- **Urban settings** tend toward darker, browner oxides from sulfur pollution
- **Sheltered surfaces** age more slowly and unevenly than exposed ones

Each piece becomes a record of where it has been and what it has endured. This is information that cannot be manufactured.
