The walls of Vethara have never been rebuilt, and yet they stand.

This is the first fact any chronicler must confront when approaching the subject of the city on the inland sea. Not that they stand despite centuries of siege and weather and the slow patient work of frost in cracked mortar, but that they have never required the intervention of human hands. The original construction occurred, by the best estimates of those who study such matters, somewhere in the years following the old empire's final collapse---a date that places the walls at eleven centuries old, minimum. Eleven centuries, and not a single stone has been reset, not a single section of curtain wall has been reinforced, not a single tower has received the structural attention that any ordinarily aging fortification would have demanded generations ago.

The scholars who are brought to Vethara to study this phenomenon tend to fall into three categories.

The first group concludes that the original construction was simply of extraordinary quality, that the builders sourced their stone from a quarry whose particular composition resists weathering to a degree not previously documented, and that with sufficiently fine craftsmanship, this result was always achievable and simply had not been achieved before. They write their reports with the careful precision of those who understand that their reputation depends on saying things that other scholars can agree with.

The second group concludes that there is something active occurring---something that does not fit the vocabulary of their discipline---and then either recants when they return to academic environments that would not survive the implications, or falls silent entirely and begins spending an unusual amount of time in the city's cathedral, sitting in the nave and staring at the vaulted ceiling with the expression of people who have asked a question they no longer wish to have answered.

The third group does not return.

## What the Citizens Know

The population of Vethara does not discuss the walls. This is not a conspiracy of silence in the way that a chronicler might recognize from dealing with courts and guilds, where the silence is maintained by hierarchy and enforced by consequence. It is something quieter and more fundamental than that: the citizens simply do not find the walls interesting. They are as they are. They have always been as they are. The question of why they are as they are is the kind of question that visitors ask, and visitors are the sort of people who do not understand yet.

The citizens do discuss the cathedral. They discuss it at length, with the particular enthusiasm of people who take genuine pleasure in a topic that matters to them. The cathedral, they will explain, was built in the first century of the city's existence, and it has been neither enlarged nor reduced since. It is not the largest cathedral in the known world. It is not the most ornate. It does not have the most famous relics (and on the subject of relics, more shortly). What it has, which no other cathedral in the known world possesses, is a quality the citizens call *abidance*.

The word does not translate cleanly into standard usage. The citizens use it to describe a kind of presence that is not divine, exactly, or not divine in the way that their official faith defines divinity. Something older than the church that now occupies the building. Something that was there before the first stone was laid, perhaps, that found the structure being built over it and decided that this was acceptable.

The cathedral's priests do not use the word *abidance*. They are aware that their parishioners use it. They have learned, over the generations, not to deliver sermons on the subject of what the word means and why it is theologically problematic. The parishioners who sit in the pews and listen to the liturgy with every appearance of sincere devotion are devoted to something that the liturgy is only approximately describing. Both parties understand this. The arrangement has proven, over centuries, to be stable.

## The Relics That Are Not Relics

Every major cathedral in the world maintains a collection of holy relics: bones of saints, fragments of scripture, objects that saints touched, objects that performed miracles when saints prayed over them. The cathedral of Vethara maintains a collection of objects that have no saints attached to them whatsoever.

There is a length of chain, very old, whose links are not quite the right shape for any metallurgical tradition the scholars have been able to identify. There is a sealed clay vessel whose contents move against the vessel's exterior when the vessel is held, though no sound accompanies this movement. There is a carved figure in a wood that does not correspond to any tree that grows in the region, depicting a figure that is either kneeling or growing from the ground, the posture being ambiguous enough that learned people disagree on this point after extended study. There is a mirror whose surface does not reflect the person standing before it but does reflect, with perfect fidelity, whatever is standing behind them.

These objects are not locked away. They are displayed in the side chapel, lit by candles that the vergers replace each morning, accessible to anyone who wishes to stand before them. The citizens of Vethara visit regularly. They do not pray to the objects. They simply stand before them for a while, in the way that people stand before something that they wish to remember accurately.

No one in the city's history has attempted to steal any of these objects. This is not because security prevents it. This is because the city does not produce people who wish to steal them.

## The Question of Age

A physician who visited Vethara three decades ago and spent six months recording observations about the population published a monograph that was largely ignored by the medical establishment and is now extremely difficult to obtain in any form, as the printer who produced the first edition died shortly after its completion, and the manuscript itself has not surfaced despite the efforts of several interested parties to locate it.

The physician's central claim, documented through extensive case studies that filled the monograph's appendices, was that the citizens of Vethara do not age at a consistent rate.

Not that they age slowly. Not that they appear younger than their years through some accident of constitution or diet or favorable circumstance. But that their aging is *inconsistent*---that individuals will appear to age at a normal rate for some years and then appear to pause for a period that varies between individuals, and then resume at something approximately normal, and then pause again.

The pauses do not correspond to any period of unusual health or unusual stress. They do not correspond to the seasons, to years of abundance or years of famine, to the individual's occupation or social position or devotional habits. They do not, as far as the physician could determine, correspond to anything externally observable. They simply occur, and then cease, and the citizens regard them with the same equanimity with which they regard the cathedral walls.

The physician asked one citizen, a woman who appeared to be in her late middle years but whom the physician's informants described as having appeared thus for at least thirty years, whether she understood why this happened.

The woman considered the question for a long moment and then said: "The city keeps the ones it wants to keep."

When the physician pressed her on this---who keeps, in what sense, wanting---the woman smiled with the particular patience of someone who has explained something simple to many complicated people and said that she meant it exactly as she had said it.

## What the Walls Hear

The gates of Vethara close at dusk and open at dawn. This is custom, not law, and the gatekeepers acknowledge that there is no formal statute requiring it. It has simply always been done.

A traveler who arrives after dusk is invited to make camp outside the walls and enter at first light. Almost all travelers accept this arrangement without difficulty. The walls of Vethara, even in darkness, convey a quality that does not encourage the sort of impatience that leads people to demand that gatekeepers make exceptions.

A chronicler who spent three years in Vethara and left to write this account---the only account of this kind to have been completed and circulated, as far as can be determined---described sitting on the hillside one evening outside the closed gates, watching the candlelight that glowed in the windows of the city beyond, listening to the wind that moved through the stones.

"The walls do not creak," the chronicler wrote. "Old stone buildings creak, in the manner of old wood, in the manner of old people. The sound is the voice of something adjusting to its circumstances, finding its accommodation with time. The walls of Vethara make no sound at all. Not silence in the passive sense. Something more deliberate than that. The walls are not adjusting. They have already adjusted. They adjusted a very long time ago, and they do not anticipate needing to adjust again."

The chronicler was inside the city for three years. The chronicler then left, and completed their account, and died six months later of causes that the physicians attending them described as consistent with extreme old age.

The chronicler was, by all accounts at the time of entering Vethara, thirty-four years old.
