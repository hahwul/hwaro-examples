The examination lasted eleven days.

Brother Aldric had requested it himself, which was the irregularity that kept the tribunal from feeling entirely comfortable with its conclusions. A man who requests an examination of his own institution's relic collection is either performing an elaborate demonstration of confidence in that collection's authenticity, or he is doing something that the tribunal could not, even after eleven days of deliberation, quite categorize. He was not performing confidence. The tribunal eventually settled on *something else* and noted in its minutes that the something else remained undefined.

The collection occupied three cases in the cathedral's side chapel: a glass reliquary containing a finger bone, attributed to Saint Morwen of the eastern provinces; a length of cloth, folded precisely, purported to have touched the wounds of Saint Aldous at the moment of his martyrdom; and a small painted icon, said to be self-originated---that is, to have appeared without human manufacture, already complete, in the locked sacristy one morning approximately two hundred years ago.

The examination found the finger bone to be that of a sheep. The cloth was of a style of weaving that had not existed in the relevant era and region. The icon had been painted by a hand traceable, through the examination of pigments and technique, to a workshop operating three generations after the date of the icon's supposed appearance, and furthermore a partial signature was found concealed in the lower right corner beneath a later layer of varnish.

The relics were fraudulent. This was documented. The documentation was thorough enough that no subsequent examination has seriously disputed it.

---

## The Problem

Three days after the tribunal published its findings, a woman who had been blind for seven years regained her sight in the side chapel.

This was not, in itself, entirely anomalous. Healings had always occurred in the cathedral in numbers that exceeded what might be attributed to chance, a fact that the tribunal had been aware of and had noted in its preliminary observations without finding a way to make it relevant to the question of the relics' authenticity. The healings had continued throughout the eleven days of examination. They continued after the conclusion.

The tribunal's position was that the healings were evidence of genuine divine presence in the cathedral and that the fraudulent relics were therefore an offense against that presence---that real miracles were occurring despite the deception, not because of it, and that the deception was a corruption that should be named and removed.

Brother Aldric's position was more uncomfortable to articulate, and he spent considerable time in correspondence with the theologians of three different orders attempting to work out whether it was heretical and, if so, in which direction.

His position was this: if the healings continued before the examination, during the examination, and after the examination; if they continued regardless of whether the worshippers who received them knew the relics were genuine or fraudulent; if the saint's finger bone had been a sheep's bone for two centuries without any apparent diminishment in the miraculous frequency of the side chapel---then what, precisely, was the theological object of the veneration?

Something was present. Something was responding. The question of what it was had been, Brother Aldric believed, seriously confused by two centuries of mislabeled bones and forged linen.

---

## The History of the Forgery

The forgeries had been introduced, as best the tribunal could determine, by a predecessor of Brother Aldric's predecessor's predecessor---an administrator of the cathedral's collections who had died two centuries ago and who was remembered, in the institution's internal records, as a man of considerable organizational talent and uncertain personal beliefs.

This administrator had, at some point in the middle of his tenure, replaced the original relics---whatever they had been---with the counterfeits. The original relics, if they had ever existed in the forms described, had not been located. What had been located, in a sealed cavity behind the reliquary case, was a letter in the administrator's own hand.

The letter was brief. It said, in its entirety:

*I have studied the records of what came before. The originals were also false. We are a cathedral that heals, and we have never understood why. I have decided that the question of the relics is secondary. Something is here. It was here before us. It will be here after us. We are the keepers of the door, not the masters of what stands on the other side. Let the congregation have their saints. They deserve the comfort. The saints are not the point.*

The tribunal found this letter troubling in ways it could not fully articulate in formal proceedings. The letter suggested that the fraud was intentional and considered. It also suggested that the man who perpetrated it had, two centuries ago, arrived at a theological position that was only now beginning to be seriously examined.

---

## What Continues

The side chapel was not closed after the tribunal's findings. Brother Aldric had requested the examination, but he had not requested the closure of anything. The cathedral's administration, after lengthy internal discussion, decided that closing the chapel would do more damage to the institution than the alternative.

The relics remained in the cases. The glass reliquary still contained the sheep's bone. The folded cloth remained folded. The icon remained on its stand. The findings of the tribunal were published and were available, to any member of the public who requested them, in the cathedral's library.

The congregation continued to come.

This was the fact that Brother Aldric found himself returning to, in the years following the examination, when he was sitting in the nave in the early hours before the morning office, with the candles low and the cathedral empty and the particular quality of silence that old stone buildings maintain independent of whether anyone believes in them.

They knew. Not all of them, but enough of them, that the information had circulated through the community as information circulates. The relics were false. The sheep's bone was a sheep's bone. The cloth was a competent forgery. The icon was the work of a hired craftsman who had signed his work in a place he expected no one to look.

And they came.

---

## A Theology of the Threshold

Brother Aldric was not a man who doubted his faith. What he doubted, with increasing clarity as the years passed, was his previous understanding of what his faith was faith *in*.

He had believed, with the comfortable confidence of the trained and orthodox, that the cathedral was a vessel: that it held divine presence in the way that a reliquary holds a bone, by formal designation, by the authority of the institution that placed the presence there. That the miracles were the consequence of the presence, and the presence was the consequence of the correct performance of the correct rites over the correct objects by people with the correct credentials.

The examination had disproven the objects. The rites and credentials remained. And the miracles continued.

He began to consider---with the caution of a man who understands that some thoughts are dangerous and that the danger is proportional to the precision with which you think them---that he had the relationship inverted.

Perhaps the cathedral was not a vessel that had been filled. Perhaps it was a door that had been built in the vicinity of something that was already there, already doing what it was already doing, and the institution had then built a framework of objects and rites and credentials around the door in order to explain why the door worked. The framework was human. The explaining was human. The comfort of the structure was real and the structure itself was not wrong, exactly, only it was not the source of the thing it was describing. The source was something that did not require the framework.

The false relics had been the wrong labels on the right door.

---

## The Question That Remains

He asked the woman who had regained her sight what she had been thinking, in the moment before the change occurred.

She said she had been thinking about her mother, who had also gone blind and had not been healed. She had been sitting in the side chapel because it was where she had sat with her mother, in the years when her mother was still alive and the blindness was new and they had not yet understood that it was permanent. She had come back to the place because the place still felt like the right place, regardless of whether anything useful was going to happen there.

"Were you praying?" he asked.

"No," she said. "I was just sitting there."

"Were you thinking about the relics?"

"Which ones?" she said, which suggested that the tribunal's findings had reached her, as they had reached many in the city.

"Any of them."

"No," she said. "I was thinking about my mother."

He thanked her. He walked back through the cathedral to the nave and sat down in his usual place and looked at the vaulted ceiling for a while. The candles were doing what candles do: maintaining their small patches of warmth against the stone cold of the building, indifferent to whether what they illuminated was authentic or not.

Something was here.

He had spent his entire professional life tending the framework built around the door. It was still necessary work. The congregation needed the framework in the way that people need landmarks: not because the landmarks are the destination but because the destination is harder to find without them. The framework was not the source of what the cathedral was. It was the map.

He had learned, over eleven days of examination and the years that followed, that the map might be wrong in significant ways and still point, approximately, toward something real.

He lit a fresh candle.

He began to write.

## Appendix: A Note on Methodology

The documents reproduced in this chronicle were recovered from the cathedral archives during a restoration of the building's records. Brother Aldric's correspondence, the tribunal's minutes, and the administrator's letter are all preserved in the original and available for examination by credentialed scholars. The account of the woman who regained her sight is drawn from a deposition taken at the time and preserved in the same collection.

The question of what the cathedral actually is, and what it is actually doing, remains open.

The candles in the side chapel continue to burn.
