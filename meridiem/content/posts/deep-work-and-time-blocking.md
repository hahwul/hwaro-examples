# Deep Work and Time Blocking

The most productive hours of any day are the ones spent in deep, uninterrupted focus. Research consistently shows that context switching -- jumping between tasks, checking messages, attending to notifications -- costs far more time than the interruptions themselves. Each switch forces the brain to reload context, and full cognitive engagement can take twenty minutes or more to recover.

## What Time Blocking Looks Like

Time blocking is the practice of assigning specific tasks to specific time slots throughout the day. Rather than maintaining a loose to-do list and hoping to get through it, you decide in advance when each task will happen.

A typical morning block might look like this:

- **06:30 - 07:00** -- Review the day's plan, adjust priorities
- **07:00 - 09:00** -- Primary deep work session (no meetings, no messaging)
- **09:00 - 09:15** -- Standup or brief check-in
- **09:15 - 10:30** -- Secondary focused work
- **10:30 - 11:00** -- Communication catch-up (email, messages)

The key is treating each block as a commitment, not a suggestion. When the block says "deep work," that means nothing else gets attention.

## Protecting AM Hours

Morning hours -- roughly 7:00 AM to 11:00 AM for most people -- tend to be the highest-quality cognitive time. Cortisol levels are elevated, attention is naturally sharper, and the accumulated fatigue of the day has not yet set in.

Guarding these hours from meetings and administrative work is one of the highest-leverage decisions a knowledge worker can make. Even shifting a single recurring meeting from 9:00 AM to 1:00 PM can reclaim an entire deep work window.

## Tracking Time to Improve It

Tracking time is not about surveillance or guilt. It is about visibility. When you can see where hours actually go, patterns emerge: the meetings that could have been messages, the thirty-minute tasks that somehow consumed ninety, the afternoons lost to unfocused browsing.

A simple time log -- task, start time, duration -- is enough. After a week of data, the improvements become obvious.

## The PM Transition

As the day moves past midday, energy and focus naturally decline. Rather than fighting this, effective time management embraces it. Afternoons are well suited for collaborative work, routine tasks, planning, and review. Save the demanding cognitive work for the morning.

The shift from AM to PM is not a drop in productivity. It is a change in the kind of productivity that works best.
