# The Value of a Daily Review

Most people end their workday by closing their laptop and walking away. The next morning, they open it again and spend the first fifteen to thirty minutes figuring out where they left off. This daily re-orientation is wasted time that compounds across weeks and months.

## A Five-Minute Practice

A daily review takes five minutes at most. Before ending work for the day, answer three questions:

1. **What did I complete today?** List the finished tasks. This is both a record and a small acknowledgment of progress.
2. **What is still in progress?** Note where you stopped and what the next concrete step is. "Continue writing the migration script, starting from the validation logic" is far more useful than "work on migration."
3. **What should I prioritize tomorrow?** Choose one to three items that deserve the best hours of the next day.

Write these down. A text file, a notebook, a task tracker -- the medium matters less than the consistency.

## Why It Works

The daily review eliminates the morning ramp-up problem. When you sit down the next day, you already know exactly what to do and where to start. There is no decision fatigue, no scanning through old tabs and messages trying to reconstruct your mental state.

It also creates an honest record. Over time, you can look back and see what you actually accomplished versus what you planned. The gaps between intention and reality reveal where your system needs adjustment.

## Connecting to Time Tracking

When combined with time tracking, the daily review becomes even more powerful. You can see not just what you did, but how long it took. Patterns emerge:

- Tasks that consistently take longer than expected need better estimation or decomposition
- Days with many completed items but low focus time suggest shallow work dominated
- Days with few completions but high focus time suggest deep work sessions that will pay off later

## Morning and Evening Rhythms

The best time for a daily review is during the natural transition point of the day. For many people, this is the last fifteen minutes of the workday -- a deliberate closing ritual. Some prefer a brief review in the morning as a starting ritual instead.

Either way, the practice anchors the day. It turns an open-ended workday into something with a clear beginning and end, with intentional direction rather than reactive scrambling.

Five minutes of review saves hours of drift.
