<p class="lead">The query list appended to a galley is &mdash; after the blue-pencil pass itself &mdash; the editor's most important composition. It is the first thing the author reads; it frames the correction that follows. A careless query list will cost the editor a week of apologies, and sometimes a book.</p>

## What a query is for

<p class="annotated">A query is <span class="annot">not<span class="proof-number">1</span></span> a correction. It is a request for clarification, issued when the editor has an interpretation but is not sufficiently sure of it to mark the change as a correction. The author's reply &mdash; "stet," or amend, or "as noted" &mdash; closes the question.</p>

<p class="annotated">A good query names the folio, the line, and the phrase in doubt. It states, briefly, the editor's interpretation. It suggests, where possible, an alternative. It does <span class="dele">not</span> <span class="insert-caret">&#x2227;</span>never<span class="insert-caret">&#x2227;</span> scold.<span class="proof-number">2</span></p>

## An example

> Q3 (f. 14, l. 22): "the Treaty of 1763." The Treaty of Paris was signed 10 February 1763, and the Treaty of Hubertusburg on 15 February of the same year. Please specify which is meant.

<p class="annotated">The query above does four things at once. It cites the location. It identifies the phrase. It offers the two candidate facts. And it concedes, by the phrase "please specify," that the author has the final word. A query that demands, rather than requests, generates resentment; a query that requests tends to be answered.<span class="proof-number">3</span></p>

## Queries to avoid

The editor should resist the temptation to query for style. If the style is amiss, mark the correction and note the reason in the margin. A style query &mdash; "Is this really the voice you want?" &mdash; invites a quarrel, and the galley is not the place for a quarrel.

<p class="annotated">Similarly, avoid the compound query. A query that asks three questions at once will be answered, if at all, in only one. Break each question out and number it. The author can skip the ones she finds dull; the editor can at least be sure which have been answered.<span class="proof-number">4</span></p>

## A query list in miniature

<div class="proof-legend">
<h3>Sample query list, galley 02</h3>
<dl>
  <dt>Q1</dt><dd>f. 3, l. 8 &mdash; spelling of "Bhattacharya" &mdash; please confirm.</dd>
  <dt>Q2</dt><dd>f. 7, l. 14 &mdash; "1893" or "1894"? House records give the latter.</dd>
  <dt>Q3</dt><dd>f. 14, l. 22 &mdash; Treaty of 1763, Paris or Hubertusburg?</dd>
  <dt>Q4</dt><dd>f. 22, l. 3 &mdash; paragraph break here? The shift in subject suggests one.</dd>
</dl>
</div>

The author's reply will be entered on the record; the galley will be returned to the compositor with the corrections applied; the next pass will generate a shorter list. The list always shortens, and the galley always clears.
