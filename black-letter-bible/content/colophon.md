## The Types

This edition is typeset principally in two digital fonts:

**UnifrakturMaguntia** (Peter Wiegel, 2007) is used for all display type, chapter openings, and illuminated initials. It is a digital reconstruction of the textura type of the mid-fifteenth century, drawn by reference to the forty-two-line Bible and to contemporary liturgical manuscripts of the Rhenish tradition. The type is released under the terms of the SIL Open Font Licence and is freely available for any use.

**Old Standard TT** (Alexey Kryukov, 2009) is used for all body text, running headlines, and text within specimen SVGs. It is a digital cutting of a nineteenth-century Russian scholarly type (used for academic editions of classical texts), chosen here for its density, its Greek and Cyrillic extensions, and its aesthetic harmony with the display type.

A small amount of supplementary body setting uses **EB Garamond** (Georg Duffner, 2010-present), a digital revival of Jean Jannon's 1615 cutting of Claude Garamond's sixteenth-century types.

## The Setting

The body text is set in a single column of approximately forty ems, with generous leading (approximately 1.7 times the body size) to permit the dense Gothic ornament to breathe. Paragraph rhythm is indicated by blank lines rather than first-line indents - a modern convention chosen for screen legibility over strict textura orthography.

The two-column setting employed on the frontispiece is a gesture toward the folio tradition; it is not employed throughout the edition because screen readers are typically narrower than the folio page and the column-break would be disruptive. Readers who wish to set this site in a two-column manner throughout may modify the stylesheet accordingly.

## The Pressmen

This digital edition has been compiled by the Office of the Pressman, a small group of typographers and scholars of the printed book operating remotely. The editorial authority is shared among three principals:

**The Compositor** has set the text and selected the types.
**The Corrector** has verified the historical claims against the sources named in each chapter.
**The Copyist** has drawn the inline SVG ornament in reference to the manuscript and printed sources.

No individual signature is attached to the edition, upon the principle that the work represents a collaborative reading of the tradition rather than the scholarly contribution of a single author.

## The Authorities

The principal textual authorities consulted for this edition are:

- The forty-two-line Bible, Mainz, Johannes Gutenberg, 1452-1455; consulted in digital facsimile from the Harry Ransom Center, Austin, and the British Library.
- The Coverdale Bible, Antwerp (or Cologne), 1535; consulted in digital facsimile from the British Library.
- The Great Bible, London, Richard Grafton and Edward Whitchurch, 1539; consulted in digital facsimile from the Bodleian.
- The Authorised Version, London, Robert Barker, 1611; consulted in digital facsimile from Cambridge University Library.
- Numerous Continental editions surveyed through the Staatsbibliothek zu Berlin digital collections.

## The Ornament

The inline SVG ornament throughout the edition (illuminated initials, column dividers, chapter head-pieces, foot-pieces) has been drawn by the Copyist upon reference to the historical sources named above. No element is a direct tracing of a source; each is an original drawing within the idiom of the tradition. The drawings are released under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 and may be reused with citation.

## The Intended Use

This edition is intended for the pedagogical use of students of typography, students of the history of the book, and students of the sacred text considered as a typographic object. It is not a Bible for liturgical use - the chapters treat the typographic tradition rather than the sacred text itself - and should not be mistaken for a substitute for a complete Bible.
