## The Majuscules

Arranged in the alphabetical order of the English alphabet. The reader will note the particularly elaborate forms of A, B, and M, which carry the decorative weight of the chapter openings; the compact forms of I, J, L, and T; and the blocky majuscule of U (which in the mediaeval tradition is drawn identically to V).

## The Minuscules

The minuscules are where the textura character is most distinctive. The uniform vertical rhythm of the minims (i, m, n, u) produces the characteristic texture from which the hand takes its name.

## The Abbreviations

The textura tradition preserves a very extensive system of scribal abbreviations: the Tironian et (&), the macron over a vowel indicating an omitted nasal (a for an, o for on), the superscript dots and strokes indicating omitted letters, and the various contracted forms of common words (D~s for Dominus, sps for Spiritus). A full textura font includes several hundred such abbreviations as distinct sorts; an abridged font may include only the most common thirty or forty.

The present edition uses UnifrakturMaguntia in its standard cut, which includes approximately sixty of the most common abbreviations but omits the more obscure scribal contractions. Readers encountering a textura Bible at first hand should be prepared to decipher abbreviations considerably more extensive than those present in this specimen.

## The Pair Kerning

The textura tradition does not typically use kerning in the modern sense - the side-bearings of each sort are set wide enough that no two sorts collide at their bounding boxes. The effect is a very uniform colour across the page, with the white space between letters as substantial as the strokes of the letters themselves. This is one of the principal visual differences between textura and the roman types which succeeded it, where kerning (the physical overlap of sorts with negative side-bearings) produces a more varied internal rhythm.
