## The Hand and its Features

The textura quadrata - "square textura," so called from the square feet of its minim strokes - is the most formal hand of the late Gothic scribal tradition. It was used in Latin liturgical manuscripts from approximately 1180 until the advent of printing in the 1450s, and persisted in certain luxury manuscripts (choirbooks, missals) for a further century.

The hand is characterised by the uniform vertical rhythm of its minims, the broken curves of its bowls (which are assembled from straight strokes rather than drawn as continuous curves), and the square feet which terminate each minim at the baseline. The result is a texture of vertical strokes which is dense, legible at a distance, and - perhaps most importantly for its liturgical use - easily reproducible from scribe to scribe, even across several centuries.

## The Relation to the Printed Type

Gutenberg and his contemporaries cut their types directly after the textura hands of contemporary manuscripts, with particular reference to the hands of the Rhenish liturgical manuscripts of the 1440s. The correspondence is close enough that early copies of the forty-two-line Bible were mistaken for fine manuscripts by their first owners, and the rubrication (of initials, of paragraph marks) continued to be done by hand in red ink by the scribes attached to the press - the technology of printing the rubrics in red from a second impression was not mastered until the 1460s.

## The Rubrication Programme

The standard programme of rubrication in a textura Bible comprises: the large illuminated initial at the opening of each book of the Bible (which may occupy a quarter of the column and incorporate figurative miniatures); the smaller rubricated initial at each chapter opening; the red paragraph mark at each verse beginning; and the running headlines in red at the top of each folio. The mature printed Bibles follow this programme almost exactly, with the figurative miniatures typically replaced by a woodcut.

## The Ornamental Divider

Between the two columns of text, the textura manuscripts habitually place a decorative element - most commonly a vertical line in red ink, punctuated at intervals by small ornamental devices (the lozenge, the quatrefoil, the mandorla). The printed Bibles of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries continued this practice; the present edition preserves it in the form of an inline SVG divider in gilt and red upon the folio ground.
