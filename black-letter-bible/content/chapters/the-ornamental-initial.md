## The Hierarchy of Initials

In the textura Bibles, the ornamental initial carries a great weight of information about the textual hierarchy. The student of the tradition can, at a single glance, distinguish by the ornamental treatment whether the initial opens the Bible entire, a book of the Bible, a chapter, a verse of particular importance, or merely an ordinary paragraph. The hierarchy typically runs as follows:

### The Opening Initial

Reserved for the first letter of the entire Bible (the I of "In principio"), this is the most elaborate of all initials. It often occupies a full column of the folio, with figurative illumination showing the Creator, the creation of light, or the seven days of Genesis. It is typically set upon a gold ground with full-colour figurative work.

### The Book Initial

At the opening of each book of the Bible (Genesis, Exodus, and so on), a large illuminated initial - typically six to eight lines of body text in height - is set upon a coloured ground with figurative or historiated interior. The figurative content refers to the particular book: Noah and the Ark for Genesis, the pharaoh and the exodus for Exodus, and so on through the canon.

### The Chapter Initial

At the opening of each chapter, a smaller initial - typically four to five lines of body text in height - is set in red or blue ink with ornamental (rather than figurative) interior. The ornament is typically foliate or strapwork, and is generally repeated across the chapters of a given book with minor variation.

### The Verse Mark

The opening of each verse is marked by a small rubricated capital (often the Roman capitulum sign) or by a coloured paragraph mark. These are not strictly "initials" in the ornamental sense but are part of the same hierarchy.

## The Evolution of the Programme

The figurative tradition of the mediaeval manuscript was transmitted substantially intact into the first generation of printed Bibles. The forty-two-line Bible retains the full manuscript programme, rubricated and illuminated by hand upon the printed sheets. Over the subsequent century, two processes reduced the programme: first, the printed woodcut replaced the hand illumination for the figurative initials; second, the Reformation theological settlement - particularly in England and the Netherlands - discouraged the figurative treatment of the opening initials, and encouraged a more ornamental (but non-figurative) programme.

By 1611 the English Bibles had substantially abandoned the figurative programme in favour of woodcut ornamental borders and strapwork initials. The Continental Catholic Bibles of the same period - the Douay-Rheims and the Louvain Bibles - retained the figurative programme for a further century.

## The Contemporary Revival

A modest revival of the hand-illuminated initial has occurred in the last three decades among the private presses, particularly in the United States and the United Kingdom. The Arion Press edition of the 1611 Bible (San Francisco, 2000) included a programme of fifty-six newly-commissioned historiated initials by the calligrapher John Prestianni; the Whittington Press edition of the Gospels (Andoversford, 2011) commissioned initials from Liz Mathews. The tradition is not dead; it is merely attenuated.
