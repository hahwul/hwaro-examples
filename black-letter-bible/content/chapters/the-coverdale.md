## The Translator

Myles Coverdale (1488-1569), an Augustinian friar who had left his order under the influence of the early English reformers, worked from the existing translations of William Tyndale (for those portions which Tyndale had published) supplemented by the Latin Vulgate, Luther's German, and the Swiss-German Zurich Bible. His English is characteristically sonorous, rhythmic, and liturgically effective - much of it survives into the Book of Common Prayer and from there into later Bibles.

## The Type

The body of the Coverdale Bible is set in Schwabacher - a vernacular German cousin of textura, with rounded rather than square bowls and a slightly less condensed letter-fit. Schwabacher had become the standard type for German vernacular printing by the late fifteenth century (Luther's own 1522 New Testament was set in it), and its use for an English Bible printed upon the Continent would have been natural for any compositor to hand.

The chapter openings are set in a heavier textura, in red and black ink by turn, with the chapter initial rubricated in an elaborate figurative cut. The running headlines are in textura at a smaller body size.

## The Printing

The printer of the first edition is disputed. The colophon names no place or printer, and the types have been variously identified with the workshops of Merten de Keyser in Antwerp and Eucharius Cervicornus in Cologne. Contemporary evidence favours Antwerp, where the sympathies of the English Protestant emigre community were strongest and where the printer would have had access both to skilled compositors and to the financial backing of the English merchants.

## The Significance

The Coverdale Bible is the first complete English Bible to appear in print - Tyndale had published only the New Testament and portions of the Old before his martyrdom. It was dedicated to King Henry VIII and was tolerated (though never formally authorised) by the Crown; within four years it had been superseded by the Great Bible of 1539, to which Coverdale himself contributed as editor.

## The Ornamental Programme

The Coverdale Bible is ornamented with a series of woodcut initials at the opening of each book, a woodcut frontispiece (in the 1535 edition) depicting King Henry VIII seated in majesty and distributing the word to his people, and a series of smaller historiated initials at the opening of each chapter. The ornamental programme is distinctly Reformation in its iconography, with particular emphasis upon the distribution of the word to the laity.
