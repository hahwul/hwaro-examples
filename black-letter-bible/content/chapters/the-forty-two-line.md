## The Setting

Gutenberg began the composition of the forty-two-line Bible in his Mainz workshop in approximately 1452 and completed the printing around 1455. The edition was financed partially by Johann Fust, who subsequently brought suit against Gutenberg for non-repayment of the loans and obtained possession of the press and types in the celebrated lawsuit of November 1455. The technical achievement of the Bible was thus accomplished at very considerable personal cost to the man who accomplished it.

## The Type

The type cut for the forty-two-line Bible is the masterwork of the early printing era. It comprises approximately two hundred and ninety distinct sorts, including the twenty-six letters of the alphabet in both majuscule and minuscule, a full range of abbreviations and ligatures drawn from the manuscript tradition, and the numerous contextual variants (a flattened r after o, a long s at the beginning and middle of words, and so on) which are necessary for the type to imitate a scribal hand faithfully.

The body of the type is approximately what we should now call twelve-point, though the measurement is only approximate; early types were not cut to a standardised point body but to the needs of the particular page. The line spacing is generous; the two columns of text run to forty-two lines of approximately thirty characters each, upon a page which measures some thirty by forty centimetres.

## The Rubrication Programme

As in the manuscript tradition upon which it was modelled, the forty-two-line Bible was rubricated by hand after the sheets had been printed. The rubrication was typically done by scribes attached to Gutenberg's press or, in a few cases, by scribes in the monastic houses which had ordered the Bible directly. The programme includes: figurative illuminated initials at the opening of each book (painted in full colour upon a gold ground); chapter initials in red or blue; paragraph marks in red; and running headlines in red.

A small number of copies have the rubrication printed in red from a second impression - an experiment which Gutenberg appears to have abandoned as too costly and too technically difficult, and which was not attempted again until the 1460s.

## The Survival

Of approximately one hundred and eighty copies originally printed, forty-nine survive in whole or in part (as of the most recent census). Twenty-one are printed upon vellum, which was the preferred material for the luxury copies intended for ecclesiastical use; the remainder are upon paper. The complete vellum copies are among the most valuable books in existence.
