# About Panopticon

Panopticon is a publication examining the architecture of observation -- from Jeremy Bentham's original 1791 design to the invisible surveillance infrastructure embedded in every connected device you carry.

## What We Cover

**Surveillance architecture** -- the physical and digital structures designed to observe, record, and control. From prison designs to smart city sensor networks, we study how spaces are built to watch.

**Privacy in the digital age** -- the erosion of personal boundaries in an era of constant data collection. We analyze how corporate and state surveillance systems operate and what they mean for individual autonomy.

**Architectural theory** -- how the built environment shapes behavior, power dynamics, and social control. The panopticon was never just a building; it was an idea about what happens when people know they might be watched.

**Power and visibility** -- the politics of who watches and who is watched. Surveillance is never neutral. We examine the asymmetries of observation and their consequences.

## The Name

The Panopticon was a type of institutional building designed by the English philosopher Jeremy Bentham in the late 18th century. The concept allows a single watchman to observe all inmates of an institution without the inmates being able to tell whether they are being watched. Although it was conceived as a prison design, Bentham described the basic plan as applicable to hospitals, schools, factories, and any institution where oversight was desired.

The genius -- and the horror -- of the design is that the prisoners internalize the surveillance. They behave as though they are always watched, because they can never be certain they are not.
