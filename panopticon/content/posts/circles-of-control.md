There is a reason that power builds in circles. The circle has no blind spots. Every point on the perimeter is equidistant from the center. The person at the center can see everything; the people at the edge can see only the center and their immediate neighbors.

This is not abstract geometry. It is a blueprint for domination.

## The Roman Model

The amphitheater is one of the earliest and most explicit demonstrations of circular power architecture. The Colosseum in Rome was not merely an entertainment venue. It was a machine for organizing social hierarchy through spatial arrangement.

The Emperor sat in the imperial box, visible to all, watching all. The senators occupied the lower tiers. The common citizens sat higher. Slaves and women were relegated to the uppermost level. The floor of the arena -- the space of violence and spectacle -- was visible from every seat.

The message was architectural: power watches, and power is watched. The Emperor could observe the crowd. The crowd could observe the Emperor observing them. The gladiators existed in the zone of total visibility, where every action was subject to judgment from above.

## Bentham's Innovation

What Bentham added to this tradition was the element of uncertainty. In the amphitheater, the watched could see the watcher. In the Panopticon, they could not. This single modification transformed the architecture of observation from a display of power into an instrument of psychological control.

The amphitheater says: I am watching you, and I want you to know it.

The Panopticon says: I might be watching you, and you will never know.

The second statement is far more efficient. It requires fewer watchers. It operates continuously, even during the inspector's absence. It converts architecture into automation -- a surveillance system that runs on the anxiety of its subjects rather than the labor of its operators.

## The Command Center

Modern institutional design has absorbed the panoptic principle into what we might call the command center model. Consider a contemporary example: the traffic management center of a major city.

Banks of monitors display feeds from thousands of cameras. Operators sit at curved workstations arranged in tiers, facing a central wall of screens. The spatial arrangement replicates the logic of the watchtower: a central point of observation, a perimeter of data sources, and a designed asymmetry between the watchers and the watched.

The citizens driving through intersections do not know which cameras are monitored at any given moment. They cannot see the command center. They know it exists. They adjust their behavior accordingly.

This pattern repeats across institutions. The nuclear command bunker. The stock exchange trading floor. The hospital nursing station designed as a circular desk with sightlines to every patient room. The open-plan office where the manager's desk faces the workers.

## The Geometry of Resistance

If circular architecture serves power, what geometry serves resistance? History suggests the answer is the network -- a structure with no center, no single point of observation, no designed hierarchy.

Underground resistance movements have always organized in cellular structures precisely because cells defeat panoptic surveillance. No single node can see the entire network. Capturing one cell does not expose the others. The network has no watchtower because it has no center.

The early internet was celebrated as exactly this kind of structure: a decentralized network of equals, resistant to central control, impossible to observe in totality. That this architecture was subsequently colonized by centralized platforms -- Google, Facebook, Amazon -- represents one of the most significant structural reversals in technological history.

The network was rebuilt as a set of concentric circles with corporate watchtowers at their centers. The geometry of resistance was converted into the geometry of control.

## Designing Against Observation

There is a growing movement in architecture that takes the politics of visibility seriously. Privacy-oriented design considers sightlines, acoustic isolation, and data flows as fundamental elements of the built environment, not afterthoughts.

Some principles of this approach:

**Asymmetric opacity.** Spaces where the occupant can see out but cannot be seen from outside. The opposite of the Panopticon cell, which is transparent to the center and opaque to the perimeter.

**Distributed centers.** Layouts that create multiple gathering points rather than a single focal point. No throne, no watchtower, no panoptic center.

**Legible boundaries.** Clear demarcations between public and private space, so occupants always know when they have entered a zone of visibility.

**User-controlled transparency.** Walls, screens, and partitions that the occupant can adjust. Curtains rather than glass. Doors that lock from the inside.

These are not radical propositions. They are simply the application of a principle that Bentham understood perfectly and exploited ruthlessly: architecture shapes behavior. If we want different behavior -- behavior characterized by autonomy rather than compliance -- we must design different architecture.

## The Circle Persists

Despite everything, the circle remains a seductive form. It promises equality, community, and connection. Knights of the Round Table. The United Nations General Assembly. The talking circle of indigenous traditions.

The difference between a circle of equals and a circle of surveillance is the location of the observer. In a circle of equals, everyone sees everyone. In a Panopticon, only the center sees the perimeter.

The question for every circular space is the same: who is at the center, and can you see them looking back?
