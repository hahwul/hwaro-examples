Rain makes readers of us all. There is something about the sound of water on glass that turns the mind inward, toward the page, toward the sentence unfinished on the nightstand.

## The Right Book

Not every book suits the rain. Thrillers are too urgent. Self-help too earnest. What rain calls for is something quieter: a book of poems, a slim novel, a collection of letters written long ago between people who had time.

The Japanese have a word, *tsundoku*, for the practice of acquiring books and letting them pile up unread. Rain is the antidote to tsundoku. It gives us permission to stop doing and start reading.

## Atmosphere

A good reading session requires atmosphere. Not luxury, but a certain alignment of conditions. A chair by the window. Light enough to read by, dim enough to feel enclosed. The sound of rain, steady and without drama.

In these conditions, the boundary between reader and book thins. You forget the room. You forget the rain. You forget yourself. This is the gift that reading offers, and rain is the occasion that makes us accept it.

## Returning

The rain stops. You look up. The world outside is washed and bright. The book is still in your hands. You are, somehow, both the same person who sat down and someone slightly different.

This is what reading does. It changes you in ways too small to name but too real to deny.
