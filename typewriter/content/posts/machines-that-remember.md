My grandmother's typewriter sits on a shelf in my study. It is a 1962 Olivetti Lettera 32, sea-green with chrome accents, and it still works. The 'e' key strikes slightly lower than the others. The ribbon is dry but legible.

## Wear as Memory

Every machine that has been used carries the evidence of its use. A piano with yellowed keys. A carpenter's saw with a handle worn smooth. These are not flaws. They are the autobiography of an object, written by the hands that held it.

The Lettera 32 remembers my grandmother not through data or files, but through the physical impression of her habits. She hit the spacebar harder on the left side. The platen has a faint indentation where she always began her letters, three lines from the top.

## Digital Amnesia

Our modern devices carry no such marks. A laptop that has processed a million words looks identical to one fresh from the factory. When we replace our phones, we transfer the data but lose the object entirely. Nothing is worn. Nothing is personal.

This is efficient. It is also a kind of forgetting.

## The Case for Imperfection

There is a Japanese concept called wabi-sabi -- the beauty of imperfection, of things that bear the marks of time. A cracked tea bowl repaired with gold. A wooden beam weathered by decades of rain.

The typewriter is a wabi-sabi machine. It does not hide its history. Every scratch, every stuck key, every faded letter on the keyboard tells you something about the life it has lived.

Perhaps that is why people are drawn to these old machines. Not out of nostalgia, but out of a desire for objects that remember.
