# Dead Mall Frequencies: Muzak as Capitalist Liturgy

Walk into any surviving shopping mall and listen. Not to the conversations,
not to the registers, not to the fountain. Listen to the space between those
sounds. That is where the muzak lives.

Muzak -- the branded name that became a generic term for background music
engineered to modify behavior -- is perhaps the purest artistic expression
of consumer capitalism. It is music designed not to be listened to. Its
purpose is to create an atmosphere in which purchasing feels natural, even
inevitable. It is the liturgy of the consumer temple.

## The Architecture of Forgetting

Shopping malls were designed to disorient. The Gruen Transfer -- named after
architect Victor Gruen, who later disavowed his creation -- describes the
moment a shopper's purposeful visit becomes aimless wandering. Skylights
eliminate the passage of time. Corridors curve to prevent you from seeing
the exit. The muzak smooths every sharp edge of awareness.

This is not background music. This is architecture rendered in sound.

## The Frequency of Purchase

Studies conducted by the Muzak corporation in the mid-20th century
established that certain tempos corresponded to certain spending behaviors.
Slower tempos encouraged browsing. Faster tempos moved people through food
courts. The science was imprecise but the ambition was total: to create an
auditory environment where the only rational response was consumption.

Vaporwave artists understood this implicitly. When they slowed down smooth
jazz and corporate pop to half speed, they were not simply creating a mood.
They were performing an autopsy on the music's original function. At normal
speed, the manipulation is invisible. Slowed down, you can hear the gears.

## What the Dead Mall Remembers

There are YouTube channels dedicated to filming empty malls. The comments
sections are filled with a specific kind of grief -- not for the stores
themselves, but for the feeling the stores produced. People mourn the
sensation of walking through a place designed to make them want things.

This is the paradox at the heart of consumer nostalgia. We do not miss the
products. We miss the wanting. The dead mall, with its dry fountains and
shuttered storefronts, is a monument to desire itself -- desire stripped of
its object, pure and purposeless and therefore, finally, honest.

## The Last Broadcast

In the dead mall, the muzak has stopped. But if you stand in the right
corridor, at the right time of day, you might hear the echo of it still
bouncing off the tile floors. It has nowhere to go. The speakers are
disconnected but the architecture still resonates at the frequency of
purchase.

Some frequencies never fully decay. They just become quieter and quieter,
approaching silence asymptotically, never quite arriving.

The mall is closed. The muzak continues.
