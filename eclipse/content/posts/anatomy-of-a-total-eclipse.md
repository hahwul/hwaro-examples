A total solar eclipse unfolds in stages, each with its own character and its own science. Understanding these phases transforms casual observation into something far more rewarding.

## First Contact

The event begins when the leading edge of the moon first touches the solar disk. At this point, nothing is visible to the naked eye -- you need proper eclipse glasses or a pinhole projector to see the small bite the moon takes from the sun.

## The Partial Phases

Over the next hour or so, the moon slides steadily across the sun. Shadows sharpen. The light takes on an eerie, steel-blue quality that no photograph fully captures. Crescent-shaped projections appear under trees as gaps between leaves act as pinhole cameras.

## Shadow Bands

In the final seconds before totality, faint rippling bands of shadow may race across light-colored surfaces on the ground. These shadow bands are caused by atmospheric turbulence refracting the thin sliver of remaining sunlight, much like the shimmering of stars on the horizon.

## The Diamond Ring

Just before and just after totality, a single bead of sunlight blazes through a lunar valley while the corona is visible around the rest of the disk. The result is a brilliant point of light set on a ring of pearly white -- the diamond ring effect.

## Totality

When the moon fully covers the sun, the corona -- the sun's outer atmosphere -- becomes visible. It is a sight that has moved observers to tears for millennia. The temperature drops. Stars and planets appear. The horizon glows a muted sunset orange in every direction.

## Baily's Beads

Named after astronomer Francis Baily, these are multiple points of sunlight shining through valleys on the lunar limb. They appear in the seconds bracketing totality and provide data about the precise shape of the moon's terrain.

## Third Contact and Beyond

Totality ends as suddenly as it began. The diamond ring flashes again on the opposite limb, and the partial phases resume in reverse. The world slowly brightens. What felt like seconds may have been two minutes.

The entire event, from first to fourth contact, spans roughly two and a half hours. Totality itself lasts at most seven minutes and thirty-two seconds under ideal geometry, though most eclipses offer far less. Every second counts.
