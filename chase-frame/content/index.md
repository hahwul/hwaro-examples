## What a Forme Is

A forme is the complete arrangement of type, spacing material, and ornament that will, on impression, become one side of one sheet of paper. It is built on the stone, a flat slab of iron kept scrupulously level, in the shop where the press will, later, print it. A forme is always built in two dimensions. It has a height (the dimension from the bed of the press to the face of the letter) and a width (the type area of the page), but not, strictly speaking, a depth; all the type is of the same height, by convention and by specification, so that the bed of the press meets every letter flush.

The specifications of a forme are given in the masthead above. This book is set in Roboto Slab, twelve on fifteen (meaning the type is twelve points tall and is leaded to fifteen points, giving three points of space between lines), to a measure of twenty-eight picas (about four and a half inches), on eighty-pound Mohawk paper, with an expected run of twelve hundred copies.

### On the Discipline of the Stone

The stone is level, iron, three inches thick, and sits at the height of a man's waist. The compositor works at the stone standing, his weight centred over his feet, the chase before him, the type to his right, the furniture in a rack at his left, the reglet on a second rack below that. His tools are the composing mallet, the planer, the quoin key, and the string.

The forme is assembled in sequence. First the type is set and tied up in galley form with string; the string is loosened on the stone and the galley emptied into the chase. The type is arranged, column by column, in the chase with adequate space for furniture. Furniture is cut to length on a small bench saw kept in a corner of the shop, from standard stock supplied by Hamilton in Wisconsin or, nowadays, from our own reserves. The furniture is placed against the type, held in by hand, and the chase is filled out to within about half an inch of the inner edge.

The remaining space is taken by the quoin. The quoin is a two-piece wedge. Closed, it is slightly narrower than the remaining space; opened by a quarter-turn of the quoin key, it is wider by about three points. The compositor opens the quoins finger-tight, then planes the type with a hardwood planer, tapping the top of the planer with the composing mallet to seat every piece of type flat against the stone. When all type is seated, the quoins are tightened further, lightly and evenly, until a tap on any letter produces no movement.

This is called locking up. A forme properly locked will hold under press pressure at several hundred pounds per square inch, will accept ink evenly, and will impression cleanly onto paper.

### On the Lift

Once locked, the forme is lifted from the stone. The compositor checks the lift by placing a hand under one corner and raising it just clear of the stone; if any letter falls, the forme is not locked. A forme that does not lift is called pied. Pied type is reset, but only after locating every letter that fell; a single letter lost is a letter that will make a wrong impression somewhere else in the run. A compositor who loses a letter from a forme is expected to find it before the forme goes to press.

## What We Print

We print small editions of fine books, ephemera for other presses, wedding stationery, broadsides for poets, and the occasional commercial job when the commercial job is interesting. We do not print trade paperbacks, we do not print price lists, and we do not print anything that cannot be pulled on a hand press at a rate we consider fair to the pressman.
