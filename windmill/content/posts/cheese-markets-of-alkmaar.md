On Friday mornings from April through September, the Waagplein square in Alkmaar becomes a stage for one of the Netherlands' most enduring traditions. The Alkmaar cheese market has been operating in some form since the early 1300s, and while much of the country has modernized its agricultural trade beyond recognition, this city of 110,000 has chosen to keep the old ways alive. The result is a spectacle that is equal parts living history and genuine commerce.

The morning begins early, well before the crowds arrive. Enormous wheels of Gouda and Edam are laid out on the cobblestones in neat rows, their golden and red wax coatings gleaming in the morning light. Cheese carriers, dressed in white with colored hats that denote their guild, work in teams of four, balancing wooden stretchers called "berries" that hold stacks of cheese weighing up to 160 kilograms. Their practiced shuffle across the square is a skill passed down through generations.

Before any cheese changes hands, the quality must be assessed. Inspectors bore small plugs from sample wheels, examining the texture, aroma, and flavor with the concentrated attention of a sommelier evaluating wine. The subsequent negotiations between buyers and sellers follow an old ritual of hand-clapping -- two traders slap palms repeatedly while calling out prices until they reach agreement. It looks theatrical to modern eyes, but it remains a binding form of contract.

What strikes the visitor most, perhaps, is not the pageantry but the seriousness behind it. The Netherlands is one of the world's largest cheese exporters, and the traditions on display in Alkmaar represent the foundation of an industry that contributes billions to the national economy. Standing in the shadow of the fourteenth-century Waag building, watching the carriers jog past with their heavy loads, one understands how deeply food, commerce, and community have been woven together in Dutch life for seven centuries.
