The Netherlands has more bicycles than people -- roughly 23 million bikes for 17 million inhabitants -- and nowhere is this more evident than on the rural paths that follow the country's extensive canal network. These waterways, many of them dug by hand centuries ago to drain marshland and transport goods, now serve as corridors of tranquility that connect villages, farms, and nature reserves across the flat Dutch landscape.

A typical canal-side ride begins in a small town, perhaps Breukelen or Oudewater, where you pick up a well-marked cycling path and set off along the water's edge. The surface is usually smooth asphalt or packed brick, separated from motor traffic by grass verges or hedgerows. Within minutes the town falls away, replaced by open fields where black-and-white Friesian cattle graze behind low wire fences. Herons stand motionless at the water's edge, and the only sounds are the soft click of gears and the occasional call of a lapwing overhead.

The infrastructure that supports this kind of riding is extraordinary by any international standard. The country maintains over 37,000 kilometers of dedicated cycling paths, many of them following historic towpaths that once served horse-drawn barges. Junctions are numbered, creating a node-to-node navigation system that allows riders to plan routes of any length without ever consulting a complicated map. Ferries, most of them free for cyclists, provide crossings at rivers and wider canals, adding variety to the journey.

What makes canal cycling truly special, though, is the pace it imposes. There are no hills to conquer, no dramatic descents to rush through. The landscape reveals itself slowly -- a drawbridge here, a row of pollarded willows there, a farmhouse with its distinctive stepped gable reflected in still water. It is a form of travel that rewards attention over speed, and that quality makes it perfectly suited to a country that has always understood the value of living close to the ground and moving at a human scale.
