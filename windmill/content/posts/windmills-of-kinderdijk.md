Nineteen windmills stand in two rows along the canals of Kinderdijk, a small village in the Alblasserwaard polder southeast of Rotterdam. Built between 1738 and 1740, they form the largest concentration of historic windmills in the Netherlands and have been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1997. On a clear day, with their sails turning slowly against a wide Dutch sky, they present one of the most photographed scenes in all of Europe.

The mills at Kinderdijk were built for a purpose that defined Dutch civilization: water management. The Alblasserwaard sits well below sea level, and without constant drainage the land would revert to marsh within months. Each mill uses wind power to turn an Archimedes screw or internal paddle wheel, lifting water from the low-lying polder into a series of progressively higher canals that eventually drain into the river Lek. Working together, the nineteen mills could move enormous volumes of water, keeping farmland dry and habitable through the wettest seasons.

Visitors today can enter several of the mills and see the machinery up close. The interiors are surprisingly compact -- millers and their families lived in these structures, and the living quarters occupy whatever space the mechanical works did not claim. Climbing the narrow stairs, you pass the great horizontal shaft that connects the sails to the pumping mechanism below, and emerge onto a platform where the view extends across the polder in every direction. It is easy to see, from this vantage point, how dependent this entire landscape is on human engineering.

While electric pumping stations have taken over the daily work of water management, the Kinderdijk mills are still maintained in working order and set turning on designated days throughout the year. Watching them operate is a reminder that the Netherlands as we know it is not a natural landscape but an invented one -- a country literally constructed from water and mud by generations of people who refused to accept the limits their geography imposed. The windmills are monuments to that persistence, and they remain, after nearly three centuries, the most eloquent symbols of Dutch ingenuity.
