There is a particular kind of silence that exists only in the early morning. Before the phone buzzes. Before the kettle whistles. Before the world remembers that it is in a hurry.

## The Ritual

A slow morning is not about doing nothing. It is about doing things deliberately.

You wake without an alarm. You lie still for a moment, listening to the birds sorting out their territorial disputes outside the window. Then you rise and pad to the kitchen, where yesterday's sourdough loaf sits on the cutting board, still fragrant.

The kettle goes on. Loose-leaf tea -- not a bag, never a bag -- steeps in a ceramic pot that has a small chip on the rim. You bought it at a village market three summers ago. It holds exactly two cups.

## What You Miss When You Rush

When mornings are compressed into a frantic sequence of alarms, showers, and commutes, you lose something essential. You lose the quality of *noticing*.

Notice the way the steam from your tea curls in the cold morning air. Notice how the light shifts from grey to gold as the sun clears the treeline. Notice the cat, who has claimed the warmest spot on the kitchen floor and is absolutely certain that the entire house revolves around her comfort.

> "Almost everything will work again if you unplug it for a few minutes, including you."
>
> -- Anne Lamott

## A Simple Framework

If you want to reclaim your mornings, start small:

- **No screens for the first thirty minutes** -- the news will still be there later, and it will still be the same
- **Make something by hand** -- tea, toast, a sketch, a few lines in a journal
- **Step outside** -- even for two minutes, even if it is raining, especially if it is raining
- **Sit with silence** -- not meditation necessarily, just the absence of input

The world moves fast enough. Your mornings do not have to.
