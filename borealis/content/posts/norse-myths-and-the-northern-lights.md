# Norse Myths and the Northern Lights

Long before physicists understood solar wind and magnetospheric reconnection, the people of the north watched the same lights and found their own explanations. Their stories were not lesser for being unscientific. They were attempts to make sense of a sky that moved and burned and changed color in the dead of winter, and many of them carry a beauty that rivals the phenomenon itself.

## The Bifrost Bridge

In Norse cosmology, the universe is structured around Yggdrasil, the great ash tree whose roots and branches connect nine worlds. Asgard, the realm of the gods, sits at the top. Midgard, the world of humans, lies in the middle. Connecting them is the Bifrost -- the rainbow bridge, guarded by the ever-watchful god Heimdall.

The Prose Edda, written by the Icelandic scholar Snorri Sturluson in the thirteenth century, describes the Bifrost as a bridge that burns with fire to prevent the frost giants from crossing into Asgard. Many scholars believe the Norse identified the aurora with the Bifrost -- a shimmering, shifting bridge of light in the sky, red and green and white, appearing to connect heaven and earth.

Heimdall stands at its far end, his golden horn Gjallarhorn ready to sound the alarm when Ragnarok begins. The lights in the sky, then, were not merely beautiful. They were a reminder of the watchman, the bridge, and the fragile boundary between the worlds of gods and mortals.

## The Valkyries

Another Norse explanation held that the aurora was the light reflecting from the armor and shields of the Valkyries -- the warrior maidens who rode across the sky to choose the worthy dead from the battlefield. The slain heroes were carried to Valhalla, Odin's great hall, where they would feast and fight until Ragnarok.

The image is vivid: armored women on horseback, galloping through the darkness, their shields catching an otherworldly light. The flickering, shifting quality of the aurora -- the way it moves like fabric in wind -- lends itself to this interpretation. The lights moved because the Valkyries moved. The colors changed because the riders turned.

## The Sami Perspective

The Sami people, the indigenous inhabitants of northern Scandinavia and the Kola Peninsula, had a relationship with the aurora that was more cautious than celebratory. In Sami tradition, the northern lights -- called *guovssahas* -- were powerful and potentially dangerous.

Some Sami believed the aurora contained the spirits of the dead. To whistle, sing, or wave at the lights was to attract their attention, and the spirits might descend to seize the disrespectful observer. Children were warned to stay inside when the lights were strong. Even making eye contact with the aurora was considered risky in some communities.

Other Sami traditions held that the lights were the energies of souls traveling to the afterlife. The Sami word *guovssahas* has been interpreted as meaning "the light you can hear," connecting to the widespread Arctic belief that the aurora produces sound -- a crackling or whispering that modern science has only recently confirmed.

## The Finnish Tradition

In Finnish folklore, the aurora is called *revontulet* -- literally, "fox fires." The story tells of a great arctic fox running across the fells of Lapland. As the fox dashes through the snow, its tail strikes the drifts and sends sparks flying into the sky. These sparks become the northern lights.

It is a charming image, and it carries a particular Finnish quality -- the sense that even cosmic phenomena can be explained by the behavior of a clever animal in a vast, snow-covered landscape. The fox runs, the snow flies, and the sky burns. The name *revontulet* remains the standard Finnish word for the aurora borealis.

## The Greenlandic Inuit

The Inuit of Greenland believed the aurora was the spirits of stillborn children or children who died at birth, playing ball in the sky. The lights were called *arsarnerit* -- "those who play ball." The game they played used a walrus skull as the ball, and the swirling, rolling motion of the aurora was the movement of their play.

This is among the most poignant of all auroral myths. The grief of losing a child, transformed into a vision of eternal play in a sky of green and violet light. The aurora became not a source of fear but of comfort -- proof that the lost children were not gone but merely elsewhere, visible on winter nights.

## The Persistent Question

What strikes the modern reader about these myths is not their distance from scientific truth but their closeness to emotional truth. The aurora is overwhelming. It fills the sky. It moves. It changes color. It appears without warning and vanishes. It is silent -- or nearly so. It resists easy description.

The Norse, the Sami, the Finns, and the Inuit all grasped something essential about the aurora: that it is a boundary phenomenon. It marks the edge of something. The edge of the atmosphere, in scientific terms. The edge between worlds, in mythological terms. The edge between the living and the dead, the mortal and the divine.

These stories endure because the experience endures. Stand beneath the aurora in northern Norway on a clear February night, and the scientific explanation -- solar wind, magnetic reconnection, excited oxygen atoms -- will feel both completely true and completely insufficient. You will understand why someone once looked up and saw a bridge to the realm of the gods.
