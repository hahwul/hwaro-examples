In the Mediterranean, eating is rarely done indoors when the weather allows otherwise. The table is set outside, often beneath a pergola heavy with grapevine, its broad leaves filtering the afternoon sun into a shifting mosaic of light and shadow on the white tablecloth. There is bread in a basket, a carafe of local wine, a dish of olives already half-eaten before the first course arrives.

The meal itself is unhurried. It begins with small things: slices of cured ham, roasted peppers glistening with oil, a bowl of white beans dressed simply with lemon and herbs. Each dish arrives when it is ready, not according to any strict schedule. The conversation flows between courses, punctuated by the sound of glasses being refilled and the occasional burst of laughter from the far end of the table. Children run between the chairs, stealing bread.

The main course might be fish, grilled whole over wood coals and brought to the table on a wide ceramic platter, or perhaps a slow-cooked stew of lamb and artichokes that has been simmering since morning. The flavors are direct and honest, built from good ingredients treated simply. There is no artifice here, only the accumulated knowledge of generations who understood that the best cooking begins with what the land and sea provide.

As the meal stretches into evening, the light changes. The vine leaves above darken against a sky that shifts from blue to amber to violet. Someone brings fruit and cheese. The wine bottle, now empty, is replaced by another. Nobody checks the time. In this tradition, the table is not merely a place to eat but a place to be together, and the meal is finished only when every story has been told and every glass has been raised.
