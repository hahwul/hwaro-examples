The best way to see the Amalfi Coast is not from the winding road that clings to its cliffs, though that journey has its own drama. It is from the water, looking back at the shore, where the true scale of the landscape reveals itself. The mountains rise sharply from the sea, their faces carved into terraces that have been cultivated for a thousand years. Villages in shades of ochre, pink, and white tumble down the slopes as if poured there by a careless hand.

From a small wooden boat, the kind that local fishermen still use at dawn, the coastline unfolds slowly. Positano appears first, its houses stacked like a pastel amphitheater around a crescent of dark sand. Then comes Praiano, quieter and more secretive, its church dome catching the midday sun. Furore is barely visible, tucked into a narrow fjord where the sea rushes into shadow. Each village has its own character, its own rhythm, its own particular shade of blue in the water below.

The sea itself is extraordinary. Close to shore it is a pale, luminous turquoise, clear enough to see the rocky bottom several meters down. Further out it deepens to a rich cobalt that seems almost too saturated to be real. Fishermen pause in their work to wave as you pass. A ferry rounds the headland, its wake sending gentle swells against the hull. The salt air carries the scent of lemon blossoms from the groves above.

By late afternoon, when the light softens and the cliffs glow warm, the boat turns back toward harbor. The engine idles as you drift past one last sea cave, its interior lit an impossible shade of blue. Ashore, someone is setting tables on a terrace for the evening meal. The coast, seen from this angle, looks like a painting that nobody would believe was real.
