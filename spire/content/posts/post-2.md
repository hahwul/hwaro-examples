Six hundred years separated the laying of the foundation stone from the completion of the twin spires. Cologne Cathedral is a monument not only to faith and craft but to persistence across centuries, surviving wars and neglect to become the definitive expression of High Gothic ambition in northern Europe.

## The Original Vision

Construction began in 1248 under Archbishop Konrad von Hochstaden, who envisioned a cathedral grand enough to house the Reliquary of the Three Kings -- a golden sarcophagus containing what were believed to be the remains of the biblical Magi. The master builder, Gerhard, drew plans influenced by the great French cathedrals at Amiens and Beauvais, but scaled to surpass them all.

The choir was completed and consecrated in 1322. Work continued on the transept and nave through the fourteenth century, but the pace slowed. By 1473, the south tower had risen to a height of approximately fifty-nine meters. Then construction stopped. A great crane sat atop the unfinished tower for four centuries, becoming as familiar a part of the Cologne skyline as the cathedral itself.

## The Medieval Builders

The original construction employed techniques that pushed the boundaries of what stone could achieve. The choir vaults rise forty-three meters above the floor, making Cologne one of the tallest Gothic interiors ever built. The window tracery of the choir clerestory is of extraordinary delicacy, its mullions and mouchettes carved with a precision that seems to deny the hardness of the material.

The builders worked with trachyte from the Drachenfels quarry on the Rhine, a pale volcanic stone that gave the medieval portions of the cathedral a warm, almost luminous quality. This material weathers poorly, however, and centuries of Rhineland rain and industrial pollution have darkened it considerably.

## The Nineteenth-Century Completion

The rediscovery of the original medieval plans in the 1810s sparked a campaign to complete the cathedral. The Prussian state, the Catholic Church, and private citizens formed the Central Cathedral Building Society in 1842. King Friedrich Wilhelm IV laid a new foundation stone, and work resumed using the original Gothic designs with nineteenth-century engineering methods.

The twin spires were completed in 1880, reaching 157 meters -- making Cologne Cathedral briefly the tallest building in the world. The completed structure followed the medieval plans so faithfully that only a trained eye can distinguish thirteenth-century stonework from nineteenth-century additions, though the difference in stone color provides a subtle guide.

## Walking the Nave

To enter Cologne Cathedral through the western portal is to be drawn irresistibly upward. The nave stretches 144 meters to the high altar, and every vertical line converges toward the vaulting far overhead. The clustered piers rise without interruption, their shafts multiplying as they ascend, finally bursting into the ribs of the quadripartite vaults.

The stained glass is remarkable in its range. The medieval Adoration of the Magi window in the north aisle sits alongside the controversial 2007 window by Gerhard Richter -- a grid of 11,500 colored squares, abstract and luminous, dividing opinion as sharply as any work of art in a sacred space can.

The cathedral endures, blackened by soot, surrounded by the noise of a modern rail junction, yet utterly commanding. Its spires are visible from every approach to the city. In Cologne, the cathedral is not a landmark among others; it is the landmark by which all others are located.
