York Minster stands at the crossroads of every major phase of English Gothic architecture. Within a single building, one can trace the evolution from the austere lancets of the Early English period through the flowing curves of the Decorated style to the soaring vertical lines of the Perpendicular. It is, in effect, a textbook in stone.

## A Cathedral of Transitions

The present building was begun around 1220, when Archbishop Walter de Gray initiated construction of the south transept in the Early English style. The lancet windows of the north transept, known collectively as the Five Sisters, date from approximately 1260. Each lancet rises over sixteen meters tall, filled with grisaille glass -- geometric patterns in grey and green that admit a cool, filtered light.

By the time the nave was begun in 1291, English taste had moved toward the Decorated style. The nave arcade shows this shift: the piers are more complex, the moldings richer, the tracery of the aisle windows more flowing and inventive. The great west window, completed around 1338, displays curvilinear tracery of extraordinary intricacy, its stonework describing flame-like forms that give this substyle its French name: Flamboyant.

The choir and east end, rebuilt between 1361 and 1405, mark the arrival of the Perpendicular style. Here the tracery straightens, the mullions run unbroken from sill to arch, and the overriding impression is of height achieved through repetition of vertical elements. The Great East Window, glazed by John Thornton of Coventry between 1405 and 1408, is the largest expanse of medieval stained glass in England -- the size of a tennis court, containing over a hundred individual panels depicting Genesis and the Apocalypse.

## The Chapter House

The octagonal Chapter House, completed around 1296, is among the finest rooms of the English Decorated period. It achieves an unsupported span of nearly eighteen meters -- no central pillar interrupts the space. The vault, a wooden structure disguised as stone, springs from slender wall shafts that seem far too delicate for the task. The canopied stall seats below are carved with scenes from everyday medieval life: a woman spinning, a man playing a viol, a fox preaching to geese.

## The Undercroft

Beneath the Minster lies evidence of every structure that has occupied this site: the Roman principia, or military headquarters, of Eboracum; the remains of the Norman cathedral begun by Thomas of Bayeux in 1080; and the massive concrete piers inserted in the 1960s and 70s when the central tower threatened to collapse.

These modern foundations, ugly and utilitarian, saved the building. They are a reminder that preservation is not always elegant, and that each generation must choose how much it is willing to invest to keep the past standing.

## The City of York

York is a city shaped by its Minster in ways both obvious and subtle. The medieval street plan radiates from the cathedral close. The city walls, nearly complete and walkable, provide elevated views toward the Minster from every direction. The narrow streets of the Shambles, with their overhanging timber-framed houses, exist in dialogue with the massive stone edifice that looms at the end of nearly every sightline.

On a winter evening, when the floodlights illuminate the west front and the city grows quiet, the Minster assumes a presence that transcends architecture. It becomes what its builders intended: a vision of the heavenly Jerusalem, planted in English soil, enduring against the darkness.
