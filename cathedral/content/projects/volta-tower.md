## Design Intent

Volta Tower rises from a triangular site in Buenos Aires' Puerto Madero district, where the city meets the Rio de la Plata. The design challenge was to provide every apartment with both river views and adequate daylight, despite the tower's slender footprint and the proximity of neighboring high-rises.

The architects' solution is a floor plate that rotates incrementally --- each level is offset two degrees from the one below, accumulating a total rotation of fifty-four degrees over the building's twenty-eight stories. This twist opens sightlines that a conventional orthogonal tower would block, ensuring that no two floors share identical view corridors.

## Structure and Materiality

The structural system is a reinforced concrete core with perimeter columns, the latter expressed as prominent white precast concrete fins that spiral up the facade following the rotational logic. These fins serve triple duty: structure, solar shading, and formal expression. Their depth varies from 300 millimeters at the base to 150 millimeters at the crown, tapering as structural loads decrease with height.

Between the fins, a high-performance curtain wall of floor-to-ceiling glass wraps each floor. The glass is lightly tinted grey, reducing glare without distorting color perception of the river and sky beyond.

The lobby and common areas at ground level are finished in Patagonian marble --- a dark, fossil-rich stone with dramatic veining --- and blackened steel. The contrast between these heavy, anchoring materials and the lightweight, twisting tower above reinforces the building's vertical narrative of ground to sky.

## Typical Floor

Each floor contains two apartments arranged around the central core, with a shared elevator lobby. The rotation means that apartment layouts are not strictly identical between floors, though the differences are subtle --- primarily in the angle of the exterior wall relative to the core.

Living spaces are oriented outward, bedrooms inward. Floor-to-ceiling glass in the main rooms maximizes the panoramic views, while bedrooms receive daylight through the gaps between the precast fins, which act as deep window reveals that frame focused views of the city.

Interior finishes are restrained: white plaster walls, engineered oak flooring, and stone-clad bathrooms. The architects specified no applied color --- the palette relies entirely on natural material tones and the changing light reflected off the river.

## Engineering

The rotational geometry required each floor's formwork to be custom-set, adding complexity to the construction sequence. The team developed a reusable adjustable formwork system that could accommodate the two-degree offset without requiring entirely new forms for each pour.

Wind engineering was critical given the tower's exposed riverside location. The twisted form actually improves aerodynamic performance compared to a prismatic tower of similar dimensions, reducing vortex-induced oscillation and allowing a more efficient structural design.
