## Design Intent

The Weave occupies a tight mid-block site in Taipei's Zhongshan district, an area where the existing urban fabric is predominantly five-story walk-up buildings with tiled facades. The architects sought to honor this material tradition while reimagining its formal possibilities.

The building's defining feature is its facade: a three-dimensional screen of custom-glazed ceramic tiles arranged in a woven pattern that projects up to 200 millimeters from the building face. The tiles, produced by a Yingge ceramics workshop, come in three shapes and two glaze colors --- a deep indigo and a warm off-white --- creating a moire-like visual effect that shifts as one moves along the street.

## Structure and Materiality

A conventional steel frame supports the six-story building, with composite metal deck floors and a flat concrete roof. The structural system is deliberately prosaic, freeing the budget for the ceramic facade and interior finishes.

The ceramic screen is mounted on a secondary framework of stainless steel clips attached to horizontal aluminum rails. Each tile is individually hung, allowing damaged units to be replaced without disturbing adjacent pieces. The air gap between screen and building envelope provides natural ventilation for the exterior wall assembly and reduces solar heat gain.

Ground-floor retail spaces feature terrazzo floors with chips of the same ceramic used on the facade, tying interior and exterior material palettes together. Upper-floor offices have polished concrete floors and ceiling-mounted linear light fixtures in anodized aluminum.

## Program

The building houses three ground-floor retail units, four floors of flexible office space, and a rooftop event terrace shaded by a lightweight steel pergola. A narrow central atrium connects all levels visually, with open-tread steel stairs providing an alternative to the elevator.

The retail units feature full-height glazing set behind the ceramic screen, creating a layered threshold between street and interior. At night, interior lighting projects through the tile pattern, casting intricate shadows onto the sidewalk.

## Urban Context

The Weave's massing follows the prevailing street wall height of its neighbors, stepping back at the fifth floor to reduce its apparent bulk. The ceramic pattern intensifies at the building's base --- tiles are more closely spaced and three-dimensional --- and loosens toward the top, where wider gaps between tiles allow views out from the upper office floors.

This vertical gradient creates a visual hierarchy that reads at both the pedestrian scale and from across the district. The architects describe it as a "thickening" of the facade at the ground level, where the relationship between building and city is most immediate.
