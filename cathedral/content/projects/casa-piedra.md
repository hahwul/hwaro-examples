## Design Intent

Casa Piedra begins with the terrain. Situated on a south-facing slope overlooking the Laja River valley, the house was designed to emerge from the hillside rather than sit upon it. The architects worked closely with local stonemasons, using cantera rosa --- the pink volcanic stone native to the region --- as both structure and finish.

The plan follows the topography. Three interlocking volumes step down the slope, each offset to capture different views and create sheltered outdoor courts between them. The roofline remains below the ridge, preserving the hill's profile when viewed from the valley below.

## Structure and Materiality

The lower level is constructed almost entirely of rammed earth walls, their layered strata echoing the geological formations visible in nearby ravines. These walls, ranging from 400 to 600 millimeters in thickness, provide substantial thermal mass that regulates interior temperatures across the region's dramatic diurnal swings.

Upper volumes are framed in reinforced concrete, clad externally in hand-cut cantera blocks laid in a running bond. The stone was quarried from a site less than fifteen kilometers away, and its warm pink tone shifts subtly in different light conditions --- cool and grey in early morning, almost amber at sunset.

Interior woodwork is exclusively mesquite, a hardwood harvested from trees cleared during site preparation. Window frames, door thresholds, and built-in shelving all share this material, creating continuity between rooms.

## Spatial Sequence

Arrival is from the upper road, where a narrow path of loose gravel leads to a recessed entrance --- a deep stone portal that transitions visitors from the bright exterior to a compressed, low-ceilinged vestibule. From here, the house opens dramatically: a double-height living space frames a panoramic view of the valley through a single, floor-to-ceiling window.

The living and dining areas occupy the middle volume, connected to a shaded courtyard on the west. The kitchen is partially open-air, with a retractable canvas canopy that can be drawn back to expose the cooking area to the sky.

Bedrooms are located in the lowest volume, dug partially into the hillside. Their rammed earth walls remain cool even on the hottest days. Each bedroom opens to a private garden enclosed by stone walls, offering seclusion within the larger landscape.

## Landscape Integration

The planting strategy uses only species found within a fifty-kilometer radius. Agave, prickly pear, and native grasses populate the terraced gardens, irrigated by a gravity-fed system that channels rainwater collected from the concrete roofs into a stone cistern.

A swimming pool, lined in dark grey plaster, occupies the lowest terrace. Its still surface reflects the sky and surrounding hills, reading from a distance as a natural rock pool rather than an artificial amenity.
