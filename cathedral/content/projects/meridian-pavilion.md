## Design Intent

The Meridian Pavilion was conceived as an open threshold between the city and its largest public park. Sited at the intersection of two pedestrian routes, the building acts less as a destination and more as a permeable frame --- a space that invites passage while offering moments of stillness.

The architects describe their approach as one of "structured openness." The pavilion's primary gesture is a sweeping CLT roof plane that hovers above a largely transparent ground floor, supported by a rhythmic colonnade of slender timber columns. The roof extends well beyond the enclosed volume, creating deep overhangs that shelter outdoor seating and a small public stage.

## Structure and Materiality

Cross-laminated timber forms the structural backbone. The roof is composed of five prefabricated CLT panels, each spanning up to twelve meters, joined with concealed steel connectors. This system allowed the pavilion to be erected in just three weeks on site.

The facade alternates between floor-to-ceiling low-iron glass and panels of charred larch cladding. The charring, done using the traditional shou sugi ban technique, provides both weather resistance and a textural contrast to the precision of the glass.

At the base, a continuous plinth of weathering steel wraps the perimeter, grounding the lightweight timber structure against the landscape. Over time, this steel will develop a deep rust patina, integrating the building further into the park's material palette.

## Spatial Experience

Entering from the south, visitors pass beneath the extended overhang into a single open room. The interior is deliberately spare: polished concrete floors, exposed timber ceiling, and minimal partitions. A long service counter in oiled oak runs along the east wall, housing a small cafe and information point.

The north facade opens entirely via a folding glass wall, dissolving the boundary between interior and the adjacent meadow. On warm days, the pavilion effectively becomes a covered terrace --- neither fully inside nor out.

Artificial lighting is restrained. Recessed linear LEDs trace the underside of the CLT panels, providing warm ambient light after dark without competing with the park's natural darkness.

## Site Response

The building sits lightly on its site, with foundations limited to a grid of screw piles that avoided excavation of the park's root systems. Rainwater from the roof is collected in a below-grade cistern and used to irrigate the surrounding planting beds.

The orientation was determined by prevailing wind patterns: the long axis runs east-west, sheltering the south-facing terrace from northerly winds while maximizing solar gain through the glass facade during winter months.
