# Veiled Forms: A New Exhibition at the Palazzo Reale

The Palazzo Reale has opened what may be the most significant marble sculpture exhibition of the decade. "Veiled Forms" brings together forty-seven works spanning four centuries, each exploring the paradox of revealing through concealment -- the artistic tradition of carving translucent veils, draped fabric, and gossamer shrouds from solid stone.

## The Collection

The exhibition is organized chronologically, beginning with a rarely displayed Antonio Corradini piece from 1720 and progressing through neoclassical, romantic, and contemporary interpretations of the veiled figure. The curatorial decision to maintain generous spacing between works allows each piece to command its own atmosphere.

The centerpiece is undeniably Giuseppe Sanmartino's contribution, placed in a purpose-built alcove with carefully calibrated lighting that emphasizes the impossible delicacy of stone rendered as sheer fabric. Visitors consistently pause here longest, circling the work in visible disbelief.

## Contemporary Voices

The final gallery introduces living artists working in this tradition. Of particular note is Yuki Ando's "Dissolution" -- a figure carved from Statuario marble in which the veil appears to be dissolving into mist at its edges. The technical achievement is remarkable; the stone is worked to near transparency at points, with light passing through mere millimeters of material.

Kevin Okafor's "Signal" takes a more conceptual approach, presenting a classical veiled bust overlaid with carved geometric patterns suggesting digital interference. The marriage of ancient technique and contemporary reference is handled with restraint that lesser artists might not have managed.

## The Space

Credit must be given to the exhibition design team. The galleries have been refinished in a warm dove gray that complements the white and gray marbles without competing for attention. Lighting is subdued but precisely directed, creating soft shadows that enhance the three-dimensionality of each piece.

## Verdict

"Veiled Forms" runs through September and deserves multiple visits. The catalog, featuring essays by Maria Grazia Conte and photographs by Hiroshi Sugimoto, is itself a substantial publication worthy of any collector's library.
