## Concerning the Collection

The library occupies the upper two rooms of a narrow house on Blackbird Street. It has no hours,
receives no grants, and asks nothing of its visitors save the ordinary courtesies owed to
small rooms full of paper &#8212; a steady hand, a dry coat, a moderate voice. Books are accessioned
as they arrive; none is ever deaccessioned. If a volume leaves the shelves, it leaves because
someone has borrowed it, and borrowed books always come home.

## What Is a Bookplate

A bookplate (or *ex libris*, literally &#8220;from the books of&#8221;) is a small printed label
affixed to the inside front board of a volume, declaring its owner. The practice dates at least to
the fifteenth century and survives, quietly, wherever private libraries survive. Plates may be
strictly utilitarian &#8212; a name, a number, a blank line for the date &#8212; or they may
carry elaborate iconography: a family crest, a motto in Latin, a miniature landscape, a literary
device. Every plate in this register has been cut on copper and printed upon handmade stock before
being pasted in.

## The Terms of the Shelf

> Books come home.

This is the first and last rule. A volume borrowed from these shelves must be returned within the
agreed span. A volume lost must be honestly reported. A volume damaged must be mourned.
Readers who cannot agree to this may still come visit, but they may not borrow.

## Corresponding

Letters and postcards may be addressed to the keeper by way of the catalogue. Enquiries
concerning specific accessions are welcome, as are offers of donation, though no book enters
the shelves without first being read through.
