## Concerning the Plate

A plate in two colours (burnt sienna and black) depicting a kneeling scribe and, beside him,
a small hand-press. The two figures face one another across a spread page, the ink-block
between them bridging a thousand years. The scroll beneath reads *MANU ET PRELO*
&#8212; &#8220;by the hand and by the press&#8221; &#8212; which struck the keeper as the
kindest possible summary of the book&#39;s argument.

## On the Argument

Caxton-Ferrars is not a polemicist. The book refuses to mourn the scribe or to celebrate
the press; instead it treats each successive technology of reproduction as a form of
attention, and asks, carefully and at some length, *what kind* of attention each form
encourages. The answers surprise.

The chapter on the photostat is particularly fine. It argues that the ability to reproduce
a page *without reading it* is a new kind of freedom and a new kind of violence, and that
libraries are the institutions which have been quietly negotiating the difference between
the two for most of the twentieth century.
