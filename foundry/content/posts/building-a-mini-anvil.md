This project involved casting a small bench anvil from scrap steel and iron collected from the shop over the past year. The pattern was carved from pine in a traditional London-pattern anvil shape, scaled down to roughly 15 pounds. The pattern included draft angles on all vertical faces and was sealed with shellac to prevent moisture absorption from the greensand mold.

The mold was rammed up in a two-part flask using petrobond sand, with the parting line running along the widest profile of the anvil body. A ceramic pouring cup and runner system directed the molten metal to the base of the mold cavity, allowing the metal to rise smoothly and push air out through vent holes poked into the cope. The pour was done with metal melted in a propane-fired crucible furnace, reaching approximately 2800 degrees Fahrenheit for the cast iron charge.

After shakeout and cleaning, the casting was ground smooth on the faces and horn using an angle grinder with flap discs. The hardy hole and pritchel hole were drilled and broached on the drill press. The face was milled flat on a vertical mill, then hardened by heating with an oxy-acetylene torch and quenching with a wet rag to produce a shallow hardened layer. The finished anvil rings clearly when struck and serves well for light forging, riveting, and jewelry work at the bench.
