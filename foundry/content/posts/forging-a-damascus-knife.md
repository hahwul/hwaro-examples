This build documents the process of forging a Damascus steel kitchen knife from alternating layers of 1095 and 15N20 steel. The billet started as a stack of eleven layers, which was drawn out, folded, and welded repeatedly in the coal forge until reaching approximately 176 layers. Maintaining consistent forge temperature was critical throughout the welding process to avoid delamination between the two steel types.

After the final draw-out and rough profiling on the anvil, the blade was ground to shape on a 2x72 belt grinder using 36-grit ceramic belts for hogging material, then stepping through 120, 220, and 400 grits. The bevels were ground freehand with a platen, aiming for a flat grind that tapers evenly from spine to edge. Heat treatment involved normalizing three times, then a Parks 50 quench from critical temperature, followed by two one-hour temper cycles at 400 degrees Fahrenheit.

The handle was shaped from stabilized walnut with brass pins and a mosaic pin at the center. After fitting the scales with epoxy, the handle was profiled on the belt grinder and hand-sanded through 600 grit before finishing with tung oil. The completed knife weighs 210 grams and holds an edge well through extended kitchen use. The Damascus pattern revealed during etching in ferric chloride shows a tight, uniform ladder pattern across the blade face.
