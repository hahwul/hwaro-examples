## Day Three of Traverse

We departed the coastal station at DYE-2 seventy-two hours ago with two sledges and provisions for twelve days. The route follows a bearing of zero-four-seven degrees magnetic, ascending gradually from the marginal ice zone onto the interior plateau.

Surface conditions have been consistent: hard wind-packed sastrugi oriented northeast to southwest, spaced at roughly half-meter intervals. Travel speed has averaged four point two kilometers per hour, which is acceptable given the sledge weight of approximately three hundred kilograms each.

## Observations at Altitude

At the current position we have gained fourteen hundred meters of elevation since departure. The air is noticeably thinner and drier. Breathing produces an immediate cloud of ice crystals that hang motionless in calm conditions before settling onto clothing and equipment.

Temperature overnight reached negative forty-one. At this threshold, exposed skin begins to freeze in under sixty seconds. All work outside the tent is conducted in full face protection with only a narrow slit for vision. Even brief removal of gloves to manipulate instruments requires immediate rewarming.

## Instrument Deployment

Installed the first of three automated weather stations at waypoint GRL-INT-07. The station records temperature, pressure, wind speed, wind direction, and snow accumulation at fifteen-minute intervals. Data is transmitted via Iridium satellite modem every six hours.

```
STATION GRL-INT-07 CONFIGURATION
---------------------------------
Lat:        72.5794 N
Lon:        38.4597 W
Elevation:  2,412 m
Sensors:    T, P, WS, WD, SD
Interval:   15 min
Uplink:     Iridium / 6 hr cycle
Power:      Li-ion / solar hybrid
```

The installation required ninety minutes. Anchoring the mast in compacted firn at this temperature demanded pre-drilling with a hand auger, as the snow behaved more like rock than crystalline ice.

## Conditions Summary

The interior ice sheet is a landscape reduced to its most fundamental elements: white below, blue or grey above, and the thin line of the horizon connecting them. There are no landmarks. Navigation is entirely by GPS and compass. If both fail, the protocol is to stop, anchor the tent, and wait.

We do not deviate from the protocol.
