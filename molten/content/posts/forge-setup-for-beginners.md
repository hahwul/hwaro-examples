# Forge Setup for Beginners

Starting metalwork doesn't require a massive investment. Here's how to set up a functional forge on a budget.

## Essential Equipment

### The Forge

**Option 1: Coal Forge** ($100-300)
- Traditional, excellent temperature control
- Requires coal/coke fuel supply
- More smoke, needs good ventilation

**Option 2: Propane Forge** ($150-400)
- Clean burning, easy temperature control
- Ongoing propane costs
- Best for beginners

**Option 3: DIY Brake Drum Forge** ($50-100)
- Repurposed car brake drum
- Hand-crank or electric blower
- Great starter option

### The Anvil

Don't spend $1000 on a pristine anvil. Alternatives:

- Railroad track section (free to $50)
- Harbor freight anvil ($50-100)
- Large sledgehammer head (mounted upside down)
- Heavy steel plate (mounted on wood block)

### Hammers

Start with three:

1. **Cross-peen** (2.5 lb) - General forging
2. **Rounding** (3 lb) - Drawing out, shaping
3. **Ball-peen** (1.5 lb) - Detail work

## Safety First

This is **not optional**:

- Safety glasses (always)
- Leather apron
- Leather gloves (when handling hot steel)
- Steel-toed boots
- Fire extinguisher (ABC rated)
- First aid kit with burn treatment

## Workshop Layout

Keep your work triangle tight:

```
     FORGE
      / \
     /   \
 ANVIL   SLACK TUB
```

Everything within 2-3 steps. Hot steel doesn't wait.

## First Project

Before attempting blades, master these skills:

1. Draw out a taper
2. Spread material (upset)
3. Bend accurate 90° angles
4. Forge weld two pieces
5. Punch and drift a hole

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- Working steel too cold (causes cracking)
- Hitting too hard (loses control)
- Poor tong grip (drops hot steel)
- Not using flux when welding
- Rushing the heat

## Moving Forward

Start small. Make hooks, bottle openers, fire pokers. Learn to read heat colors, control hammer blows, and manage your workspace.

The forge rewards patience and punishes haste. Take your time, stay safe, and enjoy the process.
