## The Erasing Light

Fog is the world's most democratic filter. It applies uniformly, softening every surface with the same indifferent hand. In color photography, fog tends to flatten and dull. In black and white, it does something remarkable instead: it separates planes.

When the middle distances dissolve into pale gray, the foreground objects are thrown into sharper relief. The tree you might have overlooked becomes the only solid thing in the frame. The hill behind it becomes a suggestion. The sky behind that becomes nothing at all.

## Gradients of Air

The technical term is aerial perspective: the further something is from the camera, the more atmosphere lies between it and the lens, and the lighter its tonal value becomes. Painters discovered this long before photographers did. Fog simply makes the effect extreme, reducing a world of complex tonal variation to three or four distinct zones of gray.

This essay was shot over three consecutive mornings in late autumn, when the valley fogs were still heavy and slow to lift. By nine o'clock, most of the effect was gone. The window for this kind of photograph is narrow.

## What Disappears

The most interesting images in this series are not the ones showing what remained visible, but the ones recording what was barely there. A fence post emerging from white nothingness. A bird line dissolved to a single dark smear against pale gray. The edge of a barn, cut off at the waist by cloud.

Absence, it turns out, is as photogenic as presence — perhaps more so.
