## Steel, Glass, and Shadow

The city is a machine for making angles. Every facade is a proposition about light: how it falls, where it gathers, what it refuses to illuminate.

This essay documents a single city block at six different hours. The same surfaces, the same materials — but the shifting sun creates what amounts to six entirely different photographs. At noon, the steel and glass flatten to near-white. At dusk, the same building contracts into deep charcoal, its edges barely distinguishable from the sky behind it.

There is a lesson here about photography that applies equally to architecture: structure only becomes visible through the contrast of what surrounds it.

## On Composition

Each frame in this series was composed around a single tonal anchor — one zone of absolute black or absolute white — and the rest of the image arranged in relationship to that anchor. The eye always needs somewhere to rest. In grayscale work, that resting place is invariably the extreme: the blown highlight or the crushed shadow.

Midtones, on their own, have no personality. They require context. This is perhaps the central truth of black-and-white photography.

## Technical Notes

Shot with a 50mm lens on a full-frame body. No filters used. Post-processing limited strictly to tonal adjustment — no content alteration. The city looked exactly like this.
