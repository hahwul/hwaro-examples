When Isaac Newton first passed white light through a glass prism in 1666, he revealed the hidden spectrum that makes up visible light. That discovery changed not just physics, but art and design forever.

<!-- more -->

## The Visible Spectrum

A prism separates white light into its component wavelengths:

- **Red** (700 nm) — Longest wavelength, least refracted
- **Orange** (590 nm) — Warm and inviting
- **Yellow** (570 nm) — High visibility, energetic
- **Green** (520 nm) — Natural, balanced
- **Blue** (470 nm) — Cool, calming
- **Violet** (380 nm) — Shortest wavelength, most refracted

## Applying the Spectrum to Design

Prismatic palettes don't use all rainbow colors at once. Instead, they pick key spectral anchors and let gradients bridge between them:

1. **Dual-anchor** — Two spectral colors with a gradient bridge (e.g., violet to cyan)
2. **Triadic spectrum** — Three evenly spaced spectral colors
3. **Full dispersion** — A controlled rainbow gradient for maximum impact

The key is maintaining contrast against dark backgrounds while letting the spectral colors provide energy and movement.

## Dark Canvas, Bright Light

Dark themes are ideal for prismatic design because they mimic the conditions inside a diamond — a dark setting that makes refracted light pop with maximum intensity.

A dark `#0a0a0f` background with spectral accents creates the same visual drama as a diamond displayed on black velvet.
