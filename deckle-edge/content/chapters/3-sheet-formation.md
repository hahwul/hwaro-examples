## The vatman's stroke

<p class="lede">Sheet formation is the moment of creation in papermaking. The vatman lifts the mould and deckle, dips them into the vat of diluted pulp, and draws them out in a single fluid motion. As the mould rises from the vat, the water drains through the screen and the suspended fibers settle into a thin, uniform mat on the mould surface. The entire operation takes perhaps five seconds, but the quality of the finished sheet depends entirely on the skill and consistency of this single gesture.</p>

The Western method of sheet formation, called tame-zuki in Japanese terminology, involves a single dip and a controlled shake. The vatman plunges the mould into the vat at an angle, scoops up a charge of pulp, and lifts the mould level. As the water begins to drain, the vatman shakes the mould gently -- first side to side, then front to back -- to distribute the fibers evenly and to encourage them to interlock in all directions. When the water has drained to the point that the mat of fibers no longer flows, the vatman removes the deckle and passes the mould to the coucher.

## Nagashizuki: the Japanese method

Japanese papermaking uses a fundamentally different technique called nagashizuki, which translates roughly as "flowing formation." Rather than a single dip, the Japanese papermaker makes multiple dips, adding thin layers of pulp to the mould in succession. Between each dip, the excess pulp is poured off the far edge of the mould, and the fibers that remain are shaken into alignment.

This layered technique allows the papermaker to control the thickness and density of the sheet with great precision. It also produces a sheet in which the fibers are more thoroughly interlocked than in Western paper, because each successive layer bonds with the one beneath it while both are still wet. The addition of neri -- a viscous plant mucilage extracted from the tororo-aoi root -- slows the drainage of water through the mould, giving the papermaker more time to manipulate each layer.

## Achieving uniformity

The greatest challenge in sheet formation is achieving a uniform thickness across the entire surface of the mould. Thin spots will be weak; thick spots will dry unevenly and may cockle. The experienced vatman develops an intuitive feel for the weight and flow of the pulp on the mould, adjusting the angle of the dip, the vigor of the shake, and the speed of the lift to compensate for variations in consistency, temperature, and the behavior of different fibers.
