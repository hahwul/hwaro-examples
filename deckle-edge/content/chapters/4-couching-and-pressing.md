## The couch

<p class="lede">Couching (pronounced "cooching," from the French coucher, to lay down) is the transfer of the newly formed sheet from the mould to a dampened felt blanket. The coucher inverts the mould onto the felt and presses it down with a gentle rocking motion, releasing the wet sheet from the wire screen. The sheet adheres to the felt by capillary action, and the mould is lifted away cleanly if the motion has been correct. A failed couch -- in which the sheet folds, wrinkles, or refuses to release -- means the loss of a sheet and the beginning of the formation process again.</p>

The felts serve a dual purpose: they provide a cushioned surface that receives the wet sheet without distorting it, and they separate the sheets in the stack so that they can be pressed without sticking to one another. Traditional papermaking felts are woven from wool, which absorbs water readily and provides a slightly textured surface. The texture of the felt transfers faintly to the paper surface, contributing to the tactile character of handmade paper.

## The post

As the vatman forms sheets and the coucher transfers them to felts, a stack gradually builds: felt, sheet, felt, sheet, felt, sheet. This stack is called the post. A day's work for a two-person team working at a steady pace might produce a post of one hundred to two hundred sheets, depending on the size and weight of the paper. The post must be pressed as soon as possible after the last sheet is couched, before the uppermost sheets begin to dry unevenly.

The press removes the bulk of the water from the post and consolidates the loose mat of fibers into a coherent sheet. The traditional instrument is a screw press, in which a heavy wooden platen is driven downward by a large central screw. Modern workshops may use hydraulic presses, which can apply more uniform pressure over a larger area.

The pressing is done gradually. The screw is tightened slowly over a period of minutes, allowing the water to escape through the felts without distorting the sheets. Pressing too quickly or too forcefully can cause the wet sheets to shift on the felts, creating wrinkles that cannot be removed. The goal is to reduce the moisture content of the sheets from approximately 80 percent (as they come from the mould) to approximately 50 percent (ready for drying).

After pressing, the sheets are carefully separated from the felts -- a process called parting. The papermaker lifts each felt from the post and peels the sheet from its surface. If the sheet has been properly formed and pressed, it will release cleanly; if the fibers have been over-beaten or the felt is worn smooth, the sheet may tear during parting.
