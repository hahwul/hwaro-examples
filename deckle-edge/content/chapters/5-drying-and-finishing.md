## Drying

<p class="lede">Drying is the final transformation. The pressed sheet, still containing roughly half its weight in water, must be dried slowly and evenly to produce flat, stable paper. Rapid or uneven drying causes cockling -- a permanent waviness that cannot be corrected. The traditional method is loft drying, in which the sheets are hung over ropes or wooden poles in a well-ventilated room, often the upper story of the paper mill. The sheets dry gradually over a period of hours or days, depending on the temperature, humidity, and the weight of the paper.</p>

Japanese papermaking uses a different drying method. The wet sheets are brushed onto smooth wooden boards -- traditionally made of gingko or hinoki cypress -- and dried in the open air or in the sun. The surface tension between the wet sheet and the smooth board holds the paper flat as it dries, producing a sheet with one smooth side (the board side) and one slightly rougher side (the air side). This asymmetry is characteristic of washi and is exploited by calligraphers and printmakers who choose which side of the paper to work on.

## Sizing

Most papers intended for writing or printing require sizing -- a surface treatment that reduces the paper's absorbency and prevents ink from spreading or feathering. Without sizing, a drop of ink on unsized paper spreads outward along the fibers in an irregular stain; on properly sized paper, the ink sits on the surface in a crisp, defined mark.

Traditional sizing agents include gelatin (derived from animal hides) and rosin (derived from pine resin). Gelatin sizing produces a hard, smooth surface that is ideal for letterpress printing and copper-plate engraving. Rosin sizing, precipitated with alum, is more common in machine papermaking but can introduce acidity that causes the paper to become brittle over time.

The sizing may be applied internally -- mixed into the pulp before sheet formation -- or externally, by dipping or brushing the dried sheets. External gelatin sizing is the traditional method for fine papers and produces the crispest surface.

## Burnishing and calendering

Dried and sized sheets may be further smoothed by burnishing -- rubbing the surface with a smooth stone, shell, or glass instrument -- or by calendering, which passes the paper between heavy rollers under pressure. These processes compress the surface fibers, reducing the paper's roughness and increasing its gloss. The degree of smoothing depends on the intended use: a paper for watercolor painting should retain some tooth to grip the pigment, while a paper for engraving must be glass-smooth to accept fine lines without distortion.

## The finished sheet

The finished sheet of handmade paper is an object of quiet beauty. Hold it to the light and you see the constellation of fibers, the faint lines of the mould, perhaps the ghostly outline of a watermark. Feel its surface and you find a texture that no machine can replicate -- slightly irregular, responsive to touch, alive with the memory of water and the craftsman's hand. Run your finger along the deckle edge and you trace the boundary where the liquid pulp thinned and feathered beneath the frame, leaving its ragged, honest mark.

This is paper as it was made for centuries before the industrial revolution, and as it continues to be made today by hand papermakers around the world. Each sheet is unique. Each sheet carries within its fibers the evidence of its making.
