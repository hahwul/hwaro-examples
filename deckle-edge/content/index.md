## About this publication

<p class="lede">Paper is so commonplace that we rarely consider its origins. Yet every sheet of handmade paper is a unique artifact -- a web of interlocked plant fibers formed by hand, one sheet at a time, using techniques that have remained essentially unchanged for nearly two thousand years. This publication traces the craft from its earliest origins in China through its transmission to the Islamic world and Europe, and examines the techniques that contemporary hand papermakers use to produce sheets of extraordinary beauty and durability.</p>

## The living material

Handmade paper begins with plant fiber. Cotton, linen, hemp, kozo, gampi, mitsumata -- each fiber brings its own character to the finished sheet. Cotton produces a soft, warm paper with a gentle rattle. Linen yields a crisp, bright sheet that holds ink without feathering. The Japanese bast fibers -- kozo, gampi, and mitsumata -- create translucent papers of remarkable strength, capable of bending thousands of times without breaking.

The choice of fiber determines not only the paper's physical properties but its visual character: the length of the fibers, their translucency, the way they catch light at the surface. A sheet of kozo paper held against a window reveals the individual fibers in a luminous web. A sheet of cotton rag paper presents a smooth, opaque surface that absorbs light evenly. These are the fundamental materials from which the papermaker works.

## Chapters

This publication proceeds through the five stages of hand papermaking:

1. **Fiber Preparation** -- The selection, cleaning, and beating of raw fibers into a pulp suitable for sheet formation.

2. **The Vat and Mould** -- The equipment of the paper workshop: the vat of diluted pulp, the mould and deckle that give the sheet its form and its characteristic torn edge.

3. **Sheet Formation** -- The act of making paper: dipping the mould, shaking the slurry, and forming a uniform mat of interlocked fibers.

4. **Couching and Pressing** -- Transferring the wet sheet from the mould to felt, and pressing the stack to remove water and consolidate the fibers.

5. **Drying and Finishing** -- Air-drying or loft-drying the sheets, and the optional steps of sizing, burnishing, and trimming that complete the paper.
