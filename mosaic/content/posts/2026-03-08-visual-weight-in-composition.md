Every element on a page has visual weight. A large photograph is heavy. A thin line of text is light. A block of saturated color pulls the eye more than a muted tone. A sharp geometric shape demands more attention than an organic curve.

The skill of composition is the skill of balancing these weights. Not symmetrically — symmetry is the simplest form of balance, and often the least interesting — but dynamically, so that the eye moves through the layout with purpose and pleasure.

## Asymmetric Balance

The most compelling layouts use asymmetric balance. A large image on the left is counterweighted by a dense block of text on the right. A bold headline at the top is grounded by a cluster of small elements at the bottom. The composition feels stable, but the tension between unequal elements gives it energy.

## Color as Weight

Color is one of the most powerful determinants of visual weight. A small spot of red on a white page draws more attention than a large grey rectangle. This principle allows designers to create hierarchy without relying on size alone.

The Bauhaus designers understood this intuitively. Their compositions use color sparingly and strategically, placing small areas of intense color against larger fields of neutral tone. The result is layouts that feel both ordered and alive.

## Practical Applications

In editorial design, visual weight determines how readers navigate a spread. The heaviest element — usually the headline or lead image — establishes the entry point. Secondary elements guide the eye through the content in a deliberate sequence.

Understanding visual weight transforms design from decoration into communication. Every choice — size, color, position, density — either supports the intended reading path or works against it.
