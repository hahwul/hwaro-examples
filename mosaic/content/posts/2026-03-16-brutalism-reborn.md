For decades, brutalist buildings were synonymous with urban decay. Their raw concrete surfaces collected grime. Their massive forms blocked light. Critics called them inhuman. Councils scheduled their demolition with enthusiasm.

Then, sometime in the mid-2010s, the tide turned. Instagram accounts dedicated to brutalist architecture gained millions of followers. Architecture schools began teaching the movement with renewed respect. Heritage organizations fought to protect buildings that had been slated for demolition just years earlier.

## What Changed

The reappraisal of brutalism is partly aesthetic and partly political. Aesthetically, a generation raised on glossy minimalism developed an appetite for rawness. The imperfection of board-marked concrete, the honesty of exposed structure, the sheer sculptural ambition of these buildings — these qualities now read as authentic in a world of polished surfaces.

Politically, brutalism represents an era when governments believed in public investment. These were buildings for everyone: housing estates, libraries, universities, civic centers. Their monumental scale was a statement about the value of public space.

## New Brutalism

Contemporary architects are not simply copying the originals. They are taking the principles — material honesty, structural expression, sculptural form — and applying them with new tools. Computer-aided design allows curves and cantilevers that Banham and the Smithsons could only dream of.

The result is a new brutalism that is lighter, more refined, and more responsive to climate. Concrete remains the material of choice, but it is now cast with greater precision, textured with greater intention, and combined with timber and glass in ways that soften its severity.
