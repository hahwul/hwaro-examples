## On The Worn Volume

A book's spine is its autobiography. A new book opens reluctantly, a tight curve with a dry cracking sound along the gutter; it does not want to lie flat. A read book opens wherever the reader has stopped most often; it has a memory of being held open. A much-read book does not close.

The essays gathered below are on the second and third categories. We are not interested in the pristine copy, still in its dust jacket, with every page at a ninety-degree angle. We are interested in the volume that has been carried in a bag, set face-down on a table, shelved spine-out next to twenty identical copies, and pulled off the shelf a thousand times. The books whose owners can identify them in a dark room by touching the spine.

## The Opening

Every entry in this volume is set across a two-page spread, the way a worn book is read. The left-hand page &mdash; the verso &mdash; carries the body of the argument. The right-hand page &mdash; the recto &mdash; holds the marginalia, the pencil notes, the exclamation marks in the fore-edge, the reader's response.

The spine between them is cracked. We make no effort to repair it.
