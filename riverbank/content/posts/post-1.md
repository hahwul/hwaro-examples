The morning was still and grey when I arrived at the lower reach shortly after seven. Mist hung low over the water, and the only sound was the soft gurgle of current around the exposed roots of the bankside alders.

## The Sighting

At approximately 07:40, a sharp, high-pitched call cut through the quiet -- unmistakable to anyone who has spent time by the water. A moment later, a kingfisher streaked upstream, flying low and fast over the surface. The bird was visible for perhaps three seconds before disappearing around the bend, but the brilliant blue of its back was clear even in the flat light.

This is the earliest spring sighting I have recorded for this stretch. Last year, the first confirmed observation was on the 28th of March. Whether this reflects a genuine shift in timing or simply a matter of being in the right place remains to be seen.

## Conditions

The water level was moderately high following last week's rain, running perhaps 15 centimetres above the summer baseline. The banks were saturated but not flooded. Visibility into the water was poor -- the river carried a slight brown tint from upstream runoff.

## Other Notes

A grey heron stood motionless on the far bank for the duration of my visit, roughly forty minutes. Two mallards moved upstream. No sign yet of sand martins, though suitable nesting banks are present further along.

> The kingfisher does not announce spring so much as confirm it. By the time you see one, the season has already quietly begun.
