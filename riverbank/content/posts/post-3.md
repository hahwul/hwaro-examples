I arrived at the eastern reed beds around 16:30, later than my usual visits. The change in timing proved worthwhile. As the afternoon light softened, the reed beds came alive with song in a way I have not encountered at this site before so early in the season.

## The Chorus

The dominant voice was the sedge warbler -- at least three males singing from different positions within the reed stand. Their song is a rapid, varied sequence of phrases, each one different from the last, delivered with an energy that seems disproportionate to the size of the bird. They sang from near the tops of the reeds, occasionally visible as small brown shapes against the sky.

Alongside the warblers, two reed buntings called from the margins of the reed bed where it meets the rough grassland. Their song is simpler -- a short, repeated phrase -- but it provided a steady counterpoint to the warblers' improvisation.

## Habitat Observations

The reed beds are in good condition this spring. Last autumn's decision by the local conservation group to delay cutting appears to have paid off: the standing dead stems from last year's growth provide structure and cover, while new green shoots are emerging strongly at the base. The water level within the reed bed itself is ideal -- shallow enough for wading birds but deep enough to deter ground predators.

## Other Wildlife

A water vole was seen briefly at the edge of Otter Pool, disappearing into a burrow at the waterline. This is the first water vole sighting I have had here since last September. Their presence is a good indicator of habitat quality.

Two mute swans moved slowly upstream through the main channel. A buzzard circled high overhead but did not descend.

## Conditions

The clear sky and gentle breeze made for excellent conditions. The temperature at the time of observation was pleasant for March -- warm enough to sit still for an extended period without discomfort. The river itself has dropped back to near-normal levels following last week's high water, and the turbidity has cleared considerably.

> There is a particular quality to bird song heard over water. The reeds and the river surface seem to carry and amplify the sound, so that even a small bird fills a wide space.
