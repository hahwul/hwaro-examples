After three consecutive days of steady rain, the river has risen substantially. This morning's visit to Willow Bend revealed the water roughly 40 centimetres above the summer baseline -- the highest I have recorded since early February.

## Flooding Extent

The lower meadow on the south bank is partially submerged, with standing water extending approximately ten metres from the normal bank edge. The path along the north bank remains passable but soft underfoot. Several of the lower willow branches are trailing in the current, and the small gravel bar that is normally exposed at the bend is entirely underwater.

## Vegetation Response

The reed beds along the central section appear unaffected -- they are well adapted to periodic inundation. However, I noticed that a section of the bank near the old footbridge has slumped, taking with it a patch of comfrey that had established itself last summer. Bank erosion at this point has been a recurring issue; the loss of root structure from the comfrey may accelerate it.

On a more positive note, the marsh marigolds along the sheltered eastern stretch are in early bud. If the water recedes within the next week, they should flower on schedule.

## Current and Sediment

The current at the bend was noticeably stronger than usual, carrying leaves and small branches. The water colour was a deep brown -- heavily loaded with sediment from the surrounding agricultural land. This turbidity will likely persist for several days after the rain stops.

## Wildlife

Surprisingly little bird activity. A single moorhen picked its way along the flooded meadow edge. The grey heron that has been a regular presence at the lower reach was absent -- possibly displaced by the higher water. No kingfisher sighting today.

> High water simplifies the river. The distinctions between bank and channel blur, and the whole landscape seems to lower itself into the water.
