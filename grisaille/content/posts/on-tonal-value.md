Before a Renaissance painter laid down a single stroke of colour, the composition already existed in grey. The underpainting -- often executed in terre verte or raw umber -- established every relationship that mattered: where light fell, where shadow gathered, how one form receded behind another.

This was not preliminary work in the dismissive sense. It was the architecture of the image.

## Value as Structure

Tonal value is the measure of lightness or darkness in a colour. Strip away hue and saturation, and value is what remains. It is the skeleton beneath the skin of every painting.

A composition that works in value works in any palette. A composition that fails in value cannot be rescued by colour, no matter how vivid.

## The Grisaille Tradition

Some painters never moved beyond the grey stage, and their work is no less complete for it. The exterior panels of Jan van Eyck's Ghent Altarpiece, painted in grisaille, depict prophets and saints with a solemnity that colour might have undermined. The restraint is the point.

Later, Gerhard Richter would return to this territory, producing photorealistic paintings in grey that interrogate the relationship between photography and painting, between document and art.

> "Value does the work. Colour takes the credit."

## Lessons for the Present

Contemporary designers often reach for colour as a first tool rather than a final one. But value -- the relationship between light and dark elements -- determines whether a layout reads clearly at any size, on any screen, in any context.

The practice of designing in greyscale first is not a gimmick. It is an inheritance from five centuries of painters who understood that structure precedes decoration.
