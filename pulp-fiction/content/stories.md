## The Cover Yarn

### She Knew Too Much

<p class="story-by">by Oreyna Clemens &middot; pp. 1&ndash;17</p>

She was halfway through the third cup of coffee when she heard it through the keyhole, and by the time she set the cup down she already knew her Thursday was ruined. The voice on the other side of the wall said a name she had no business knowing, and then it said a number, and then it said what had happened to the man who had the number and what was going to happen to the woman who overheard. The woman who overheard was Violet Greer, thirty-one, fifth-floor typist at the Wexler &amp; Shea Insurance Office on East 28th. Violet Greer finished her coffee without blinking, picked up her handbag, and walked out of the door at a steady pace, and by seven that evening her apartment on Lexington was full of detectives.

## The Novellas

### The Tenth Floor

<p class="story-by">by Perrin Ordway &middot; pp. 22&ndash;41</p>

Murray Kessler had operated the elevator at the Hotel Wayburn for eleven years, and in those eleven years he had made a habit of writing down every strange thing he had seen on the tenth floor. The list ran to sixty-three entries. Most of them were small: a woman crying in the corridor at three in the morning, a man checking in under the name of a famous singer, a piece of luggage left standing outside 1008 for a week. One or two were not small. By the spring of 1952, Murray Kessler had begun to notice that the people on his list were, one by one, no longer on any list.

### Tonight At Delaney's

<p class="story-by">by Oreyna Clemens &middot; pp. 106&ndash;123</p>

Delaney's did not have a piano player, because Delaney had fired the piano player three winters before for reasons nobody would tell you, and the piano stood in the corner of the barroom getting dusty. So when the evening of the eleventh of May arrived, and a man in a grey coat sat down at the piano and began to play, four people at the bar turned to look, and every one of them recognised him and every one of them decided, in that same second, not to say so.

### A Name In Lights

<p class="story-by">by Margo McCarty &middot; pp. 79&ndash;93</p>

The top-billed actress at the Plaza Burlesque went by the stage name of Della Dupris. Her real name was Gloria Shea, and she was twenty-seven years old and had been at the Plaza for three seasons. The matinee on Saturday the tenth ran from two to four. The evening show began at eight. In the four-hour gap between them, Gloria Shea left the theatre by the side door, walked seven blocks to a warehouse on Eleventh, and killed a man who had been threatening her sister for six months. She was back at the theatre by seven-fifteen, in character for the opening number by seven forty-five, and on the stage on time.

### The Pelican Job

<p class="story-by">by Perrin Ordway &middot; pp. 124&ndash;137</p>

The Pelican was a thirty-eight-foot Chris-Craft registered to a widow in Boca Raton who had never been on the boat in her life. The job, if one could call it a job, was to take the Pelican from Boca to Key West by sundown on the fourteenth of May, to transfer a brown leather case to a second boat at a marker buoy two miles out, and to return the Pelican to its slip before dawn. The six men and three women aboard had been promised two hundred dollars each, and they had been told, more than once, not to open the case.

## The Short Yarns

### Black Canal Blues

<p class="story-by">by Jiro Nakada &middot; pp. 48&ndash;62</p>

The barge was found at dawn, half sunk at the mouth of the Newtown Creek, with the stevedore's cap floating five feet away and the ledger, weighted down with a cast-iron wheel, still locked in the small wooden cabin. The ledger, when it was dried and read, turned out to be in code, and the code, when it was broken, turned out to lead back to a card game in a basement off Canal Street, which led in turn to a prizefighter in Queens, whose sister had, until two nights earlier, been the stevedore's wife.

### The Whistling Man

<p class="story-by">by F. J. Greville &middot; pp. 63&ndash;78</p>

The woman in 3B had been hearing the whistling for a week. Every night at nine, as steady as the clock at the Metropolitan Life tower, the whistler arrived at the lamppost across the street and whistled a song she did not know. After a week she put on her coat, went down the four flights, and crossed the street to ask him to stop. He was fifty years old, clean-shaven, wearing a grey suit, and when she spoke to him he looked at her for a long moment and said, "I was wondering how long it would take you to come down."

### Last Train South

<p class="story-by">by Eliot Hawke &middot; pp. 94&ndash;105</p>

The last train south from Penn Station that night was the 11:40 to Miami, and every seat on it was sold. Jack Callahan bought a one-way ticket from a boy on Eighth Avenue for twice the face price, boarded at eleven twenty-five, found his berth, and was told by the conductor that there was a mistake with his seat and that he would have to move. He was given the seat at the end of the car, number 14. The man already in 14 had, for the past two hours, been dead. Callahan and the conductor stood looking at him for a full minute before either of them spoke.

## Poems

### Rhymes for May

<p class="story-by">by Lucia Suare &middot; pp. 42&ndash;47</p>

Three poems. The first, "A Streetcar In Yorkville," about an old streetcar line being torn up. The second, "The Pier At Coney," about a childhood summer. The third, "Letter From My Cousin In Havana," about a cousin who does not write often enough. Suare's first appearance in these pages. We hope not her last.
