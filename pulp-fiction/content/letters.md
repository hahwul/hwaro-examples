The letters column is usually a monthly feature, but the April issue was crowded with a three-part serial by Oreyna Clemens and we had no room. The letters below arrived between the first of February and the fifteenth of April. We have published what we could, edited lightly for spelling and length.

## On Matters Of Taste

### A Reader In Milwaukee

<blockquote>
<p>Dear Sir, I have been reading PULP FICTION for six years and I am writing for the first time to ask you to stop printing so much Jiro Nakada. His stories are well written but they are all set on or near water, and I live in the middle of a continent. Please print more indoor stories.</p>
<p>-- E. T. McHenry</p>
</blockquote>

*We will take the point under advisement. For what it is worth, Mr Nakada's June story is, for the first time since 1949, entirely indoor: it is set in a hotel basement.*

### A Reader In Oklahoma

<blockquote>
<p>Editor: The cover of your March issue was a disgrace. I do not mind a lurid cover. I buy the magazine partly for the covers. But the woman on the March cover was holding the revolver backwards, with her finger on the cylinder rather than the trigger. No woman who handles a revolver that way can hit anything. Please see to this in future.</p>
<p>-- Mrs M. Villavicencio</p>
</blockquote>

*We are embarrassed. The cover artist, who is new to us, has been instructed to visit a gun shop before next assignment. The artist has since telephoned our office to apologise and to report that he has done so.*

## On Matters Of The Writer's Craft

### A Reader In The Bronx

<blockquote>
<p>Dear Editor, I am seventeen years old and I want to be a writer. I have written four short stories and two of them were returned by your office with a printed rejection slip. Could you please tell me what is wrong with my stories?</p>
<p>-- Harry Abelow</p>
</blockquote>

*Mr Abelow, we are not able to offer manuscript criticism by mail; we receive too many stories to read that carefully. What is most likely wrong with your stories is that you are seventeen years old. Please continue writing, send us the stories when you are twenty, and if they are good we will buy them. If they are not good, we will send you the printed rejection slip again. This is how the trade works. We wish you well.*

### A Reader In Philadelphia

<blockquote>
<p>Dear Editor, Your magazine is the best pulp on the stand and I want to write for it. I have enclosed a manuscript. Please do not return it with comments; just tell me yes or no.</p>
<p>-- C. Valdez</p>
</blockquote>

*Yes. The cheque is in the post. The story is scheduled for August. Welcome to these pages.*

## On Matters Of The Magazine Itself

### A Reader In Maine

<blockquote>
<p>Dear Editor, My newsstand says you are raising the price to twelve cents in the summer. I hope this is not true.</p>
<p>-- Andrew Gibbs</p>
</blockquote>

*It is true. Beginning with the June issue, the price will be twelve cents in the United States and fifteen cents in Canada. Paper costs have risen three times in eighteen months, and we cannot absorb any more. We have held the price at ten cents for five years and we believe we have done as long as we could. Twelve cents buys you, as before, one hundred forty-four pages of yarns and no advertising except the back cover and the classifieds.*

### A Reader In Los Angeles

<blockquote>
<p>Dear Sir, I have, over four years, accumulated forty-one consecutive issues of your magazine. My wife would like me to throw them out. Can you suggest a buyer?</p>
<p>-- C. R. Mendez</p>
</blockquote>

*See the classified advertisement on page 144 from Franklin's Books on Amsterdam Avenue, who buy used pulp magazines. Please do not throw them out. Some day those forty-one issues will be worth more than the ten cents you paid for each of them, though we cannot guess when.*

## A Word On The Writer Of The Cover Yarn

Oreyna Clemens will be answering letters about *She Knew Too Much* directly. If you wish to write to her, please send your letter care of our office and we will forward it. She has asked us to say, in advance, that she will not confirm or deny the rumour that the story is based on a real incident at the Wexler &amp; Shea Insurance Office in 1949. Please do not ask.
