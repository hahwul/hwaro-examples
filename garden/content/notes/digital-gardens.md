A digital garden sits between a personal notebook and a polished blog. It is a public collection of ideas in various stages of development, tended over time rather than published once and forgotten.

## Origins

The term gained traction in the early 2020s through writers like Maggie Appleton and Joel Hooks, though the concept traces back to earlier hypertext experiments. Mike Caulfield's 2015 essay "The Garden and the Stream" articulated the distinction between chronological streams (feeds, timelines) and topological gardens (wikis, webs of ideas).

## Principles

**Learning in public.** By publishing unfinished thoughts, you invite feedback and create accountability for your learning process.

**Growth over perfection.** Notes are planted as seedlings and revised as understanding deepens. This stands in contrast to the "publish once" model of traditional blogging.

**Topological navigation.** Rather than browsing by date, readers follow links between related notes. This mirrors the associative nature of human thought and the [Zettelkasten Method](/notes/zettelkasten-method/).

## Growth Stages

Most digital gardens use some form of growth metaphor:

- **Seedling** -- rough, newly planted ideas
- **Budding** -- developed but still growing
- **Evergreen** -- mature, well-maintained notes

These stages set reader expectations and signal which notes are works in progress versus stable references.

## Building a Garden

Static site generators with good interlinking support work well for gardens. The key features to look for are support for backlinks, taxonomies, and flexible content organization.
