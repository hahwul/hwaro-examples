## More Than a Color

In the Korean philosophical tradition, black is one of the five cardinal colors (obangsaek). It represents the north, winter, water, and wisdom. It is the color of the scholar's ink stone, the magistrate's hat, and the night sky over the mountains.

In design, black performs a similar role. It is the color of authority, finality, and precision. A black headline does not suggest — it declares.

## The Ink Stone

The traditional Korean ink stone (byeoru) is where solid ink meets water to become liquid. The calligrapher controls the density of the ink by how much water they add and how long they grind. This produces a spectrum from deep, saturated black to the palest gray wash.

This is the origin of the grayscale. Not a digital abstraction, but a physical practice refined over centuries:

- **Concentrated ink** — For bold strokes and headlines
- **Medium dilution** — For body text and secondary elements
- **Light wash** — For backgrounds and subtle accents
- **Dry brush** — For texture and atmosphere

## Black in Modern Design

Contemporary Korean design uses black with intention:

| Context | Black Means |
|---|---|
| Fashion | Sophistication, urban edge |
| Architecture | Structural honesty, modern minimalism |
| Typography | Authority, readability |
| Packaging | Premium quality, restraint |
| Interface | Focus, reduction of noise |

Seoul's Gangnam district at night is a study in black and light. The dark facades of buildings become canvases for typography, signage, and illumination. The blackness is not empty — it is a stage.

## Using Black Well

The key to using black effectively in design:

1. **Use true black sparingly.** Reserve `#000000` for moments of maximum emphasis. For body text, a softer `#111111` or `#222222` reduces eye strain while maintaining authority.

2. **Let black breathe.** A black element surrounded by white space has more impact than one crowded against other elements.

3. **Vary the weight.** Different shades of gray create depth and hierarchy. A design with only pure black and pure white feels flat. The grays between them give it dimension.

> The heaviest stroke on the page is the one that says the least and means the most.
