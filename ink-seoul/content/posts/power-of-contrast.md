## The Simplest Tool

Contrast is the most fundamental principle in visual design. Before color theory, before grid systems, before responsive breakpoints — there was the simple act of making one thing different from another.

In monochrome design, contrast operates along a single axis: light to dark. This simplicity is its strength.

## Types of Contrast

Even without color, a designer has several contrast levers:

- **Weight** — Light text next to bold text creates immediate hierarchy
- **Size** — A 48px heading above 16px body text establishes order
- **Density** — A block of text against empty space draws the eye
- **Texture** — Serif against sans-serif creates typographic tension
- **Value** — Black (#000) against white (#FFF) is maximum contrast

## Contrast as Communication

Consider a newspaper. The headline is bold and large. The byline is small and light. The body text is medium weight at a comfortable size. No color is needed — the contrast system alone tells you what to read first, second, and third.

| Element | Weight | Size | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Headline | Bold | 32px+ | Attract attention |
| Subhead | Medium | 20px | Provide context |
| Body | Regular | 16px | Deliver content |
| Caption | Light | 13px | Support imagery |
| Metadata | Regular | 12px | Secondary info |

This hierarchy works because each level is clearly distinct from the others. Contrast is the mechanism, clarity is the result.

## The 60-30-10 Rule in Grayscale

In color design, the 60-30-10 rule suggests using a dominant color (60%), secondary color (30%), and accent (10%). The same principle applies to monochrome:

- **60% White** — Background, breathing room, negative space
- **30% Gray** — Secondary text, borders, subtle elements
- **10% Black** — Headlines, emphasis, interactive elements

This distribution prevents a monochrome design from feeling either too stark or too flat. The grays do the heavy lifting of creating depth and atmosphere.

## Lessons from Photography

Black and white photography teaches a critical lesson: removing color forces you to see form, texture, and composition. The same is true in design. A monochrome interface reveals its bones — and those bones had better be strong.
