The tradition from which this studio draws its practice is principally the Jewish tradition of the ketubah - a formal marriage contract in Aramaic whose origins reach back to the period of the Second Temple, and whose ornamentation flowered particularly in the mediaeval communities of Italy, Spain, the Maghreb, and Persia. The studio also draws, where its work calls for such sources, upon the Christian marriage licences of the early modern period, upon the civil contracts of the Italian Renaissance, and upon the ornamental certificates of guilds, confraternities, and academies.

## The Italian School

The richest surviving corpus of illuminated contracts comes to us from the Jewish communities of Italy between the fifteenth and the eighteenth centuries. The Venetian, Roman, and Ferrarese workshops developed distinct ornamental vocabularies - Venetian contracts favour architectural cartouches with columns and pediments; Roman contracts prefer garlands of flowers and fruit; Ferrarese contracts incorporate the heraldic devices of the contracting families. Our studio's Florentine Baroque style takes its lead from the Ferrarese examples preserved in the collection at the Jewish Museum of Venice.

## The Sephardic School

The Sephardic tradition, arising in al-Andalus and carried by the expulsions into North Africa and the eastern Mediterranean, is characterised by geometric interlace of the type seen in the architecture of Cordoba and Granada. The studio's Sephardic Andalusi style is drawn from this lineage, and in particular from a small number of surviving contracts of the Toledano family of Tetouan, whose descendants commissioned our work in 2025.

## The Persian and Central Asian Traditions

The Persian and Bukharan communities produced contracts of extraordinary refinement, with gilt backgrounds, floral borders drawn after the rose-and-nightingale manner of miniature painting, and micrographic inscriptions in which the entire text of the Song of Songs might be arranged in the form of a garden. Our studio has produced three contracts in this manner during its existence; all were commissioned by families with ancestral ties to Isfahan or to Bukhara.

## The Modern Period

The nineteenth century saw the industrialisation of the ornamental contract through chromolithographic printing, and many fine mass-produced ketubot survive from this period - notably the contracts produced in Trieste, Vienna, and Livorno for the emigrant communities. The handwork tradition persisted among Orthodox communities throughout this period and revived more broadly in the last quarter of the twentieth century, particularly through the work of the American scribe Jay Greenspan, to whose example our studio's principal is indebted.

## The Contemporary Studio

Our studio takes its place within this long tradition without making any claim to innovate upon it. The ornamental vocabularies we use are drawn from historical examples; the letterforms we letter are descended through a continuous chain of apprenticeship from the mediaeval scribes; the pigments we grind are prepared according to recipes set down in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. Our contribution, if any, is the adaptation of these inherited materials to the particular wishes and circumstances of the couples who come to us, and the careful attention to the craft which that adaptation requires.
