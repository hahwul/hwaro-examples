### The Same Body On Both Sides

Source Serif 4, 17 point, 28 point leading. This is the body face of both verso and recto across every spread in the magazine. The faces of display type differ; the body text does not.

### Why Not Match The Body To The Display

If the verso body were set in Cormorant Roman and the recto body in Inter Regular, the spread would read as two distinct articles. The reader would encounter the verso as one unit and the recto as another, with the change of body face operating as a hard divider. This is not what we want. We want the reader to experience the spread as one composed article with two distinct display voices.

### The Floor

The shared body face is the *floor* of the spread. The display faces are the furniture. A reader can redecorate the furniture of a room; they cannot redecorate the floor. The body face is the thing that does not change &mdash; the thing that says, &ldquo;this is all one room.&rdquo;

Every display decision on a spread pulls the two pages apart. The italic of the verso and the bold of the recto are opposing voices. The classical weights differ from the contemporary weights. The leading of the verso (1.55) is more generous than the leading of the recto (1.4). Every small decision reinforces the fact that the spread is a duet, not a monologue.

## The Unifying Element

If we did nothing to counter this, the spread would read as two magazines bound together. The reader would experience the verso as one article and the recto as another, and the editorial project &mdash; &ldquo;a spread is a single composed object&rdquo; &mdash; would collapse.

The unifying element is the body face. Both pages set their running body text in **Source Serif 4** at 17-point on 28-point leading, identical across the spread. The display faces diverge; the body text unifies.

> Two voices above a single floor. The voices may quarrel; the floor does not move.

## Why Source Serif

Source Serif 4, designed by Frank Griesshammer for Adobe, is a transitional serif text face with the warmth of an old-style body and the clarity of a contemporary text. It is not as classical as Cormorant and not as modern as Inter; it sits between them, and that is the correct position for a shared body voice.

It also has a generous set of italic weights, small caps, and oldstyle figures, which matter for a magazine that relies on typographic nuance. A body face without these features would force us to set footnotes, pull quotes, and decorative passages in a *different* face, which would reintroduce the fragmentation we are trying to avoid.

## The Test

A reader blind to the design of the magazine should read one spread and, when asked, describe it as &ldquo;a single article with two pages in different display styles.&rdquo; Not &ldquo;two articles bound together.&rdquo; If the test fails, the spread has failed.
