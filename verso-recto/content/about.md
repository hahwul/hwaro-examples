The editorial argument of *Verso &amp; Recto* is simple: the spread is the unit of composition. Not the page; not the article; not the issue. The two-page spread, read as a single composed object, is what we offer the reader. Everything else in the magazine follows from this.

## The Case For Facing Pages

A book is a stack of spreads. When the reader opens a codex, they see not one page but two &mdash; a verso and a recto, separated by a gutter that is also a spine. The eye does not read one page and then the other; it holds both simultaneously, shifting left-to-right and back, anchoring at the gutter. Classical book design acknowledges this: the inner margins are larger than the outer, the folios on verso and recto balance one another, the running heads echo across the gutter.

Modern publication design mostly ignores it. Articles are composed as single columns; design elements break across the gutter indifferently; the spread is treated as two independent pages bound together by accident.

*Verso &amp; Recto* takes the other path.

## The House Rule

Every article is exactly one spread &mdash; two facing pages, four columns, set in the two house faces. No article runs more than one spread. No article runs less than one spread. When the argument will not fit, the argument is shortened.

This is a severe constraint, and it produces a different kind of writing. Essays that might run to four thousand words in a standard magazine must be reduced to eleven hundred. The reduction is not loss; it is the exercise. The editor's work is to hold the spread together, and the writer's work is to survive the fit.
