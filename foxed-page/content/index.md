<h1>On Foxing, &amp; Other Evidences of Age</h1>

<div class="byline" style="text-align:center; font-style:italic; color: var(--muted); margin-bottom:2rem; border-bottom:1px solid var(--rule); padding-bottom:1rem;">From the press of a private bibliophile &mdash; Reissued, with original errata</div>

<p class="dropcap">Foxing is the name given, by bibliographers and conservators alike, to the small rust-brown spots that bloom upon the leaves of old books. The word is of uncertain origin &mdash; perhaps from the colour of a fox's coat, perhaps from the old verb *to fox*, meaning to discolour or mislead. Whatever its etymology, the phenomenon is unmistakable: a scattered dusting of spots, each no larger than a full stop, appearing first upon the fore-edge and spreading, by imperceptible degrees, toward the gutter.</p>

The causes of foxing are disputed among the learned. Some hold that it is a fungal infection, dormant in the paper since its making, waking in the presence of humidity. Others maintain that it is the oxidation of iron compounds deposited in the pulp by the vatman's mould. A third school, drawing upon recent chemical analyses, attributes it to metallic impurities in the sizing. The prudent reader will not choose among these, but will accept foxing as a compound fact, and will value a foxed page for what it records rather than for what it conceals.

<hr>

## The Moral of the Foxed Page

<p>A foxed page, unlike a pristine one, bears witness to its own passage through time. It has sat upon a shelf; it has survived a damp winter; it has been opened, read, closed, and forgotten for some lengthy interval, and then opened again. Each spot is a small chronicle of the book's life outside the reader's hand.</p>

<p>There is, the present author ventures, a quiet honesty in a foxed page. A new sheet of paper makes no claims; it is a field of undifferentiated potential. A foxed sheet admits &mdash; openly, and without apology &mdash; that it has been somewhere, that it has waited, that it has endured. One learns, from the habit of reading foxed books, to read such signs elsewhere: upon the faces of old friends, upon the walls of old houses, upon the hands that have held this volume before one's own.</p>

<blockquote>It is not the book that is beautiful for being old. It is the accumulation of all the small acts of reading that it has patiently absorbed, and the visible record of that absorption, that one calls its age.</blockquote>

<hr>

## Concerning the Water Ring

<p>A water ring &mdash; the brown circular mark left by a vessel placed carelessly upon the page &mdash; is of a different order than foxing. Foxing is intrinsic; the water ring is accidental. The first is a slow, impersonal process; the second is a single, regrettable act by some former reader, whose identity one can only guess at from the circumference of the cup.</p>

<p>Yet both marks, one resists calling them blemishes, are equally part of the book's present state. The conservator who removes them &mdash; and it is possible, with patience and the right solvents, to lighten both &mdash; removes something of the book's character along with them. The question is not whether to restore, but whether restoration itself is not a kind of forgetting.</p>
