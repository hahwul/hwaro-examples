<p class="lead">There is, among the younger conservators of books, a tendency to over-restore which the present writer cannot altogether approve.</p>

The modern art of paper conservation has reached a high pitch of technical accomplishment. It is now possible, by a combination of solvents, enzymatic treatments, and careful mechanical cleaning, to lighten or remove nearly every visible sign of foxing, water staining, and the ordinary accumulations of age. The restored sheet is astonishingly bright, the print astonishingly sharp, and the book, after rebinding in fresh calf, astonishingly young. It has shed its years like a garment.

## The question this raises

<p>It is the considered view of this writer that such restoration, while a marvellous feat of craft, is not always justifiable as an act of scholarship. A book is not simply a text: it is an object, with a history, and the marks upon it are the record of that history. To remove them is not to reveal the book as it was when new; it is to produce a third thing, neither the book as made nor the book as aged, but the book as imagined by a conservator.</p>

<blockquote>The only books that stay young are those that have never been read.</blockquote>

<p>This is, of course, a philosophical position, and I do not wish to press it further than the common practice of collectors warrants. There are cases in which restoration is plainly justified &mdash; where active mould is eating the sheet, where iron gall ink is burning through the paper, where a fragile binding will no longer open without tearing. In such cases, to do nothing is to permit the book's destruction, and intervention is clearly right.</p>

## Between the two extremes

<p>Most cases lie between the two extremes, and in these the judgement is finer. The present writer prefers a minimum of intervention: stabilisation, where stability is threatened; a light dry-cleaning of the surface, where loose dust would otherwise abrade; a discreet repair of a torn corner or a detached board. Beyond this, I would leave the book as it has become.</p>

<p>The foxed almanac described in the preceding chapter has received, from my hand, no treatment whatever. It sits upon my desk as I found it, spotted, water-ringed, foxed from cover to cover; and I believe the book is the better for my restraint.</p>

## A practical register

The following register lists the treatments commonly offered by the conservation trade, with a brief note on when each is appropriate &mdash;

- **Dry-cleaning.** Removal of surface dust with a soft brush. Always appropriate.
- **Aqueous washing.** Immersion in purified water to remove acids. Reserved for active deterioration.
- **Bleaching.** Chemical lightening of foxing and stains. Rarely appropriate; reversible in skilled hands only.
- **Resizing.** Restoration of the paper's original size. Appropriate after washing.
- **Mending.** Repair of tears with wheat paste and Japanese tissue. Appropriate for active fracture.
- **Rebinding.** Full replacement of the binding. Appropriate only when the original is lost or utterly failed.

<hr>

<p>The learned reader will have reasons of his own, and will disagree with some of the above. I offer the list not as a doctrine, but as a point from which the judicious reader may begin.</p>
