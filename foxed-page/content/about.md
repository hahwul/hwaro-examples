<p class="lead">This small book has been composed, set, and impressed in the following manner &mdash;</p>

## Of the Types

The text of the present volume is set in **Old Standard TT**, a historical revival drawn from the nineteenth-century scientific and encyclopaedic faces, chosen for its narrow, upright presence upon the aged page. The display and italic faces are set in **Cormorant Garamond**, whose open counters and restrained ornament recall the early-sixteenth-century punches of Claude Garamont, master of the royal type.

Where a roman and an italic are mingled in the same line, the reader will observe that the italic does not merely slope the roman, but is a wholly separate design, with narrower counters and a finer stroke, after the practice of the oldest types.

## Of the Paper

The sheet upon which this book is printed &mdash; the screen upon which you read &mdash; is imagined as a laid paper of the latter seventeenth century: creamy, mottled, with a pronounced chain-line visible against the light. The foxing has been placed with some restraint; the water rings are ornamental.

The colour of the paper varies across the sheet. This is intentional. Paper ages unevenly: darker at the edges, where the air has played upon it longest; lighter at the centre, where the pages have been pressed together. A book that has aged uniformly has probably not aged at all.

## Of the Impression

This is the second impression, corrected. The first impression carried a number of small errors of orthography, which have here been put right. A list of the corrections has been entered in the errata, appended to the back.

## On Restoration

<p>It is the settled policy of this press not to restore the appearance of age. The marks are part of the book; to remove them is to remove a witness. The reader who finds the foxing distasteful is free to consult one of the many bright, uniform, untroubled editions produced by the commercial presses, which will meet every need except one: the need to hold in one's hands something that has endured.</p>

<hr>

## An Epigraph

<blockquote>All books grow old, even in the making. The press sets a book's youth; the years set its age. To reject either is to reject reading itself.</blockquote>
