Every board you pick up is a cross-section of a life. The grain is not decoration. It is biography. The tight, even lines of a slow-grown white oak speak of a tree that spent decades in the shade of its neighbors, reaching upward in small, patient increments. The wide, sweeping curves of a fast-grown poplar tell of open fields and abundant sunlight. If you learn to read the grain, the wood will tell you where it came from, what it endured, and --- critically --- how it wants to be worked.

## What Grain Patterns Tell Us

### Straight Grain

The most cooperative pattern. Boards with straight, parallel grain lines are stable, predictable, and a pleasure to plane. They come from the trunk wood of trees that grew tall and straight without much lateral stress. Straight-grained boards are ideal for structural members, table tops, and any application where predictable wood movement matters.

### Interlocked Grain

Some species, particularly tropical hardwoods like **mahogany** and **sapele**, develop grain that alternates direction from one growth layer to the next. This interlocking pattern makes the wood exceptionally resistant to splitting --- and exceptionally frustrating to plane. If your hand plane tears out no matter which direction you push it, you are likely dealing with interlocked grain. The solution is a higher cutting angle or a well-tuned scraper.

### Figured Grain

This is where wood becomes art. **Birdseye maple** shows hundreds of tiny dimples across the surface, each one caused by a localized growth disturbance that science still does not fully understand. **Curly maple** (also called tiger maple or fiddleback) displays alternating bands of light and dark that shift as you change your viewing angle. **Quilted maple** produces a three-dimensional rippled effect that looks like fabric draped over an uneven surface.

> Figured wood is not just beautiful. It is a puzzle. Every piece demands that you adjust your approach --- sharper tools, lighter cuts, more attention to direction. It teaches you humility.

## Grain and Movement

Wood is never truly still. It absorbs and releases moisture with the seasons, expanding and contracting across the grain. A 12-inch wide board of flat-sawn red oak can move a full quarter inch between summer humidity and winter dryness. This is not a defect. It is the nature of the material, and every joint and design must account for it.

**Key principles of wood movement:**

- Wood moves most **across the grain** (tangentially)
- Wood moves minimally **along the grain** (longitudinally)
- **Quarter-sawn** boards move roughly half as much as flat-sawn boards
- Movement is proportional to the change in moisture content

Understanding this is what separates a piece that lasts from one that cracks. A tabletop must be attached in a way that allows it to expand and contract freely. A panel in a frame must float in its groove, never glued along its edges. These are not optional refinements. They are structural necessities.

## Learning to Read

The next time you are at the lumber yard, pick up a board and look at the end grain. Is it flat-sawn, with wide arching growth rings? Quarter-sawn, with rings running roughly perpendicular to the face? Rift-sawn, at an angle between the two? Each orientation has its own movement characteristics, its own appearance, its own ideal uses.

Run your hand along the surface. Feel the difference between early wood and late wood in a piece of ash. Notice how the grain rises slightly on one side of a cherry board --- that is the side that will tear out if you plane the wrong direction.

The grain is always talking. The craft is learning to listen.
