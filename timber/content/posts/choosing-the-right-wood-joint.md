Joinery is the skeleton of woodworking. You can have the finest lumber and the most elegant design, but if the joints are wrong, the piece will fail. Not immediately, perhaps, but inevitably. Choosing the right joint for each situation is one of the most important skills a woodworker can develop.

## Understanding the Forces

Before selecting a joint, ask yourself what forces it needs to resist. There are three primary types:

- **Tension** --- pulling the joint apart along its length
- **Shear** --- sliding the pieces past each other
- **Racking** --- twisting or rocking the joint diagonally

A bookshelf shelf, for instance, primarily resists shear and some racking. A chair leg resists all three. The joint you choose must address the specific forces at play.

## The Essential Joints

### Butt Joint

The simplest connection: two pieces of wood glued face to face or edge to edge. Despite its reputation, a well-executed **edge-to-edge butt joint** with modern glue is remarkably strong --- often stronger than the wood itself. The key is perfectly flat mating surfaces. If you can see light between the boards, you are not ready to glue.

### Mortise and Tenon

The backbone of furniture making. A tenon (tongue) fits into a mortise (slot), creating a joint that resists tension, shear, and racking. Variations include:

- **Through mortise and tenon** --- the tenon passes fully through and is visible
- **Blind mortise and tenon** --- the tenon stops inside the mortise
- **Haunched tenon** --- a stepped tenon that prevents twisting

> When in doubt, use a mortise and tenon. It has been the default joint for furniture makers for centuries, and for good reason.

### Dovetail

The dovetail is the joint that non-woodworkers recognize. Its interlocking pins and tails create a mechanical bond that resists being pulled apart in one direction. **Through dovetails** are traditional for drawer construction and carcass work. They are slower to cut than most joints, but the payoff in strength and visual appeal is significant.

### Dado and Rabbet

These are channel joints, cut across or along the grain, used primarily for shelving and carcass construction. A **dado** is a channel cut across the grain; a **rabbet** is a step cut along an edge. Both are fast to execute and provide good resistance to shear.

## Matching Joints to Projects

| Project Type | Recommended Joint | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Drawer | Dovetail | Resists pulling forces during use |
| Table apron | Mortise and tenon | Handles racking and shear |
| Shelving | Dado or through-tenon | Supports weight, resists shear |
| Panel glue-up | Edge butt joint | Maximum glue surface, invisible seam |
| Frame and panel | Cope and stick or M&T | Allows seasonal wood movement |

The right joint is not always the most complex one. It is the one that solves the structural problem with the least wasted effort. Learn to read the forces at work in your design, and the correct joint will usually make itself obvious.
