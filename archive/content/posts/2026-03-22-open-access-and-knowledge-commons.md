The open access movement rests on a simple proposition: research funded by the public should be accessible to the public. For most of the twentieth century, scholarly communication was mediated by subscription journals. Libraries paid -- often steeply -- for access to research that had been produced by publicly funded scholars and reviewed by unpaid peer reviewers.

## The Budapest Declaration

The 2002 Budapest Open Access Initiative articulated two complementary strategies. "Green" open access permits authors to deposit copies of their published work in institutional or disciplinary repositories. "Gold" open access shifts the cost of publication from subscriber to author, making the final published version immediately and freely available.

Both strategies have gained significant traction. Major funders -- including the National Institutes of Health, the European Research Council, and the Gates Foundation -- now require that research they support be made openly accessible within a defined period.

## Institutional Repositories

University libraries have been central to the green open access movement, building and maintaining institutional repositories where faculty can deposit preprints, postprints, data sets, and other scholarly outputs. These repositories serve as a permanent, discoverable record of an institution's intellectual production.

The library's role here is a natural extension of its historical function: collecting, organizing, and preserving the scholarly record. The difference is that the library now publishes as well as collects.

## Tensions and Tradeoffs

Open access is not without complications. The article processing charges associated with gold open access can be substantial, raising concerns about equity between well-funded and under-resourced institutions. Predatory publishers have exploited the model to profit from authors without providing genuine editorial oversight.

Despite these challenges, the direction of travel is clear. The norm in scholarly communication is shifting from restricted to open, from proprietary to shared. The question is no longer whether research should be freely accessible, but how to build sustainable systems that make it so.
