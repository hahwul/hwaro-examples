A catalog record is a surrogate for a physical or digital item. It contains enough information to identify the item, locate it within a collection, and determine whether it is relevant to a particular inquiry. The art of cataloging lies in compressing a complex work into a structured set of fields without losing the essential character of the original.

## Core Fields

The minimum viable catalog record answers five questions: Who created it? What is it called? When was it published? Where can it be found? What is it about?

In MARC format -- still the dominant standard for library catalog data -- these questions map to specific fields:

- **100 - Main Entry / Personal Name**: The primary creator
- **245 - Title Statement**: The work's title and any subtitle
- **260/264 - Publication Information**: Place, publisher, and date
- **050/082 - Call Number**: The shelf location (LC or Dewey)
- **650 - Subject Headings**: Controlled vocabulary terms describing the content

## Subject Headings

Of all the fields in a catalog record, subject headings are the most intellectually demanding to assign. The Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) provide a controlled vocabulary of hundreds of thousands of terms, each precisely defined and cross-referenced. A cataloger must read or examine a work, determine its principal subjects, and select the most appropriate headings from this vocabulary.

The goal is consistency: every book about the same topic should receive the same subject heading, regardless of which cataloger processes it. In practice, this ideal is difficult to achieve. Works often span multiple subjects, and the boundaries between disciplines are inherently fuzzy.

## The Card and Its Legacy

Before computerization, each catalog record was typed on a three-by-five-inch card and filed alphabetically in a wooden cabinet. The physical constraints of the card enforced a discipline of conciseness that persists in digital catalogs. Even today, a well-crafted catalog record is a small marvel of compressed information -- every field serving a purpose, every abbreviation meaningful to those who know the code.
