The genus *Drosera* comprises roughly 194 recognized species, making it the largest genus of carnivorous plants. First formally described by Linnaeus in 1753, sundews have since attracted the attention of field botanists, physiologists, and evolutionary biologists alike -- most notably Charles Darwin, who devoted an entire monograph to the genus in 1875.

## Morphological Diversity

Sundews exhibit a remarkable range of growth forms:

- **Rosetted species** such as *D. rotundifolia* form flat rosettes close to the ground, common in temperate peat bogs
- **Erect species** like *D. indica* grow upright with elongated stems, often found in tropical grasslands
- **Climbing species** including *D. macrantha* in Western Australia, which use surrounding vegetation for support
- **Pygmy sundews** -- a uniquely Australian group of miniature species, many under 2 cm in diameter

## The Flypaper Trap

All *Drosera* species employ a flypaper-style trapping mechanism. The leaves are covered with stalked glands called tentacles, each tipped with a droplet of viscous mucilage. When an insect lands on the leaf:

1. The mucilage adheres to the prey, preventing escape
2. Nearby tentacles bend toward the prey over a period of minutes to hours
3. The leaf blade may curl inward to maximize contact
4. Sessile glands on the leaf surface secrete digestive enzymes
5. Nutrients are absorbed directly through the leaf epidermis

The speed of tentacle movement varies considerably between species. *D. burmannii* can complete the process in under a minute, while *D. rotundifolia* may take several hours.

## Ecological Role

Sundews typically occupy nutrient-poor habitats where carnivory provides a competitive advantage. Peat bogs, sandy heathlands, and seasonally wet savannas are the most common environments. The nitrogen, phosphorus, and trace minerals obtained from prey supplement what the soil cannot provide.

## Conservation Status

While many *Drosera* species remain common, habitat loss -- particularly the draining of wetlands -- threatens numerous populations. Several Australian species are considered critically endangered, known only from single populations. The illegal collection of rare species for the horticultural trade adds additional pressure.

Continued field surveys and taxonomic work remain essential, as new species are still being described regularly -- a testament to how much we have yet to learn about this fascinating genus.
