## Description

The cactus dahlia was unknown to English gardeners until the introduction of the Juarez form from Mexico in 1872, by way of a Dutch nurseryman who had received a single tuber from a returning Aztec antiquarian. The long narrow petals, recurving at the margins, give the flower an appearance quite unlike the ball or show types familiar from exhibitions of the preceding decades, and the cultivar has since been crossed upon a thousand parents to produce the modern race.

The figure is from a specimen exhibited by Mr Turner of Slough at the Crystal Palace show of the season just past. The pressman has chosen a difficult problem in the separation of the deep crimson petals, and has elected to impress the red stone twice - once lightly upon a pink underlay, and a second time at full strength upon the tips and margins. The result, viewed under a strong glass, resolves into two distinct impressions, though the eye at reading distance perceives a single deep gradation.

## Of Culture

The dahlia is an herbaceous perennial of the composite family, grown from a tuberous root which must be lifted before the first severe frost and stored in dry sand through the winter. The roots are divided in spring, each piece retaining a portion of the crown bud; the divisions are started in heat and planted out toward the end of May, when all danger of frost is past.

## A Note upon Judgement

In the show ring, the cactus form is judged for evenness of petal, depth of colour, and freedom from disc. The judges of the Royal Horticultural Society have laid down a standard which is not easily met, and the exhibitor is advised to disbud rigorously through the summer to obtain a single bloom of the first water.
