## Description

The Cattleya occupies the place of honour in this volume, being the most celebrated of the epiphytic orchids brought to our gardeners from South America. Mr Swainson's first specimen, shipped to Liverpool in 1818 as packing material for a consignment of lichens, flowered upon Mr Cattley's stove some months later and was thereupon named in his honour.

The figure before the reader is life size and was taken from a plant in flower at the Royal Botanic Gardens in the autumn of the year preceding publication. The draughtswoman has taken some pains to render the corrugated lip faithfully, and the separation of the colours upon the stones has been arranged so that the deep magenta of the labellum is impressed from a single stone, the lighter pink of the sepals from a second, and the golden throat from a third. The fourth stone carries the blue of the column, and the fifth the black key which unites them.

## On the Structure

The inflorescence is terminal, carrying from three to five large flowers upon a single peduncle. Each flower measures some six inches across in a well-grown specimen, though the figure is reduced where necessary to preserve the proportion of the plate. The sepals are narrower than the petals and of a paler hue; the labellum is trilobed, with the middle lobe recurved to form the showy lip.

## Cultivation

The plant is easily maintained in the warm stove, given a compost of sphagnum and crocks, a season of rest in winter, and frequent syringings in summer. It flowers most freely when pot-bound and resents disturbance at the root.
