A retiring projectionist in Oslo discovers a hidden reel that rewrites the history of her family's cinema. What unfolds is part mystery, part elegy for the analog age of film.

<!-- more -->

## A Love Letter to Celluloid

Maren Solberg's fourth feature is her most personal work yet. *The Last Projection* follows Ingrid (Lena Olin, in a career-best performance) as she prepares to close the repertory cinema her family has operated for three generations.

The discovery of an unmarked 35mm print in the basement vault sets the story in motion, but Solberg is less interested in the mystery itself than in what the act of searching reveals. Each scene in the hidden reel triggers a memory, and the film moves fluidly between past and present without ever feeling disjointed.

## Craft and Composition

The cinematography by Hoyte van Hoytema deserves special mention. The present-day sequences are shot in cool, muted tones -- the theatre's faded velvet seats, the dust motes in the projection beam -- while the flashbacks glow with the warmth of Kodachrome. The contrast is never heavy-handed, but it lands every time.

Oscar Isaac appears in a supporting role as a film preservationist, and brings a quiet intensity that elevates every scene he shares with Olin. Saoirse Ronan's brief but pivotal appearance in the third act is devastating.

> "Every film is someone's memory of a day that never existed."

## The Verdict

*The Last Projection* is a rare film that manages to be both intellectually rigorous and deeply moving. It asks what we lose when we let go of the past, and whether the stories we project onto screens are any less real than the ones we live. Essential viewing.
