When specifying lasers for a show, the first decision is whether to use full-color RGB units or dedicated single-wavelength projectors. Each approach has distinct advantages.

## Single-Color Lasers

A single-wavelength laser produces one pure color. Common choices:

- **532 nm green** — The brightest perceived color; cuts through haze easily
- **638 nm red** — Warm, aggressive; excellent for high-energy moments
- **450 nm blue** — Cool and deep; creates a sense of vast space

Because there is no color mixing, the output is maximally coherent. Beam divergence is minimal, producing razor-sharp lines even over long distances. For outdoor shows where beams must travel hundreds of meters, single-color green lasers remain unmatched.

## RGB Laser Systems

RGB projectors combine red, green, and blue diodes through dichroic optics into a single output. This enables:

- Full-spectrum color mixing (16M+ colors)
- Color transitions within a single effect
- White balance for text and graphic projection

The trade-off is power distribution. A 10W RGB system might allocate 3W red, 4W green, and 3W blue. When projecting a single color, only a fraction of total power is used. A dedicated 10W green laser will always outperform the green channel of a 10W RGB unit.

## Practical Guidance

Most professional shows combine both types. RGB units handle aerial effects where color variety matters, while dedicated green or blue lasers handle long-throw beam work and crowd scanning.

> Use RGB for art. Use single-color for impact.

The best laser designers think of their rig like an orchestra — different instruments for different moments, all serving the same composition.
