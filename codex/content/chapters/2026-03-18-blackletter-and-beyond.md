The history of Western writing is a history of shapes. From the stately capitals carved into Roman monuments to the fluid minuscules of Carolingian scriptoria, each era developed letterforms suited to its tools, materials, and aesthetic ideals. Among all these scripts, none is more closely associated with the medieval world than blackletter — the dense, angular, and supremely decorative hand that dominated European book production from the 12th to the 15th century.

Blackletter, also called Gothic script or *textura*, emerged as scribes sought to fit more text onto expensive parchment. The letters were compressed laterally, their curves straightened into angular strokes, creating a texture of dark verticals that gave the script its name — *textura* from the Latin for "woven." The overall effect on the page was of a dark, rich fabric of ink, dense and rhythmic.

## The Hands of Europe

Blackletter was not a single script but a family of related hands that varied by region and purpose:

- **Textura quadrata** — the most formal variety, with precise diamond-shaped feet on the minims. Used for the finest liturgical manuscripts.
- **Textura prescissa** — similar to quadrata but with flat, unseriffed feet. Associated with English manuscripts.
- **Rotunda** — a rounder, more open variant preferred in Italy and Spain, reflecting the influence of the Romanesque aesthetic.
- **Bastarda** — a cursive adaptation blending blackletter forms with the speed of documentary hands. Popular in France and Burgundy for vernacular texts.
- **Schwabacher** and **Fraktur** — later German developments that would persist well into the 20th century.

Each of these hands carried cultural associations. Textura connoted authority and sacredness; rotunda suggested humanistic learning; bastarda implied courtly elegance.

## The Carolingian Foundation

To understand blackletter, one must look back to its predecessor. In the late 8th century, scholars at the court of Charlemagne developed Caroline minuscule — a clear, regular script designed to bring uniformity to the diverse and often illegible hands then in use across the Frankish Empire. Caroline minuscule was an act of standardization, and its rounded, well-spaced letters were the foundation on which all subsequent medieval scripts were built.

As the centuries progressed and the demand for books grew, Caroline minuscule gradually compressed and angularized. By the 12th century, the transformation into blackletter was complete. The circle of script history would close again in the 15th century, when Italian humanists, rediscovering ancient Roman texts written in Caroline minuscule, mistook them for authentic Roman scripts and modeled their own "Roman" typefaces on them — the direct ancestors of the fonts we read today.

## Gutenberg's Choice

When Johannes Gutenberg designed his movable type in the 1450s, he chose textura as his model. The 42-line Bible, the first major book printed with movable type, replicated the appearance of a handwritten manuscript so faithfully that it was initially difficult to distinguish print from script. Gutenberg's types included hundreds of variant letters, ligatures, and abbreviations — a testament to the complexity of the scribal tradition he sought to mechanize.

The printed blackletter held its ground for centuries in the German-speaking world, where Fraktur remained the standard typeface until it was officially abandoned in 1941. In the rest of Europe, roman and italic types prevailed. Today, blackletter survives primarily in decorative contexts — newspaper mastheads, diplomas, beer labels, and, of course, themes like this one.
