# About Tessellation Art

## The Escher Legacy

Maurits Cornelis Escher (1898-1972) was a Dutch graphic artist who made mathematically inspired woodcuts, lithographs, and mezzotints. His work features impossible constructions, explorations of infinity, and tessellations—patterns of interlocking shapes that tile a plane with no gaps or overlaps.

## Mathematical Beauty

Tessellations are found throughout nature and have been used in art and architecture for millennia. From the honeycomb structure of beehives to the intricate tile work of the Alhambra palace, these patterns represent the intersection of mathematics, art, and nature.

## This Gallery

This digital gallery celebrates the Escher aesthetic through:

- **Monochromatic Palette**: Black, white, and shades of grey with gold accents
- **Geometric Precision**: Clean lines and perfect symmetry
- **Infinite Patterns**: Seamlessly repeating tessellation backgrounds
- **Impossible Figures**: Visual paradoxes and optical illusions
- **Metamorphosis**: Gradual transformations between forms

## Design Philosophy

The gallery's design itself incorporates tessellating patterns as background elements, creating an immersive environment that mirrors the artworks it displays. The impossible cube decoration and geometric accents pay homage to Escher's fascination with perspective and dimensional paradoxes.
