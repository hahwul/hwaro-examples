# Hyperbolic Geometry

While most tessellations exist in flat Euclidean space, this pattern explores the curved world of hyperbolic geometry. In hyperbolic space, the familiar rules of geometry bend—literally.

## Beyond Euclidean Space

In standard Euclidean geometry:
- Parallel lines never meet
- Triangle angles sum to 180°
- Space is flat

In hyperbolic geometry:
- No parallel lines exist (all lines eventually diverge)
- Triangle angles sum to less than 180°
- Space has negative curvature

## Escher's "Circle Limit" Series

Escher created his famous "Circle Limit" woodcuts (1958-1960) to visualize hyperbolic tessellations. These works compress infinite hyperbolic space into a finite circular disk, with forms growing exponentially smaller toward the boundary.

### Pattern Properties

This tessellation demonstrates:

- **Exponential Growth**: Each successive ring contains more tiles
- **Poincaré Disk Model**: Projects hyperbolic space onto a Euclidean circle
- **Conformal Mapping**: Preserves angles but not distances

## Artistic Interpretation

The pattern begins with recognizable forms at the center, then multiplies them infinitely toward the edge. What appears crowded is actually uniform spacing in hyperbolic space—our Euclidean perception distorts the hyperbolic reality.

> "By keenly confronting the enigmas that surround us, and by considering and analyzing the observations that I had made, I ended up in the domain of mathematics." — M.C. Escher

## Mathematical Significance

Hyperbolic geometry was discovered in the 19th century by mathematicians Bolyai, Lobachevsky, and Gauss. It revolutionized mathematics by proving that Euclid's parallel postulate was not a universal truth but merely one possible geometric system.

Escher, with no formal mathematical training, intuitively grasped these concepts through artistic exploration. His visualizations of hyperbolic space remain among the most accessible introductions to non-Euclidean geometry.
