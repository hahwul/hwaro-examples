The transition happened gradually. First a scattering of birch at the meadow's edge, their white trunks standing like sentinels. Then oak and ash, growing closer together, their branches reaching across the gaps until they interlocked overhead. Within a hundred paces, the open sky was gone.

## The Canopy

A forest canopy is its own kind of sky. Light does not arrive directly but is filtered, scattered, and redirected by thousands of leaves. It reaches the forest floor in patches and coins, shifting as the wind moves the branches above. The effect is of being underwater -- a green, dappled light that moves constantly but gently.

The temperature dropped as I went deeper. The air was cooler here, held in place by the trees, carrying the rich smell of decomposition and growth happening simultaneously. Moss covered everything that held still long enough -- stones, fallen trunks, the north sides of standing trees.

## Listening to Wood

The forest speaks in creaks and groans. Wind that barely registers as a breeze in the open becomes audible here as the upper canopy catches it and the trunks flex in response. Each tree has its own voice, determined by its size, its species, and the soundness of its wood. A healthy oak produces a deep, slow groan. A dead pine, hollowed by insects, rings almost like a bell.

I stopped walking and stood still for several minutes, just listening. The forest rewarded patience with detail. A nuthatch spiraling down a trunk. The tap of a falling acorn. Somewhere distant, the rhythmic knock of a woodpecker testing wood for cavities.

## The Understory

Below the canopy, a second world exists. Ferns unfurl in the gaps between tree roots. Fungi push through leaf litter in improbable shapes -- shelf brackets jutting from bark, pale fingers rising from the soil, caps so delicate they would dissolve in rain. This is the forest's engine room, where dead matter is broken down and returned to the system.

The path -- if it could be called that -- wound between the trunks following the path of least resistance. I followed it deeper, the light dimming with each step, until the forest closed around me completely and the meadow became a memory.
