The grass was waist-high and silver with dew. It bent in slow waves under a wind that carried the smell of wet earth and wild thyme. The sun had not yet cleared the eastern ridge, but its light was already spreading -- a pale wash that turned the mist into something luminous.

## First Light

There is a particular quality to the first minutes of daylight that cannot be replicated later. The world is not yet fully itself. Colors exist in a narrower range, muted and cool, as though the landscape is still deciding what it wants to be. Greens are bluer. Shadows are softer. Sound carries differently through air that has not yet been warmed.

I stood at the edge of a meadow that seemed to extend in every direction. There were no paths. The grass had grown without interruption, each blade leaning against the next, forming a continuous surface that rippled like water when the wind passed through.

## The Weight of Stillness

Morning stillness is not silence. A meadow at dawn is full of small sounds -- the click of insects, the rustle of a bird adjusting its grip on a stem, the distant report of a woodpecker beginning its work. But these sounds exist inside a larger quiet, a spaciousness that makes each one distinct.

I walked without direction. The grass parted ahead of me and closed behind, leaving a trail that would disappear within the hour. There was no urgency. The day was long and the landscape was patient.

## Threshold

Every journey needs a beginning, and beginnings are best when they are unhurried. The meadow was not a destination. It was a threshold -- the place where ordinary ground becomes the start of something. The mountains were visible in the distance, faint and blue, more suggestion than substance. They would sharpen as the day progressed. For now, they were a promise.

I adjusted my pack and kept walking. The sun cleared the ridge. The mist began to thin. The meadow turned from silver to gold.
