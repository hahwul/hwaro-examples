Neon and fluorescent aesthetics have been a constant presence in visual culture. From the glowing streets of Shinjuku to the cover art of synthwave albums, these colors carry meaning beyond mere decoration.

## The Neon Cityscape

Cities like Tokyo, Hong Kong, and Las Vegas built their visual identities around neon. The layered glow of signs competing for attention created an accidental art form — one that filmmakers like Ridley Scott and Wong Kar-wai turned into cinematic poetry.

## Music and Neon

The connection between fluorescent visuals and music runs deep:

- **New Wave** (1980s): MTV-era graphics dripping with hot pink and electric blue
- **Rave Culture** (1990s): UV-reactive everything, from flyers to fashion
- **Synthwave** (2010s): A nostalgic revival of neon grid aesthetics
- **Hyperpop** (2020s): Maximalist digital art with clashing fluorescents

## Why It Endures

Fluorescent color triggers a primal response. It signals energy, urgency, and excitement. In a world of muted minimalism, going fluorescent is a deliberate choice to be seen and remembered.
