## Departure from Tarifa

Tarifa is the southernmost point of continental Europe, and it looks the part. The town perches on a narrow spit of land that juts into the strait, battered by winds from both the Atlantic and the Mediterranean. On the day I crossed, the Levante was blowing -- the hot east wind that funnels through the strait and whips the water into short, steep waves. The ferry terminal is a small building at the edge of the port, and the passengers waiting inside were a mix of Moroccan families returning home, Spanish day-trippers, and a handful of travelers like me who had come specifically for the crossing.

The ferry itself is a modern catamaran that makes the journey in roughly an hour, though the company's schedule says thirty-five minutes, which I suspect is achievable only in conditions that rarely exist. We pulled away from the dock at ten in the morning, and within five minutes the boat was pitching in the chop.

## The strait

The Strait of Gibraltar is fourteen kilometers wide at its narrowest point, and everything about it is compressed and intensified. The currents are powerful -- Atlantic water flows east into the Mediterranean along the surface, while a deeper counter-current flows west -- and the result is a body of water that is almost never calm. Container ships and tankers pass through in a continuous procession, and from the ferry deck they appear startlingly close, their hulls rising like walls of painted steel.

Halfway across, you can see both continents simultaneously. Europe behind, a line of brown hills above the white buildings of Tarifa. Africa ahead, the Rif Mountains appearing first as a dark smudge on the horizon, then resolving into green slopes and, eventually, the white city of Tangier climbing its hillside above the port.

## Arrival in Tangier

The approach to Tangier is one of the great arrivals in travel. The new port is modern and efficient, but the city beyond it is ancient and layered in a way that becomes apparent within minutes of stepping through the terminal doors. The medina rises steeply from the port, its narrow streets climbing toward the kasbah at the summit, and the sound of the city -- car horns, the call to prayer, vendors calling out prices, the clatter of cart wheels on stone -- envelops you immediately.

I had crossed a body of water that takes less time than a commuter train, and yet everything had changed. The architecture, the language, the smell of the air, the quality of the light. The strait is narrow enough that you can see across it on a clear day, but the distance it represents is immense.

## The return

I stayed in Tangier for three days and then took the ferry back. The return crossing was calmer, the Levante having died, and I stood on the upper deck and watched Africa recede and Europe approach. The two continents seemed to lean toward each other across the water, as though the strait were not a separation but a conversation -- two old neighbors exchanging words across a narrow street.

## Notes for the crossing

Book the ferry in advance during summer months. The fast ferries from Tarifa to Tangier Ville run several times daily. Bring a passport -- this is an international border crossing with full customs and immigration. The Tangier port is well-organized, but the taxi negotiation outside the terminal requires patience and a willingness to name your own price before the driver names his.
