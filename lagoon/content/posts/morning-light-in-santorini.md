## Before the crowds

There is a window of time on Santorini that belongs to almost no one. It opens around five-thirty in the morning, when the sky over the caldera shifts from deep indigo to a pale, washed-out blue, and it closes by eight, when the first tour buses begin their climb up the switchbacks to Oia.

I had come to the island expecting the postcard version: white-washed walls, blue domes, sunsets that look engineered for photographs. What I found in those early hours was something quieter and more interesting. The light before sunrise does not flatter Santorini. It reveals it. The plaster walls show their cracks. The famous blue domes appear grey and slightly weathered. The narrow lanes between the buildings are still dark, and the cats that patrol them are just beginning to stir.

## The caldera path

Walking the path from Fira to Oia at dawn is a different experience from the midday march that most visitors endure. The volcanic rock underfoot is cool. The air smells of wild thyme and the sea. Below, the caldera water is so still it looks like poured metal, and the islands of Nea Kameni and Palea Kameni sit on its surface like pieces of dark sculpture.

At several points along the path, the ground drops away sharply to the left, and you can see straight down the cliff face to the water hundreds of meters below. The erosion patterns in the volcanic layers are vivid -- bands of red, black, and cream stone that record eruptions spanning thousands of years. This is not a gentle landscape. It is the rim of a collapsed volcano, and in the early morning silence, with no one else on the path, that fact feels very real.

## Oia at the golden hour

By the time I reached Oia, the sun had cleared the eastern horizon and the famous golden light was beginning to do its work. The white walls turned warm. The bougainvillea glowed. A baker was opening his shop, and the smell of fresh bread mixed with the salt air drifting up from the port of Ammoudi far below.

I sat on a low wall near the ruins of the Venetian castle and watched the light change. Within the hour, the first visitors would arrive, and by noon the narrow lanes would be nearly impassable. But for now, the village belonged to the baker, the cats, and a single traveler who had traded sleep for the privilege of seeing the island as it is, rather than as it performs.

## Practical notes

The Fira-to-Oia path is roughly ten kilometers and takes three to four hours at a comfortable pace. Carry water. There is no shade for long stretches. The path is well-marked but uneven in places, particularly near Imerovigli. A headlamp is useful if you start before dawn.
