## The Sunday walking street

Every Sunday evening, the main road through Chiang Mai's old city closes to traffic and becomes a market that stretches for nearly a kilometer. It is one of those events that sounds overwhelming in description but in practice unfolds at its own unhurried pace. The vendors set up around four in the afternoon. By five, the first shoppers are drifting between the stalls. By seven, the street is full, but the crowd moves slowly and there is a gentleness to the compression that you do not find in markets elsewhere.

The goods for sale are a mix of handcraft and commerce: carved soaps shaped like flowers, hill-tribe textiles in indigo and red, paintings on rice paper, silver jewelry from workshops in the surrounding mountains. But the real attraction is the food.

## The smoke and the steam

Every third stall, it seems, is cooking something. The charcoal grills send columns of fragrant smoke into the evening air -- pork skewers glazed with a sweet chili sauce, whole fish stuffed with lemongrass, chicken wings that have been marinated in something involving turmeric and coconut milk. Between the grills, there are steamers releasing clouds of white vapor over baskets of dumplings and buns. And everywhere, the flat griddles where vendors press out roti -- thin, crisp, folded around banana or egg or sweetened condensed milk and served on a square of wax paper.

I ate my way down the length of the market over three hours. Khao soi from a stall near Wat Phan Tao, the curry broth rich with coconut and spice, the egg noodles crisp on top and soft beneath. Sai oua, the northern Thai sausage packed with herbs and just enough chili to build a slow heat. Mango sticky rice from a woman who assembled each portion with the precision and care of someone performing a ceremony.

## Temple grounds

Several of the temples along the route open their grounds to the market, and these are the best places to eat. You buy from the stalls outside, then carry your food through the temple gates and find a place to sit on the stone steps or the low walls surrounding the courtyards. The temples are lit with lanterns and small electric lights, and monks occasionally pass through on their way to evening prayers, moving through the crowd with a quiet self-possession that the rest of us can only admire.

At Wat Chedi Luang, I sat on the base of the ancient chedi -- the great ruined stupa that dominates the compound -- and ate papaya salad while watching the market flow past. The chedi is six centuries old and still enormous despite having lost its upper section to an earthquake. Sitting against its warm stone with a plate of som tum and the sound of the market drifting over the wall, I felt the particular satisfaction that comes from being exactly where you are supposed to be.

## Timing

Arrive by five to see the market take shape. The food stalls are busiest between six and eight. By nine, some vendors begin packing up. The Saturday market on Wualai Road is smaller but less crowded and follows the same rhythm. Both are worth visiting if your schedule allows.
