## Origin

This series began with an accident. A drop of water landed on a lens element during a shoot. Instead of wiping it away, I shot through it. The resulting image -- a sharp subject surrounded by a halo of prismatic color -- became the starting point for six months of deliberate experimentation.

## Method

Each image in The Refraction Series is created by placing a refractive element between the camera sensor and the subject. Water droplets on glass. Cut crystal held against the front element. Acrylic prisms taped to the lens hood. The refractive element bends light paths differently for each wavelength, creating controlled chromatic separation across the frame.

## The Macro Perspective

At macro distances, depth of field collapses to millimeters. This extreme shallow focus amplifies chromatic aberration dramatically. A point of light that would show only slight color fringing at normal distances explodes into a full spectrum at 1:1 magnification. The bokeh -- the quality of the out-of-focus areas -- takes on distinct color channels, with each blur disc showing red, green, and blue rings.

**Lens**: Macro-Takumar 50mm f/4 (1968) with extension tubes
**Working distance**: 15-30cm
**Aperture**: f/4 to f/5.6
**Light**: Controlled LED panels, single-source

## Water as Optic

A single water droplet on a sheet of glass acts as a tiny convex lens. It focuses light, but imperfectly. The chromatic aberration of a water lens is extreme -- far beyond what any manufactured glass would produce. This imperfection creates vivid rainbow edges around every element in the frame.

The challenge is control. Water droplets are unstable. They evaporate, merge, slide. Each composition exists for seconds before it changes. The photography becomes a collaboration with entropy.

## Prints

Selected images from this series are available as archival pigment prints on baryta paper. The paper surface holds the color separation visible in a way that screen reproduction cannot match. Details in the prints reveal spectral bands that compress to solid colors on a monitor.

## What Comes Next

The next phase of this project will explore refraction through moving water -- streams, fountains, rain. The chromatic aberration of a static droplet is beautiful but frozen. The aberration of water in motion should produce something closer to how light actually behaves in the natural world: constantly shifting, never repeating, always splitting.
