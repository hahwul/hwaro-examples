## The City as Prism

At night, a city becomes a machine for scattering light. Sodium vapor streetlamps, LED signage, neon tubes, headlights through rain-slicked asphalt -- each source emits its own narrow band of wavelengths, and each interacts differently with the atmosphere, with window glass, with the optics of the camera.

## Shooting Conditions

These images were captured over three nights in late winter. Overcast skies reflected the city light back down, creating a low orange ceiling that compressed the tonal range and pushed the color palette toward the warm end. Rain on the second night added a layer of reflection that doubled every light source and introduced motion blur into the chromatic fringing.

## The Aberration as Subject

In conventional night photography, chromatic aberration is the enemy. Purple fringing around streetlights. Color shifts at the edges of the frame. Photographers spend hours in post removing these artifacts.

This series takes the opposite approach. Every image was shot with a lens chosen specifically for its strong chromatic aberration characteristics. The resulting color separation -- red, green, and blue halos radiating outward from each point of light -- transforms ordinary street scenes into something that looks closer to how a spectrometer sees the world.

**Lens**: Jupiter-9 85mm f/2 (1965)
**Aperture**: f/2
**ISO**: 800-1600
**Shutter**: 1/30 to 1/125, handheld

## Signal and Noise

At high ISO on a dark street, digital noise becomes visible. But noise, like aberration, has a structure. The red channel is noisier than the green. The blue channel is noisiest of all. When you split the channels and examine them independently, each tells a different story about the same scene. The green channel is sharp and informative. The blue channel is soft and impressionistic. The red sits somewhere between.

## What the Eye Misses

The human eye performs its own chromatic correction in real time. We never see the color fringing that is always present in our peripheral vision. The camera, with the right lens, reveals what our neural processing hides.
