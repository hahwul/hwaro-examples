A slim novel that contains an entire era. Fitzgerald's prose is so polished it practically gleams, and beneath that surface lies a profound sadness about the American experiment.

<!-- more -->

Nick Carraway arrives in New York in the summer of 1922, rents a small house next to a mansion, and is drawn into the orbit of Jay Gatsby -- a man who has reinvented himself so thoroughly that the original person has been entirely consumed.

### The Prose

Let us start with what everyone agrees on: Fitzgerald writes sentences of extraordinary beauty. The famous closing passage is justly celebrated, but the entire novel operates at this level. Every metaphor earns its place. Every description serves double duty as characterization.

The green light across the bay. The eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg. The shirts thrown from the closet. These images have entered the collective literary consciousness because Fitzgerald understood that great writing is precise, not elaborate.

### Nick as Narrator

Nick Carraway is one of literature's most interesting narrators precisely because he is not interesting. He is the still point around which the more vivid characters revolve. His reliability is deliberately questionable -- he claims tolerance and open-mindedness while making sharp moral judgments throughout.

This tension makes the novel richer on every rereading. Whose story is Nick really telling, and why?

### The American Dream

The novel's reputation as a critique of the American Dream is well-earned but sometimes oversimplified. Gatsby is not merely a cautionary tale about wealth and ambition. He is something more complex: a man whose capacity for hope is both his greatest quality and his fatal weakness.

Fitzgerald does not condemn the dream itself so much as the corruption that attends its pursuit. The difference matters.

### Revisiting a Classic

Reading *Gatsby* as an adult, after years of life experience, is a different experience from reading it in school. The parties are sadder. Tom Buchanan is more frightening. Daisy is more sympathetic. And Gatsby himself becomes less a symbol and more a person -- which only makes his story more affecting.

A book that deserves its reputation, which is the highest compliment you can pay a classic.
