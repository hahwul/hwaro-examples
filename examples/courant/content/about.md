The Courant started in 2019 as a group chat between three critics who agreed on nothing except that most weekly round-ups were cowardly — three stars for everything, no verbs, no risk. We split off into our own sheet a year later. The name is an old print term for a newsletter of record; we kept it because we still believe a review should read like it was filed on deadline, not workshopped into mush. The full run of what we have filed since sits in the [notices archive](/notices/); the genres we return to most are indexed on the [genre page](/categories/).

## What we cover

Modular and generative systems, post-club and its splinters, drone, dub techno, noise, and the wider edges of electronic and experimental music — anything built from patch cables, tape, field recordings, or code, and released with enough intent that it deserves an argument rather than a shrug. We do not cover reissues unless the reissue itself makes an editorial claim, and we do not cover anything with a marketing budget larger than our own operating costs.

## How scoring works

Every notice closes with a score out of ten, set by the critic who wrote it and never softened by the desk afterward. We do not do half-hearted sixes to be polite. A five means the record is genuinely unresolved, not a hedge. A nine means we will still be playing it in five years. The stacked numerals that run down the side of every notice are not decoration — they are the one part of the piece a reader can absorb from across the room, so we make them earn that attention.

## Submissions

Labels and artists can send WAV or FLAC plus one paragraph of real context — no one-sheets, no mood boards — to the desk address on file. We do not guarantee coverage, we do not promise a good score for coverage, and we read everything in the order it arrives. If a record is going to get filed, expect it within three weeks of release, not before. For a sense of tone, start with this week's lead notice on the [front page](/).

## The desk

Six critics rotate the weekly notices: R. Okonkwo-Linde on modular and generative systems, Mira Sequeira on post-club and its aftershocks, Dev Kallis on drone and long-form listening, Tam Voss on dub techno and low-end physics, Odalys Fenn on noise and its more hostile cousins, and K. Brannigan on ambient and interior music. Bylines run in caps because the record is the subject of the sentence, not the critic.
