Kessel Drift's last EP, *Load-Bearing Wall*, was twenty-two minutes of genuinely mean club music — kicks like structural failure, a mid-range that felt personally hostile. *Vertical Bruises* is the difficult follow-up, and it is difficult in the least interesting way a record can be: it sounds like an artist who got a better plugin bundle and forgot why the old, uglier tools were working.

<!-- more -->

Every track here is technically immaculate. The transient design on "Stress Test" is genuinely impressive, a kick drum that seems to arrive from four directions at once, and "Undercroft" builds a granular vocal texture that would sound extraordinary in a four-minute standalone piece. The problem is that nothing on the record is allowed to be a standalone piece. Every idea gets interrupted at the ninety-second mark by the next technically impressive idea, and the cumulative effect is a portfolio reel rather than a set of tracks anyone will actually want to hear again.

There are flashes of the old meanness. "Bruise (Reprise)" locks into four minutes of genuinely punishing low end before dissolving into a texture that, for once, feels earned rather than demonstrated. If Kessel Drift had built the whole record around that instinct — commit first, decorate later — this would be a different notice. As it stands, *Vertical Bruises* is a producer's record in the worst sense: made to impress other producers, not to move a room. Readers after a record that actually commits should skip ahead to [Ussa Nyland's *Long Corridor Weather*](../ussa-nyland-long-corridor-weather/) instead.
