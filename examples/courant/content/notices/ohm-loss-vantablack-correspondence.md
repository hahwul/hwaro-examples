Ohm Loss has spent three records refusing to resolve a chord, and *Vantablack Correspondence* is the one where the refusal finally sounds like a decision rather than a limitation. The album is built from a single patch — a Buchla-style complex oscillator feeding a chain of comparators that never quite lock into a stable rhythm — recorded live in five unedited takes and sequenced by how badly each one wants to end.

<!-- more -->

"Corridor Six," the eighteen-minute centerpiece, is the clearest statement of the method. A low pulse establishes itself in the first ninety seconds and then spends the rest of the track trying to escape its own quantization, sliding a few cents sharp, correcting, overshooting flat. It should read as an error. Instead it reads as an argument about what modular music is for — not the patient unfolding of a system, which is the genre's usual selling point, but the sound of a system fighting its own leash.

The record's second half is less disciplined, and the two short interludes that separate the long pieces feel like they were included for pacing rather than conviction. But "Vantablack, Reprise" closes the album on the year's best single gesture in this genre: forty seconds of total silence broken by one comparator click, unprocessed, dry, gone before you can decide if it was intentional. It was. Ohm Loss has made a record about the discomfort of an unresolved system and had the nerve to let the discomfort be the whole point.
