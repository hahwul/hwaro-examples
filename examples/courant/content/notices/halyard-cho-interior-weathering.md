The premise of *Interior Weathering* sounds thin on paper: six months of field recordings taken from inside a single third-floor apartment — the refrigerator's compressor cycling, a radiator's tick as it cools, rain read through a specific pane of glass — layered under long synthesizer pads and released without much further elaboration. Ambient records built on a gimmick like this usually run out of ideas by the second track. Halyard Cho's does not, because the gimmick is never actually the point.

<!-- more -->

What holds the record together is Cho's ear for how those source recordings sit against the harmonic material. The refrigerator hum on "Compressor, February" is tuned almost imperceptibly against the pad beneath it, so the two sit a few cents apart and generate a slow, barely audible beating that gives the whole seven-minute piece its only real motion. "Radiator Tick (Slowing)" does something similar with rhythm rather than pitch, letting a cooling metal tick fall gradually out of time against a synthesizer pulse until the two have drifted a full bar apart by the track's end.

The record's back half loses some of this precision — "Rain Through the Kitchen Pane" leans on its field recording without giving it enough to do harmonically, and runs long as a result. But at its best, *Interior Weathering* makes a genuinely persuasive case that the most interesting ambient music being made right now is not about evoking a landscape, but about really listening to the room you are already in.
