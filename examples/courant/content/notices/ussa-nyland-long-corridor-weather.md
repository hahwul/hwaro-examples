There is a school of drone criticism that treats duration as an obstacle to be justified — a two-hour record has to earn its length or it is simply an indulgence. *Long Corridor Weather* rejects the premise entirely. Ussa Nyland has built a piece where the length is not a container for the ideas but the primary material, four looped tape sources drifting in and out of phase across a span too long to hold in memory, so that the record is less something you listen to than something you are inside of for a while.

<!-- more -->

The technique is simple and the execution is not. Four loops of processed string recordings, each roughly nineteen minutes long, are set running at slightly different speeds, so their alignment drifts continuously across the full runtime and never repeats. There is no arrangement in the conventional sense — no build, no drop, no ABA structure — only the slow mathematics of four cycles falling in and out of sync with each other, occasionally locking into a chord that could not have been composed, only arrived at.

What makes the record extraordinary rather than merely rigorous is how much Nyland trusts the listener to notice the small things: a loop point that briefly clicks before the processing smooths it out, a moment near the ninety-minute mark where all four sources nearly align and the room seems to hold its breath. This is demanding work, and it will lose anyone unwilling to give it the time it asks for. For anyone who does, *Long Corridor Weather* is the best argument this year for what patience actually sounds like.
