I have walked these halls for longer than memory permits. The mirrors no longer reflect the living. The roses in the conservatory bloom black at their hearts.

It began with the perfume. A single vial left uncorked in the east drawing room one autumn evening. By morning the velvet curtains had begun to weep. By the third night the chandeliers sang in minor keys when the wind moved through the broken panes.

They still come, the guests. Or what remains of them. They arrive in carriages drawn by horses with too many eyes, wearing gowns stitched from funeral silk and moth wings. They dance until the floorboards weep sap and the piano keys stick with old blood.

I record what I can. These pages are my only remaining duty. When the last candle gutters and the final waltz ends, perhaps someone — something — will find them and know that once, in this place, beauty and horror wore the same face.

Do not linger too long in the cellars.

Do not answer the voices behind the portrait of the woman with no mouth.

The estate remembers your name even if you do not speak it aloud.

— The Curator
