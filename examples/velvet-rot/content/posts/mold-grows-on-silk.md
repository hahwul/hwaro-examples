The Countess had been dead for eleven months when we discovered the mold had memorized the pattern of her favorite ballgown.

It began as a faint chartreuse shadow along the train. We thought at first it was simply the damp of the east wing. But the filaments arranged themselves into the exact embroidery of peonies she had worn the night she danced until her heart stopped beneath the musicians' gallery.

By the fourth night the mold had climbed the bodice. Tiny fruiting bodies pushed through the silk in the precise locations where her pearls had once rested against her throat.

The seamstress who tried to remove it was found three days later in the linen closet, her own mouth filled with the same delicate green threads. She was still breathing. The mold was not.

We stopped trying to clean after that.

Now the gown hangs in the portrait gallery beside her painting. At certain hours of the night the mold pulses in time with music no one is playing. If you stand very still you can hear it whispering the names of every partner she ever took across the floor.

Mold does not forget.

It only waits for new silk to arrive.
