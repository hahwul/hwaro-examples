The first time they sang, only three of us heard it.

It began as a low crystalline hum beneath the usual creaking of the house. Then the individual prisms began to ring in sequence, each note distinct and perfectly tuned to the others. A waltz in a minor key no living composer has ever written.

We stood beneath them in our finest remaining clothes, the ones that still held the scent of the last real winter. And we danced. Not to the music we had once known, but to the new music the house had decided to give us.

<!-- more -->

The chandeliers have sung every night since. Different melodies. Sometimes they weep glass tears that fall and shatter into perfect eighth notes on the marble. The servants (those who remain) have learned to sweep in time with the rhythm so as not to disturb the phrasing.

I have begun to wonder if the house is trying to remember how to be loved. Or if it is teaching us a new way to die beautifully.

Last night the largest chandelier in the ballroom struck a single sustained note so pure that the remaining mirrors finally cracked in sympathy.

We applauded until our hands bled.

The house took a bow.
