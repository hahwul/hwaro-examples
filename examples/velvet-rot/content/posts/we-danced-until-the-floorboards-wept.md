We told ourselves it was only one more dance.

The orchestra had long since become part of the walls, their bows moving even when no hands held them. The guests arrived in clothes that had never been made by human fingers. Their faces were beautiful in the way that certain fungi are beautiful: intricate, perfect, and already beginning to dissolve at the edges.

We danced.

<!-- more -->

By the third night the floorboards had started to weep a thin amber resin that smelled of every perfume ever worn in this room. Our shoes stuck with each step. By the fifth night the resin had risen to our ankles. We kept dancing. What else was there?

On the morning of the eighth day the floor had sealed around our feet. The dancers who remained upright were now part of the pattern — living ornaments in the house's final waltz.

I alone managed to tear myself free. My left shoe remains embedded somewhere beneath the third chandelier, still tapping in time.

Sometimes at night I hear the others still dancing beneath the boards.

They sound happy.

The floorboards remember every step we ever took.

They are still waiting for the music to end.
