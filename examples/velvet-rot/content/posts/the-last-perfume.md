The night the last guest arrived, the air itself had begun to curdle.

She wore a gown the color of old blood and carried a single crystal bottle stoppered with a cork carved from the thigh bone of a saint no one could name. When she smiled, the chandeliers dimmed as if in deference.

"Try it," she whispered, pressing the vial into my palm. The liquid inside was the deep violet of a bruise just before it yellows. "It is the final scent. After this, there will be no more flowers. Only the memory of flowers."

I unstoppered it beneath the largest chandelier. The notes unfurled like silk torn from a coffin lining: night-blooming jasmine that had rotted on the vine, amber warmed against fevered skin, something metallic and sweet that I later understood was grief rendered into oil.

By morning the roses in every vase had turned the color of spoiled wine. The velvet upholstery on the divans grew small white filaments that smelled, impossibly, of the same perfume. The mirrors began to fog from the inside.

I keep the vial in the drawer beside my bed. Some nights I open it just enough to remember what it felt like to be desired by something that was never human.

The last perfume does not fade.

It waits.
