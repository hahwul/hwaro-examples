**Difficulty:** Journeyman
**Brewing Time:** 2 hours
**Shelf Life:** 48 hours (deteriorates rapidly in heat)

## Description

The Whispervine Infusion opens the drinker's perception to the chemical signaling networks that plants use to communicate. For approximately two hours after consumption, the practitioner can perceive -- though not in words -- the general state and intentions of nearby plant life.

The experience is described not as hearing voices but as a subtle awareness. A tree under stress from drought will register as a feeling of thirst. A flowering plant will communicate something akin to contentment. Poisonous plants often project a sense of warning that experienced practitioners learn to recognize.

## Ingredients

- Whispervine tendrils (3, freshly cut and still curling)
- Linden blossom tea (1 cup, brewed strong)
- Rosemary (1 sprig, fresh)
- Morning dew (2 tablespoons, collected before sunrise)
- Birch bark (a thumb-sized piece, shaved thin)
- Fennel seeds (half a teaspoon, lightly crushed)

## Preparation

1. Brew the linden blossom tea at full strength and allow it to cool to warm.
2. In a glass vessel, combine the warm tea with the morning dew.
3. Add the whispervine tendrils. They should begin to uncurl slightly in the liquid -- this is the desired reaction.
4. Add the rosemary sprig and birch bark shavings.
5. Cover and let steep for ninety minutes at room temperature. Do not apply heat.
6. Strain through muslin cloth. The liquid should be pale gold with a faintly green tint.
7. Add the crushed fennel seeds to the strained liquid and stir three times clockwise.
8. Consume within 48 hours for full effect.

## Warnings

- Whispervine must be cut with copper tools. Steel causes the vine to retract its communicative properties.
- The perception of plant distress can be overwhelming in areas of ecological damage. Begin practice in healthy gardens.
- Do not combine with other perception-altering preparations. The sensory overlap can cause disorientation and severe headaches.

## Notes

This infusion is particularly valued by herbalists and gardeners, as it allows them to assess the health and needs of their plants directly. Some practitioners use it diagnostically, walking through forests to identify areas of disease or contamination that would otherwise go unnoticed.
