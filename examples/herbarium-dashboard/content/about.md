A *hortus siccus* &mdash; a dry garden &mdash; is a collection of plants pressed flat, dried, and fixed to sheets so that a season's growth might be studied long after it has withered in the field. This register keeps the count.

We work in the manner of the nineteenth-century field naturalist: collect sparingly, label faithfully, and let the specimen speak before the theory does. Every sheet carries a typed label, a catalog number, and the hand of whoever gathered it.

## Method of the cabinet

1. **Collection.** A single representative specimen is taken, with root, leaf, and where possible flower and fruit. Locality and date are noted on the spot.
2. **Pressing.** The plant is laid between blotting sheets under even weight and dried slowly to preserve form and colour.
3. **Mounting.** The dried specimen is fixed to acid-free rag card, the typed label affixed to the lower corner, and the sheet filed by family.

> A specimen without its label is a leaf without a name &mdash; pleasant to look upon, useless to science.

## Materials

- **Paper:** unbleached rag stock, neutral pH, gathered in folios of twenty-five.
- **Ink:** iron-gall for labels, archival carbon for catalog numbers.
- **Cabinet:** sealed wooden presses kept dark, cool, and dry against mould and beetle.

The figures on the register are revised at each inspection and compiled into a static record, so the cabinet's state on any given day can be read at a glance.
