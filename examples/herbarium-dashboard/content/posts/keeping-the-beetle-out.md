Every collector learns the same hard lesson: the enemy is not damp alone but the small brown beetle that thrives in it. *Stegobium* and its kin will reduce a mounted fern to dust and frass while the cabinet sits closed and unsuspected.

## The three conditions

A dried specimen has no defence of its own. The cabinet must supply it.

- **Cold.** A spell of deep freezing kills eggs and larvae alike. New accessions go into cold storage for a week before filing.
- **Dark.** Light fades pigment and dries adhesive to brittleness. The cabinet stays shut.
- **Dry.** Held near forty-eight percent relative humidity, mould cannot establish and the beetle's young cannot feed.

## A simple inspection rhythm

I open the cabinet on a fixed schedule and look for frass, the fine powder that betrays an active infestation:

```
Monthly   open folios, look for frass
Quarterly freeze any incoming exchange material
Annually  full census, re-mount loose sheets
```

> A clean cabinet is a quiet one. If you hear nothing and see no dust, the beetle is not there.

This season's census turned up two affected sheets, both isolated and frozen before the damage could spread. The remaining eleven hundred are sound.
