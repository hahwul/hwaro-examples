The spring ephemerals give the collector no second chance. *Anemone nemorosa* opens, closes, and is gone before the canopy leafs out; by the time you have walked back to the cabinet, the petals have already begun to brown.

## What the field bag must hold

The secret is to press in the field, not at home. I carry a light frame of two boards and a strap, with blotting paper cut to half-sheet size.

- Blotting paper, twenty sheets, kept perfectly dry.
- A single sharp knife for clean cuts at the root.
- Pencil and pre-numbered labels; ink runs in damp woodland air.

A specimen laid flat within the first hour keeps its form. One carried loose in a vasculum for an afternoon will press as a tangle.

## On the matter of colour

White flowers betray you most. *Anemone* and wood-sorrel both bruise to a translucent grey if pressed warm. Change the blotters morning and evening for the first three days:

```
Day 0  press in field, full weight
Day 1  change blotters AM + PM
Day 2  change blotters AM + PM
Day 3  reduce to once daily
Day 7  inspect; mount if bone-dry
```

> Dry slow and dry cold. Heat fixes the green but kills the blue.

Filed eleven sheets this week; two of the *Pulsatilla* are circulated for a second opinion before they earn a catalog number.
