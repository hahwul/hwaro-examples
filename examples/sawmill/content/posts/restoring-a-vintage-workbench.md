## Planning

This workbench came from a school woodshop that was closing down. It had seen decades of student use, and it showed: the top was covered in gouges, saw marks, and dried glue. The front vise was seized. The tail vise was missing its screw entirely. One leg had a crack running six inches from the foot. But underneath the damage, the bench was built from solid hard maple, and the joinery -- heavy mortise and tenon throughout -- was still fundamentally sound.

The goal of this restoration was not to make the bench look new. It was to make it work again. A workbench is a tool, not a showpiece. The surface needs to be flat. The vises need to function. The structure needs to be rigid. Everything else is secondary.

### Assessment

Before any work began, a thorough assessment documented every issue:

- Top surface: deeply gouged, out of flat by approximately 3/16 inch over the full length
- Front vise: jaw mechanism frozen, wooden jaw cracked
- Tail vise: missing screw and guide rods, wooden jaw intact
- Left rear leg: crack from foot to lower stretcher mortise
- All stretcher joints: slightly loose from years of racking
- Finish: multiple layers of varnish, paint, and unidentified coatings
- Dog holes: clogged with debris, several with chipped edges

### Materials for Restoration

- Hide glue
- Epoxy (for the leg repair)
- Replacement vise screw and guide rods
- Hard maple stock for the new vise jaw
- Boiled linseed oil
- Paste wax
- 80, 120, and 180 grit sandpaper

## Cutting

### Flattening the Top

This is the most labor-intensive part of the restoration. The bench top was removed from the base (it was attached with bolts from below) and placed on sawhorses.

A No. 7 jointer plane was used to traverse the top diagonally in both directions, working from the high spots toward the low areas. The first pass removed the worst of the gouges and brought the surface within 1/16 inch of flat. A second pass, working with the grain, brought it to true.

The edges were squared with the same plane, and the dog holes were cleaned out with a drill press and chisels.

After planing, the top was sanded to 180 grit. Finer grits are unnecessary on a workbench top -- it needs to grip the work, not be polished smooth.

### Preparing the New Vise Jaw

The original front vise jaw was cracked through its full width. A new jaw was milled from a piece of 8/4 hard maple, dimensioned to match the original. The screw hole and guide rod holes were drilled to match the existing hardware.

The tail vise required a new screw mechanism. A replacement bench vise screw was sourced from a specialty woodworking supplier. The mounting holes in the bench end cap were re-drilled to accept the new hardware.

## Assembly

### Structural Repairs

The cracked leg was repaired with epoxy injected into the crack under clamp pressure. Epoxy was chosen over wood glue for this repair because the crack could not be fully opened and cleaned -- the leg needed to stay in place to maintain the bench's geometry. Epoxy fills gaps and bonds well to contaminated surfaces where traditional wood glue would fail.

After the leg repair cured, all stretcher joints were disassembled one at a time. Each tenon was cleaned of old glue residue, and fresh hide glue was applied before reassembly. Working one joint at a time maintained the bench's overall alignment while allowing each joint to be properly clamped and set.

### Vise Installation

The front vise was reassembled with the new maple jaw. The screw mechanism was cleaned, lubricated with paste wax, and tested through its full range of motion. The jaw faces were checked for parallel -- any misalignment here means the vise will not grip work evenly.

The tail vise was fitted with its new screw and guide rods. This required careful alignment to ensure the dog in the vise jaw lined up with the dog holes in the bench top. Several test assemblies were needed before the final fit was right.

### Reassembly

The top was reattached to the base with new bolts. A strip of leather was placed between the top and the base frame to dampen vibration during heavy planing and chopping.

## Finish

A workbench finish needs to be simple, renewable, and functional. Three coats of boiled linseed oil were applied to all surfaces, with a day between coats. The oil soaks into the maple, providing moderate moisture resistance and making glue squeeze-out easier to clean up later.

After the final coat of oil dried, a thin layer of paste wax was rubbed into the top surface and the vise jaws. The wax reduces friction, allowing workpieces to slide into position easily, and provides an additional layer of moisture protection.

The underside of the top and the base components received oil only -- wax on these surfaces is unnecessary and would attract sawdust.

## Notes on Workbench Restoration

A workbench accumulates the marks of its use, and that history has value. The goal is not perfection but function. A flat top, working vises, and a rigid structure are what matter. The old saw marks, the faded ink stains, the slight rounding of edges from years of hands sliding across them -- these are marks of a tool that has been well used, and they deserve to remain.

This bench, rebuilt, will serve for decades more. It is heavier than a new bench of similar size, denser from years of oil absorption and compression. When a hand plane is pushed across its surface, it does not flex or chatter. That solidity is something no new bench provides on day one. It is earned through years of work, and preserving it was worth every hour of this restoration.
