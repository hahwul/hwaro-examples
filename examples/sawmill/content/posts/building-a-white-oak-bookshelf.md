## Planning

This project started with a simple need: a bookshelf that could hold a growing collection of woodworking references and technical manuals without sagging under the weight. The design calls for a floor-standing unit, 72 inches tall by 36 inches wide, with five fixed shelves and one adjustable shelf.

Quarter-sawn white oak was chosen for several reasons. It is exceptionally strong, resists warping due to the orientation of the grain, and displays the distinctive ray fleck pattern that makes white oak one of the most visually striking domestic hardwoods.

### Materials

- 40 board feet of 6/4 quarter-sawn white oak
- One sheet of 1/4-inch white oak plywood for the back panel
- Wood glue (Titebond III)
- Shelf pins (brass, 1/4-inch)
- Danish oil finish

### Tools

- Table saw with a crosscut sled
- Router with straight bit and edge guide
- Drill press with 1/4-inch brad point bit
- No. 5 hand plane
- Chisels (1/4-inch and 3/4-inch)
- Bar clamps (at least eight)
- Orbital sander

## Cutting

All boards were rough-milled to approximate dimension, then left to rest in the shop for three days. White oak has significant internal stress, and rushing this step invites warping after final dimensioning.

After resting, each board was jointed on one face and one edge, then planed to a final thickness of 7/8 inch. The cut list:

- 2 side panels: 72 x 11.5 x 7/8 inches
- 6 shelves: 34 x 11 x 7/8 inches
- 1 top cap: 37 x 12 x 7/8 inches
- 1 back panel: 71 x 35.5 x 1/4 inches (plywood)

Each shelf is slightly narrower than the sides to allow for a 1/4-inch plywood back to sit in a rabbet along the rear edges.

### Joinery Preparation

The five fixed shelves sit in 3/8-inch-deep dado grooves cut into the side panels. A router with a straight bit and a shop-made jig ensures consistent spacing and depth across both panels.

For the adjustable shelf, two columns of shelf pin holes were drilled into each side panel using the drill press and a spacing jig. Each hole is 1/4 inch in diameter and 3/8 inch deep, spaced 1.5 inches apart.

## Assembly

Dry-fit everything first. This is not optional. With six shelves and two side panels, there are twelve dado joints that all need to align simultaneously. Any error found during dry assembly is far easier to correct than after glue is applied.

Once the fit was confirmed, glue was applied to each dado and the shelves were inserted from bottom to top. Bar clamps held the assembly together while checking for square at every stage. A diagonal measurement confirmed the case was true before the glue set.

The back panel was glued and pinned into the rabbets with small brads. This panel is structural -- it prevents the bookshelf from racking under load.

The top cap was attached with pocket screws from underneath, hidden from view.

## Finish

After assembly, the entire piece was sanded through 220 grit. Special attention was given to the ray fleck surfaces, which can show sanding scratches if the grit progression is rushed.

Three coats of Danish oil were applied, with light sanding at 320 grit between coats. Danish oil penetrates the wood rather than sitting on the surface, which preserves the tactile quality of the oak while providing protection against moisture and handling.

The final result is a bookshelf that feels substantial and will hold weight without concern. Quarter-sawn white oak was the right choice here. Its stability and strength are matched by few other domestic species, and the visual character of the ray fleck pattern makes every board unique.

## Lessons Learned

The most valuable lesson from this build: let your wood acclimate. Two of my initial shelf blanks developed a slight cup after milling because the resting period was cut short. Taking the extra days to let the wood settle saves rework and frustration later.

Also, invest in good clamps. This project required eight bar clamps working simultaneously during the main glue-up. Trying to manage that with fewer clamps would have compromised the joints.
