There is a class of software that refuses to grow. These are tools that do one thing, do it well, and have no ambition to become a platform. A plain text editor. A single-purpose calculator. A notes app that stores files as Markdown on your local disk.

These tools share a philosophy: that constraint is a feature, not a limitation. They resist the gravitational pull of feature creep, the pressure to add collaboration, the temptation to require an account.

## The Platform Trap

Most software starts as a tool and ends as a platform. The text editor gains a plugin system, then a marketplace, then an AI assistant. The to-do app adds calendar integration, then team features, then a subscription tier. Each addition is individually reasonable. Collectively, they transform a sharp instrument into a blunt one.

The users who loved the original tool are now a minority. Their simple workflows are buried under menus designed for enterprise customers. The software has grown, but it has not improved — not for them.

## The Economics of Restraint

Building software that deliberately stays small is economically irrational by conventional metrics. Venture capital demands growth. Growth demands features. Features demand complexity. Complexity demands support staff, which demands revenue, which demands more features.

The alternative is to build for sustainability rather than scale. Charge a fair price. Keep the team small. Say no to most feature requests. Accept that the user base will be modest but loyal.

## Craft Over Scale

The best slow software feels handmade. You can sense the opinions of its creators in every interaction. The keyboard shortcuts are thoughtful. The defaults are sensible. The documentation is written by someone who uses the product daily.

This is craft. It does not scale, and that is precisely the point.
