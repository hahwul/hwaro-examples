## Frame

The exposure was metered at 1/125 second, f/5.6, on Kodak Portra 400. The camera was an inherited Pentax K1000 with the 50mm f/1.7 SMC lens, focused at roughly 1.2 meters on the rim of a ceramic mug. The window faced east. The frame was made at 7:14 in the morning, before the sun cleared the neighbor's roofline, so the light entering the room was still indirect — a flat, blue-shifted ambient light reflected off the painted clapboard across the alley.

## Surface

Portra 400 handles this kind of cool ambient light well. The film's color science was designed for skin tones, but the same gentle tonal compression renders cream-colored mugs and unpolished wood with a similar restraint. Highlights fall softly. The window mullion on the right edge of the frame holds detail in both the painted white surface and the shadow it casts on the sill.

The negative was developed at a small lab using standard C-41 chemistry. Scanning was done at 3200 dpi on a flatbed scanner with the negative held flat by a glass carrier. No color correction was applied beyond the scanner's default profile for Portra 400.

## Notes

The grain is visible but quiet. At a 4-by-6 print size the grain disappears entirely; at 8-by-10 it becomes a faint texture that the eye reads as fabric or paper rather than as film artifact. This is one reason 400-speed color negative film remains a working choice for domestic photography: it produces prints that match the perceptual softness of the rooms they were made in.

The print is mounted on the third page of the spring 2026 album, opposite a contact sheet from the same morning. The contact sheet shows seventeen frames; this is the eleventh exposure on the roll.
