## Paper

The paper used for an archival photo album must meet two competing requirements. It must be soft enough to handle without abrasion against the print surface, and it must be chemically inert enough to avoid transferring acids or sulfur compounds to the silver image. Standard cardstock fails both tests. Most decorative scrapbook papers contain lignin, optical brighteners, or buffering compounds that interact with photographic emulsions over decades.

The album under discussion uses 130 gsm acid-free paper, buffered with calcium carbonate to a pH of 8.5, and tested to ISO 18902 for photographic compatibility. The surface is uncoated and matte. Prints are mounted with photo corners rather than adhesives, so the print itself never contacts the paper directly.

## Binding

The binding is a screw-post binding. Three brass posts pass through punched holes along the spine of the album, allowing pages to be added or removed without disturbing the existing sequence. The brass is uncoated; over time it develops a patina that does not transfer to adjacent pages. Plastic-coated bindings were considered and rejected: the plastic outgasses slowly and contributes to fade in adjacent silver-gelatin prints.

The covers are bookcloth over 2.5 millimeter binder's board. The cloth is mercerized cotton, dyed with reactive dyes that do not bleed under humidity. The endpapers are the same buffered stock as the interior pages, glued to the boards with PVA adhesive certified for archival use.

## Sleeves and Storage

Some pages use polyester sleeves to hold prints that are not yet selected for permanent mounting. The polyester is uncoated Mylar D, 2 mil thickness. It is chemically stable, transparent, and does not interact with silver emulsions. Polyvinyl chloride sleeves were specifically excluded; PVC outgasses hydrochloric acid as it ages and is the most common cause of preventable damage to amateur photographic collections.

The album is stored horizontally on a shelf at room temperature, away from direct sunlight, in a cabinet with passive ventilation. The relative humidity in the cabinet is monitored and held between 35 and 50 percent. These conditions are not ideal — a true archival environment runs cooler — but they are sustainable in a domestic setting and are sufficient to extend the life of the prints by several human generations.
