# The Frontispiece

<p class="lede">A frontispiece is the illustration that faces the title page. In older books it carried a portrait of the author, an allegorical scene, or an engraving that announced the book's subject before any words were read.</p>

## Origin

<p>The frontispiece is older than the printed book. Manuscript codices placed presentation miniatures opposite the opening text, and early printers carried the convention forward, commissioning engravings that could be tipped in or printed on a separate sheet. By the seventeenth century, a portrait frontispiece was standard for serious works, and the pairing of image and title page became a recognizable opening gesture.</p>

## Form

<p>The frontispiece is a recto image facing a verso title, or, in modern practice, a verso image facing a recto title. The two leaves are read together: the image gives the book a face, and the title gives the face a name. A good frontispiece prepares the reader without explaining; it sets a mood, not an argument.</p>

<blockquote>The frontispiece is the threshold between cover and contents -- a picture that asks to be looked at before the reading begins.</blockquote>
