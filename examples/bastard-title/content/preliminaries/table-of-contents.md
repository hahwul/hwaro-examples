# The Table of Contents

<p class="lede">The table of contents is the map a reader consults before deciding where to begin. It lists the parts, chapters, and sections of the book in the order they appear, with a page number for each entry. Its purpose is navigation, but its form is also a quiet portrait of the book's argument.</p>

## Structure

<p>A contents page mirrors the hierarchy of the text. Parts are set in the largest size, chapters below them, and subsections, if any, below those. Page numbers align to the right margin, separated from the titles by leaders -- dotted, dashed, or, in the most refined editions, simple white space. The reader's eye moves from title to number along a horizontal rule that the typographer has tried to make invisible.</p>

## Practice

<p>Contents pages are set after the rest of the book has been paginated, which is why they are often among the last leaves to be typeset. A long contents -- five pages, ten pages -- can become a small composition in itself, with running heads, part dividers, and decorative rules. Short contents disappear into the front matter, doing their work without drawing attention.</p>

<blockquote>The contents page is the only place in a book where the whole text is visible at once -- a small, ordered diagram of everything that follows.</blockquote>
