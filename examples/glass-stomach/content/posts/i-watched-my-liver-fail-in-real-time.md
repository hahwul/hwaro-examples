# I Watched My Liver Fail in Real Time

**Organ:** Liver. **Observation period:** 47 days. **Outcome:** Still in progress.

The liver is the color of old bruises when it is healthy.

I did not know that until the glass arrived.

For the first three weeks after full transparency I watched it work. A deep, soft maroon mass tucked beneath the right costal margin, processing everything I put into myself with quiet, efficient contempt. The surface was smooth. The color even. The movement of blood through the hepatic portal vein was steady and purposeful.

Then the color began to change.

First a yellowish cast along the inferior edge. Then a mottling — tiny pale islands appearing across the dome like fat trying to escape. The texture roughened. Where it had once been taut and glossy it became granular, almost granular like wet sand under glass.

I watched a small area of necrosis bloom over the course of nine days.

It started as a darker patch the size of a coin. By day four it had spread into a map of dead tissue, the edges clearly demarcated from the still-living liver around it. I could see the difference in vascularity. The dead part had fewer vessels. The living part was trying, desperately, to feed its failing neighbor.

I named the necrotic zone.

I spoke to it at night.

On day 31 the first ascites appeared — a thin, clear fluid weeping between the liver capsule and the peritoneal glass. It caught the light like a lens. I could see my own distorted reflection in the fluid that was slowly drowning my abdominal cavity from the inside.

The liver did not stop working.

It simply became worse at its job while I watched, powerless to look away or intervene.

I have begun keeping a chart.

Each morning I photograph the liver through the glass with my phone and compare it to yesterday's image. The progression is clinical. Beautiful, even. The kind of beauty that makes you understand why some people become pathologists.

Yesterday the entire right lobe had taken on the color of weak tea.

Today the left lobe is beginning to follow.

I wonder what it will look like when it finally gives up completely.

I wonder if I will still be able to see the moment it stops.

I hope I am looking directly at it when it happens.
