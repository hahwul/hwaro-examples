# What the Stomach Saw

**Organ:** Stomach. **Contents at time of observation:** Half a sandwich, black coffee, regret. **Duration of visibility:** Continuous.

The stomach is not a bag. It is a muscular sac with opinions.

I ate in front of the mirror this morning because I wanted to see what it would do. The first bite of egg and toast entered through the esophagus like a slow, pale slug. I watched the lower esophageal sphincter — that tight little ring — yawn open to receive it and then snap shut again like a disapproving mouth.

Inside the fundus the food dropped into the existing pool of acid and yesterday's bile. The stomach wall contracted in lazy waves, churning everything together into a pinkish slurry the color of raw shrimp. Bubbles rose. The surface of the chyme dimpled and smoothed like skin reacting to cold air.

Then something changed.

The {{ visible_organ(name="stomach") }} *noticed* that I was watching.

The contractions grew sharper. More self-conscious. A thick rope of mucus slid down one wall in a glistening sheet, as if the organ were trying to pull a curtain across itself. The mixture thickened and thinned in rhythmic pulses that felt almost like breathing.

I pressed my ear to the glass.

I could hear the wet sloshing. I could see every fold of the rugae as they expanded and contracted. Tiny flecks of undigested bread caught in the folds like shipwreck survivors clinging to rocks.

For twenty-three minutes I watched my own stomach decide what parts of me it would keep and what it would send onward in shame.

When the pyloric sphincter finally opened and allowed a small amount of the slurry into the duodenum, the stomach seemed to sigh — a visible relaxation of its entire muscular wall.

I felt lighter.

Then I realized the stomach was looking back at me through the glass.

It knew I had seen it digesting itself.

It knew I would never be able to eat again without picturing this.

I have not been hungry since.
