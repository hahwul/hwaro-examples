## About This Logbook

Unroll a paper chart on a wide table and the first thing you notice is that it is *busy*. Numbers everywhere. Thin lines fanning out from a flower-shaped star. A scale of latitude marching up the margin in even ticks. Tiny italic figures scattered across the blue like fallen confetti. None of it is decoration. Every mark is an instruction, a warning, or a measurement paid for in someone's time at sea.

**Nautical Chart** is a logbook about that language — the visual grammar of the admiralty sea chart, the instruments that filled it, and the quiet judgement calls a cartographer makes when the round Earth refuses to lie flat.

---

## About the Cartographer

I am *the cartographer*: a former hydrographic surveyor who now keeps the lead line coiled in a drawer and writes instead. I spent enough seasons swinging a sounding lead and squinting at a sextant to develop a deep respect for the people who turned all that raw measurement into something a stranger could trust at three in the morning in a rising sea.

I write about:

- **Depth soundings** — how charted depths are measured, reduced, and printed, and why the datum matters more than the number
- **The compass rose and the rhumb line** — loxodromes, magnetic variation, and why old charts radiate those elegant fans of line
- **Projection and distortion** — what every flat map gets wrong on purpose, and why the lie is a kindness
- **Chart design as craft** — type, hierarchy, and restraint on a sheet that may have to save a life

> "A chart is a promise written in ink: *here is what we found, this is where we stood, and this is how much we are willing to swear to.*"
