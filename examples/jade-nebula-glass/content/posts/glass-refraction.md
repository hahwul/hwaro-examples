Frosted glass is not a uniform diffuser. It is a stack of microscopic surfaces, each one refracting incident light at a slightly different angle. The cumulative effect is to spread a point source into a soft disk, but the spreading is anisotropic and its details depend on how the glass was finished.

## Manufacturing

Ground glass is produced by abrading a polished surface with progressively finer grit until the residual roughness is on the order of the wavelength of visible light. At that scale, the surface acts as a phase grating with a continuous distribution of slopes. Light striking the grating is redirected, but because the slopes are random, the redirection is incoherent and the transmitted beam loses its directional character without losing its spectrum.

Acid-etched glass produces a similar result by chemical means. The etch attacks the surface non-uniformly, leaving pits whose depth follows the same distribution that grinding would produce mechanically. Etched glass is more durable than ground glass because the pit walls are rounded rather than fractured.

## Optical Behavior

A layer of frosted glass placed in front of a directional source converts that source into an extended one. The size of the extended source is determined by the distance between the original source and the glass and by the angular spread of the diffuser. Two layers of frosted glass spaced by several centimeters produce a more uniform output than a single layer of equivalent total thickness, because the second layer averages over the residual structure of the first.

This is the principle behind the diffuser stacks used in scientific photography and in the soft lighting of architectural interiors. The cost is transmission. Each layer absorbs a few percent of the incident light, and a stack designed for high uniformity may pass only a fraction of the original flux. The trade-off between uniformity and transmission is fundamental and cannot be evaded by any choice of materials.
