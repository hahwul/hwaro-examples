# Spring Auction Season: What to Watch

The spring auction season opens next month with major sales at all three leading houses. After a period of recalibration in the contemporary market, early consignment announcements suggest renewed confidence -- particularly in post-war European sculpture and old master drawings.

## Key Lots

Several exceptional pieces have already been confirmed for the March and April sales:

**A previously unrecorded Bernini terracotta study** will headline the European sculpture sale. The piece, a preparatory model for a fountain figure, has been in private hands since the eighteenth century and comes with extensive provenance documentation. Estimates are being kept deliberately conservative at 800,000 to 1.2 million, though informed observers expect significantly higher results.

**A collection of twelve Henry Moore maquettes** from a single-owner estate represents a rare opportunity to acquire working models that illuminate the sculptor's creative process. Offered as individual lots, the group is estimated at a combined 2 to 3 million.

**A pair of Roman marble portrait busts**, dated to the Antonine period, will test continued appetite for antiquities in a market increasingly sensitive to provenance questions. Both pieces have documented histories extending back to an eighteenth-century Grand Tour collection.

## Market Trends

Several patterns are worth tracking this season:

The market for post-war Italian sculpture remains robust, with Fontana, Manzoni, and Pomodoro consistently achieving strong results. However, there are signs that younger collectors are beginning to look at overlooked mid-century figures -- particularly women sculptors whose work was undervalued during their lifetimes.

Stone sculpture generally has shown steady appreciation over the past five years, outperforming bronze in percentage terms. This may reflect a growing collector preference for unique works over editioned multiples.

## Advice for Collectors

For those considering entering the market this season, the single-owner estate sales traditionally offer the best opportunities. Provenance is clean, estimates tend to be realistic, and the competitive dynamics are often more favorable than in curated evening sales where trophy-hunting can push prices beyond rational levels.

As always, condition matters more than name recognition. A pristine work by a secondary figure will often prove a sounder investment than a damaged or restored piece by a major name.
