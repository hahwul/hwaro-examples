## No Spine, No Gutter

The defining feature of the concertina is what it lacks: a spine. A codex must have a
spine because it is made of folded gatherings sewn together at one edge; without the
spine, the gatherings fall apart. A concertina is a single strip. Its structural integrity
is held in the paper itself, not in a binding.

## What Is Gained

- **Continuous reading surface.** A diagram, image, or line of text may cross a panel boundary without interruption.
- **Dual reading modes.** The book may be read panel by panel or opened out all at once.
- **No registration constraint.** Because panels are not stitched together, there is no issue of binding pulling content into a gutter.
- **Collapsibility.** The object compresses to the volume of a single panel for storage or transport.

## What Is Lost

- **Random access.** A concertina is harder to flip through than a codex; the reader must unfold to reach a specific panel.
- **Durability at the fold.** Repeated opening and closing wears the creases; a well-used concertina eventually reveals soft lines along the valleys.
- **Index marking.** Without a spine, there is nowhere to put a title or shelf-mark; concertinas are usually kept in boxes or slipcases.

## The History of the Form

The concertina form has independent origins in several bookmaking traditions &#8212; the
Japanese *orihon*, the Chinese *jingzhe zhuang*, the Western accordion map, the
nineteenth-century photographic album. In each case the form was chosen for a particular
reason: to keep a long text continuous, to show a single long image, to pack a large
surface into a small volume. Our publication draws chiefly on the Japanese tradition,
which seems to us the most refined.
