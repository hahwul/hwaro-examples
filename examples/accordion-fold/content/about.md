## The Paper

Each concertina is printed on long-grain Kozo, a white handmade paper pulled in sheets of
three metres by nineteen centimetres. The paper is selected for its long fibres, which take
a fold without breaking and hold their alternation even after being opened and closed many times.

## The Sequence of Folds

A single sheet is marked into eight equal panels and creased seven times at the division
points. The creases alternate: mountain, valley, mountain, valley, mountain, valley,
mountain. When collapsed, the sheet occupies the volume of a small book; when extended, it
stretches across a length of wall.

## Printing

Each panel is printed in two passes: a first run in black for body text and a second run in
a single accent colour (this number: tile red) for diagrams and panel numerals. The
registration is hand-checked on a glass table, because misalignment between the two passes
would be uncorrectable once the concertina is folded.

## The Reading

A concertina may be read in two modes. The *panel mode* resembles the reading of a
conventional book: one panel at a time, front to back, turning a new face into the light at
each pause. The *spread mode* opens the entire strip against a wall, inviting the reader to
walk along its length and take in the whole at once. Most readers oscillate between the two.

## On the Choice of Form

The concertina was chosen not as an affectation but for what it permits: a single image,
idea, or argument to extend across multiple panels without the interruption of a binding.
There is no spine, no gutter, no moment where the text must jump across a fold it cannot
cross. The form suits long-form essays on single subjects; it suits sequences of images
that wish to be understood as one. It does not suit reference works.
