We borrowed the shape of this page from a mission profile because it is the most honest way we know to describe the work: a project moves through distinct, named phases, each with a clear exit condition, and nobody moves to the next phase until the current one is done.

1. **Pre-flight.** Two weeks of audit and interviews before a single pixel moves. We read your existing materials, sit in on two engineering meetings, and talk to whoever answers your support inbox. We leave with a written brief you sign off on.
2. **Trajectory.** A short concepting phase — usually one week — where we test two or three directions against the brief, not against our own taste. You see rough work early and often; nothing arrives as a surprise reveal.
3. **Burn.** The system gets built, in the open, in weekly working sessions. This is the longest phase and the one where most of the real decisions get made: type scale, component anatomy, documentation structure.
4. **MECO.** Main engine cutoff — the point where the core system is feature-complete and we shift from building to refining. We run the guideline against real content from your team, not placeholder copy.
5. **Separation.** We hand off source files, a written guideline, and office hours for your team to ask questions without us in the room. Nothing ships in a format only we can open.
6. **Telemetry.** A check-in at thirty and ninety days after handoff. Systems drift once real teams start using them; we would rather hear about it early than find out a year later that nobody followed the guide.

Most engagements run twelve to eighteen weeks end to end, depending on how much of the pre-flight audit is already done when we arrive. If that timeline works for you, [start a project](/contact/), or read the [services](/services/) each phase typically covers.
