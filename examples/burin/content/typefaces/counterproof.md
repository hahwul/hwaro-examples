A counterproof is a mirror-pulled impression taken directly off a freshly inked plate, made to check the engraving before it goes to press, and mine came from a conservator who had three worn bookplate copper sheets that had each been re-engraved and reused for a different family's crest at least once. Where most revivals pick the cleanest surviving pull, Counterproof deliberately works from the counterproofs instead, because they show the plate's true state — ink pooling in re-cut grooves, faint ghost lines from the erased earlier commission still sitting under the newer engraving.

<!-- more -->

That's an unusual thing to want in a typeface, and I want to be honest about why I kept it: the wear reads as texture, not damage, once you're setting text rather than looking at a single letter, and it gives Counterproof a slightly unsteady, handwritten warmth that a clean revival of the same source wouldn't have.

## Source

The three plates came from the same small engraving workshop, identity unrecorded, operating somewhere between roughly 1780 and 1810 based on the costume details in the crests. Copper plates were routinely re-engraved for a new client once the original family's copies had all been printed, which is why ghost lines from an earlier, erased commission are visible under raking light on all three sheets I worked from.

## Building the italic from three hands

Because the three plates likely represent the same workshop across a couple of decades rather than one engraver's single sitting, I treated Counterproof as a composite italic rather than a strict revival of any one plate, choosing whichever version of each letter showed the clearest, least-damaged cut across the three sources. The slant sits at eleven degrees, matching the most consistent measurement across all three, and the connecting strokes keep a slight forward drag that shows up whenever the counterproof caught ink still wet from the previous letter.

## Where it wants to be used

Counterproof is built to sit beside [Plate Mark](/typefaces/plate-mark/) as an emphasis italic — pull quotes, captions, and the occasional aside — rather than to carry long italic passages on its own. The wear texture that makes it interesting in short bursts gets tiring across a full paragraph.

```css
em, .aside {
  font-family: "Counterproof", Georgia, serif;
  font-style: italic;
}
```
