In the early nineteenth century, New York City was facing a dire water crisis. The local wells were contaminated, fires frequently ravaged wood-frame neighborhoods, and cholera outbreaks were common. The solution was the Old Croton Aqueduct, a monumental feat of hydraulic engineering completed in 1842. This gravity-fed system transported fresh water from the Croton River in northern Westchester County over forty miles down to Manhattan. The water crossed the Harlem River via the High Bridge, which still stands as the city's oldest bridge.

<!-- more -->

The aqueduct terminated at two massive masonry reservoirs: the receiving reservoir in what is now Central Park, and the distributing reservoir on Fifth Avenue, where the New York Public Library now stands. Built in an Egyptian Revival style with heavy granite walls, the distributing reservoir was a popular promenade for citizens seeking fresh air. The Croton system transformed New York from a disease-prone seaport into a sanitary, modern metropolis, paving the way for the vertical expansion of Manhattan.
