In 1811, a three-man commission appointed by the New York State Legislature published a map that would forever define the spatial experience of Manhattan. The Commissioners' Plan of 1811 imposed a rigid, orthogonal grid of 12 numbered avenues and 155 orthogonal cross-streets onto the island's rolling terrain. Prior to the plan, Manhattan was a landscape of rocky hills, salt marshes, and winding agrarian lanes. The commissioners, seeking efficiency and ease of real estate development, chose to level the topography in favor of uniform rectangular blocks.

<!-- more -->

The decision was highly controversial at the time. Critics lamented the loss of natural beauty and the destruction of existing farms. Yet, the grid proved to be a remarkably resilient framework for growth, allowing the city to absorb millions of new residents and adapt to changing technologies. Today, the grid creates the famous Manhattanhenge phenomenon, where the setting sun aligns perfectly with the cross-streets. It is a monument to rational planning that turned a wild island into the ultimate machine for commerce.
