The neighborhood of Soho contains the largest concentration of cast-iron architecture in the world. In the mid-to-late nineteenth century, the area was a bustling commercial district dominated by textile mills and warehouses. Cast iron emerged as a revolutionary building material that allowed for rapid, inexpensive construction. Unlike traditional load-bearing brick walls, which required thick masonry and small windows, cast-iron frames were incredibly strong and could support large expanses of plate glass to illuminate showroom floors.

<!-- more -->

Moreover, the facades were prefabricated in local foundries like Badger's Architectural Iron Works and bolted onto the fronts of buildings. Architects could choose elaborate Italianate or French Renaissance designs from a catalog, creating the illusion of hand-carved stone at a fraction of the cost. Although originally intended for industrial warehouses, these cast-iron buildings were eventually rediscovered by artists in the 1970s. Their high ceilings and natural light made them perfect lofts, initiating the neighborhood's transition into a premier cultural district.
