To understand the original shape of Manhattan, one must look at the street names of the Financial District. Water Street, Front Street, and Old Slip were once exactly what their names imply: the edge of the East River. During the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the Dutch and British colonial administrations expanded the island's southern tip by systematically dumping landfill into the harbor. Merchants who owned waterfront property were granted rights to 'make land' by filling in the slips where ships docked, pushing the shoreline further out to create new real estate.

<!-- more -->

Old Slip, located near the foot of William Street, was originally a canal where small merchant vessels could dock to unload goods. As shipping vessels grew larger and required deeper berths, the slip was filled in during the 1830s. Today, it is a wide, paved plaza dominated by the historic Neo-Renaissance police station designed by Hunt & Hunt. The buried remnants of old ships and colonial timber docks still lie beneath the asphalt, serving as a silent reminder of the city's constant territorial expansion.
