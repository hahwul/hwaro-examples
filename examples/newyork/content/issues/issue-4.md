Before 1916, skyscrapers in Manhattan were built straight up from their lot lines, threatening to turn the city's narrow streets into dark, sunless canyons. The construction of the massive Equitable Building in Lower Manhattan, which cast a continuous seven-acre shadow over neighboring properties, finally prompted action. The result was the 1916 Zoning Resolution, the first comprehensive zoning code in the United States. This innovative law sought to preserve light and air at street level by introducing the sky exposure plane.

<!-- more -->

Under the new regulations, buildings could rise vertically only to a certain height, after which they had to setback from the street as they went higher. This created the iconic wedding-cake silhouette that defines Manhattan's mid-century skyline. Architects like Ralph Walker and Hugh Ferriss turned these mathematical zoning envelopes into an art form, designing dramatic Art Deco towers that stepped back like ancient pyramids. The law shaped the corporate monuments of Midtown, proving that the geometry of city regulations can be just as influential as structural engineering.
