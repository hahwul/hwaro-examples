The deposition of the last Western Roman Emperor, Romulus Augustulus, by the Germanic chieftain Odoacer is traditionally cited as the end of the Western Roman Empire. This event marks a conventional boundary between antiquity and the medieval period.

<!-- more -->

The "fall" of Rome was less a sudden collapse than a gradual transformation. For centuries, the empire had been adapting to pressures both internal and external: economic strain, political fragmentation, and the movement of peoples across its borders. What emerged was not a void but a new political landscape of successor kingdoms that blended Roman, Christian, and Germanic traditions into the foundations of medieval Europe.
