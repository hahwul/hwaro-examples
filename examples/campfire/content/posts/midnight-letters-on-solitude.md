What follows is an exchange of letters between Maren Cole, a novelist living in Oslo, and Sam Redfield, a park ranger stationed in the Boundary Waters Canoe Area in northern Minnesota. They have never met in person.

<!-- more -->

---

**Maren Cole, Oslo**
*February 3, 2026*

Sam,

I have been thinking about your last letter, where you described the sound of the lake freezing. You wrote that it groans. I have lived near water my whole life and never heard that. It makes me wonder how much of the world I have missed by always being around other people.

I write novels about isolation, which is ironic because I have rarely been truly alone. Oslo is a small city, but it is still a city. There is always someone in the next room, on the next tram, at the next table. My characters spend months alone in cabins and on boats and in forests, and I write their solitude from the safety of a coffee shop. Does that make me a fraud?

I am asking sincerely. You live the thing I only imagine.

---

**Sam Redfield, Boundary Waters**
*February 11, 2026*

Maren,

You are not a fraud. But I think you might be confusing solitude with silence, and they are not the same thing.

I spend weeks out here without seeing another person. The silence is real and it is heavy at first. But solitude is not the absence of company. It is the presence of yourself. That sounds like something you would find on a bumper sticker, but I mean it literally. When there is no one else around, you start to notice your own patterns. The way you talk to yourself. The things you reach for when you are bored. The thoughts that come at three in the morning when there is nothing to distract you from them.

The lake does groan when it freezes. It sounds like a giant turning over in sleep. The first winter I was here, the sound terrified me. Now it is company.

---

**Maren Cole, Oslo**
*February 19, 2026*

Sam,

"The presence of yourself." I am going to sit with that for a while.

I think the reason I write about isolation is that I am afraid of it. My characters do what I cannot. They face the emptiness and find something in it. In my last novel, a woman spends a winter alone on Svalbard and by the end she has rebuilt her understanding of who she is without the mirror of other people's expectations. I wrote that ending in a crowded bar.

But here is what I think fiction does that experience cannot: it lets you rehearse. I may not have heard the lake groan, but I have imagined it so completely that when you described it, I recognized it. That has to count for something.

---

**Sam Redfield, Boundary Waters**
*February 28, 2026*

Maren,

It counts for a great deal. I think imagination and experience are two languages for the same thing. You approach the truth through words. I approach it through weather and wood smoke and the sound of my own breathing in a cold tent.

The question is not whether your way is less real. The question is whether it is enough for you. And only you can answer that.

If you ever want to hear the lake, you are welcome here. Bring warm socks and a book. The nights are long.

---
