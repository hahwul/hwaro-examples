We brought together three woodworkers --- Jun Tanaka, a furniture maker based in Kyoto; Rosa Figueroa, who builds guitars in Oaxaca; and David Ashworth, a boat builder from Cornwall --- to talk about what the word "craft" means to them now.

<!-- more -->

**Campfire:** Let us start with the word itself. Has "craft" been ruined?

> **Jun Tanaka:** Not ruined. Diluted. When everything is called craft --- craft beer, craft marketing, craft content --- the word stops pointing at anything specific. But the thing it used to point at still exists. Wood still behaves the way it has always behaved. Joints still need to fit. The word may be confused, but the work is not.

> **Rosa Figueroa:** I think about this differently. In Mexico, what you call "craft" we often call work. My grandmother made tortillas by hand every morning for sixty years. Nobody called her a craftsperson. She was just a woman making food. The word "craft" sometimes says more about who is watching than who is working.

> **David Ashworth:** That is a fair point. In my trade, there is a useful distinction between someone who can follow a plan and someone who can look at a piece of oak and know what it wants to become. Both are skilled. But the second thing is harder to teach, and I think that is closer to what people mean when they say craft --- or what they should mean.

**Campfire:** Jun, you trained in the traditional Japanese apprentice system. How does that shape your view?

> **Jun Tanaka:** The apprenticeship lasted eight years. For the first two, I was not allowed to touch a tool. I swept floors, sharpened blades, and watched. In the West, this is sometimes romanticized, but the purpose was practical. You cannot use a plane properly until you can sharpen it properly. You cannot sharpen it properly until you understand the steel. Everything is connected. Craft is understanding the connections.

**Campfire:** Rosa, you build instruments that will outlive you. How do you think about that?

> **Rosa Figueroa:** Every guitar I make will sound different in twenty years than it does today. The wood opens up. The vibrations reshape the grain over time. So when I am building, I am not just making something for now. I am starting a conversation between the wood and whoever plays it, a conversation that will continue long after I am gone. That is a strange and beautiful responsibility.

**Campfire:** David, you work in a trade that is disappearing. Does that concern you?

> **David Ashworth:** There are fewer of us, yes. But the boats still need building, and fiberglass cannot do everything wood can. What concerns me more is the loss of the knowledge that does not live in books. How a particular species of timber behaves when it is been soaking in salt water for three months --- that knowledge only exists in the hands of people who have done it. When the last person who knows that thing dies, it is gone. That is what we should be worried about. Not the word "craft." The knowledge.

> **Rosa Figueroa:** This is why I teach. Not because I think every student will become a luthier, but because the understanding of materials --- real, physical materials --- changes how people think about everything. Once you know what it takes to make one good thing, you never look at the world the same way again.
