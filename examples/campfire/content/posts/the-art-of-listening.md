Elena Marsh has spent twenty years making radio documentaries. Her work has taken her from fishing villages in Newfoundland to night markets in Taipei, always with the same portable recorder and the same quiet disposition. We met at her studio in Brooklyn --- a converted closet, really --- to talk about the craft of listening.

<!-- more -->

**Campfire:** You have said before that your job is not to ask good questions. What do you mean by that?

> **Elena Marsh:** I mean that the question is almost never the interesting part. The interesting part is what happens in the three seconds after the question, when the person decides whether to give you the real answer or the safe one. My job is to make that gap feel safe enough for the real answer.

**Campfire:** How do you do that?

> **Elena Marsh:** Mostly by shutting up. People are terrified of silence. If you ask someone a question and they give you a surface-level answer, most interviewers will immediately jump in with a follow-up. But if you just wait --- if you let the silence sit there --- they will almost always go deeper. They cannot help it. The silence creates a kind of vacuum and people rush to fill it with truth.

**Campfire:** That sounds uncomfortable.

> **Elena Marsh:** It is. For both of us. But discomfort is where the interesting stuff lives. I once interviewed a retired firefighter about a rescue that went wrong. He gave me the official version three times. On the fourth silence, he told me what actually happened. That moment is in the documentary, and it is the reason people remember it.

**Campfire:** Do you prepare extensively before an interview?

> **Elena Marsh:** I research the person, but I do not write questions in advance. If I have a list of questions, I will be thinking about the next question instead of listening to what they are saying right now. The best follow-up question is always hiding inside the answer they just gave you. You just have to hear it.

**Campfire:** What is the most common mistake new interviewers make?

> **Elena Marsh:** They want to show the subject that they have done their homework. So they ask these elaborate, multi-part questions that are really just performances. The subject sits there waiting for the question to end, and by the time it does, the energy has left the room. Ask short questions. Then listen.

**Campfire:** Is there a question you always ask?

> **Elena Marsh:** I always end with the same one: "What did I not ask you that I should have?" About half the time, the answer is the best part of the conversation. People know what their own story is about better than you do. You just have to give them permission to tell it.
