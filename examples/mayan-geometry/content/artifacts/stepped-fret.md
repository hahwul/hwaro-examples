The stepped fret, sometimes called the xicalcoliuhqui, appears across more than a thousand years of Mesoamerican stone carving. It joins a right-angled spiral with a tiered stair, and it can be read as either rising or descending depending on its orientation. The motif occurs at Mitla, Uxmal, and on painted vessels recovered from the Maya highlands.

### Construction

The pattern resolves cleanly to a square grid. A typical band uses a six-by-six unit, with the spiral occupying four units and the stepped tail filling the remainder. Once the unit is established, masons can mirror or rotate it to fill any rectangular field.

- Spiral arm: four units, terminating in a single open square.
- Stepped tail: three risers, each one unit deep.
- Negative space: the surrounding ground is recessed by a uniform depth, exposing the carved relief.

### Symbolism

Scholars have proposed several readings. One links the spiral to wind or breath and the stair to the layered cosmos. Another sees it as a stylized serpent, with the spiral standing for the head and the stair for a coiled body. The lack of a written gloss from the period leaves the question open.

What is clear is that the motif was generative. Workshops produced it in stone, in stucco, and in painted slipware. The same proportions appear on a stone facade three meters wide and on a polychrome bowl that fits inside a hand. This portability across scale and material is one reason the stepped fret outlasted the cities that first carved it.
