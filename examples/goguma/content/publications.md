The following academic publications and research reports document Dr. Park's contributions to horticultural science, root architecture mapping, and dryland agricultural trials. These documents represent collaborations with researchers worldwide, aiming to bridge molecular genetics with practical farm conservation. Over the last decade, our team has gathered sample accessions from diverse ecological zones to study how distinct sweet potato cultivars adapt to stress environments.

Dr. Park's research methodology combines molecular genetics with longitudinal field observations. Each publication listed here focuses on a specific aspect of root crop biology, analyzing starch configurations, drought adaptability, or the genomic properties of heritage landraces. These papers serve as foundational material for researchers studying food security in semi-arid zones, where crop failure presents an immediate challenge to local livelihoods.

## Journal Articles & Research Papers

### 1. Genomic Mapping of Ipomoea batatas Landraces (2026)
*Published in the Journal of Arid Agronomy (Vol. 42, No. 3, pp. 201-215)*
This research documents the genomic sequencing of 14 heritage sweet potato landraces from East Asia, detailing specific alleles associated with drought resistance. The sequencing depth reveals highly conserved regions responsible for osmotic regulation in the root cortex, indicating that ancient farmers selected for these features over centuries of local cultivation.

### 2. Soil Horizons and Root Architecture in Volcanic Soil (2025)
*Published in Root Crop Quarterly (Vol. 19, No. 1, pp. 45-58)*
An analysis of how soil layering affects tuber development, demonstrating that compact clay horizons alter vascular structures. The study suggests that minimal-tillage methods preserve subsoil moisture and allow sweet potatoes to develop deeper, more uniform storage roots than conventional deep-tillage systems.
