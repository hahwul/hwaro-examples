Dr. Ji-Min Park is a principal investigator at the Institute for Arid Crop Conservation, where she leads the sweet potato genetics team. With over fifteen years of research experience, Dr. Park has dedicated her career to understanding the ecological and genetic factors that allow root vegetables, particularly heritage landraces of *Ipomoea batatas*, to thrive in hostile environments.

Dr. Park received her PhD in Plant Biology from Seoul National University, where she studied the enzymatic responses of root crops under acute drought stress. Following her doctorate, she completed a post-doctoral fellowship at the International Center for Tropical Agriculture, collaborating on projects to map the genetic diversity of sweet potatoes across East Asia and South America.

Her current projects focus on reconstructing historical soil profiles and understanding how root architecture adapts to varied soil horizons. By preservation of heritage cultivars, Dr. Park aims to secure genetic material that could prove vital for agricultural resilience under changing climate conditions. She regularly coordinates field trials with farming cooperatives to test crop performance under saline and arid conditions, ensuring that scientific discoveries translate directly to rural communities.

This dashboard serves as an active research record, documenting genetic accessions, publications, and real-time field trials conducted at our dryland agricultural test facilities. Through combining traditional cultivation knowledge with modern molecular mapping, the research seeks to preserve biodiversity and establish sustainable farming practices for future climates. Every entry in the field logs describes a specific cultivar trial, tracing how the plants adapt to soil variations and temperature shifts. By making these trials open and accessible, we hope to encourage collaboration among agro-ecologists and plant scientists globally.
