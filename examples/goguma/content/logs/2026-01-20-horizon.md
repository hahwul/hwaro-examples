In this winter greenhouse experiment, we observed how the root system of Haenam Golden (IB-2025-G04) changes as it traverses a multi-layered soil profile. We built transparent observation chambers filled with sand on top, a dense silt layer in the middle, and organic-rich clay at the bottom, reflecting natural dryland soil horizons.

<!-- more -->

As the primary roots transitioned from the loose sandy topsoil to the dense middle silt horizon, we noticed a sharp forty-five degree change in root growth angle. The root tips underwent mechanical thickening, increasing in diameter by twenty percent to penetrate the compacted silt layer. Feeder root branching was suppressed in the silt horizon but resumed aggressively once the clay layer was reached.

These observations show that sweet potato roots respond dynamically to mechanical impedance. Understanding this architectural plasticity helps us select cultivars that can successfully bypass compacted subsoils to reach deep nutrient reserves. This research will guide our crop recommendations for degraded soils that have suffered compaction from heavy machinery.
