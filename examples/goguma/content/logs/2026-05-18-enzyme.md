In late spring, we analyzed the activity of starch-synthesizing enzymes in Yeoju Purple tubers during a high-temperature stress period. Cultivation beds were subjected to ambient daytime temperatures reaching thirty-eight degrees Celsius for fourteen consecutive days, simulating early heat waves. Tuber tissue samples were collected every forty-eight hours for laboratory assays.

<!-- more -->

Our enzyme assays revealed significant differences between heritage cultivars and modern high-yield varieties. While modern commercial varieties experienced a severe drop in starch synthase activity at thirty-five degrees, Yeoju Purple maintained stable enzymatic conversion. This enzyme stability is correlated with the expression of heat-shock proteins that protect starch complexes from thermal denaturation.

Starch analysis of the treated tubers showed that amylose content remained stable at twenty-four percent, preserving the structural integrity and cooking quality of the root crop. These molecular findings help explain why heritage strains consistently survive heat waves that damage commercial monocultures. We plan to expand these assays to evaluate beta-amylase activity during post-harvest storage.
