I'm Noor Kessler, and Cerulean is a one-person studio for data visualization — mostly for newsroom climate desks, election teams, and the research groups that feed them numbers. I trained as a physical geographer, spent four years inside a metro newsroom's graphics desk, and now work as an outside specialist for teams that need a chart built to survive a deadline rather than a design review.

## What I actually deliver

Every engagement ends the same way: an SVG chart, hand-built from the underlying dataset, that lives directly in the page as markup — not a PNG export, not a screenshot of a spreadsheet chart, not an embed pointing at someone else's hosted widget. That means a reporter can change a headline number at 11 p.m. without waiting on a designer, and it means the chart still renders if the third-party charting library that made it goes down or changes its pricing. The six studies on the home page are drawn from real project figures; the axis ticks and data points you see there are the same ones printed in each write-up.

## How I work

Most projects start with a spreadsheet, a PDF appendix, or a government API that outputs something ugly. I clean the data, pick the smallest chart form that tells the truth about it — a line before a stacked area, a bar before a donut — and build it in SVG with plain, readable markup so a newsroom's own developers can maintain it after I leave. I annotate for the reader who arrives with no context: a callout on the number that matters, gridlines that make the scale legible at a glance, and axis labels that don't require a caption to parse.

## Standing engagements

I keep a short client list on purpose — usually two or three retainers at a time, plus one-off pieces around elections and major climate reports. Recent and ongoing work includes Boreal Dispatch's climate desk, The Meridian Times city desk, Halcyon Climate Lab's public briefings, Civic Signal's elections coverage, the Cascadia Report, and Ledger Quarterly's markets team.
