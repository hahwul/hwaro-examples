Every retrospective makes an argument through hanging before a single wall label gets read, and the Ashwell Museum's Vera Solheim retrospective makes an unusually blunt one: the four galleries devoted to her final decade are printed and framed at nearly twice the scale of the three galleries covering the first twenty years of her career. A market portrait from 1974 that Solheim herself printed no larger than nine by twelve inches now hangs, in a late-period companion piece from 1998, at thirty by forty. Nothing in the wall text explains the discrepancy, and I don't think the curators intended it as an argument at all — which is exactly the problem.

<!-- more -->

Solheim's early work rewards the intimate scale it was originally printed at. Her 1970s market and doorway pictures are built on close, almost private looking — you lean in, and the picture rewards the lean with detail that a casual glance would miss, a hand at the edge of frame, a reflection in a shop window that recontextualizes the whole image. Blown up to gallery scale, that intimacy becomes spectacle; you no longer lean in, because there is nothing left to find that a glance from across the room doesn't already deliver. The exhibition print, in other words, is not a neutral vehicle for a photographer's negative. It is an interpretive act, and this one interprets Solheim's early, quiet pictures as though they were minor sketches for the confident late work — a reading her own printing choices explicitly rejected.

The late work does hold the larger scale better, and here the show's instincts are sound rather than merely convenient: Solheim's final decade genuinely moved toward compositions built for the wall, deep architectural interiors with the kind of layered depth that rewards standing at a distance and letting the eye travel. Gallery six, devoted entirely to her unfinished cathedral series, is the strongest room in the museum this season, and it is strong specifically because the prints were sized for the pictures rather than the other way around. The failure is comparative, not absolute — it is the seven galleries read together, early rooms shrunk in effect by the scale of the late ones, that produces a distorted career arc in which Solheim appears to have been merely warming up until her final decade.

A retrospective's job is to let a career be read on its own terms across its full span, not to let its most recent, most marketable rooms retroactively diminish the rest. The Ashwell has the material and, in gallery six, the installation instincts to do this well. It should send someone back through galleries four and five with a tape measure and Solheim's own 1970s print sizes in hand, and reprint accordingly before the show travels.
