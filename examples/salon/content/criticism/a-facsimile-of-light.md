Every facsimile photobook makes a bet: that a good enough reproduction of an old print is more useful to more readers than the twelve surviving originals locked in three archives on two continents. Vellum Press's reissue of Halvard Coen's 1889 albumen album of the Trosne quarry workers is, on the terms of that bet, a genuine success — and a useful test case for how much an albumen print actually survives translation into modern tritone offset.

<!-- more -->

Albumen prints are defined partly by qualities a book cannot reproduce: a glossy, slightly warm surface sheen from the egg-white coating, a physical fragility and yellowing that dates the object as much as any caption could, a way of catching raking light in a room that a matte book page never will. Vellum's tritone approach — three passes of ink rather than the single black of standard offset — gets remarkably close on tonal range, which was the correct thing to prioritize; Coen's highlight detail in the quarry-face plates, easy to lose in a cheaper reproduction, survives here with the fine gradation intact. What is lost, and lost completely, is scale and surface. The originals were printed small, roughly cabinet-card size, meant to be held; the facsimile enlarges every plate to fill a page nearly four times that area, on stock with none of the albumen sheen. You are not looking at a smaller, better version of the original experience. You are looking at a different object that shares the tonal information and nothing else.

The book is honest about this, which is to its credit. An afterword by the reissue's editor addresses the surface and scale losses directly rather than letting the word "facsimile" do unearned work, and includes four color plates comparing an original print under raking light to the offset reproduction under flat scan light — a genuinely useful piece of visual argument that most facsimile reissues skip because it undercuts their own marketing. I wish more of this candor had made it into the main sequence; the plates themselves carry no indication of which pages reproduce prints in better or worse physical condition, a distinction the original archive catalogue makes explicit and that matters enormously for a body of work this dependent on subtle highlight survival.

Coen's quarry pictures deserve the wider readership this facsimile will bring them — the sequencing, largely unseen outside two university archives until now, turns out to be the real discovery, moving from wide quarry-face surveys into unexpectedly close, almost tender portraits of individual laborers on their meal breaks, a structure the original 1889 binding apparently obscured. *A Facsimile of Light* will not tell you what an albumen print feels like in the hand. It will tell you, more clearly than the scattered originals currently allow, what Coen was actually building across two hundred plates — and for most readers, that trade is the right one.
