*Contact Sheet No. 4*, the current show at the Konstantin Print Room, makes an argument so simple it took the medium a century to get around to trying it: hang the sheet, not the frame. Thirty-one contact sheets by the street photographer Reza Ambrus line the room, each one a grid of thirty-six exposures from a single roll, printed at actual contact size and left unmatted so the sprocket numbers and grease-pencil crop marks are visible under glass. Nowhere in the gallery is there an enlargement of the frame Ambrus eventually chose to print big. You have to find it yourself, buried in the grid, and once you do, the whole show becomes a lesson in how much of a photographer's reputation was built by an editing pencil rather than a shutter finger.

<!-- more -->

The pleasure of the show is comparative and slightly voyeuristic. Sheet fourteen documents a single afternoon on a pedestrian bridge: frames one through nine are Ambrus finding his distance from a woman in a yellow coat, frames ten through twenty-two are the yellow coat gone and Ambrus visibly restless, testing a fire escape, a dog, a reflection in a bus shelter that goes nowhere. Frame twenty-three is the published picture — the woman in the yellow coat has reappeared, now paired with a man in near-identical yellow forty feet behind her, a coincidence Ambrus could not have staged and had perhaps twelve seconds to recognize before it passed. Seeing the eleven false starts around it does not diminish frame twenty-three; it makes the twelve seconds legible as a decision rather than luck, which is a more interesting claim about the photographer than the single print alone could make.

Where the show is less successful is in its wall text, which leans hard on the word "honesty" — the contact sheet as confession, the unedited roll as the true self behind the curated image. This is sentimental and, worse, backwards. A contact sheet is not less constructed than a final print; it is differently constructed, a record of physical positioning and shutter timing rather than of darkroom choices, but a record all the same, and Ambrus is visibly performing for his own contact sheet as much as any street photographer performs for a frame. The interesting claim the show could make — that all photographic selection, sheet or print, is authorship, and the myth of the single decisive click was always a way of hiding the editing — gets buried under a much softer one about vulnerability.

Go for sheet fourteen and sheet twenty-two, the strongest pairings in the room, and skip the reading room at the back, where a looping slideshow "restages" several sheets as if the frames were being selected in real time. It is the one moment the show doesn't trust its own central image — the grid, printed flat, forcing your eye to do the editing Ambrus already did — and reaches instead for a gimmick a contact sheet render obsolete on its own. Bartholomew Ilic's essay on scale and curatorial choice in [*The Late Rooms*](/criticism/the-late-rooms/) makes a related case for how much authorship happens after the shutter closes.
