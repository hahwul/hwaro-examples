Platinum printing is often chosen for what it flatters — skin, cloud, the long grey scale that makes a landscape look eternal rather than merely photographed. Petra Lindvall's *Interior with Unmade Bed* uses the same process to do something closer to the opposite: it slows down looking at rooms that most photographs would consider beneath the process entirely. An unmade bed. A drying rack with two shirts on it. A kitchen counter mid-meal, onion skins not yet swept away. The platinum's extraordinary tonal range, usually spent on drama, here is spent on making a wrinkle in a duvet legible in eleven distinct greys, and the effect is to make the domestic interior feel examined rather than merely occupied.

The book's central claim, never stated but structurally obvious by plate ten, is that the ethics of photographing a home are inseparable from the choice of process. A gelatin silver print of an unmade bed reads as documentary, even surveillant — evidence of a life, filed. A platinum print of the same bed, with its longer exposure times and its refusal of harsh contrast, reads as something closer to portraiture of the room itself, unhurried in a way that implies consent rather than intrusion. Lindvall has said elsewhere that she photographed only rooms she was invited into and only after living with the household for at least a week, and whether or not you take the studio process as a formal echo of that patience, the pictures behave as though it were.

Sequencing matters here more than in most interior photobooks. Lindvall resists the obvious move of grouping by room type — no run of kitchens, no run of bedrooms — and instead cuts associatively, a drying rack followed by a window followed by a child's shoe left mid-floor, so that the book reads less like a survey of domestic space and more like a single long visit, memory-ordered rather than catalogue-ordered. The one place this falters is a run of three consecutive plates near the book's center, all close studies of unmade linen from slightly different angles, which regardless of platinum's tonal generosity begins to feel like the same observation restated rather than deepened. A stronger edit removes two of the three.

What stays with you after *Interior with Unmade Bed* is not any single image but the accumulated argument about looking slowly at things nobody dresses up for a photograph. Lindvall has picked the one process patient enough to earn that kind of attention and then pointed it, deliberately, at the least performed rooms in the house. It is a quiet, almost stubborn book, and it rewards being read in the same unhurried register in which it was evidently made — not in one sitting, but returned to, the way you might actually revisit a room. Selin Marchetti's review of [*The Weight of Water*](/criticism/weight-of-water/) makes the same case for a very different subject: that a print's density is itself an argument, not a mood.
