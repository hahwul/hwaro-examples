Salon is a small quarterly of photography criticism. It began, in 2024, from a complaint several of its founding critics shared: that photography writing had quietly become photography captioning, a paragraph of throat-clearing beneath a reproduction doing all the actual work. We wanted a publication where the writing had to carry the argument by itself, because it was the only thing on the page.

So the policy is simple and absolute. Salon does not print, license, embed, or link to a single photograph, in any essay, ever. A review here has to describe tone, grain, cropping, sequencing, and scale precisely enough that a reader who has never seen the book or stood in the gallery can still follow the argument — and a reader who has seen it can test the critic's eye against their own memory of the print. We think this makes for stricter writing and a more durable kind of attention than a thumbnail ever produces.

## What we cover

Each issue runs new criticism of photobooks, monographs, and exhibition prints — first editions and reissues, museum retrospectives and single-room gallery shows, historical processes and contemporary ones. We are not a news outlet; a book from three years ago is fair game if a critic has something to say about it that hasn't been said. We do not run interviews, artist statements, or portfolio features. Every piece is an argument, not an announcement. The current run is collected in the [criticism archive](/criticism/).

## The masthead

Salon is edited by a rotating panel of critics rather than a single editor-in-chief, on the theory that no one person's eye should set the house style for very long. Contributing critics write under their own names and are expected to have handled the physical object — the book, the print, the wall — before filing. We do not commission reviews of work our critics have only seen reproduced.

## Writing for Salon

We read unsolicited pitches year-round. A pitch should name the specific book or exhibition, the print process or edition if it matters to the argument, and the critical claim you intend to make — not a summary, a claim. We are especially interested in criticism that argues against the consensus reading of a well-known body of work, and in attention to photobooks that have gone out of print without getting the reading they deserved. Query the editors with a paragraph, not a full draft; if the claim is sharp, we will ask for the essay.

## A note on access

We know the no-image policy asks something of readers: you may finish an essay wanting to see the print and not be able to, immediately, for free, in the same tab. That friction is intended. Photographs are worth going to see. We would rather send you to a library, a gallery, or a secondhand bookshop than let a compressed reproduction stand in for the thing itself.
