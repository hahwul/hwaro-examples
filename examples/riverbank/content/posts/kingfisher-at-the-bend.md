The morning was quiet when I arrived at the river bend shortly after seven. Cloud cover was thick but not heavy, the kind of flat grey that sometimes holds for hours before breaking. The water was calm, running at a normal level for mid-March, with good clarity -- a marked improvement on the turbid conditions of the previous week.

## The Perch

At 07:25, a kingfisher arrived at a low branch overhanging the water on the outside of the bend. This is a known perch -- I have recorded sightings from this exact spot on several previous occasions. The branch extends roughly one metre over the water at a height of perhaps 80 centimetres above the surface. It offers the bird an unobstructed view of the deeper water where the current has scoured a pool against the outer bank.

The bird sat motionless for approximately two minutes, head angled downward in the characteristic hunting posture. The blue of its back was vivid even under cloud, though the orange of the breast appeared more muted than in direct sunlight.

## The Dive

At 07:27, the bird dived. The movement was sudden and complete -- one moment it was still on the branch, the next it had entered the water with barely a splash. It surfaced immediately, rising back to the perch in a single fluid arc. A small fish was visible in its bill, held crosswise. The bird struck the fish against the branch twice before swallowing it headfirst.

The entire sequence, from dive to swallowing, took no more than eight seconds.

## Subsequent Behaviour

The kingfisher remained at the perch for a further twelve minutes, during which it dived twice more. The second dive, at 07:33, was successful -- another small fish, similar in size to the first. The third dive, at 07:38, appeared to fail. The bird returned to the perch without prey and sat for several minutes before flying upstream and out of sight, calling as it went.

## Habitat Notes

The river bend offers ideal kingfisher habitat. The outer bank is steep, providing potential nesting sites in the exposed earth face. The pool below the perch is deep enough to hold fish and clear enough for the bird to hunt by sight. The overhanging branches provide multiple perch options at varying heights.

I noted that the bank face on the outside of the bend shows signs of fresh excavation -- a roughly circular hole approximately 5 centimetres in diameter, positioned about a metre above the current water level. This could be the beginning of a nesting burrow, though it is too early to confirm.

## Other Observations

A dipper was seen further upstream, working a stretch of faster water over a gravel bed. Two mallards loafed on the inside of the bend where the current is slack. The grey heron was again absent from its usual station at the lower reach.

> The kingfisher hunts with a patience that is easy to mistake for stillness. But watch the head -- the constant, tiny adjustments of angle as it tracks movement below the surface. The bird is never still. It is always calculating.
