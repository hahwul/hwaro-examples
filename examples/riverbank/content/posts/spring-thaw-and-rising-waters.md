The shift arrived not with rain but with warmth. After a week of temperatures climbing steadily into double figures, the snowmelt from the higher ground has reached the valley. The river this morning was noticeably higher than my last visit -- I estimate 30 centimetres above the late-winter baseline, and still rising.

## Water Conditions

Walking from Willow Bend downstream to Otter Pool, the change was evident at every point. At Willow Bend, the lower branches of the bankside willows are now trailing in the current, their tips gathering small rafts of debris -- leaves, twigs, fragments of reed stem. The gravel bar that is normally exposed at low water has been submerged for at least three days, judging by the waterline marks on adjacent stones.

The colour of the water has shifted from the clear, dark tone of winter to a lighter brown, carrying fine sediment from the upland streams. Visibility into the water is reduced to perhaps 20 centimetres -- poor conditions for any bird that hunts by sight.

## Bankside Changes

The rising water has reworked several sections of the bank. Near the old footbridge, a section of earth roughly a metre wide has collapsed into the river, taking with it a clump of lesser celandine that had been in early flower. The roots of a mature alder nearby are now partially exposed where the bank has been undercut. This kind of erosion is a normal part of the river's cycle, but it is worth monitoring -- the footbridge foundations sit in similar soil.

Further downstream, the low-lying meadow between Willow Bend and Otter Pool is partially flooded. Standing water extends roughly fifteen metres from the normal bank edge across the meadow grass. A pair of teal were feeding in the shallow flood water -- the first teal I have recorded at this location since November.

## Signs of Spring

Despite the high water, the season is advancing visibly. Marsh marigolds along the sheltered eastern stretch are in full bud and should be in flower within a week. The blackthorn along the hedgerow above the flood meadow is beginning to show white blossom. Coltsfoot has appeared on a patch of bare ground near the footbridge -- one of the earliest flowering plants along this stretch.

The willows themselves are showing the first signs of catkin development. The buds are swelling but have not yet opened. In another week, the silver catkins should be visible, and the bees will not be far behind.

## Wildlife Response

The high water has redistributed the wildlife along the river. The grey heron, usually stationed at the shallows below Otter Pool, has moved upstream to a quieter stretch where the bank is higher and the margins less disturbed. Two little egrets -- uncommon visitors here -- were seen standing in the flooded meadow, their white plumage stark against the brown water.

The kingfisher was absent from its usual perch at the river bend. The turbid water makes hunting difficult, and I suspect the bird has moved to a tributary stream where conditions may be clearer.

A water vole was seen swimming across the main channel near Otter Pool, a crossing of perhaps four metres. The animal swam with purpose, nose raised above the surface, and disappeared into the bankside vegetation on the far side.

## Looking Ahead

The forecast suggests continued mild weather. If the upland snow has largely melted, the river should begin to drop within a few days, assuming no significant rainfall. The transition period as the water recedes will be worth watching -- the newly deposited silt on the meadow will bring nutrients, and the exposed margins often attract waders and other birds looking for easy feeding.

> A rising river is not a crisis but a conversation. The water asks the bank where it would like to be, and the bank, over time, answers.
