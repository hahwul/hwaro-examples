I arrived at the upper reach just before six, while the sky was still more grey than blue. The river was barely visible. A dense, low-lying mist sat over the water, extending perhaps two metres above the surface and thinning gradually toward the tree line. The air was cold and perfectly still.

## The Mist

Standing on the bank at Alder Pool, the far side of the river was entirely obscured. The mist moved slowly, not as a single mass but in layers -- denser close to the water, thinner above. Occasionally a gentle current of air would open a brief window through the white, revealing the dark surface of the pool before closing again. The effect was of a landscape in constant, quiet rearrangement.

By seven, the first warmth of the sun began to reach the valley. The mist responded slowly. It did not lift so much as thin, becoming translucent before finally dissolving. By half past seven, the river was fully visible, the water smooth and dark, reflecting the alders along the far bank with unusual clarity.

## Sound and Silence

The mist had a muffling effect on sound. The usual background of birdsong was subdued, as though the birds themselves were waiting for the air to clear. A robin sang from somewhere in the hedge behind me, its notes carrying well in the damp air. A wren called briefly from the bankside undergrowth but did not persist.

The river itself was nearly silent. The water level was moderate, and the current at Alder Pool is always slow. The only audible movement was a faint ripple where the flow met an exposed root at the water's edge.

## First Light on the Water

As the mist cleared, the low angle of the sun created a brief period of remarkable light on the water. The surface took on a pale gold colour, and the reflections of the bare alder branches appeared almost etched into it. This lasted perhaps fifteen minutes before the sun rose high enough to flatten the effect.

## Wildlife

A grey wagtail worked the shallows below the pool, bobbing along the exposed stones with characteristic energy. A pair of moorhens moved through the marginal vegetation on the far side, their movements marked by the gentle shaking of dead reed stems.

No kingfisher was seen or heard. The heron that has been regular at this stretch was absent, though a single heron was visible in flight further downstream, silhouetted against the brightening sky.

> Mist on a river does not hide the landscape. It replaces it with something else entirely -- a place where sound is softened, distances lose their meaning, and the water exists only as a presence felt rather than seen.
