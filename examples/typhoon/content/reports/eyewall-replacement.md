The system underwent a complete eyewall replacement between 02:00 and 09:30 UTC. During this period, the inner eyewall began to weaken as a secondary band of convection consolidated approximately 60 kilometers from the storm center. Sustained winds dropped from 215 km/h to 185 km/h while the eye expanded from 22 to 48 kilometers in diameter.

## Observed Sequence

The transition followed the classical pattern documented in long-track Western Pacific systems. A concentric outer eyewall formed around hour zero, gradually choking the moisture supply to the original inner core. By hour six, the inner eyewall had collapsed entirely, leaving a single, broader eyewall in its place.

- Initial inner eyewall radius: 12 km
- Outer eyewall formation radius: 60 km
- Final consolidated eyewall radius: 24 km
- Pressure rise during replacement: 18 hPa
- Pressure recovery after replacement: 9 hPa over 14 hours

## Intensity Implications

Replacement cycles typically produce a temporary weakening followed by reintensification once the new eyewall contracts. Operational forecasts for this system anticipate a return to peak intensity within 18 to 24 hours, assuming continued favorable shear and sea surface temperature conditions above 28 degrees Celsius.

## Coastal Impact Forecast

Although the central winds dropped during the cycle, the wind field broadened considerably. The radius of tropical-storm-force winds expanded from 180 to 290 kilometers. Coastal regions in the projected northwest quadrant should expect a longer duration of damaging gusts, increased storm surge potential, and prolonged rainfall accumulation regardless of the temporary intensity reduction.

Marine warnings remain active across all four quadrants. Mariners are reminded that the broader wind field presents hazards far outside the eyewall radius itself.
