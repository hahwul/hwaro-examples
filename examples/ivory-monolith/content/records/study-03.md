The grid of this design carries forty percent empty space by area. That figure is not a leftover. It is specified, measured at draft time, and protected through every revision. The empty cells are doing work.

## Pressure and Release

A page of unbroken text creates uniform optical pressure. The eye has no place to rest, so it skips, and comprehension drops. Inserting a wide margin or a blank column releases that pressure at intervals the reader does not consciously notice. The text that follows reads as deliberate rather than dense.

Margins are not equivalent to padding. Padding is interior to a block. Margin is the negotiated distance between blocks, and that negotiation determines how the page is parsed. A wide margin between sections signals independence. A narrow margin signals continuation. The same content, with different margins, becomes a different document.

## Asymmetric Voids

Symmetric layouts are stable but inert. The eye registers the balance and stops looking. Asymmetric voids, in contrast, propose a path. A column left blank on the right of a page directs reading down the left column, then forces a horizontal jump that the layout has already prepared with leading and color.

The monolith treats negative space as the primary structural element and the typography as the secondary one. The grid first, the words second. This is the inverse of the conventional approach, and it is why the page reads as architecture rather than as decoration applied to a page.

## Verification

A layout that depends on negative space cannot be verified by counting components. It must be verified by squinting until the type blurs and seeing what shape remains. If the residual shape is rectangular and uniform, the negative space is failing. If it is uneven, intentional, and recognizable as a composition in its own right, the layout is working.
