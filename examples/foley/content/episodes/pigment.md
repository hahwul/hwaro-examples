Pigment started as a mobile color-matching puzzle game with a conventional free-to-play structure — rewarded ads, a hint currency, a soft energy timer — and it made real money for Two Tone for two years before Callum Ng and Bea Solheim decided they didn't like what the design had become and rebuilt it as a paid PC release with none of that in it.

<!-- more -->

## The pitch

Two Tone is a two-person studio that started as a mobile-first shop out of necessity — it was the format they could afford to make and market with no funding. Pigment's mobile version was profitable, clearing enough monthly revenue to support both of them, but Bea in particular had grown uncomfortable with an energy-timer system explicitly tuned, using their own analytics, to lapse right when players were most engaged.

## The number

Internally, the number that triggered the decision was the rewarded-ad completion rate among their most engaged cohort: 71 percent of players who'd logged in on five or more consecutive days were watching an ad specifically to skip the energy timer, which Callum describes as "our best players paying us in attention because we'd built a wall in front of a game they already loved." That statistic, more than any single complaint, is what convinced them the mobile design had drifted from something they were proud of.

## What broke

The PC conversion wasn't a simple export — every puzzle in the mobile version had been authored with the energy timer's pacing in mind, so levels were short and interruption-friendly by design. Removing the timer entirely, for the one-time-purchase PC version, exposed how thin that pacing was without the artificial gate; early PC playtesters blew through what had been "a week of mobile sessions" in ninety minutes and called the game short. Two Tone ended up authoring 60 new puzzles specifically for the PC release, nearly doubling the original count, to give the paid version pacing that held up without a timer doing the work.

## What they'd keep

Submitting for Steam Deck verification twice rather than shipping with a "playable" rating. The first submission failed on default control glyphs not matching the Deck's layout in two menu screens; the fix took four days, and the second submission passed with full "verified" status. Callum is adamant that the extra week was worth it — verified badging visibly affects wishlist conversion on Steam's Deck-filtered browsing views, and Two Tone now treats verification as a launch requirement, not a nice-to-have, for every future PC release.
