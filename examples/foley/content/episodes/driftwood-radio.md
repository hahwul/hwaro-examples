Driftwood Radio is a narrative walking sim about a late-night radio DJ broadcasting to a coastal town during a slow-moving evacuation, built solo by Marguerite Sol over four years of part-time development. Two months before the launch date she'd already announced publicly, she deleted the game's entire third act.

<!-- more -->

## The pitch

The original scope was three acts across three in-game nights, each one a different DJ shift as the evacuation order escalates from advisory to mandatory. Marguerite had scripted, recorded, and partially implemented all three by early 2025, working nights and weekends around a full-time job, for close to four years.

## The number

Not a sales figure — a personal one. Marguerite tracked her own weekly development hours in a spreadsheet the entire project, and in the six weeks before the cut, that number had fallen from a sustained average of roughly 14 hours a week to under 3, with two weeks at zero. She read that number, out loud, as the reason she couldn't finish what she'd planned, rather than as a personal failing to push through.

## What broke

Nothing broke in the game — the burnout was the thing that broke, and it broke on a schedule Marguerite had built around a version of herself from four years earlier who had more free time and no announced release date creating pressure. The third act existed mostly in outline and partial voice recordings; finishing it properly would have meant another four to six months at a pace she no longer had, or a rushed version she didn't want to ship with her name on it.

{{ alert(type="the decision", body="Marguerite cut the third act entirely, rewrote the second act's ending to function as a full close, and shipped a two-night, roughly ninety-minute game instead of the planned three-night, four-hour one — six weeks after the cut, on the original announced date.") }}

## What they'd keep

Announcing a release date at all, even though it's the thing that created the pressure that led to burnout. Marguerite is clear that without a public date, Driftwood Radio might still be in development, endlessly expanding toward a version of itself that never becomes real. The lesson she draws isn't "don't announce dates" — it's "build in permission to cut," which she now does explicitly, marking each act in her production notes as either load-bearing or optional before she starts building it, so that a future cut is a known option instead of an admission of failure.
