Nine Days at Sea is a three-person team's narrative survival game about a fishing crew stranded after a storm, and it is the only episode this season where the guests are two people who still aren't sure they'd do it again. Priya Anand wrote and directed; Tomas Ruiz built the boat-management systems and, four months after launch, broke the game with a balance patch that took the Steam rating from Very Positive to Mixed in eleven days.

<!-- more -->

## The pitch

Low Tide Games is three people — Priya, Tomas, and a composer, Elin Sarr, working part time — and the entire marketing budget for launch was $400, spent on a single sponsored post in a sailing-and-survival-games newsletter. Everything else was organic: a devlog series, a demo on itch.io eight months before launch, and a Discord server that Priya moderated personally every day for a year.

## The number

Day-one revenue wasn't the number that mattered — it was fine, not remarkable. The number that mattered was the Discord server's active member count the week before launch: 1,900 people who had played the demo and stuck around for updates. That community is what generated the initial wave of reviews, and it's also what Tomas had to face directly when the balance patch went wrong.

## What broke

The patch in question rebalanced fishing yields to fix an exploit where players could ignore the storm-timing mechanic entirely. The fix worked — the exploit was gone — but it also cut late-game income by roughly 35 percent without anyone catching it in testing, because the internal test save had accumulated enough currency that the change was invisible. Players starting fresh hit a wall around day six of nine and the game became, by consensus, unwinnable on release settings. The rating dropped from 94 percent positive to 61 percent positive in under two weeks, almost entirely from players who'd loved the game before the patch.

{{ alert(type="what worked", body="Tomas reverted the yield change within 36 hours and posted a developer letter — no corporate language, first person, admitting the testing gap directly — pinned to the Steam page and the Discord. Review sentiment recovered to 89 percent positive within a month, higher than some players who'd never played the broken version.") }}

## What they'd keep

Priya moderating the Discord herself, even during the worst week. She read every angry message personally instead of delegating to a community manager they didn't have budget for anyway, and several of the harshest early critics became some of the game's most active advocates once they saw a real person responding in real time, at 11pm, clearly rattled but not defensive. Tomas is less sure he'd repeat the testing process that let the bug through — he's since started keeping a fresh, unmodified test save specifically to catch this class of problem — but both agree the direct, unpolished response to the fallout is the reason the game has a community left to talk about it.
