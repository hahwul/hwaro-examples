Art Nouveau was the first design movement to fully embrace the structural possibilities of cast iron and large-pane glass. The forms it produced — Métro entrances, conservatory roofs, department-store atria — would have been physically impossible a generation earlier.

## What the Materials Allowed

Cast iron could be poured into the asymmetric curves the movement preferred. Once the molds existed, the same shape could be reproduced cheaply for railings, brackets, and lamp posts. A whiplash line stopped being a hand-drawn flourish and became a structural component.

Large-pane glass made the buildings transparent in a way Gothic stained glass never was. Light passed through the structure rather than being filtered by it. The resulting interiors had a clarity that the heavy stone construction of the previous century could not match.

## A Note on Decay

Many of these structures have been lost. Iron rusts, glass breaks, and Art Nouveau buildings often occupied the same urban space that later movements wanted to demolish. The surviving examples — the Casa Batlló, the Maison Tassel, the Petite Ceinture stations — are the exception, not the norm.
