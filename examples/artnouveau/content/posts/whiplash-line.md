The whiplash line is the gesture every Art Nouveau designer drew before they drew anything else. A single curve that turns back on itself, accelerating into one peak and decelerating out of the other — the line of a wave caught at the moment it breaks.

## Where It Came From

The artists of the period borrowed it from botanical illustration. The stem of a vine, the unfurling of a fern, the arc of a flower stalk under its own weight — all of them carry the asymmetry that the whiplash captures. Art Nouveau took the line out of the botanical specimen and made it the structural element of furniture, ironwork, and lettering.

## How It Reads

A whiplash line creates a sense of forward motion that a symmetric curve never does. The eye follows the steeper side first, slows at the apex, and releases through the slower side. That asymmetric pacing is why a Hector Guimard Métro entrance still feels like it is *about to move*, even though it has stood unchanged for over a century.
