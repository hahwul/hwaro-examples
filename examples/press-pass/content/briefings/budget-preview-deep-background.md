A senior Treasury official briefed correspondents Thursday on the coming quarterly budget submission on deep-background terms. No material from this briefing may be quoted or attributed; the substance informs reporting but may not be stated as Treasury's position until the formal submission is made.

## What The Briefing Covered

The official walked correspondents through the framework of the coming submission. Key elements, none of which may be quoted:

- The submission will hold total discretionary spending roughly flat in nominal terms, representing a modest real decrease given projected inflation
- Mandatory spending categories will rise in line with prior projections; no major structural reforms are contemplated in this submission
- Revenue projections assume the tax policy baseline extended from the prior submission; no new revenue measures are proposed at this stage
- The submission anticipates a continued primary deficit of roughly the same order as the prior two submissions, with a modest narrowing attributable to improved revenue collection rather than spending adjustments
- Debt service costs are projected to increase substantially over the coming two years, reflecting the higher-rate environment; the submission treats this as a passive pressure rather than an active policy target

## What Was Not Covered

The briefing did not address:

- Specific agency-level allocations, which the official indicated are still being finalised
- The disposition of the supplemental appropriations request made in March; a Treasury view on that request was not offered
- The President's prospective tax policy agenda, which the official explicitly excluded from the briefing, noting that &ldquo;tax policy is not a Treasury matter to announce&rdquo;

## On The Primary Deficit

The briefing was clearest on the primary deficit trajectory, which the official characterised (without saying so quotably) as &ldquo;stable at an elevated level, not improving, but not accelerating.&rdquo; The submission does not contemplate structural spending reductions to address the deficit, on the grounds that the politically available reductions are small relative to the fiscal gap.

This is consistent with the Department's posture in prior submissions and is not a change in posture. The official indicated that the Department expects Congressional debate on the deficit trajectory in the current session but does not anticipate that the debate will substantially alter the submission's framework.

## Expected Submission Timeline

The formal submission is expected no later than 15 May. Correspondents should expect:

- A **formal public briefing** from the Secretary or Deputy Secretary at the submission
- A **detailed release** of tables and supporting documentation at the submission
- A **congressional hearing schedule** to be announced by the relevant oversight committees within the following week

Questions in the interim may be directed to the Treasury press office through the standard channels. The next deep-background session is scheduled for 10 April, one week before the submission.

## Correspondents Present

Six correspondents attended the briefing, representing the financial and policy press. Pool notes have been circulated to accredited colleagues. The next on-record fiscal briefing is scheduled immediately following the formal submission.
