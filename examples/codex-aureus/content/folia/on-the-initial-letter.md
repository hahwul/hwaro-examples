## The Geometry

An initial is almost always larger than it has any practical need to be. It occupies a
space that could, in principle, hold several lines of the running text. This is not an
extravagance but a discipline: the initial holds the reader for long enough that the
reading mind slows, shifts gear, and prepares for what comes after.

## The Ornament

The fields surrounding the letter-form are filled with interlace, with zoomorphic figures,
or with abstract geometric ornament. In the insular codices the interlace is overwhelmingly
preferred; on the continent, particularly in the Carolingian centres, geometric figures
and vine-scrolls are more common. The Codex Aureus of Echternach is notable for combining
both traditions in a single codex.

## The Practice

Three preparatory studies are typical before any gold is laid:

1. A pencil outline of the letter-form alone, to size.
2. A pencil outline of the enclosing field, with the ornament indicated in grey.
3. A finished cartoon with the colours indicated by letter-codes.

The gold leaf is applied first, over a gesso ground, and burnished with an agate stone
before any of the colours are laid. If this order is reversed, the colours are bruised by
the burnisher and the whole initial must be reworked.
