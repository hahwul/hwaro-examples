## The Stone

Lapis lazuli is a metamorphic rock of sodium aluminosilicate, mined chiefly in the upper
Kokcha valley of what is now northeastern Afghanistan. The stone has been worked for
pigment for at least six thousand years. It reached Europe by way of Venetian merchants,
who carried it across the Black Sea from Constantinople, and across the Silk Road from
further east.

## The Grinding

Raw lapis produces, when ground, a disappointing grey-blue. The clear, deep ultramarine
that characterised the finest medieval illuminations required a separation process that
Cennino Cennini, in his *Libro dell&#39;Arte*, describes at considerable length:

> Take a bowl of pine wood, and a pound of lapis lazuli well ground. Have ready of pine resin, mastic, and new wax, of each six ounces; and melt them together over a slow fire&#8230;

The resinous mass was kneaded with the powdered stone, then washed repeatedly with a mild
lye. Each washing carried away a little of the blue and left behind the grey impurities.
The first washing yielded the finest pigment; later washings yielded progressively poorer
grades.

## The Price

At the height of the medieval trade, the best grade of ultramarine cost by weight more
than gold. This is why the illuminator used it sparingly and chiefly for the robes of the
Virgin: a reservation that was both theological and economic.

## The Present Practice

A small quantity of properly-washed ultramarine is still made from lapis in the workshop
of a single artisan in Chioggia, whom we consult for the great feast-day folios. For
ordinary use we mix a mineral-grade pigment with a little lamp-black, which approximates
the Cennino recipe closely enough.
