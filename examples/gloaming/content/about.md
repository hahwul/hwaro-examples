Gloaming is kept by Nell Harrow, who repairs sails by trade and keeps bees by temperament. The hives stand on the roof of the old Cordage Works on Wharf Street in Saltmere, a coastal town that manufactures fog the way other towns manufacture traffic. Six storeys up there are no bears, no badgers, and no neighbours close enough to complain — only gulls, salt wind, and a landlord who accepts rent in honey.

## The two hives

**Lark** sits on the east parapet and catches first light. She flies early, builds early, and swarms early if I let her. A National hive on a weighted stand, headed since spring 2025 by a dark local queen I never marked and never regretted not marking.

**Vesper** faces west over the harbour mouth and works the evening shift. Her foragers come home in the gloaming long after Lark has gone quiet, which is where this log got its name. She is slower to build in spring and better at keeping her stores through autumn. Of the two, she is the one I would trust with my own winter.

## The place

Saltmere gives the bees an odd, generous forage map: street lindens along the esplanade, escaped garden herbs in a hundred window boxes, brambles on the railway cutting, and sea aster on the salt marsh when everything else has finished. The wind is the tax on all of it. Everything on this roof is strapped, weighted, or sorry.

## The rules of the log

Each entry is written within a day or two of an inspection, dated to the inspection itself, and filed under its season — the whole record is in [the inspection log](/apiary/), oldest cell at the bottom. Numbers are real numbers from the hive scale and the mite-wash jar, not tidy ones. When I guess, I say I am guessing. When I am wrong, the next entry says so. Nothing here is advice — Saltmere's wind, bloom, and fog are their own jurisdiction, and your roof will have different laws.
