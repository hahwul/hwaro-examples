Dalbit — 달빛, moonlight — is a single room. It holds one wall, a bench, and whatever plates have been hung on it so far. There is no other lighting. The room was built on the belief that most images fail not because the subject is weak but because they are shown next to too many other things; a nocturne loses its glow the moment it has to compete with a neighbor.

The plates themselves come from a printmaker who works alone, outdoors, at night, with a single long exposure per sitting. Water is the easiest subject and the hardest to get right, because it holds the light and moves it at the same time. Stone gives back only what falls directly on it, a thin rim of brightness on an otherwise unlit mass. Snow does something in between — it seems to generate its own low glow even when the sky above it is nearly black. All three appear across the collection, never staged, never combined into a single frame.

Each nocturne is logged with the date it was exposed and the phase of the moon that lit it. That is where the crescent mark comes from: rather than a plain number or a bullet point, every plate in a listing carries a small waxing moon that matches the sequence in which it was hung, thinnest first. By the fifth plate the mark is nearly full — not because the collection is complete, but because that is simply how far the moon had come by the time it was made.

New plates are added slowly and without a fixed schedule. The room would rather stay quiet for a season than hang something before it is ready. To see what is hanging now, start with [Tide Glass](/nocturnes/tide-glass/), the first plate hung, or walk through the [full sequence of nocturnes](/nocturnes/).
