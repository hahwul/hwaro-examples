## A City of Letters

In 1377, a Buddhist text called Jikji was printed in Cheongju, Korea, using metal movable type — 78 years before Gutenberg's Bible. Korea's relationship with printing, typography, and the editorial craft runs deeper than most people realize.

Seoul, as the cultural capital, became the center of this tradition. Royal printing offices produced scholarly texts. Private publishers circulated novels and poetry. The city developed a culture of reading and design that persists to this day.

## Hangul as a Design System

King Sejong's creation of Hangul in 1443 was itself an act of design. The alphabet was engineered for visual consistency: each syllable occupies an identical rectangular block, creating a natural grid when set in text.

```
  ㅎ + ㅏ + ㄴ = 한
  ㄱ + ㅡ + ㄹ = 글

  Each block: same width, same height.
  The text forms a perfect grid.
```

This structural property makes Hangul uniquely suited to typographic experimentation. The blocks can be stretched, compressed, stacked, and arranged in ways that Latin alphabets cannot easily achieve.

## Modern Seoul Publishing

Seoul's publishing industry remains one of Asia's most active. Bookstores like Kyobo and Arc N Book are cultural landmarks. Independent publishers in neighborhoods like Mangwon and Seongsu produce beautifully designed volumes that treat the book as an object of visual art.

Korean magazine design is particularly noteworthy for its:

- **Bold use of white space** — Pages breathe in ways that Western magazines often do not
- **Typographic mixing** — Hangul and Latin scripts combined with confidence
- **Minimal color palettes** — Many Korean publications rely heavily on black and white
- **Grid discipline** — Clean, structured layouts that guide the eye

## Lessons for Web Design

The editorial tradition teaches web designers three things:

1. **Typography is content.** The way text is set is not decoration — it is part of the message.
2. **Structure creates freedom.** A strong grid lets content feel organized even when it is complex.
3. **Restraint is confidence.** A page with fewer elements, each given proper space, communicates more authority than a page filled to the edges.

Seoul's editorial culture reminds us that the best design serves the reader first.
