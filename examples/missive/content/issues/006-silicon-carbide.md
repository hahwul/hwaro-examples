Reaction-bonded silicon carbide is made by infiltrating a porous carbon-silicon carbide preform with molten silicon, which fills the pores and reacts to form more silicon carbide in place — a process that yields a tile hard enough to blunt a hardened steel indenter and cheap enough to armor a vehicle panel with. We ran two tests on this batch: a Vickers hardness survey across the tile face, and a ballistic limit program on an armor coupon backed by a witness plate.

<!-- more -->

The Vickers indenter, loaded at 98 N, left barely visible diamond impressions and returned a hardness above 2400 HV — more than double hardened tool steel, in the range that makes this ceramic effective at blunting and fracturing a projectile's nose on contact rather than being pierced by it. The ballistic coupon, backed with a clay witness block per the V50 protocol, stopped every round through the test series up to the batch's rated velocity with no complete penetration.

The part of the test worth reporting is what the tile looked like afterward, not whether it stopped the round. Ceramic armor does not survive an impact intact even when it "passes" — the strike face fractures into a cone of comminuted material that absorbs and spreads the projectile's energy sideways before the fragments can be pushed through. Every coupon in this batch showed that fracture cone on sectioning, radiating from the impact point through the full tile thickness, which is the expected and correct failure mode: the tile is designed to sacrifice itself completely on the first hit in exchange for stopping it. Multi-hit performance on the same panel location is a separate, harder number, and one this batch was not tested against.

Closing out the year's run: [Inconel 718](/issues/007-inconel/), held at 650 degrees Celsius until it creeps to rupture — the slowest failure the floor has clocked.
