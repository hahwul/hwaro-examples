Every metal on this floor before today has had a crystal lattice — a repeating, orderly grid of atoms with grain boundaries running through it, and it is almost always those grain boundaries that decide how the metal fails. This issue's specimen has none of that. A zirconium-copper-aluminum-nickel alloy, cooled from the melt fast enough — on the order of a few kelvin per second, unusually slow for a glass-forming process, which is what makes this particular chemistry notable — that the atoms never had time to organize into a lattice at all. It froze as a glass.

<!-- more -->

Bulk metallic glasses are, alloy for alloy, often stronger than the crystalline version of the same elements and famously springy: no grain boundaries means no easy path for dislocations to slide, so BMGs can elastically strain past 2 percent before yielding, several times what a crystalline alloy can manage. We loaded a Zr-Cu-Al-Ni bar in three-point bending expecting exactly that springiness. It held it, right up until it didn't — and then it did not yield or neck at all. It fractured in a single event, releasing an audible crack, along a fracture surface with the same vein-like pattern you see on broken window glass.

That pattern is the signature of a shear band: with no grain boundaries to interrupt it, deformation in a metallic glass localizes into an extremely thin band, tens of nanometers wide, that runs through the entire cross-section almost instantly once it nucleates. There is no crystalline metal's slow, absorbing plastic zone soaking up energy ahead of the crack — the shear band is both the yielding and the fracture, back to back, with nothing in between. It is the reason BMGs excel in golf club faces and surgical blades, where you want elastic spring and a hard edge, and are treated cautiously in anything that needs to fail gracefully.
