The highest compliment you can pay an interface is to forget it exists. When the tool disappears and only the work remains, design has fulfilled its purpose.

## Visibility and Invisibility

Every design decision exists on a spectrum between visibility and invisibility. A bright red button is highly visible. A well-set paragraph of text is nearly invisible as a design element -- you see the words, not the typography. Both have their place, but the invisible decisions are the ones that matter most.

The typeface, the line height, the margin between paragraphs, the weight of a heading, the darkness of the background -- these choices accumulate into an atmosphere. The reader does not consciously notice any of them. But change one, and the entire experience shifts.

## The Sfumato Approach

In Leonardo's paintings, the sfumato technique makes brushwork invisible. You cannot see where one tone ends and another begins. The transitions are so gradual that they disappear, leaving only the impression of form.

We can apply the same principle to digital interfaces:

- **Transitions** should be felt, not seen
- **Hierarchy** should emerge from content, not from visual noise
- **Navigation** should be discoverable without being insistent
- **Structure** should support without constraining

## What Remains

When you strip away every unnecessary element -- every decorative border, every gratuitous animation, every clever visual trick -- what remains is the relationship between the content and the reader.

That relationship is the only thing that matters. Everything else is smoke.
