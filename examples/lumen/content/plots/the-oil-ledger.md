Before electricity reached the cape, Aldermere ran on oil, and every gallon was written down. This streamgraph stacks thirteen years of the purchase ledger into a single flowing band, each layer a grade of oil — colza at the bottom, refined mineral above, the thin bright seam of the trial paraffin that the 1931 inspector insisted on.

## Why a stream, not a bar

A stacked bar chart would have told the totals but hidden the *story*, which is one of substitution. As mineral oil got cheaper and cleaner it swelled from a sliver into the dominant current, pushing colza to a nostalgic trickle by the mid-1920s. The streamgraph's centered baseline lets each grade breathe, so you read the transition as a tide rather than a table.

The widest point in the whole band is the winter of 1929, when a broken supply cutter forced the keepers to burn everything they had. The ledger for that January is smudged and doubled, as if someone recounted the drums by lamplight and did not trust the first figure.

|------|------------:|--------------:|------------:|
| 1904 | 612 | 140 | 31 |
| 1918 | 240 | 690 | 54 |
| 1929 | 88  | 1,204 | 143 |
| 1938 | 12  | 980 | 22 |

## The waste seam

The faintest layer is spillage and skimming — oil logged as bought but never accounted for as burned. It is never large, but it never vanishes either, and in three separate years it spikes just after a change of principal keeper. The archive draws no conclusions. It only keeps the seam visible, the way the ledger did.
