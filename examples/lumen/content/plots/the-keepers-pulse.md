A lighthouse is a machine that must be tended in rhythm, and the duty roster is its pulse. This cadence chart drops one dot for every maintenance act logged in 1929, sorted into six lanes — the recurring chores that kept Aldermere alive. Read left to right it is a year; read top to bottom it is a job description, written in dots.

## The fast lane and the slow

The densest lane is the oil top-up, a nightly ritual whose dots blur into an almost solid line. Above it, the wick trim beats out its slower fortnightly rhythm. The clockwork lane clusters — the rotation train was serviced in bursts, usually the day after an outage, so its dots huddle where the storm strip spikes. The glazing lane is nearly empty: you only reglaze a lantern when the sea has broken a pane, and 1929 broke only five.

## What the gaps mean

The most human detail is in the fog-horn lane, which goes suddenly quiet for eleven days in March. Cross-referenced with the personnel ledger, that gap is the fortnight the assistant keeper was ashore for his wedding, and the principal — single-handed — logged only what could not wait. The cadence chart cannot tell you a man got married. But it can show you the exact shape of his absence, and then let the archive fill it in.
