A rotating lens does not, in practice, spend equal time pointing in every direction. Bearings toward the open sea are swept fast and clean; bearings that rake the cliff are shadowed, clipped, or deliberately screened. This rose bins a year of the mechanical rotation counter into sixteen compass petals, and the asymmetry is immediately, satisfyingly obvious.

## The screened arc

The stubbiest petals face landward, toward the cape itself. There the lantern carried a fixed copper screen — a deliberate blackout so the beam would not dazzle the coast road or the keepers' own cottages. The counter still turned through those bearings, but the light did not leave the tower, and the archive draws the petal short to honour the difference between *turning* and *shining*.

The longest petals reach west-southwest, straight down the main shipping approach. That is where the light did its real work, and where the keepers aimed their attention on foul nights.

## Bins, not spokes

Sixteen bins is a compromise. Fewer and the screened arc blurs into its neighbours; more and the counter's own coarseness — it ticked once per eight degrees — starts to invent detail that was never measured. The rose is drawn from the counter's honest resolution and no finer. Where the data is coarse, the petals are coarse, and that is exactly as it should be.
