Nineteen seventy-one was the worst year on record for outages, and this strip counts them week by week. Each bar is the total hours the light was dark that week; most weeks the light held, so the strip runs low and calm. Then the winter gales arrive and the bars leap, the tallest marking the three-day storm that took the whole lantern offline.

## The anatomy of a dark night

An outage in the incident log is never just "the light failed." The keepers wrote causes, and the causes cluster. The tall bars are almost always weather: a shattered storm pane, a flooded engine room, a lightning strike that welded the rotation motor. The low, persistent stubble between storms is maintenance — the scheduled hour or two when a lamp was swapped or the lens washed, dark by choice rather than by disaster.

Plotting outages as a strip rather than a running total does something a cumulative curve cannot: it preserves the *silence* between events. You can see, at a glance, the long quiet stretches the keepers were proudest of, and the exact weeks the sea won.

## The three-day storm

The record spike is the second week of December. The engine room flooded to the second step, both generators drowned, and the keepers ran the emergency oil lamp by hand — a fifth-order light where a first-order should have been. Ships were warned by radio. The bar is tall, but it is not full: even drowned, Aldermere never went completely dark. That partial height is the point of the plot, and the point of the place.
