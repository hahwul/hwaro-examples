The flagship plot of the archive reads a single year as a set of rings. Each of the twelve concentric arcs is one month of 1962; the length of an arc is the fraction of that month the great lens was actually rotating, drawn from the keeper's hourly tick sheet. Summer burns nearly all the way round. February, storm-bitten and short, barely closes half its circle.

## Reading the rings

The innermost ring is January, the outermost December, so the eye travels outward through the year the way the coast travels from winter into the long fishing summer. Where an arc breaks, the light was dark: a lamp change, a fouled clockwork train, or weather so thick the beam was pointless and the keeper let it rest.

Two things surprised the archive when the marks were first plotted. The first is how *regular* the burn was — once the acetylene conversion settled in the late 1950s, the rings grew almost seamless. The second is the stubborn notch that recurs at the same bearing every autumn, visible as a gap aligned across several rings. It corresponds to the equinoctial gales that swing in from the west-southwest and, for a few nights each year, made the shoals too violent to serve.

## From tick sheet to arc

Each hourly mark in the ledger becomes one unit of arc. The transform is deliberately plain — no smoothing, no interpolation — so the plot stays honest to the paper it came from:

```python
# hours_lit: list of 12 monthly totals from the tick sheet
circumference = 2 * math.pi * radius
for month, hours in enumerate(hours_lit):
    lit = (hours / hours_in_month[month]) * circumference
    draw_arc(radius=radii[month], length=lit, rotation=first_bearing[month])
```

The rotation of each ring is set to the beam's bearing at the first lit hour of the month, which is why the arcs do not all begin at north. It is a small liberty, but it lets a century of first-light bearings settle into a slow spiral you can follow with a fingertip.
