The light was only half of Aldermere's warning; the other half was sound. When the beam could not cut the murk, the diaphone horn took over, and the keepers logged every hour it groaned. This heatmap lays 1950 out as twelve month-rows of day-cells, the brightness of each cell the hours the horn sounded — a calendar of the coast's held breath.

## Two fog seasons

The plot reveals something the running totals hid: Aldermere has *two* fog seasons, not one. The bright late-summer band is advection fog, warm air sliding over the cold shoal water in July and August. The second, dimmer bloom in late winter is radiation fog off the marshes behind the cape, drifting seaward on still nights. Spring and early summer, when the winds are briskest, stay nearly dark on the plot — clear air, silent horn.

## The cost of sound

The diaphone was thirsty. Every hour of horn meant compressed air, and compressed air meant the oil engine running, and the engine meant fuel and wear. A footnote in the 1950 log totals the fog hours and converts them, in a tired hand, into gallons and shillings. The keepers knew exactly what a foggy August cost, down to the penny, and they sounded the horn anyway. The heatmap is, in the end, a ledger of that decision repeated three hundred and sixty times.
