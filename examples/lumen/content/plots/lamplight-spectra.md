When the trustees debated converting Aldermere to a sealed-beam array in 1994, they ran a bench trial: eight candidate lamps, each measured through its warm-up and burn on the tower's own photometer. This ridgeline stacks the eight intensity curves, oldest incandescent at the back, brightest metal-halide at the front, so a decade of lamp technology piles up like a range of glowing hills.

## Peaks and plateaus

Each ridge is one lamp's intensity over a standard burn cycle, normalised to its own steady-state output. The oldest lamps rise gently and sag — filaments dimming as they heat and the glass sooted. The newer discharge lamps spike hard at strike, dip into a warm-up trough, then climb to a plateau far above anything the old filaments could hold. Overlaid as a ridgeline, the improvement is not a number in a report; it is a skyline.

## Why the trustees hesitated

The brightest lamp won on paper and lost in the room. Its strike spike, clearly visible as the tallest front peak, meant a half-second of dazzle every time it relit after a flicker — dangerous for the very ships it was meant to guide. The trustees chose the second ridge from the front: a little dimmer, but flat and forgiving. The archive keeps all eight, because the point of a spectrum is not to crown a winner. It is to show the whole field, and let a later eye judge.
