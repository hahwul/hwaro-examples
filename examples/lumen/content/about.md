Aldermere Light stood on the Morran Shoals for exactly one hundred years, from the night it was first lit in the autumn of 1904 to its automation and de-staffing in 2004. For all of that century, its keepers did what keepers everywhere did: they wrote everything down. Hours lit and hours dark, the bearing of the beam, gallons of oil bought and burned, the groan of the fog horn, the weather that beat the lantern, and the small daily maintenance that kept a first-order lens turning against the sea.

Lumen is a fictional archive project that takes those logbooks — thirteen keepers' worth of them — and redraws them as data-art. Nothing here is a dashboard. Each plot is meant to be looked at the way you would look at an instrument on the lamp-room bench: framed in engraved hairline rulework, glowing faintly, honest about the coarseness of the century that produced it.

## The method

Three rules govern every plot in the collection:

1. **No smoothing without saying so.** Where the ledger recorded whole hours, the plot draws whole hours. Interpolation is a lie the archive tries not to tell.
2. **Keep the gaps.** A dark night, a missing week, an absent keeper — these are data too. Blank space in a plot is never decoration; it is something that did not happen, drawn faithfully.
3. **One light, one accent.** The whole gallery is rendered in a single cyan against an abyssal ground, because Aldermere only ever had one job: to be the one bright thing on a dark coast.

## The source material

The logbooks are held, in this fiction, by the Aldermere Trust, and were transcribed hour by hour from water-stained paper. A typical night's entry looks like this:

```text
1962-07-14  lit 20:41  bearing 247°  wind WSW 5  clear
            oil 2.1gal  rotation OK
1962-07-15  dark 04:58  bearing 251°  no incidents
```

Every plot on this site is an aggregation of thousands of lines like those. The keepers never imagined their tick sheets would become pictures. Lumen is an attempt to let a hundred years of patient, unglamorous record-keeping finally be seen all at once.

The people, the lighthouse, and the coast are invented for the Hwaro examples gallery. The habit of writing everything down is entirely real.
