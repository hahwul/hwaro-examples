## Project Overview

The Thunderbird Mask is a full-face ceremonial mask carved from a single block of red alder wood. It represents Thunderbird -- the great supernatural bird who creates thunder with the beat of its wings and lightning from the flash of its eyes. In Pacific Northwest mythology, Thunderbird is among the most powerful beings, capable of carrying whales in its talons.

## Design Philosophy

The mask follows the classical transformation mask concept, though this version is a fixed-face design. The Thunderbird's features are rendered in bold formline: a powerful hooked beak dominates the lower face, while enormous ovoid eyes stare outward with commanding presence. The brow line sweeps upward into stylized feather forms that frame the face like a crown.

The color palette is deliberately restrained to three traditional pigments:

- **Black** -- primary formlines defining the major shapes of beak, eyes, and feathers
- **Earth Red** -- secondary formlines filling the cheek areas and inner beak
- **Turquoise** -- tertiary accents on the eye pupils and the small feather details above the brow

## Carving Process

### Block Preparation

A section of red alder was split along the grain and the interior hollowed using an elbow adze. Alder is the preferred wood for masks because of its fine, even grain that holds intricate detail without splintering. The walls were thinned to approximately three-quarters of an inch -- thick enough for structural integrity, thin enough to be worn comfortably.

### Face Sculpting

The face was carved from the outside, working from the high points (brow ridge, cheekbones, beak tip) down to the recessed areas (eye sockets, mouth interior). The beak was shaped with particular care, as it defines the entire character of the mask. A Thunderbird beak must be strong and hooked, suggesting the raptor nature of the being, but stylized according to formline conventions rather than naturalistic.

### Eye Construction

The eyes are the spiritual center of any mask. For this Thunderbird, the eyes are large ovoids -- nearly a third of the total face width -- with deeply carved sockets that create dramatic shadows when the mask is worn in firelight. The irises are raised circles within the ovoid, and the pupils are small recessed points that seem to follow the viewer.

### Interior Finishing

The interior was smoothed and shaped to fit the wearer's face, with a carved nose bridge and padded contact points. A cedar bark headband system allows the mask to be worn securely during movement and dance.

## Cultural Context

The Thunderbird appears across virtually all Pacific Northwest Coast cultures. Among the Kwakwaka'wakw, Thunderbird masks are featured in the Winter Ceremonial dances, where dancers embody the power of this supernatural being. The mask transforms the wearer -- they are no longer human but become the Thunderbird itself, channeling its power and authority.

This mask was designed as a contemporary homage to that tradition, meant for display and artistic appreciation rather than ceremonial use. It respects the visual language while acknowledging the boundary between artistic practice and sacred tradition.

## Specifications

- Material: Red Alder (*Alnus rubra*)
- Dimensions: 12 x 9 x 7 inches
- Weight: 3.2 pounds
- Pigments: Natural mineral and earth pigments with hide glue binder
- Mounting: Cedar bark headband with cotton padding
