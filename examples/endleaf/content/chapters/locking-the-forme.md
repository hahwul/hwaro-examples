A page of composed type is a fragile thing: a few hundred loose metal pieces standing on their feet, held upright only by their neighbours. Between the composing stick and the press lay the craft of turning that loose assembly into a single rigid block, level and immovable, that could take the shock of the impression thousands of times without shifting a hair.

<!-- more -->

The compositor first tied the finished page tightly with cord and slid it, on a smooth stone or metal **imposing surface**, into position with the other pages of the sheet. Books were not printed a page at a time. Pages were arranged — **imposed** — so that when the large printed sheet was folded down into a **signature**, the page numbers ran in the right order. Getting the imposition wrong meant a book that read 1, 4, 3, 2; getting it right required thinking backwards through every fold.

## Furniture, chase, and quoins

Around and between the pages went **furniture**: precisely made blocks of wood and metal, in standard pica multiples, that filled the blank margins and gutters. The whole assembly sat inside a **chase**, a heavy rectangular iron frame. To lock it all solid, the printer drove **quoins** — expanding wedges, tightened with a key — against the furniture, squeezing everything toward the chase walls until the type was gripped from every side.

The test was simple and absolute. The printer **planed down** the locked page, laying a flat block across the type and tapping it gently to seat every sort to the same height, then lifted the whole chase a few inches off the stone. If nothing fell out, the **forme** was ready. If a letter dropped, the whole page came apart and the lock-up began again. A forme lifted successfully was one of the quiet satisfactions of the trade.

## The presses

For its first three and a half centuries, letterpress ran on the **common press** — a wooden screw press, little changed from Gutenberg's, worked by two people. One inked the forme with leather-covered ink balls; the other laid the damp sheet in the **tympan**, folded down the **frisket** to mask the margins, ran the carriage under the platen, and hauled the bar to bring the screw down. Two pulls printed one sheet, because the platen was too small to cover a full forme in a single squeeze. A good team might print 250 sheets in an hour.

The iron hand press changed the economics. Lord Stanhope's cast-iron frame around 1800 let a single pull cover a full forme; the later Columbian and Albion presses replaced the screw with a system of levers and a toggle joint, so a lighter pull gave a heavier, more even impression. Then came power. The **cylinder press** rolled the paper against a flat, moving forme on a rotating drum, and the **rotary press** curved stereotype plates around the cylinder itself, printing from a continuous reel. What had been 250 sheets an hour by hand became tens of thousands. The forme, locked up on the same principles a compositor would recognise from 1500, was now feeding the newspaper age.
