Letterpress did not die suddenly. It was displaced in three long waves, each of which kept the previous craft alive a little longer while quietly making it unnecessary. By the time the last wave broke, a technology that had set every book for four hundred years had been swept out of commercial printing almost entirely — not by decree, but by simple arithmetic of speed and cost.

<!-- more -->

The first wave was **hot metal**, and it came from within letterpress itself. Composing by hand was the bottleneck: a fast compositor set perhaps 1,500 characters an hour, and every one had to be distributed again afterward. The **Linotype**, from the 1880s, cast a whole justified line as a single slug of fresh metal from brass matrices assembled by keyboard, then melted the slug back down when the job was done — no distribution at all. The **Monotype** did the same with individual cast sorts driven by a punched paper ribbon. Both still printed by relief impression; both were unmistakably letterpress. But the hand compositor's craft, and much of the trade's employment, was gone within a generation.

## The second wave: offset

The second wave came from outside, and it was fatal to relief printing as a commercial default. **Offset lithography** printed not from raised metal but from a flat aluminium plate, chemically treated so that ink stuck only where the image was, and transferred the image first to a rubber blanket and then to paper. It needed no type, no lock-up, no make-ready of the old kind. It printed photographs beautifully, ran faster, and — crucially — its plates were made photographically, from any artwork at all. Through the 1950s and 1960s, commercial printing moved to offset in a rout. Type foundries that had cast metal for centuries closed. Working presses were sold for scrap iron; cases of type were tipped into the melting pot for their lead.

## The third wave: light, then bits

The third wave removed metal from typesetting entirely. **Phototypesetting** projected letters from a spinning glass or film master through a lens onto photographic paper, which was then pasted up and shot to make an offset plate. Type was now an image of light, sized by lens instead of by casting a new font. Within two decades even that was gone, replaced by digital fonts, the laser printer, and the imagesetter — type as pure data, output straight to film or plate or screen.

By the early 1980s, hand-set letterpress had no commercial reason to exist. It was slower, costlier, and more limited than anything that had replaced it. The presses that survived did so in the margins: a few fine-press bookmakers keeping the craft as an art, university and museum shops preserving equipment, jobbing printers too small or too stubborn to convert. The knowledge thinned to a handful of aging practitioners.

It looked, for a while, like an ending — the endleaf of the whole story. That it was not is the subject of [the final chapter](@/chapters/the-tabletop-revival.md).
