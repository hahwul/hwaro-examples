Movable type did not begin with a press. It began with a file. Before anything could be printed, a punchcutter had to make one perfect letter — not on paper, but in steel, cut in relief and reversed, on the end of a bar no thicker than a pencil. Everything downstream of that letter, every impression in every copy of every edition, inherited its shape from that one hand-cut original.

<!-- more -->

The punchcutter worked at the smallest scale in the trade. A capital letter for a text size might stand three or four millimetres tall, and it had to be cut by eye, filed and stoned and checked against a smoke proof — a quick print made by holding the punch in a candle flame and pressing the sooted face onto paper. Counters, the enclosed white spaces inside letters like *e* and *a*, were cut first with a small counterpunch, then the outer shape was filed down around them. It was slow, unforgiving work, and a good punchcutter could finish perhaps one or two letters in a day.

## From punch to matrix

The finished steel punch was hardened, then driven with a hammer into a small block of softer copper. The blow left a clean, right-reading intaglio impression: the **matrix**. But the strike also distorted the copper, so each matrix had to be *justified* — filed square on its sides and to a uniform depth, so that every letter would cast to the same height and align on a common baseline. A misjustified matrix produced type that stood proud or sank below its neighbours, and no amount of skill at the press could fix it afterward.

One punch could strike many matrices, and one matrix could cast effectively unlimited type. This was the true invention: not printing, which is ancient, but the division of labour into a reusable master (the punch), a working mould-face (the matrix), and a cheap, disposable product (the cast letter, or **sort**).

## The hand mould

The matrix was clamped into the base of a **hand mould** — a two-part brass-and-steel jig, wrapped in wood so it could be held while full of molten metal. The mould's clever trick was that it was adjustable in width but fixed in body height, so an *i* and an *m* cast to different widths but exactly the same height-to-paper. The caster ladled molten type metal into the mould, gave it a practised upward jerk to force the alloy into the fine counters of the matrix, opened the mould, and flicked out a finished sort. A skilled hand could cast several thousand letters in a day.

The metal itself mattered as much as the mould. Pure lead is too soft and shrinks unevenly; the trade settled on a ternary alloy that cast sharp, released cleanly, and stood up to the press:

```text
Type metal (a typical text-face alloy)
  lead ........ 60%   body, low melting point, cheap
  antimony .... 25%   hardness; expands slightly on cooling
  tin ......... 15%   fluidity; sharp casting of fine detail

Antimony is the essential ingredient: nearly alone among metals
it expands as it solidifies, so the alloy fills the matrix
completely instead of shrinking away from the letter's edges.
```

Every sort left the mould with a small tail of surplus metal, the **jet**, snapped off at the break. The letters were then dressed — rubbed on a stone, trimmed to exact height — and sorted into the case, ready for [the compositor](@/chapters/a-case-of-sorts.md). A single hand-cut letter had become a bin full of identical, interchangeable objects. The book was now possible.
