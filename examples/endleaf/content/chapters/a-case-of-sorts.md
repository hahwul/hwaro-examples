A compositor stood all day at a sloping frame that held two shallow wooden trays, each divided into dozens of compartments. Every compartment held one sort — one letter, figure, or space — and the compositor's hands learned their positions so thoroughly that reading the copy and picking the type became a single unbroken motion. The good ones did not look at the case at all.

<!-- more -->

The arrangement was not alphabetical. It was frequency-optimised centuries before anyone used that phrase: the letters you reach for most sat closest to your hand, in the largest boxes. In the once-standard American **California job case**, lowercase, capitals, figures, and spaces all shared a single tray, laid out so the busiest sorts lived dead centre.

```text
      A schematic of the lower-left quarter of a job case
      (larger boxes hold the letters used most often)

   .------------------------------------------.
   |  b  |  c  |  d  |  e   |  i  |  s  |  f | g |
   |-----+-----+-----+------+-----+-----+---+---|
   |  l  |  m  |  n  |  h   |  o  |  y  | p | w |
   |-----+-----+-----+------+-----+----------+--|
   |     |     |     |      |     |          |  |
   |  v  |  u  |  t  | SPACE|  a  |   r      |  |
   '------------------------------------------'

   The three widest boxes — e, the word space, and n/t — sit
   under the hand's natural resting arc. This is why the phrase
   "mind your p's and q's" survives: adjacent, mirror-image
   sorts, easy to confuse when your eyes are on the copy.
```

The two trays gave the trade two of its most durable words. The tray of small letters sat below the tray of capitals on the frame; printers called them the **lower case** and the **upper case**, and the terms outlived the wood by a century.

## The stick and the line

The compositor set into a **composing stick**, a small hand-held tray adjustable to the exact measure — the line width — of the job. Type was assembled upside down and back to front, reading left to right so that it would print correctly. Between words went a **space**; the craft was in choosing which one.

Spacing was pure arithmetic, and its unit was the type's own body. Printers measured in **points** and **picas**, and space material came in fixed fractions of the square of the body size, the **em**:

```text
Spacing material, by fraction of the em quad
  em quad      = 1     em   (the full square of the body)
  en quad      = 1/2   em
  thick space  = 1/3   em   (the default word space)
  mid space    = 1/4   em
  thin space   = 1/5   em
  hair space   = ~1/12 em

  12 points = 1 pica        6 picas ≈ 1 inch
```

To **justify** a line — to make it fill the measure exactly, flush at both ends — the compositor added or subtracted these spaces by hand between every word until the line was tight enough to lift without a letter dropping, but not so tight it buckled. A page of justified text was a page of hundreds of tiny arithmetic decisions, and an even, quiet colour of spacing was the surest sign of a master at the case. When the job was printed and the type no longer needed, it was **distributed** — every sort returned, by hand, to its right box, ready to be composed into something new.
