Everything before the press was preparation. The impression itself — the moment the inked forme met the paper — was where a print became good or bad, and where the printer's judgement did its real work. Two things had to be right at once: the ink, and the pressure. Get either wrong and the sharpest type in the world printed grey, or blurred, or bruised.

<!-- more -->

Letterpress ink is nothing like writing ink. It is a stiff, tacky paste, closer to a soft wax than a liquid: pigment ground into a **varnish** of boiled linseed oil, cooked until it strings between the fingers. It has to be stiff so that it clings to the raised faces of the type and refuses to flow down into the counters and hollows. In the hand-press era it was dabbed on with **ink balls**, later rolled on with a composition **brayer** of glue and molasses, and later still by the automatic rollers of the powered press. The printer judged the film by eye and ear — a properly charged roller hisses, a *shush* of tack lifting off the ink slab.

## Damp paper and the pull

Handmade paper was **dampened** before printing, sometimes a full day ahead, interleaved and pressed so the moisture was even throughout the stack. Damp paper is soft and receptive; it moulds itself around the inked letter under pressure and takes a crisp, full impression at a fraction of the force dry paper needs. The sheet was laid against **register points** — small pins that pierced the same two holes on every sheet — so that when the paper was turned to print the second side, the pages backed up exactly, and so that a second colour could be printed in perfect register with the first.

## The kiss and the bite

How hard should a letter press into the paper? For most of the trade's history the answer was: as little as possible. The classical ideal was the **kiss impression** — just enough pressure to transfer a solid, even film of ink and no more, leaving the back of the sheet almost smooth. A fine book compositor considered a visible dent a fault, evidence of a careless pressman punching type into paper instead of printing from it. Type is expensive, and a heavy impression batters the faces, rounding the crisp edges that the punchcutter fought for.

Getting an even kiss across a whole forme required **make-ready**: the patient building-up of the packing behind the paper, adding tissue-thin patches under areas that printed light and cutting away over areas that printed heavy, until every letter on the page struck with the same force. A complex forme could take longer to make ready than to print.

The argument was never fully settled, and it is worth flagging because it returns, inverted, at [the very end of this history](@/chapters/the-tabletop-revival.md). The deep, sculpted **bite** that a modern studio prints into thick cotton stock — the dent a book printer would have called a defect — is now, for many, the whole point. The same craft, the same presses, the same physics; only the taste reversed.
