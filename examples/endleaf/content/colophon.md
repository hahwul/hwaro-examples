A colophon is the printer's note at the back of a book, the place where the makers record how the thing in your hands was actually made. It is a letterpress habit worth keeping, so here is this edition's.

## The types

The text of *Endleaf* is set in **Vollkorn**, a sturdy, slightly dark book face designed by Friedrich Althausen — drawn expressly for the "bread and butter" of long reading, with generous counters and old-style figures that sit quietly in a line. Running heads, folios, labels, and the table of contents are set in **Assistant**, a humanist sans by Ben Nathan, chosen for the clean, machine-set feel it lends to the furniture of the page while the reading matter stays warm and serifed.

The two faces divide the labour the way a printer would: the serif carries the argument, the sans carries the apparatus. Old-style numerals are used throughout, because lining figures in running text look like intruders from a spreadsheet.

## The impression, such as it is

There is no metal here and no dent to run a finger over — this is a screen, and honesty is also a letterpress value. What the design borrows from the press is its logic, not its lie: a warm stock instead of clinical white, an oxblood ink for the one accent, wide margins with true folios, and running heads that name the page. In the lamp-lit dark mode the stock deepens to a pressroom brown and the ink warms to terracotta, the way red ink shifts under a work lamp late in a shift.

## The ornament

Every section break in this book is set with a centered row of **printer's fleurons** — the small floral sorts, descendants of the Aldine leaf, that compositors kept in the case for exactly this purpose. The sorts alternate orientation across the row, mirror to mirror, the way a printer would set them from a single fleuron flipped end for end. It is the one purely decorative move in the edition, and it is the oldest trick in the case.
