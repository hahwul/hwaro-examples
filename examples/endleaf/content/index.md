The endleaf is the sheet that joins a book's body to its cover — half pasted down, half free, the last plain page before the reading begins. It is a fitting name for a history of letterpress, because letterpress itself is the plain, structural craft beneath five centuries of reading: the raised metal that pressed language into paper long before language was made of light.

This book gathers that history into six chapters, from the punchcutter filing a single letter into the end of a steel bar, to the compositor picking sorts from a wooden case, to the pressman coaxing an even impression from a tabletop platen in a rented studio today. It is written for the curious reader and the working printer alike — specific where it can be, honest about what was lost when the trade nearly died, and unsentimental about why it came back.

Read it in order or wander by the [table of contents](@/chapters/_index.md). Either way, mind the impression: everything here was, at some point, squeezed into being.
