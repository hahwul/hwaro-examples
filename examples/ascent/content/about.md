Ascent started in the back of a station wagon. The founders, a type designer and a sign photographer who met arguing about a hardware store lettering in a preservation forum, spent a summer in 2015 driving a six-state loop of small towns whose main streets hadn't been repainted since the Eisenhower administration, photographing lettering that was, in most cases, on a building already listed for sale.

## What a revival is, specifically

We use the word revival on purpose, and not interchangeably with reproduction. A reproduction copies a sign's letters as closely as possible, flaws and all, at the size and in the context the sign existed in. A revival asks what those letters would look like as a working typeface: spaced for paragraphs they were never going to set, drawn with a consistent stroke logic the original sign painter improvised letter by letter, and extended with characters &mdash; lowercase, numerals, punctuation &mdash; the sign never needed.

That means every typeface in the catalog is an interpretation, not a scan. We keep what we believe is essential to the letters' character and adjust what would make them unusable as a text face. Where the source material disagrees with itself &mdash; and hand-painted signage always does, letter to letter and even side to side on the same panel &mdash; we make a call and document it in the specimen notes.

## The archive

Every typeface ships with the photograph it came from, the town, and the year we shot it, because we think that context is part of what you're licensing. As of this year the archive holds photographs of lettering from eleven states, most of it from buildings that have since been sold, repainted, or demolished. Two of the sources for typefaces currently in the catalog no longer exist in any form; the typeface is what's left.

We do not photograph or trace lettering that is still actively used by the business it belongs to without permission first. A closed shop's signage is, in our view, historical material. An open one's is somebody's identity, and we ask before we touch it.

## Who does the work

Ascent is a small studio: two full-time type designers, one of whom also does the location scouting and photography, and a rotating pool of freelance draftspeople for high-character-count revivals. We do not have a production pipeline that could be described as automated. Every letter in the catalog was traced by a person looking at a photograph, which is slower than the alternative and, we think, the only way to get it right.

## Values, briefly

- We would rather ship four excellent weights than twelve mediocre ones.
- Source material gets credited, not laundered into an anonymous "vintage" style.
- A typeface with a documented, specific origin is more useful to designers than one that could have come from anywhere.
- If a building is coming down and the lettering can be saved first, that takes priority over the release calendar.
