Most of the catalog started the same way a commission does: someone sent us a photograph and said, this needs to exist as a typeface before it's gone. We still take that kind of project, and we also draw new lettering from scratch when a client wants a mark that reads like it has always been there.

<!-- more -->

## Two kinds of commission

**Revivals.** You send photographs of an existing sign &mdash; yours, a building you're restoring, a piece of local history nobody else is going to save. We trace it, clean it up, and deliver a working typeface with the letters you need. Turnaround is four to eight weeks depending on how complete the source lettering is; a full alphabet with numerals takes longer than a wordmark.

**Originals.** You want new lettering that looks hand-built rather than typeset &mdash; a diner sign, a brewery logo, a mural. We work from reference (other signage, a neighborhood, a decade) and draw letterforms by hand before vectorizing them, the same process as a revival, minus the fieldwork. Turnaround is typically six to ten weeks.

## How it runs

1. **Brief.** A short call or a written brief: what the lettering is for, where it will be used, and any source material you already have.
2. **Reference and sketches.** We send rough sketches or marked-up photographs showing the direction. This is the cheapest place to change course, so we ask for real feedback here.
3. **Full draft.** A complete first pass of every character you need, set in a sample layout so you can see it in context, not just as isolated letters.
4. **Revisions.** Two rounds of revisions are included. Most projects need one.
5. **Delivery.** Final files in the formats you need &mdash; OpenType for print and desktop use, WOFF2 for the web, SVG outlines for signage fabrication or laser cutting.

## Recent commissions

**A brewery taproom sign, drawn from a torn photograph of the building's original 1938 hardware store lettering.** The client had one water-damaged snapshot and a rough idea of the letterforms underneath decades of repainting. We rebuilt the missing characters by matching stroke contrast and terminal shapes from the letters that survived.

**A full alphabet for a restored movie palace, built from six surviving marquee letters.** The venue kept a box of the original channel letters after the marquee was replaced in the 1980s. We measured the physical letters directly and extrapolated the rest of the character set from their proportions.

**A wordmark for a laundromat that wanted to look forty years older than it is.** No source material — just a mood board of five regional laundromats and a description of the feeling the owner wanted. We drew four full alphabets and delivered the one that tested best on a hand-painted mockup of the storefront.
