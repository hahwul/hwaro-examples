X-ray fluorescence assigns an obsidian artifact to a known geological source without removing material. The technique works because each obsidian flow carries a distinctive trace element fingerprint inherited from its parent magma and the wall rocks it interacted with during ascent.

## The Fingerprint

The diagnostic elements are the high-field-strength elements: zirconium, niobium, yttrium, rubidium, and strontium. These are present at concentrations of 10 to 1,000 parts per million. They do not migrate appreciably during the rapid quenching that produces obsidian, and they resist post-depositional alteration.

A handheld XRF spectrometer measures these elements in 60 to 180 seconds per specimen. The instrument reports concentrations as a vector that is then plotted against a reference library of known sources. A matching cluster identifies the source within a defined statistical confidence.

## Practical Workflow

A typical session proceeds as follows:

1. Clean the specimen surface of soil and soluble salts
2. Position the specimen against the analyzer aperture
3. Run a dual-beam analysis, low and high energy
4. Record the spectrum and extract trace concentrations
5. Compare the result against the regional source library

Sources within a few hundred kilometers can normally be discriminated. Closely related flows from a single volcanic complex sometimes overlap and require neutron activation analysis or laser ablation ICP-MS to resolve.

## Why It Matters

Provenance studies reveal trade networks. A specimen knapped in a coastal site whose source lies in the inland highlands documents a thousand-year-old supply route. Aggregated across thousands of artifacts, the data show how obsidian moved between communities and how those movements shifted over time.

The non-destructive nature of XRF makes it the preferred first pass. Destructive methods are reserved for cases where source assignment remains ambiguous after the initial screening.
