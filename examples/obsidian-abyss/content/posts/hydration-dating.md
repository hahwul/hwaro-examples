Obsidian hydration dating exploits a slow, predictable reaction. When a fresh surface is exposed, atmospheric water diffuses into the glass, producing a thin rind of hydrated material whose thickness grows with the square root of time.

## The Diffusion Rind

The hydration rind is visible under a petrographic microscope at 500x magnification. It appears as a band of slightly darker glass at the surface, sharply bounded against the unhydrated interior. The boundary marks the front where the diffusing water has not yet penetrated.

Rind thickness is measured to the nearest 0.1 micrometer using a calibrated reticule. Multiple readings are taken from each specimen to average out local irregularities introduced by surface topography.

## Rate Constants

The diffusion rate depends on temperature, glass composition, and effective humidity. A useful approximation is:

```
x^2 = k * t
```

Where `x` is rind thickness in micrometers, `t` is age in years, and `k` is the hydration rate in micrometers squared per year. The rate constant `k` typically ranges from 1 to 20 depending on the source.

Because the rate varies between obsidian sources, an effective dating program requires a calibrated rate for each chemical group present in the assemblage. Trace element analysis by X-ray fluorescence is normally used to assign each artifact to a source before applying the appropriate rate.

## Limitations

The method does not work on weathered or burned surfaces. It does not date the parent rock; it dates the moment a fresh surface was created, which for archaeological specimens is the moment of knapping.

Obsidian hydration is most reliable in the range of 500 to 50,000 years. Older surfaces approach a saturation thickness where the rind itself begins to spall. Younger surfaces produce rinds too thin to measure reliably with optical methods.
