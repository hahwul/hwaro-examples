Obsidian fractures conchoidally because it is amorphous. Without a crystal lattice to direct cleavage along preferred planes, propagating cracks instead follow Hertzian cone mechanics, producing the smooth, curved surfaces that resemble the inside of a clamshell.

## Mechanics

When a force is applied to a point on an obsidian surface, stress radiates outward. The crack initiates at a microscopic flaw and propagates along the surface of equal stress, which curves away from the impact point. The result is a hollowed-out flake whose ventral surface carries diagnostic features: a bulb of percussion, ripples, and a striking platform.

The angle at which the force is applied determines flake geometry. A steeper angle produces shorter, thicker flakes. A more oblique angle generates longer, thinner flakes suitable for blade production.

## Edge Properties

A freshly knapped obsidian edge can be 3 nanometers thick at the cutting plane, narrower than the working edge of a steel scalpel. This is why obsidian was historically prized for cutting tools and continues to see use in specialized surgical blades, where the smoothness of incision is correlated with reduced scarring during healing.

The tradeoff is brittleness. The same lack of plastic deformation that makes the edge sharp also makes it fragile. An obsidian blade chips against bone or struck against stone in a way that a metal blade would not.

## Identification

Three features confirm a conchoidal fracture surface in the field:

- A bulb of percussion below the platform
- Concentric ripple lines radiating from the point of impact
- Termination either by feathering, hinging, or stepping

These features distinguish a knapped flake from a naturally weathered fragment, which usually shows planar breaks along microcracks induced by frost or thermal cycling.
