The second revelation concerns the structure of the upper atmosphere as it has been recorded in the layered notation of the Empyrean. The clouds are not arranged at random. They occupy precise altitudes, corresponding to the seven registers of light, and each register holds a specific category of phenomenon.

The lowest register sits at the threshold of mortal sight. It is occupied by what the early scribes named the pale stratum, a pearled diffusion that softens the ascent and prevents the eye from attempting too direct a transition. Above this lies the silver register, where reflected light multiplies and produces the impression of a second sky inverted above the first.

The third and fourth registers contain the cloud architectures proper. These are the structural foundations upon which the higher domains rest. They are not vapor in the meteorological sense. They behave as solid matter when approached with the correct intent and as transparent medium when approached without. The fifth register marks the transition from material to immaterial. Few accounts of it survive intact.

The sixth and seventh registers belong to the sustained light, which has no analogue in the lower world. The sustained light does not originate from any source that can be traced. It is the medium through which all the other registers become visible. To enter the seventh register is, by definition, to no longer require the lower six.

This revelation does not provide instruction for ascent. It records only what has been observed by those who have already passed through. The reader who attends carefully will understand that the description itself is the instruction.
