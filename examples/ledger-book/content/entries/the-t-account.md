## The Diagram

A T-account is nothing more than a capital letter T drawn on a scrap of paper. The account
name is written above the horizontal bar. Debit entries are written on the left side of the
vertical stem; credit entries on the right. Nothing else is required.

## The Rule

Debits go on the left of the stem; credits go on the right. That is the whole of the rule.
The clerk posts each half of a transaction &#8212; the debit and its equal credit &#8212;
to the appropriate side of the appropriate T-account. At the end of the period, the sum of
the debit side of each T-account is compared against the sum of the credit side; the
difference, positive or negative, is the balance of the account.

## The Simplicity

Many apprentice clerks, encountering double-entry for the first time, find it baffling
until the T-account is drawn. Once it is drawn, the logic becomes visible: every transaction
is two halves, one debit and one credit, and the two halves must go to different accounts.
The T-account makes the geometry of the bookkeeping visible on the page.

> Every debit has a credit, and every credit has a debit, and the two are always equal.

## The Modern Survival

The T-account has no formal place in modern computerised accounting systems. Postings are
made directly against database records, and the T-diagram exists nowhere as a primary
data structure. Yet every accounting textbook still introduces double-entry through the
T-account, and the editor has never met a bookkeeper who did not, at some point in their
working week, still draw a T on a scrap of paper to work out a tricky posting.
