<span class="chapter-label">Chapter I</span>

<blockquote class="epigraph">
<p>We do not remember days, we remember moments.</p>
<cite>-- Cesare Pavese</cite>
</blockquote>

The first thing I remember is the sound of the river. Not its name, not where it went, but the way it spoke to the stones beneath the old wooden bridge behind our house. My mother used to say that the river had been talking since long before any of us arrived, and that it would keep talking long after we were gone. I believed her then, and I believe her still.

Our house stood at the end of River Street, which was less a street and more a gravel path that had been given a name by people who felt that all roads, no matter how modest, deserved the dignity of being called something. It was a small house, two stories of whitewashed clapboard with green shutters that my father painted every spring, though each year the paint seemed to last a little less than the year before.

## The Front Porch

The porch was where the life of the house happened. It wrapped around the front and one side, wide enough for a row of rocking chairs and a small table where my grandmother kept a pitcher of lemonade from May through September. She made it from scratch, squeezing the lemons by hand and adding just enough sugar to soften the sourness without hiding it entirely. "Too much sugar and you lose the point of the lemon," she would say, which I later came to understand as a philosophy that extended well beyond citrus.

I spent hours on that porch as a child, watching the world move at its own unhurried pace. Neighbors would walk by and pause to talk, leaning against the railing as if time were a thing that could be safely ignored. Mr. Harmon from three houses down came by every afternoon on his way home from the hardware store he ran in town, always carrying something -- a bag of nails, a length of rope, a can of paint. He never seemed to buy anything for himself. Everything was for someone else, some small project he had agreed to help with, some repair that needed doing.

The light on the porch was different from the light anywhere else. In the morning it came through the eastern rail slats in long golden bars that slowly swept across the floorboards as the sun climbed. In the evening it was softer, more amber, filtering through the leaves of the old oak that stood in the front yard like a patient guardian. I can close my eyes now and see that light, feel the warmth of those boards under my bare feet, hear the creak of the rocking chair as my grandmother moved back and forth, back and forth, keeping time with something only she could hear.

## The Kitchen

If the porch was where life happened in public, the kitchen was where it happened in private. My mother ruled that room with a quiet authority that needed no enforcement. She cooked by instinct rather than recipe, adding pinches and handfuls and splashes of things with a confidence that came not from measuring but from years of paying attention.

The kitchen smelled different depending on the season. In autumn it was apples and cinnamon and the sharp sweetness of cider vinegar used for preserving. In winter it was bread, always bread, the yeasty warmth of dough rising in a ceramic bowl covered with a damp cloth near the stove. Spring brought herbs from the garden -- basil, thyme, rosemary -- their green fragrance mixing with the breeze that came through the screen door. And summer was stone fruit: peaches and plums and cherries, their juice staining the cutting board and my mother's fingers alike.

There was a wooden table in the center of the kitchen, scarred with decades of use. Every mark told a story. The deep gouge near one corner was from the Thanksgiving my uncle tried to carve the turkey with a knife that was too dull and pressed too hard. The ring of stains near where I always sat came from the countless cups of tea my grandmother drank, always placing her cup in exactly the same spot, as if guided by some internal compass.

### The Radio

On the counter by the window sat a radio, an old Zenith with a dial that glowed amber when it was turned on. My mother listened to it while she cooked, tuning to a station that played big band music and jazz standards. I grew up knowing the words to songs that were already old before I was born, and to this day the sound of a clarinet immediately transports me back to that kitchen, to the smell of bread rising and the sight of my mother's hands, dusted with flour, moving with a grace that made everything she did seem effortless.

## The Garden

Behind the house, between the back door and the river, lay the garden. It was my father's domain, though he would never have claimed it so directly. He was a quiet man, more comfortable with soil than with conversation, and the garden was the place where his particular kind of eloquence found expression.

He grew vegetables mostly -- tomatoes, beans, peppers, squash -- arranged in neat rows that followed the gentle slope of the land down toward the water. But along the edges he planted flowers, not for any practical reason but because, as he once told me when I asked, "a garden without flowers is just a farm." The marigolds along the border were there to keep the rabbits away, he said, but I noticed he always planted more than strictly necessary, and in colors that pleased him.

The garden was where I learned patience. You cannot rush a tomato. You cannot argue with rain, or lack of it. You plant, you water, you wait, and you accept what comes. Some years the harvest was abundant, the kitchen table piled with more produce than we could eat or preserve. Other years the frost came early or the rain came late or the beetles found the beans before we did, and we made do with less. My father took both outcomes with the same equanimity, as if abundance and scarcity were simply different verses of the same song.

> The garden taught me more about life than any classroom ever did. It taught me that growth takes time, that care matters more than perfection, and that the most important work is often the quietest.

I did not appreciate these lessons then. I was a child, impatient and easily bored, more interested in the river and the woods beyond it than in the slow miracle of a seed becoming a plant becoming food. But the lessons took root anyway, as lessons often do when they are offered without insistence, planted in the soil of daily life and left to grow at their own pace.

## Evening

The best part of the day came after dinner, when the heat began to break and the shadows stretched long across the yard. We would return to the porch, my grandmother and I in the rocking chairs, my parents on the steps, and we would watch the sky change colors as the sun went down behind the trees across the river.

My father would sometimes bring his guitar out, a battered acoustic with a tone that had mellowed with age, and play songs that I later learned were folk standards but which at the time I believed he had written himself. My mother would hum along, and occasionally my grandmother would sing, her voice thin but true, carrying the melody across the yard and out over the water.

These evenings had a quality I have spent my whole life trying to name. It was not simply peace, though it was peaceful. It was not simply happiness, though we were happy. It was something more fundamental than either of those things -- a sense of being exactly where you were supposed to be, doing exactly what you were supposed to be doing, with exactly the right people. I have felt it since, but rarely, and never with the same completeness.

The fireflies would come out as the light faded, their tiny lanterns blinking on and off in the gathering dark. I would chase them across the yard, catching them in a jar that my grandmother had punched holes in the lid of, watching them glow against the glass. I always let them go before bed. Even then I understood, in some wordless way, that light is not something you can keep.
