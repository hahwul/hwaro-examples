Every other room in a building is designed around what happens in it. A kitchen is a grammar of preparation; a bedroom, of rest; an office, however unconvincingly, of work. The waiting room is the one room built entirely around what has not happened yet, and I think this makes it, structurally, closer to a sentence than a space — specifically to a sentence that has not reached its verb. You sit in the clause before the thing occurs. The magazines are old because updating them would imply the room believed in its own permanence, and a waiting room that believed in its own permanence would be, I think, a small architectural tragedy, a room built to hold a future tense that has started pretending to be a present one.

I have spent enough time in waiting rooms over the past four years, for reasons that belong to a different essay, to have noticed that they enforce a strange tense discipline on the people inside them. Nobody in a waiting room discusses the present in any real detail, because the present is the room itself, and the room is, by definition, not the point. Conversation retreats instead to the past — how long the wait has been so far, what happened at the last appointment, what the weather was like on the drive over — or leaps entirely past the room into a future it cannot actually see yet, rehearsing questions for a doctor, a lawyer, a caseworker who has not yet opened the door. The present tense of the waiting room itself goes almost entirely unspoken, because there is nothing in it to report. This is, I would argue, the room's whole design brief, quietly succeeding: to become so grammatically empty that no one in it can find a sentence to put there.

The exceptions interest me more than the rule. Children in waiting rooms have not yet learned the tense discipline and will narrate the present relentlessly — the carpet, the fish tank if there is one, the specific quality of a stranger's cough — and adults around them tend to find this either charming or unbearable depending on how urgently they need the future tense to arrive. And there is a second exception, rarer, that I have only witnessed a handful of times: two strangers, waiting for entirely unrelated things, who fall for a few minutes into a present-tense conversation about nothing consequential — a bad parking situation, a song playing too quietly to place — and who seem, for exactly as long as it lasts, to have briefly broken the room's grammar rather than merely occupied it. I do not think this happens because waiting rooms are secretly friendly places. I think it happens because two people, faced with the same syntactic vacuum, sometimes decide independently to fill it, and discover, briefly, that they filled it the same way.

I no longer bring a book to a waiting room. I used to, on the theory that the room's emptiness was an insult that reading could correct. I have come around to a different theory, which is that the emptiness is the room's only honest feature, and that pretending it is full — with a novel, with a phone, with anything that lets you skip the clause — is a kind of grammatical cheating I would rather not do anymore, if only because the door, eventually, always opens, and I have started to want to notice the exact tense I am standing in when it does.
