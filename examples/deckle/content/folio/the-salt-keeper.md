## I

The lower archive flooded twice a year now instead of once, and Greet had stopped reporting the second flooding to the municipality, because the municipality's solution to the first flooding had been a letter recommending the archive move to higher ground, as if higher ground were a drawer you could simply open the building into. She had been keeper of the lower archive for thirty-four years. She intended, privately, to be keeper of it for whatever remained of her working life, water or no water, and had developed in that time a method for the flooding that the municipality had never asked about and she had never offered: she copied.

Not everything — there was no copying everything, not at her age, not with the ledgers going back to 1791 in a hand so cramped it took her twenty minutes to transcribe a single baptismal entry. She copied by triage, working from the bottom shelves upward, on the theory that whatever the sea reached first was whatever the sea considered most valuable, and she had come, after three decades of watching the water's preferences, to trust its judgment more than the municipality's.

## II

The record she was copying out the week the letter arrived — a second letter, this one recommending digitization, a word Greet found almost funny in a building where the electricity itself failed twice a winter — was the marriage register of 1847, the year the dike had broken at Ruigenhil and half the lower town had married in a single overcrowded spring, as though the flood itself had convinced everyone at once that waiting was no longer a reasonable position to hold. She had copied this register once before, eleven years earlier, in a hand she now found embarrassingly quick. This time she went slower. She had learned, in the intervening years, that speed was a way of not really reading what you were copying, and that the register of 1847 rewarded reading: two names married in April who reappeared, separately, remarried to other people, in September; a note in the margin, not in the registrar's hand, that simply read gespaard, saved, next to an entry for a child whose birth and whose parents' marriage were recorded in the wrong order and never corrected.

## III

She finished the register on a Thursday, the tide unusually high for February, water already inching under the lower door as she blotted the final page. She did not know, copying it, whether the original would survive the season — the paper had gone soft in a way she recognized, the particular softness that meant water had visited before it was supposed to and would visit again sooner than the calendar promised. She carried her copy upstairs herself, refusing the intern's offer to help, and set it on the dry shelf beside the eleven-year-old copy of the same register, the quick one, the one she was no longer proud of but was not willing to throw away either, because a bad copy of something is still, she had decided, a very great deal better than the sea's version of remembering it, which was to keep nothing at all.

The letter about digitization sat unanswered on her desk through March. She was, by then, three registers into 1852, and had made her peace with being slow.
