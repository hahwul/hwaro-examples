## I

Their father had been drawing the map of the old quarter for thirty-one years, and he died with the northeast corner still in pencil, the ink pen capped and dry beside it as if he had simply stepped away from the table to answer a door and never come back to finish the thought. Rania, the eldest, wanted to ink the corner in herself, using his notes, which were extensive and precise and entirely useless for the one thing that mattered: he had stopped, in his final months, drawing the quarter as it stood and started drawing it as he remembered it standing, and the two versions had diverged so quietly that none of his daughters could say exactly where memory had taken over from measurement.

Hind, the middle daughter, wanted to burn it. Not out of anger — she was careful to explain this, more than once, to sisters who did not entirely believe her — but out of a conviction that a map of a place that no longer existed was not a map at all anymore, only a very detailed grief, and that their father had spent three decades of his one life making a grief so precise it had streets and door numbers.

## II

The youngest, Suha, did neither. She took the map to the quarter itself, now mostly parking structures and a single café that had kept its original tilework out of what the owner called stubbornness and everyone else called nostalgia, and she walked it against her father's pencil lines the way he must have walked it himself, forty years earlier, before the demolitions that took the quarter's western half in a spring none of the sisters were alive for. She found that his ink was accurate. She found that his pencil — the northeast corner, the unfinished part — was not a record of what stood there before the demolitions. It was a record of what he had wanted to stand there, a version of the quarter that had never existed except as thirty-one years of a man's slow, private correction of the city that had actually been built.

She brought this back to her sisters, and it did not resolve anything, because a discovery is not the same as an answer. Rania still wanted to ink it — the pencil was still his hand, still his thirty-one years, wanted or invented made no difference to the fact of the labor. Hind still wanted to burn it, differently now: not to erase a grief but to release one, on the theory that a wish kept sharp on paper for three decades had earned the right to stop being kept.

## III

What they did, in the end, was the thing none of the three had proposed alone. They left the northeast corner in pencil, exactly as he had left it, and they had the rest of the map — the ink, the measured, verifiable three-quarters of a city partly gone now regardless — reproduced and bound, one copy for each of them, with a translator's note on the final page that none of them wrote, because none of them could agree on how the pencil corner should be described, and so they left that argument, too, unfinished, the way he had left his. The note simply says: here the cartographer stopped measuring and started remembering, and the map does not tell you which line is which. You will have to walk it yourself, if the quarter is still standing when you go.
