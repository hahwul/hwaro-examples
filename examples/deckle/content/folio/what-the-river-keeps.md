## I

The river gave back my father's boot on a Tuesday, which Marta Cernov took as evidence of nothing, because a boot is a boot and the Ilva has been known to return smaller things than boots — spoons, once, an entire crate of them, still nested, from a wedding no one in the village could remember hosting. But three weeks later it gave back the oar, the good one, the one with her father's initials burned into the handle by her father's own father, and Marta stopped calling it nothing.

She did not tell her mother. Her mother had made a private peace with the river the year it took him — a peace built out of not naming it, not fishing it, not even walking the near bank after dark — and Marta was not going to be the one to break a peace that had taken eleven years to build, not over an oar. She cleaned it. She hung it in the shed where the other oars still hung, in the space he had left for it, as though the shed itself had been keeping the peace her mother made, waiting out eleven years with the patience only a wall of tools can manage.

## II

The third thing was not a thing at all. It was a Thursday in October, cold enough that the Ilva had started its early skin of ice at the shallow bend, and Marta was there with a bucket for the last of the cress before the frost took it, when she heard, from the deep channel where the current runs black even in daylight, something that was almost her name. Not a voice. She wants to be exact about this, even now, even to people who will not believe the boot or the oar either: not a voice, but the shape a voice makes when it says a word, worn down to just the shape, the way a stone in a streambed keeps the memory of being a mountain without keeping anything you could call mountain about it anymore.

She did not answer. You do not answer a river; her grandmother had taught her that before she had taught her to swim, on the theory, apparently sound, that the second lesson is useless without the first. But she went back the next Thursday, and the one after, with no bucket, no excuse, just herself standing at the black water's edge in the cold, and on the fourth Thursday the river gave her something it had never given her mother in eleven years of the two of them refusing to speak to each other across a bank of cress and ice: it gave her the truth, or a version of it, or as much of a version as a river is capable of telling, which came to her not in words but in a picture, sudden and complete, of her father's hands letting go of the boat on purpose, in the last second before the current had him, so that the boat — with her mother's second child in it, four months along and unannounced even to her father — would not go where he was going.

## III

Marta told her mother in November, in the kitchen, with the oar between them on the table like a piece of testimony neither of them had asked to give. Her mother did not cry, which Marta had expected, or accept it, which she had not. She only reached out and turned the oar over, so the initials faced up, and said that the river had never given her anything in eleven years and she was not about to start believing it now on the word of a nineteen-year-old with cold hands and too much time at the water.

But she did not tell Marta to stop going. And when the ice broke the following March and the Ilva ran high and brown with the last of the mountain snow, it was Marta's mother who was standing at the bend before dawn, alone, with no bucket and no cress and nothing in her hands at all, waiting to hear whatever shape of a voice the river had decided, after eleven years, it was finally willing to spend on her.
