My grandmother kept a shoebox on the top shelf of a closet she otherwise used for coats, and when she died it came to me the way odd, unglamorous things come to the grandchild who lives closest: not because I asked for it, but because someone had to take the car to the house and I was the one with the car. Inside were forty-one letters, each addressed, each stamped, none of them sent.

They spanned thirty years. The earliest was written to a sister in Antwerp in 1962 and concerned, mostly, the weather and a disagreement about a recipe for stewed rabbit. The latest was written to me, four months before she died, and said almost nothing at all — a paragraph about the garden, a question about my work she did not wait for me to answer, a closing line that trailed into an ellipsis instead of a signature. I do not know why she stamped a letter she had no intention of sending. I do not know why she stamped forty-one.

What I have come to think, after two years of reading and not-quite-reading them, is that the stamp was the point and the sending was not. A stamped, addressed, unsent letter is a strange object: it has done the work of imagining an audience — has performed all the labor of correspondence, chosen its recipient, pitched its tone to a specific person's ear — and then stopped one motion short of becoming correspondence at all. It is not a diary; diaries do not pretend to be spoken to anyone. It is not quite private, either, since privacy does not usually bother addressing an envelope. It occupies a category we do not have a good word for: rehearsal, maybe, if rehearsal did not imply a performance still to come.

I have tried to find a pattern in which letters she sent and which she kept back, and the only rule I can find is disappointing in its plainness: the ones she kept were the ones that told the truth. The sent letters — I have some of these too, kept by the people she sent them to, forwarded to me in the same terrible season everyone was cleaning out a house — are cheerful and competent and slightly beside the point, letters about weather and rabbit stew that arrive at the real subject only by circling it for four paragraphs and then, at the last possible moment, not landing. The unsent ones land. The unsent ones say the sister in Antwerp was wrong about something more than a recipe, and had been wrong about it for eleven years, and that my grandmother had decided, sometime in 1973, that the wrongness was no longer worth the argument it would take to fix. She wrote that decision down. She did not mail it. She kept it in a box instead, the way you might keep a stone from a beach you are fairly sure you will never go back to — not because the stone is useful, but because putting it down and picking it back up again would require going back to the beach, and some coastlines are easier to hold onto than to revisit.

I think about the ethics of reading them more than I think I should. Nobody wrote me permission into that box; nobody could have, since the entire premise of an unsent letter is that its author has not yet decided, and may never decide, that anyone besides the intended reader gets to see it. But I have decided that a woman who stamped forty-one envelopes over thirty years was, at some low hum beneath her own certainty, hoping that stamping was a kind of insurance — that if the letter never got sent by her hand, it might still, eventually, by some other hand, get read. I am the other hand. I try to read the way she might have wanted the sister in Antwerp to read: slowly, and without writing back.
