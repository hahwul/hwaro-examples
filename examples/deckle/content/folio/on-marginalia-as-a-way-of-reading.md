I own a copy of a novel I have read four times, and each reading is dated in the top corner of the title page in a different pen, and each of the four readings argued, in the margins, with at least one of the readings that came before it. The first, from a term of university I mostly remember for the cold of the library, is enthusiastic and underlines too much, the way a nineteen-year-old underlines, as if enthusiasm were a substance that could be applied with a straightedge. The second, six years later, crosses out several of the first reading's exclamation points with a flat, unimpressed line, and writes, next to one particularly breathless passage, the single word "no." The third reading does not argue with the first two at all. It simply adds — small, careful notes in the margin that have nothing to do with agreement or disagreement, only with noticing, as though by the third reading the book and I had stopped needing to have opinions about each other and could just sit in the same room.

The prevailing wisdom about marginalia treats it as a kind of vandalism with good intentions — something you are permitted to do to a paperback you own outright and forbidden, on pain of librarian, to do to anything you do not. This is a reasonable rule for the preservation of books as objects and a fairly useless rule for the preservation of books as experiences, because the experience of a book is not a fixed thing sitting quietly between its covers waiting to be received. It is closer to a conversation, and a conversation without a record of what you said in it is not a conversation you can return to — it is just an evening you remember having, increasingly badly, for the rest of your life.

What the margin preserves, that memory alone will not, is the specific shape of your attention on a specific day. I do not actually remember what I thought of that novel at nineteen. I remember what I think of it now, which has quietly and thoroughly replaced the original, the way a photograph replaces a memory it was meant only to support. But the margin does not let the replacement stand uncontested. It sits there in blue ballpoint, underlining a sentence I would not underline today, and it insists, against my present certainty, that I once found this passage — this exact one, not a version of it smoothed over by hindsight — the most important sentence in the book. I can disagree with my nineteen-year-old self. I cannot un-know that he existed, and had opinions, and wrote them down where I would eventually have to read them.

There is a school of thought, mostly digital, that treats the highlight as marginalia's natural successor — the yellow bar under a sentence in an e-reader, syncable, searchable, exportable to a note-taking app that promises to make something of it later. I have made my peace with highlights; they capture the underline's function well enough. What they do not capture, and what no amount of tagging will retrofit into them, is the argument — the crossed-out exclamation point, the flat "no" written next to your own earlier enthusiasm, the strange tenderness of finding, on a fourth reading, that you have started making margin notes not to the book anymore but to yourself, twenty years back, in a library that was cold, about a passage neither of you has stopped being right about.
