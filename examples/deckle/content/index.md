Six pieces make an issue of Deckle: three long essays, three works of fiction carried over from another language into this one. We set them side by side on purpose — an essay thinking in argument next to a story thinking in scene, so that a reader moving from one to the other feels the different muscles working.

Translation is not a garnish here. Each translated piece runs with its translator's name at the same size as its author's, because a sentence that crosses a language does not arrive by itself, and the person who carried it across deserves to be read as more than a footnote. Where the source language matters to how a piece was made, we say so in the margin.

This issue took its epigraph from a note Élodie Marchand left in the back of a proof copy decades before this journal existed, about the deckle edge of hand-laid paper — the soft, feathered border left where pulp meets the mould's frame rather than a blade. We kept the metaphor, and, this once, the name.
