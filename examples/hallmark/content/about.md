## What "Hallmark" means here

A hallmark is an assay mark — a small stamp cut into precious metal once it has been tested and proven genuine. I borrowed the word for the studio because it describes the standard I hold a page to: nothing leaves here until I've checked it the way an assayer checks a piece of silver, closely, and more than once.

## How I work

Every commission starts on paper. I sketch grids longhand before I open a layout program, because a grid decided on screen too early tends to bend to whatever the software makes easy rather than what the content needs. Once a structure holds, I move to type — usually one display face and one text face, sometimes a third for captions and running heads, never more. Proofs get marked up by hand and re-sent; I've yet to find a screen that shows me how a spine reads from three metres away or how a title survives being printed a centimetre smaller than the layout suggested.

For magazines, that means building a system flexible enough to survive an editor changing their mind at the last stage — a grid with real options in it, not a template with one setting. For jackets, it means reading the whole manuscript before I touch a typeface, because a cover that only reflects the pitch usually looks like it.

## Outside the client work

I teach a term of editorial design at Falbright College of Art each year, mostly because critiquing student work keeps my own opinions honest. I also keep a small risograph press in the studio, which I use for proofing text-heavy interiors and, occasionally, for nothing useful at all.
