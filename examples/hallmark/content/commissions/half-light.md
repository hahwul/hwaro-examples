Half-Light is a debut novel told through a lighthouse keeper's diary entries, written in the gaps between the beam's rotations. The editor's brief was one line: "the reader should feel the dark between the flashes." Everything else was mine to work out.

<!-- more -->

I started with four directions and killed three of them inside a week. Photography felt too literal — a lighthouse on a lighthouse-keeper novel reads as illustration, not atmosphere. A type-only cover came closer, but nothing in a font menu moved like a beam does. The version that survived hand-lettered the title in a single stroke of white ink that thins and widens across the word, so "Half-Light" itself brightens and dims the way the keeper's entries describe the beam sweeping the room.

The background is a near-black plum rather than true black — close enough to the beam-lit navy of the manuscript's opening chapter that booksellers who'd read a proof recognised the colour on sight. Author name and imprint sit in Documenta, small and steady, the one part of the jacket that doesn't move.

The publisher printed four proof rounds to get the ink density right on the lettering; too thin and it vanished on the shelf, too thick and it lost the taper that makes it read as light rather than paint. It shortlisted for Jacket of the Year at the British Book Design Awards the following spring, and three booksellers have since asked for the file to make a shelf-talker, which I take as the actual review.
