The Quarterly Review has run since 1986 with three mastheads and, by the editor's own count, no grid system anyone could describe out loud. Every issue was assembled from whatever the outgoing issue's InDesign file happened to contain. I was brought in to fix that without the readership noticing the surgery.

<!-- more -->

The constraint that mattered most wasn't typographic — it was trust. A literary quarterly's subscribers are disproportionately the kind of reader who cancels over a font change, and the Review's circulation had already dipped once during a redesign a decade earlier. So the rollout was staged across six issues rather than landing all at once: issue one kept the old masthead but introduced the new twelve-column grid underneath it; issue three swapped the body type; the new masthead didn't appear until issue five, by which point the interior had already quietly become a different magazine.

The system itself is built on a baseline grid tuned to the body type's x-height, with a kicker-and-rule treatment borrowed from the Review's own 1986 launch issue — a detail three separate subscribers wrote in to say they'd recognised. Section openers get a full-bleed initial in Libre Caslon, sized to the page rather than the text, which gives each piece a threshold without needing a photograph to earn it.

Subscriber churn over the rollout period was lower than the previous year's average. The Review's editor now describes the grid, not entirely fondly, as "the thing that makes me lay out an issue in half the time and complain about it twice as much."
