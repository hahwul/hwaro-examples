Nobody defends parking structures, which is what makes Loom Street Deck the most interesting fight in this issue. Halvard Stroud's 1969 garage was condemned for demolition twice, in 2011 and again in 2019, both times on the reasonable grounds that a six-story concrete ramp with no elevator and questionable spalling on the fourth level was not obviously worth the cost of saving. What saved it, in the end, was not preservationists but a produce vendor who leased the ground floor cheaply during a downtown vacancy wave and discovered that Stroud's deep structural bays — sized for cars, at eighteen feet on center — were exactly right for market stalls.

The conversion that followed, completed this year, keeps four of the six levels as parking and turns the ground and second floors into a produce and prepared-food market, with a climbing gym occupying the third level's lower-clearance bays where the structural ramp made car parking awkward anyway. This is adaptive reuse working with a building's constraints rather than against them: the market stalls sit inside the original eighteen-foot structural grid without a single new column, and the climbing gym's route-setters have built directly onto Stroud's exposed waffle-slab ceiling, using the coffers themselves as anchor points, which is either an inspired reuse of embedded structure or a liability lawsuit waiting to happen, depending on which engineer in town you ask.

The market's real achievement is acoustic and thermal, not visual. A parking structure is, by design, naturally ventilated and daylit at every level through the same operable perimeter that once let exhaust escape, and the conversion team leaned into that rather than enclosing it, so the market operates as an open-air shed for eight months of the year with no mechanical cooling at all — a passive-design outcome Stroud never intended but that his structure happens to make possible. The spalling concrete on the fourth level, meanwhile, has been repaired rather than concealed, and you can still read the original repair scars if you know where to look, a small honesty that most reuse projects sand away.

Loom Street Deck will not convince anyone that all parking structures deserve saving. What it proves is narrower and more useful: that a building type designed purely around a dimension — the width of a car, the height of a bumper — can absorb an entirely different program if that program happens to share the dimension. Markets and cars, it turns out, want almost exactly the same amount of room.
