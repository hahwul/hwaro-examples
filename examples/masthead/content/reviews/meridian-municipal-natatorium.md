Public pools rarely survive to be reviewed as architecture; they survive to be closed for a season and then quietly, without ceremony, demolished for a parking lot. That the Meridian Municipal Natatorium made it to its restoration at all is the first thing worth saying about it. Greta Vandenhal's 1962 design wrapped a fifty-meter tank in a folded-plate concrete roof whose ridges run north to south, catching low morning light through a continuous clerestory band and throwing it, rippled and doubled by the water below, across the underside of the folds themselves. It is, unfashionably, a beautiful room to swim laps in.

The restoration, completed after eleven months of closure that the local paper covered mostly as an inconvenience, replaced the entire mechanical plant — filtration, chlorination, the underfloor heating that keeps the deck from sweating in humid months — without touching the folded roof or the original teal-and-cream terrazzo, which was ground back rather than replaced and now shows a honest map of fifty years of foot traffic worn into its surface near the diving well. This is preservation done by engineers rather than by architects, and it is better for it: nobody tried to make the new chlorine plant photogenic, they just put it where the old one was and made it quieter.

The one real design decision in the restoration is the new accessible ramp into the shallow end, which replaces a 1962 ladder-only entry that had made the pool functionally unusable for a meaningful share of the town. The ramp is stainless steel, low-walled, and follows the same folded geometry as the roof above it, so it reads as an addition that understands the building's grammar rather than a compliance afterthought bolted onto the side. It is the best new piece of architecture in the room, and it was built to move water, not to be photographed.

What makes Meridian worth the trip is how little anyone involved seemed to want credit. There is no interpretive plaque, no donor wall, no press release calling this an adaptive reuse story — because it isn't one. It is a municipal pool that stayed a municipal pool, kept working, and got better at its one job. Civic modernism rarely gets to age this quietly, and it deserves more chances to.
