Ines Calloway-Marsh did not design a library so much as a small city that happened to hold books. Basin Point Central Library's 1974 plan stacks six half-levels around a central light well in a section that owes more to a parking megastructure than to any reading room precedent, with the stacks themselves cantilevered into the void on a waffle-slab structure so the whole building reads, from the atrium floor, as an inverted ziggurat of shelving receding upward into a skylight. It is aggressive architecture for a genuinely quiet program, and it has never been anything but divisive in Basin Point, where half the town calls it the town's proudest building and the other half calls it the parking garage that ate the books.

The expansion under construction this year — a new wing on the library's east side, holding a children's collection, a maker space, and the town's first dedicated teen room — was never going to be able to out-argue Calloway-Marsh's original section, and the design team, wisely, did not try. Instead of continuing the waffle-slab cantilevers, the new wing is a single flat-floored volume with a shallow butterfly roof, deliberately calm where the original is deliberately dramatic, connected to the 1974 building by a glazed link that lets you see both structures at once from the connector's midpoint. It is the clearest built argument I have seen for restraint as a form of respect: the addition does not compete with the megastructure, it frames it.

Where the expansion struggles is circulation. Because the new wing sits a half-level off from the original's stacked geometry, the connector includes two short ramps and a lift that together add nearly ninety seconds to the walk between the children's collection and the general stacks, an awkwardness the design team has acknowledged in public meetings and attributes, credibly, to the near impossibility of matching floor heights with a building whose entire logic was built around half-level stacking. It is a real cost, and families with strollers will feel it daily.

Fifty years on, Calloway-Marsh's central library is aging into the thing megastructures rarely get to become: not a folly, not a preservation battle, but simply infrastructure, expanded rather than replaced because the town could not imagine Basin Point without it. That is a higher compliment than most 1974 buildings receive, and this one earns it.
