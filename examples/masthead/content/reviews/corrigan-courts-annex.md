The Corrigan Courts Annex was never going to win a monograph. Priya Osei-Larsen built it in 1971 as an overflow courthouse for a county that had run out of room in its Victorian original, and she made the deliberate choice to clad it in the same soft red brick as the buildings around it rather than reach for the raw concrete then fashionable in civic work. Critics at the time called it timid. Reading her notes in the county archive, timid was exactly the intention: she wanted a courthouse that did not announce the state's authority over a town that already distrusted outsiders, and brick, laid in a running bond with deep raked joints that catch shadow at low angles, was her way of lowering the building's voice.

Half a century later that same modesty is what makes the annex's second life as a housing mediation center work as well as it does. The building's small courtrooms — designed for minor claims and traffic hearings, never for anything with cameras — convert almost without alteration into mediation rooms sized for six or eight people around a table, and the corridor's deep punched windows, which Osei-Larsen oriented east to catch morning light without the glare that plagued the original courthouse across the square, mean none of the rooms need supplemental lighting before noon. The retrofit architects, working with what was clearly a tight budget, wisely spent almost nothing on the shell and almost everything on acoustics, since a building designed for the formal silence of traffic court is a poor fit for the messier, overlapping conversation of a landlord-tenant mediation, and the added ceiling baffles do real, unglamorous work.

If there is a complaint, it is that the building still looks and feels like a courthouse from the outside, down to the flagpole and the granite entry steps worn smooth by decades of subpoenas, and the mediation center's clients — many of them facing eviction, few of them there voluntarily — have told local reporters that the building's unchanged exterior makes the process feel more adversarial than the mediators intend. That is a legitimate criticism of signage and threshold, not of Osei-Larsen's architecture, but it is a reminder that a second life does not automatically erase a building's first reputation. Brick remembers what concrete forgets.
