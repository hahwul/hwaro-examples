Masthead started as a shared grievance: postwar civic architecture gets discussed twice in most towns, once when it is built and once when someone proposes tearing it down, and almost never in between. We exist for the in-between. Every review here looks at a courthouse, pool, library, garage, or trade school built roughly between 1955 and 1980, and asks what it is actually like to use now — whether it has been converted to something else entirely, quietly restored, or simply left to keep doing its original job for a public that has largely stopped noticing it. The full run sits in the [review archive](reviews/); the rubrics we return to most, brutalism, adaptive reuse, and preservation among them, are indexed on the [category page](categories/).

## What we cover

Civic buildings only — no private commissions, no corporate headquarters, no houses, however architecturally significant. If a building was paid for with public money and built to serve a public function, it qualifies, regardless of whether it is celebrated, contested, or scheduled for the wrecking ball. We are especially interested in the buildings currently getting a second life: adaptive reuse projects, sympathetic restorations, and the occasional demolition-deferred structure still arguing for its own survival.

## How a review gets assigned

A critic proposes a building, states plainly whether they have a personal stake in its outcome, and files within six weeks of the site visit. We do not review buildings from photographs alone. Every review that runs here reflects at least one in-person visit, usually two — once before any conversion work and once after, when that is possible.

## The desk

Six critics rotate the weekly assignments: Odalys Marchetti on megastructures and civic modernism generally, Desmond Kwiatek on preservation and restoration practice, Fenella Rourke on regionalist and vernacular civic work, Tobias Sennett on brutalism and its harder-to-love relatives, and two contributing critics who file less frequently but whose bylines you will see in the archive. We disagree with each other often enough that Masthead has no house style of opinion, only a house style of attention: materials, light, structure, and who a building was actually built to serve.

## Get in touch

If you know a postwar civic building worth a second look, especially one mid-conversion or facing a demolition vote, send a note to the desk with the address, the year built if you know it, and what is currently proposed for it. We read everything and reply to most of it, eventually.
