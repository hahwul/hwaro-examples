Parallax remains the only direct method for measuring distances to nearby stars. The principle is simple. Observe a star from two points on Earth's orbit, separated by six months. The shift in apparent position against the background sky is the parallax angle. The smaller the angle, the further the star.

A parallax of one arcsecond corresponds to one parsec, roughly 3.26 light-years. Proxima Centauri shows a parallax of 0.7687 arcseconds, placing it at 1.3 parsecs. Beyond a few hundred parsecs, the angles become too small to resolve from ground-based instruments. Atmospheric blur and sensor noise dominate the signal.

The Gaia mission shifted the baseline. Operating from the Earth-Sun L2 point, Gaia measures parallaxes for nearly two billion stars with microarcsecond precision. The catalog now reaches the galactic core. Distance errors that once exceeded fifty percent now sit below one percent for most targets within ten kiloparsecs.

```text
parallax (arcsec) = 1 / distance (parsec)
```

The technique scales poorly past the local arm. For the Magellanic Clouds and beyond, astronomers fall back on standard candles. Cepheid variables. Type Ia supernovae. Each rung of the cosmic distance ladder rests on the parallax measurements of a few hundred nearby stars. A miscalibration there propagates outward.

The structure holds because the geometry is simple. A triangle, two known sides, one measured angle. Everything else in extragalactic distance estimation derives from that triangle.
