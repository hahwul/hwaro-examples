The long aprons went into their legs this week, drawbored with riven oak pegs and no clamps anywhere in sight. There is a particular sound a drawbore makes when the offset is right — a low creak as the peg drags the shoulder home, then silence, the joint closed as tight as it will ever be. I stood there afterward with the mallet still in my hand, slightly amazed, the way I am every time.

The top is another matter. It is one board, sixteen inches of straight-grained black walnut, and it has a cup in it that I have been sneaking up on for three evenings with the jointer plane. Machine logic says take it all in one pass. Bench logic says the board decides the pace: traverse, check with winding sticks, let it sit overnight, listen to what it did while you slept.

That is the whole discipline of this shop, really. The tools are slow on purpose. The slowness is where the accuracy lives.
