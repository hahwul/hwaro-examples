A wall cabinet is a good winter project: small enough to heat the shop for, serious enough to demand real joinery. This one holds sharpening stones and strops, which means it earns its keep four feet from where it was built.

<!-- more -->

## Case

The carcase corners are half-blind dovetails — tails in the sides, pins in the top and bottom — so the end grain shows only where I chose to show it. Half-blinds are mostly a sawing exercise you finish with a chisel: saw the pin sockets on the diagonal as far as the two gauge lines allow, then chop and pare out the triangle of waste the saw cannot reach. Cherry is kind here. It chops clean, it pares crisp, and it forgives the third-corner fatigue that oak punishes.

The shelves sit in stopped dadoes, routed with a batten and a router plane in the old sense of the word — the wooden kind with a blade like a bent chisel. Two passes per wall, a shaving at a time, until the shelf slides in with hand pressure and stays put without glue.

## Door and back

The door is a frame-and-panel, mortise and tenon at the corners, with a raised panel floating in grooves — fielded with a badger plane, angled across the grain first so the long-grain passes erase the tearout. It hangs on knife hinges let into the case with a chisel, which took longer than the door itself and was worth every minute: a knife-hinged door swings like it weighs nothing.

The back is shiplapped boards, nailed in the center only, each free to move under the next. Blonde shellac, three thin coats, rubbed to satin with steel wool and wax. Cherry will darken from salmon to auburn inside a year; the finish just needs to stay out of its way.
