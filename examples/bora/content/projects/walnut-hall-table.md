The commission is simple on paper: a hall table, 44 inches long, narrow enough to live against plaster in an old house with uneven floors. Everything interesting about the build is in the base. Eight mortises, eight tenons, every one of them drawbored so the frame closes itself without a single clamp.

<!-- more -->

## Stock

The walnut came from a single flitch I bought two years ago and stickered in the loft. Rift-sawn billets for the legs so all four faces show straight grain, and one wide board — sixteen inches, no sapwood on the show face — reserved for the top since the day I unloaded it.

```text
CUT LIST — HALL TABLE
4   legs          1-5/8 x 1-5/8 x 29     rift-sawn
2   long aprons     7/8 x 4-1/2 x 38
2   end aprons      7/8 x 4-1/2 x 11
1   top           13/16 x 15 x 44        single board
8   drawbore pegs  5/16 dia. x 2-1/2     riven white oak
```

## The drawbore

The offset is the whole trick. I bore the peg hole through the mortise cheeks first, assemble the joint dry, mark the tenon through the hole with the bit, then shift the tenon mark a strong 3/32 of an inch toward the shoulder. Riven oak pegs, whittled to a blunt taper, driven with hot hide glue in the joint. The peg bends through the offset and pulls the shoulder in permanently — a mechanical lock that will outlive the glue, the finish, and probably the house.

Legs got a gentle taper on the two inside faces, cut with a jack plane and finished with a spokeshave, starting an inch below the apron line. From across a room the table reads as a straight, quiet rectangle. Up close the tapers give it lift.

## What is left

The top still has a whisper of cup to plane out, then buttons to fasten it — wooden clips riding in grooves inside the aprons so the board can swing through the seasons without cracking. Oil and wax after that. No stain on walnut, ever; the sun will do the coloring better than I can.
