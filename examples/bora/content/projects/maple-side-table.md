The Shakers drew this table two hundred years ago and there is still nothing to remove from it: four tapered legs, four aprons, one drawer, a top that overhangs just enough. The only liberty I took is under the top, where two battens run in tapered sliding dovetails instead of the usual screws in slotted holes.

<!-- more -->

## The tapered slide

A straight sliding dovetail binds along its whole length the moment it starts to close, which is why the old work tapers them. The batten's dovetail key loses a strong sixteenth of width over its run, and the socket in the top loses the same, so the joint is loose for nine-tenths of its travel and then draws tight in the last two inches with a couple of mallet taps.

Cutting the socket across a twenty-inch top is batten-and-saw work: knife lines, a guide batten clamped at the dovetail angle, a fine backsaw riding against it for each wall, then a router plane to level the bottom in three depth settings. The keys were shaped with a moving fillister and a shoulder plane, sneaking to the taper line with tests every pass. Fit dry, no glue except a dab at the front inch — the top shrinks toward the battens and simply grips harder.

## Everything else

The legs taper on the inside faces only, from a full inch and three-eighths to seven-eighths at the floor, planed to the line in about four minutes per face once the jack was set rank. The drawer is half-blind dovetails at the front, throughs at the back, with a pine bottom slid in from the rear and a single walnut knob turned slightly out of round because I turned it by eye and I like knowing that.

Hard maple takes an edge grudgingly and burnishes under the plane like glass. With an amber shellac over oil it goes the color of good honey, and the customer's first question was what wood it could possibly be.
