A blanket chest is the oldest exam in the joiner's book: four wide boards, four corners, nothing to hide behind. Quartersawn white oak made it harder and better — the ray fleck is spectacular and completely indifferent to a wandering saw.

<!-- more -->

## Corners

Eleven tails per corner, laid out with dividers rather than arithmetic, walking the spacing until it looked right and only then striking the baselines with the cutting gauge. I cut tails first, gang-sawing both end boards in the vise to keep the angles honest, then transferred to the pin boards with a knife off the tails themselves.

White oak transfers beautifully and chops miserably. The rays that make the figure also make the wood split ahead of the chisel if you lever carelessly, so waste came out with a coping saw to within a strong sixteenth, and the baseline paring was done in two bites with the chisel dead vertical in the knife line. Off the saw, three corners went together from the mallet alone. The fourth needed two shavings off one pin. I can live with that ratio.

## The till

Every proper chest has a till — the small lidded box inside for the things too small for blankets. Mine rides in dadoes in the front and back boards, and its lid is pinned so that, opened past vertical, it props the main lid: the traditional trick that lets you dig with both hands.

The bottom is white pine, shiplapped and nailed with cut nails into a rebate, free to shrink board by board. Garnet shellac and dark wax on the outside; the inside stays bare so it smells like oak and pine every time it opens, which is the part the client mentions most.
