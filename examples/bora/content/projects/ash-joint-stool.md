This one started as a log. A neighbor lost an ash tree to the borer and I took a straight six-foot section of the butt, split it into quarters with wedges in the driveway, and rived the billets out with a froe like it was 1650. Ash splits along the grain so cleanly that the rived faces barely need the plane — and because riving follows the fibers instead of cutting through them, every stick is as strong as that tree could make it.

## Wet joinery on purpose

The trick of a joint stool is that it goes together green. The rails were worked to size while the wood was still damp, but the tenons were superdried — cooked over the stove pipe for a week until they stopped moving. Then the joint works for you: the wet mortise shrinks onto the dry tenon as the leg seasons, and the joint tightens over its first year instead of loosening.

The stretchers carry wedged through tenons, kerfed before assembly and wedged with dry oak driven in from outside so each tenon dovetails itself inside its mortise. Between the shrink-fit, the wedges, and the drawbored top rails, there is not one fastener and not much glue in the whole stool, and you could throw it off a roof.

## The look of it

The legs were turned to a simple vase pattern on the lathe, left a hair oversize where the pole marks show. Everything else keeps its tool marks: scrub-plane scallops under the seat, riven texture inside the aprons where no hand will ever reach but mine.

Two coats of iron-oxide red milk paint, burnished with a handful of shavings while the second coat was barely dry, then a wipe of linseed oil to deepen it. Milk paint on coarse ash looks a hundred years old by the following Tuesday, which is exactly the point.
