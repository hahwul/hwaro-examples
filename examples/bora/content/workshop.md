The whole shop is eleven feet by nineteen. That sounds like a constraint and it is actually the floor plan's best feature: nothing is more than two steps from the bench, and a shop you can cross in two steps is a shop that stays warm on one small stove.

## The bench

A split-top Roubo in soft maple, built in this room and too heavy to leave it — five hundred pounds of bench for planing that never skates. A leg vise with a wooden screw on the front, a planing stop, and a grid of holdfast holes do all the workholding; the tail vise I planned was never missed. The split in the top swallows a plane on its side and gives clamps a way through the middle of a panel.

## The chest and the wall

Daily tools live in a floor chest at the end of the bench: three bench planes (jack, try, smoother), a shoulder plane, two backsaws, and the chisel roll. Everything else hangs on the wall behind the bench where a missing tool announces itself as a silhouette. The rule is strict — a flat surface that can hold a tool will, within a week, hold forty.

| Plane | Length | Set for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Jack | 14 in | Coarse work, cambered iron |
| Try | 22 in | Straightening edges and faces |
| Smoother | 9 in | Final surface, tight mouth |
| Router | — | Dado and tenon-cheek depth |

## Sharpening and the loft

The sharpening station is a shelf at elbow height under the window: two waterstones, a strop, and a jig I never use. It stays uncovered and ready because a covered stone is a dull shop.

Overhead, the loft holds the lumber — rough boards stickered on edge, ends waxed, each stack labeled with species and the year it came in. Nothing comes down until it has been up there through at least one full summer and one heating season. The wood decides when it is ready. The loft is just where it thinks.
