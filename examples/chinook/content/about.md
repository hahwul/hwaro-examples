I am Wren Halloran, and this is my kitchen: a north-facing galley in a rented
cabin at 1,048 metres, with a temperamental gas oven, a cheap digital scale I
trust more than anything else I own, and a window that fogs the moment the
proofing box comes out. Chinook is where I keep the record.

## Why altitude changes everything

Above a thousand metres the physics quietly rearranges your recipe. Atmospheric
pressure drops, so water boils near 96&nbsp;degrees Celsius instead of 100, and
doughs proof faster because gases expand against less resistance. The air is
also dry, which pulls moisture out of an open crumb long before it reaches the
plate. Left alone, an enriched dough over-proofs, then bakes out dry and
crumbly. The fixes are small and specific: a little less yeast, a little more
water, a shorter final proof, and a lid to trap steam for the first minutes in
the oven.

## How to read a bake

Every entry opens with a bake sheet:

- **Hydration** — total water as a percentage of flour weight, tangzhong
  included. Higher hydration buys tenderness but costs handling.
- **Proof** — bulk fermentation time, then the final proof after shaping. At
  altitude these windows are always shorter than a sea-level recipe claims.
- **Flour** — the blend, because protein content changes how much water the
  dough will take.
- **Altitude tweak** — the one deliberate change from the batch before.
- **Verdict** — an honest one-liner. Some of these say "compost."

The numbers are the whole point. A recipe is a story you tell once; a bake log
is a story you can actually repeat.
