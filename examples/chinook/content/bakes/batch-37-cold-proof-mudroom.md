The recurring problem at 1,048 metres is speed. Warm dough proofs so fast up
here that I am always racing it, shaping while it is already ballooning,
guessing at the final window. This batch I decided to stop fighting the clock
and use the cold instead.

<!-- more -->

The mudroom sits at about six degrees this time of year — colder than a fridge,
steadier than the kitchen. I gave the dough a brief thirty-minute warm start to
wake the yeast, shaped the rolls straight away, then carried the covered pans
out to the mudroom for the night. Cold slows fermentation to a crawl, so the
frantic altitude proof becomes a long, gentle one. Enzymes keep working on the
starches even when the yeast is nearly asleep, and that is where the flavour
comes from.

Fourteen hours later the rolls had risen maybe half again, no more. I brought
them in, let them come up for an hour by the oven while it preheated, and baked
straight from there. The flavour was noticeably deeper — a faint malty
tang under the cinnamon that a rushed warm proof never develops.

One tray was a touch under-proofed and baked a little tight in the crumb; I had
crowded the pan, and the centre rolls did not get their warm hour. Next time
fewer rolls per pan, or a longer bench rest. But the method holds: at altitude,
cold is the brake you have been missing.
