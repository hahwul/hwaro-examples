Forty batches in, and this is the one I will keep. The trick was not more
water thrown at the dough — I have tried that, and at altitude it just makes a
slack mess that proofs before you can shape it. The trick was cooking some of
the water in first.

<!-- more -->

Tangzhong is a water roux: you whisk a portion of the recipe's flour with about
five times its weight in liquid and heat it to 65&nbsp;degrees Celsius, until it
thickens to a loose paste. The starch gelatinises and traps the water so it
cannot flash off during the bake. Up here, where the dry air is forever pulling
moisture out of an open crumb, that locked-in water is exactly what keeps a roll
tender an hour after it leaves the oven.

I cooked 60&nbsp;g of the flour with 300&nbsp;g of milk, cooled it to room
temperature, then built the dough around it: strong highland flour for
structure, a little cake flour to soften the bite, one egg, butter worked in
last. Effective hydration landed near 78%, but the dough handled like something
far drier — smooth, elastic, easy to roll thin.

Bulk was quick, as always at this elevation: one hour fifty in the proofing box
and it had nearly doubled. I shaped, filled, and gave the pan a mercifully short
final proof — forty-five minutes, no more, because an over-proofed enriched
dough collapses into gumminess the moment it hits the heat.

The lid stayed on for the first eight minutes to trap steam — the trick from
[batch 15](/bakes/batch-15-cast-iron-lid/) — then off to let the tops take
colour. When I pulled them, the tops had that faint crackle I have
been chasing, and the spiral pulled away in a single soft ribbon. I left one on
the counter as a control. Six hours later it was still tender. Batch 41 goes in
the permanent file.
