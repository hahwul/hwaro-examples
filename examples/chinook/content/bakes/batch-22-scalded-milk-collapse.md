Scalding milk — heating it to just under a simmer, then cooling it — denatures
the whey proteins that otherwise tighten a crumb. It is a classic move for soft
rolls, and it did soften this dough noticeably. Then I got greedy with the final
proof and paid for it.

<!-- more -->

Here is the log, because the whole point of this site is that I write the
failures down too:

| Time  | Step                          | Note                          |
|-------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 07:10 | Mix, dough at 26°C            | Slack but workable            |
| 08:50 | Bulk done, ~doubled           | Faster than I expected        |
| 09:05 | Shaped, into the pan          | Twelve rolls, snug            |
| 10:10 | Final proof still going       | Looked puffy — I waited more  |
| 10:20 | Into the oven                 | Fifteen minutes too late      |
| 10:47 | Out — and already sinking     | Centres caved on cooling      |

At altitude the final proof is the knife's edge. The dough had actually been
ready at 9:55; the extra twenty-five minutes let the gluten stretch past what it
could hold, so when the heat hit, the structure had nothing left in reserve and
the rolls rose, stalled, and fell. On cooling they wrinkled inward like
deflating balloons. The crumb, where it survived, was gummy.

This is the exact over-proof trap I first named back in
[batch 8](/bakes/batch-8-why-rolls-dry-out/): thin air rushes the rise, and a
wetter dough rushes it further. The scald was not the villain — it did its job.
The lesson is that a softer,
wetter dough proofs even faster, so it needs a shorter window, not a longer one.
I have written **55 minutes, hard stop** on the recipe card and underlined it.
Batch 22 fed the compost. Batch 23 will not.
