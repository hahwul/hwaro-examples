The dry mountain air does its worst in the oven. Without moisture the tops of
the rolls set within the first couple of minutes, locking the crumb before it
can spring, and the surface bakes to something closer to a biscuit. Batch 15 was
about giving the tops time.

<!-- more -->

Steam is the whole secret behind bakery-soft crusts, and up here you have to
bring your own. I set the pan of rolls inside a wide cast-iron braiser and put
its heavy lid on for the first eight minutes. The dough's own moisture
evaporates into that closed space and keeps the surface humid, so the tops stay
supple and pliable while the interior does its rising. Then the lid comes off
for the last twelve minutes to let the tops take an even, gentle colour.

The difference was obvious the moment I lifted the lid: the rolls had sprung
taller and the surfaces were smooth and slightly glossy rather than dull and
crazed. Off came the lid, and in twelve minutes they browned to the shade of
weak tea without ever drying out. Pressed with a fingertip, the tops gave and
came back — the faint crackle-then-give I keep chasing.

Hydration was fairly conservative here at 71%, which in hindsight left a little
tenderness on the table; the lid clearly gives me room to push wetter. That is
the thread I pulled all the way to the tangzhong batches. But batch 15 is where
the steam problem finally got solved.
