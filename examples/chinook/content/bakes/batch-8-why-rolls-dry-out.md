This is the batch where the penny dropped. I had carried a trusted sea-level
recipe up the mountain and watched it fail eight times in a row: fast, chaotic
proofs and rolls that were fine warm but sandy and dry within a couple of hours.
Batch 8 is where I wrote down the diagnosis instead of the disappointment.

<!-- more -->

Three things change above a thousand metres, and all of them dry out an enriched
dough:

- **Lower pressure.** Gases expand harder, so the dough proofs faster and can
  over-proof before you notice, spending its structure early.
- **A lower boiling point.** Water leaves as steam at around 96&nbsp;degrees
  Celsius instead of 100, so the crumb loses moisture sooner in the bake.
- **Dry air.** Mountain humidity is low, and a fresh crumb keeps giving up water
  to the room long after it has cooled.

My sea-level recipe, in baker's percentages, was the wrong starting point:

```text
flour ......... 100%
water/milk .... 62%
butter ........ 14%
sugar ......... 12%
egg ........... 10%
yeast .......... 2%   # far too much for this elevation
salt ........... 2%
```

Everything after batch 8 is an argument with those numbers. The water goes up
and gets partly cooked into a tangzhong so it cannot escape. The yeast comes
down on a formula. The final proof gets shorter and, when I am patient, colder.
A lid goes on to trap steam. None of it was mysterious once I stopped treating
the oven as haunted and started treating the air as the ingredient it is.

I keep this entry pinned in my mind as the baseline: 68% hydration, too much
yeast, no lid, dry by noon. Every batch since has been chipping away at that
sentence.
