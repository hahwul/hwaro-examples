I wanted the filling to taste like the inside of a good toffee, so I browned the
butter first — cooked it past melting until the milk solids turned the colour of
an old penny and the kitchen smelled like caramel. It worked. It also taught me
why bakers respect a dry filling.

<!-- more -->

Browned butter is butter with the water cooked off and the milk solids toasted.
Whisked with brown sugar and a heavy hand of cinnamon, it made a paste that
smelled incredible spread across the rolled dough. But I kept the sugar ratio
from my old soft-butter filling, and browned butter cannot hold as much syrup in
suspension. During the bake the excess sugar liquefied, ran to the bottom of the
pan, and caramelised straight past amber into bitter. The rolls themselves were
the best-tasting yet; the bottoms were lacquered to the tin.

At altitude this is worse than it sounds. Lower boiling points mean sugar syrups
concentrate and colour faster, so a filling that merely browns at sea level can
scorch up here in the same minutes. The fix for next time is straightforward:
cut the added sugar by a fifth, add a spoon of flour to the filling to bind the
melt, and line the pan bottom with a round of parchment as insurance.

Filed under delicious near-misses. The flavour is staying; the puddle is not.
