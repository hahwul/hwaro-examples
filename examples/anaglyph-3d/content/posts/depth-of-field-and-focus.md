Not everything should be in focus.

This is a counterintuitive statement in an era that prizes clarity, resolution, and the *4K everything* aesthetic. But shallow depth of field — the soft blur behind a sharp subject — is one of the most powerful compositional tools available to anyone who makes images.

It is also a philosophical position.

---

## The Optics

Depth of field is the zone within a scene that appears acceptably sharp. Everything outside this zone blurs into **bokeh** — a Japanese word for the quality of out-of-focus light.

The size of this zone is controlled by three variables:

- **Aperture** — a wider opening (lower f-number) creates shallower depth of field
- **Focal length** — longer lenses compress depth more aggressively
- **Distance** — the closer you focus, the shallower your depth of field becomes

A portrait shot at f/1.4 on a 85mm lens at two meters isolates a face so precisely that one eye can be sharp while the other falls softly out of focus.

> "Blur is not the absence of sharpness. Blur is a decision. Everything outside the frame of focus has been told: you are not the subject."

---

## Depth of Field as Editorial Choice

When a photojournalist photographs a person against a blurred background, the visual hierarchy is unambiguous: *this person matters, the background does not*. When a food photographer renders a single strawberry tack-sharp against a soft wash of red, they are staging desire.

Depth of field does not merely describe space. It **assigns value**.

This is why the shallow depth of field aesthetic spread so aggressively beyond photography into:

1. **Interface design** — blur layers (like iOS's frosted glass) establish Z-depth visually
2. **Video game rendering** — games simulate shallow DOF to direct player attention
3. **Typography** — layering text at different perceived depths guides the reading eye
4. **Illustration** — digital painters fake lens blur to create photographic realism

---

## The 3D Connection

Anaglyph stereoscopy and depth of field speak the same language: *distance from the viewer*.

In a stereoscopic image, near objects have high disparity (large horizontal offset between the two channels). Far objects have low disparity. The brain converts this disparity into perceived depth.

In a shallow DOF image, near and far objects blur according to their distance from the focal plane. The brain reads this blur as spatial information.

Both are **depth cues** — signals that the visual cortex uses to construct a three-dimensional model of a flat image.

---

## Practical Applications for Flat Design

You can use DOF logic without a camera:

- Apply `blur()` filters sparingly to background elements
- Use lower opacity and reduced contrast for elements meant to read as "farther away"
- Stack box shadows to simulate objects at different planes
- Reduce saturation on background elements (atmospheric perspective)

The goal is the same as the photographer's goal: **tell the viewer where to look**.

---

## A Note on Sharpness

Here is the thing about sharpness: it exhausts.

A design where everything competes for attention is a design where nothing receives it. Sharpness is currency. Spend it where it counts.

`focus_depth = intention / noise`

Red is sharp. Cyan is sharp. The dark space between them is where depth lives.
