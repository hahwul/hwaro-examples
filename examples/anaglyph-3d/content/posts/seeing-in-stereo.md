Your brain is doing something extraordinary right now. Two flat images — one slightly left-shifted, one slightly right-shifted — are being merged by your visual cortex into a single perception of *depth*. No computation required. No GPU. Just wetware honed by hundreds of millions of years of evolution.

That miracle is what anaglyph 3D exploits.

---

## The Physics of Two Eyes

Human eyes are set roughly 63 millimeters apart. This gap — called **interpupillary distance** — means each eye receives a slightly different view of the world. Your left eye sees a little more of the left side of an object; your right eye sees a little more of the right. The visual cortex fuses these two images and *infers depth* from the difference.

This is **stereopsis**. And it is the basis of everything we do here.

### How the Glasses Work

Anaglyph images encode both views in a single print using color channels:

- The **left-eye view** is filtered to red
- The **right-eye view** is filtered to cyan

Your red lens blocks cyan and passes red — so your left eye sees only the left-eye image. Your cyan lens blocks red and passes cyan — so your right eye sees only the right-eye image. Your brain does the rest.

> "The anaglyph is not a trick. It is an instruction to the brain. The brain follows that instruction perfectly, every time."

---

## Why Chromatic Aberration Feels Right

Imperfect lenses split white light into its component colors, displacing red and cyan channels by fractions of a millimeter. This is chromatic aberration — a flaw that photographers spend thousands of dollars correcting.

But notice what it *looks like*: red fringing on one edge, cyan fringing on the other. It looks like an anaglyph slightly out of register.

It looks like depth.

This is why chromatic aberration became an aesthetic rather than just an error. It *signals* depth to a brain primed for stereoscopic cues. Our eyes read color fringing as spatial information.

## Building Depth into Flat Design

You do not need physical glasses to use stereoscopic logic in design. Consider:

1. **Offset shadows** — red shadow left, cyan shadow right, creates the impression that text floats above the page
2. **Layered borders** — stacking box-shadows at different offsets suggests planes at different depths
3. **Color temperature contrast** — warm reds advance, cool cyans recede
4. **Motion parallax** — elements that shift at different rates on scroll read as occupying different Z-planes

None of these require 3D hardware. They all speak directly to the brain's depth-processing instincts.

## The Retro-Cinema Connection

The peak of anaglyph 3D was the 1950s. *Creature from the Black Lagoon* (1954), *House of Wax* (1953), *Dial M for Murder* (1954) — these films were not using 3D because they had to. They were using it as a *statement*.

The statement was: *this is not flat. This is not ordinary. Look closer.*

That is still the statement worth making.

`depth = perception + intention`

Design that pops is design that demands presence. Red on the left. Cyan on the right. Eyes open.
