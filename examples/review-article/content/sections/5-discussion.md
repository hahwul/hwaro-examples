## Principal Findings

This systematic review and meta-analysis of 24 RCTs (N = 8,412) provides moderate-certainty evidence that digital CBT-I effectively reduces insomnia severity in adults. The pooled effect size (SMD = -0.82) corresponds to a large clinical effect, with meaningful improvements in both self-reported insomnia severity and objectively measured sleep efficiency.

## Comparison with Face-to-Face CBT-I

The effect size for dCBT-I (SMD = -0.82) is somewhat smaller than the estimated effect for face-to-face CBT-I (SMD = -1.09, from Trauer et al. 2015), but the confidence intervals overlap substantially. The subgroup analysis showing larger effects for therapist-supported dCBT-I (SMD = -0.94) suggests that human support may partially close this gap.

## Clinical Implications

1. **First-line recommendation:** dCBT-I can serve as a first-line treatment for uncomplicated insomnia, consistent with recent guideline updates from the European Sleep Research Society
2. **Stepped care:** Fully automated dCBT-I is appropriate as a first step, with therapist-supported dCBT-I or face-to-face delivery reserved for non-responders
3. **Comorbid conditions:** Evidence is insufficient to recommend fully automated dCBT-I for patients with comorbid psychiatric disorders; therapist-supported models should be preferred
4. **Access:** Digital delivery can substantially expand access in underserved populations, reducing the gap between clinical need and therapist availability

## Limitations

1. **Heterogeneity:** Substantial heterogeneity across studies limits the precision of the pooled estimate. Sources include variation in comparator type, platform design, and population characteristics.
2. **Blinding:** Blinding of participants is inherently difficult in behavioral interventions. Self-reported outcomes may be influenced by expectation bias, particularly in waitlist-controlled trials.
3. **Attrition:** Four studies had high attrition rates (>20 pct), which may bias results if dropout is differential.
4. **Long-term follow-up:** Only 12 of 24 studies reported follow-up beyond the intervention period. The sustainability of dCBT-I effects beyond 6 months remains uncertain.
5. **Representation:** Most included studies were from high-income Western countries. Generalizability to low- and middle-income settings is unknown.

## Future Research Priorities

- Head-to-head trials comparing dCBT-I platforms to establish which program features drive effectiveness
- Long-term follow-up studies (12+ months) with objective sleep measures (actigraphy)
- Trials in low- and middle-income countries to assess cross-cultural transportability
- Individual participant data meta-analysis to identify patient-level predictors of treatment response
- Cost-effectiveness analyses comparing dCBT-I with pharmacotherapy and face-to-face CBT-I
