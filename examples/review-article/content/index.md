<section class="abstract-box">
  <h2>Abstract</h2>
  <dl>
    <dt>Background</dt>
    <dd>Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is the first-line treatment recommended by clinical guidelines, but access remains limited. Digital delivery (dCBT-I) may overcome barriers of cost, geography, and therapist availability.</dd>
    <dt>Objectives</dt>
    <dd>To estimate the efficacy of dCBT-I compared with control conditions on insomnia severity, sleep efficiency, and daytime functioning in adults, and to assess the certainty of the evidence using the GRADE framework.</dd>
    <dt>Methods</dt>
    <dd>Systematic search of PubMed, PsycINFO, Cochrane CENTRAL, and Embase through December 2025. Eligible studies were RCTs comparing dCBT-I (app, web, or automated program) with waitlist, treatment-as-usual, or active control in adults (age 18+) with insomnia symptoms. Risk of bias assessed with Cochrane RoB 2.0. Random-effects meta-analysis with restricted maximum likelihood estimation.</dd>
    <dt>Results</dt>
    <dd>24 RCTs (N = 8,412 participants) met inclusion criteria. dCBT-I significantly reduced insomnia severity versus control (ISI: SMD = -0.82, 95 pct CI: -0.98 to -0.66, p &lt; 0.001; k = 22). Sleep efficiency improved by 6.8 percentage points (95 pct CI: 4.9 to 8.7). Effects were sustained at 6-month follow-up (SMD = -0.64, k = 12). GRADE certainty was moderate for insomnia severity and low for daytime functioning.</dd>
    <dt>Conclusions</dt>
    <dd>dCBT-I is effective for reducing insomnia severity and improving sleep efficiency in adults, with moderate certainty evidence. Implementation should prioritize therapist-supported models for patients with comorbid conditions.</dd>
    <dt>Keywords</dt>
    <dd><em>systematic review; meta-analysis; insomnia; CBT-I; digital therapy; PRISMA; GRADE; sleep efficiency</em></dd>
  </dl>
</section>

4. **Section 4. GRADE Evidence Profile** -- certainty of evidence assessment with detailed rationale
5. **Section 5. Discussion** -- clinical implications, limitations, and future research directions
