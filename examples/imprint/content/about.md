Imprint publishes once a year, in a single volume, on the history and practice of bringing old letterforms back into working use. We are not a foundry and we do not sell type. Every essay here treats a revival — a redrawn face, a recut punch, a working relationship between a historic design and the century that needs to set it again — as an argument worth reading closely, not a specimen sheet worth admiring quietly. The current volume's five [specimens](/specimens/) run from a 1929 Caslon recutting to a century-long mix-up over whose hand actually drew Garamond.

## Editorial method

Each essay is commissioned from a single scholar with direct archival access to the material under discussion: punch collections, specimen books, correspondence, or the working notes of a foundry's drawing office. We do not run anonymous or aggregated pieces. Every essay carries one byline, one credential, and one argument, because a revival is always somebody's specific decision, made under specific constraints, and we think the writing should be answerable in the same way.

Essays are filed under exactly two kickers. **REVIVAL** marks a historic typeface redrawn for a later century — the work of bringing a design back. **FOUNDRY** marks the working practice of the house or workshop that cut a design the first time, before any question of revival existed. A single face can appear under either kicker in different volumes, depending on which question a given essay is asking.

## Submissions

We read proposals from typographic historians, working type designers with archival grounding, and bibliographers year-round, with a commissioning deadline of the fifteenth of January for the following volume. A proposal should name the specific archival material the essay will draw on — we do not commission surveys. Query the editor before sending a full draft, and browse the [subject index](/tags/) first to see which foundries and centuries the current volume already covers.

## Colophon

This volume is set in Fredoka for display and Inter for text, on a page built to the proportions of a hand-bound spread rather than a screen. The palette is ivory and indigo throughout: warm, undyed paper against a single cold accent, on the theory that an annual about restraint in letterforms should not argue against itself in its own margins. Essay openers carry the volume's one signature mark — an outlined specimen glyph, the size of a full margin, standing in for the drop cap a printed edition would set in the same place.
