John Baskerville came to printing late, in his forties, after making a fortune in japanned ware in Birmingham, and he approached type the way a manufacturer approaches a product line rather than the way a scribe approaches a letter. He was not satisfied to cut a new roman. He built a hot-rolling process to smooth his paper to a glassy finish, formulated his own ink from a recipe he refused to disclose, cast type with sharper counters than the wove paper of the day could normally hold without the ink bleeding into them, and pressed the whole assembly hard enough that the impression itself became part of the letterform's final shape.

This is the detail every isolated Baskerville revival struggles with: the crispness people praise in his type was never purely a property of the punches. It was the product of the punches meeting paper and ink and pressure that Baskerville controlled as tightly as the letters themselves, four variables solved together rather than one variable solved and shipped to whatever printer happened to buy the type. Contemporary printers who bought Baskerville's matrices but printed on their own ordinary stock with their own ordinary ink got a noticeably softer, muddier result — recognizably the same design, and recognizably worse, because three-quarters of the system that made it work had been quietly left behind.

Baskerville's own countrymen were, famously, unmoved. English printers of the 1750s found the contrast harsh and the glossy paper vain; it was continental admirers, and eventually Bodoni and Didot themselves, who took the transitional sharpness further into the high-contrast romans that came to define the following half century. Baskerville's type sold better as an influence abroad than as a product at home, which is its own quiet lesson about how revivals travel: rarely along the route their maker would have chosen, and rarely intact.

What this means for anyone drawing a Baskerville revival today is a harder problem than matching the letterforms. A screen has no paper to hot-roll and no ink to bleed predictably into a sharp counter; the crispness has to be rebuilt inside the outline itself, hinted and spaced to fake, at every size, the specific collision of press and stock and formula that Baskerville spent a fortune perfecting and never wrote down. The types that succeed at this are not really typeface revivals. They are quiet, unglamorous engineering revivals — machine problems solved again for a machine Baskerville never imagined, in service of a coldness he built his whole workshop to produce on purpose.
