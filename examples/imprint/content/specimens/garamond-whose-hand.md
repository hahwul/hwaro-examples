In 1927, the Imprimerie Nationale in Paris published a specimen it believed to be a faithful printing of Claude Garamond's original sixteenth-century roman, cut for the Crown a full generation before Jenson's Venetian influence had fully worked its way into French printing. Type historians and foundries across Europe treated the specimen as the closest thing to primary evidence anyone was likely to get, and a wave of "Garamond" revivals followed it closely for the next several decades, each one a serious, careful attempt to bring a masterwork back into working use.

<!-- more -->

The specimen was not Garamond's. Later archival research, comparing the punches held in the Imprimerie's own collection against securely dated books, established that the type in question had actually been cut in the 1620s by Jean Jannon, a Protestant printer working nearly a hundred years after Garamond's death, in a style that consciously imitated the earlier master's manner without being his hand. The mistake was not a minor cataloguing slip. It had been treated as the foundational evidence for an entire generation's understanding of what "Garamond" looked like, and by the time the correction was widely accepted, the revivals built on it — most consequentially the version cut for hot-metal composition and later adapted for photocomposition and digital use — had become so thoroughly the working definition of the name that correcting the record barely dented their use.

What makes this worth dwelling on is not the error itself, which is a fairly ordinary archival mistake with an unusually long half-life. It is what the error reveals about how a revival earns its authority in the first place. Nobody chose the Jannon-based version because they had compared it letter by letter to Garamond's actual punches and preferred it. They chose it because an institution with the standing to be believed said it was Garamond, and that institutional say-so did more work in establishing the revival's legitimacy than the letterforms themselves ever could have done alone.

There is now a genuine, well-documented revival of Garamond's actual punches, drawn from type securely attributed to his hand and held in archives that postdate the 1927 mistake. It is, by most informed accounts, a genuinely different letter from the "Garamond" most working designers still reach for — narrower, sharper in the serifs, less generously spaced. Almost nobody uses it. The name Garamond, at this point, refers less to a set of curves than to a century of accumulated institutional habit, and habit turns out to be the more durable inheritance.
