William Caslon cut his roman in the 1720s with no ambition to be beautiful. He wanted a face that stayed legible at eight and ten and twelve point, that forgave bad ink and worse paper, and that a working house could cut in the quantities ordinary printing actually consumed. What he got was a lower case slightly heavier than the cap height wanted, serifs that thicken and thin in ways no ruling pen would allow, and a page texture that eighteenth-century printers came to trust the way a mason trusts a stone he has already dressed once. "When in doubt, use Caslon" was never a compliment to any single letterform. It was a compliment to the grain of the whole page, built sort by sort from a hand that was consistent without ever being identical.

In 1929, Monotype's drawing office went back to Caslon's own specimen sheets to prepare a version for the composition caster — a machine that could not forgive the tolerances an eighteenth-century punchcutter took for granted. Every sort had to sit on a fixed unit width, repeatable to the thousandth of an inch, cast thousands of times over from the same matrix without drift. That single requirement did more to shape the revival than any aesthetic judgment did. Where an irregularity read as character — the slightly high-shouldered R, the stubby descender on the g — the drawing office kept it and drew every instance of that sort to match it exactly, turning a hand-cut accident into a mechanically enforced rule. Where an irregularity was simply noise, two different widths for the same letter surviving from two different pages of specimen, it was quietly resolved in favor of whichever version served the composition caster's arithmetic.

That is the part of the story revival histories tend to skip: a revival is not a redrawing of a typeface so much as a redrawing of a *tolerance*. The original had none, in the modern sense — every punch was its own small decision, cut by hand against no fixed standard but the eye of the man holding the graver. The revival needed one absolutely, because a caster running at speed cannot ask which Caslon R the compositor meant. Somewhere in that gap, an argument about authenticity got settled by a mechanical engineer's spec sheet, and mostly nobody noticed.

> The revival did not ask what Caslon's letters looked like. It asked what a Caslon letter could still be, once every copy of it had to be exact.

Which is why the twentieth century ended up with several unrelated typefaces all called Caslon, each one true to a different part of the original and false to the rest — a friendlier one for magazine display, a spikier one that kept every irregularity the composition caster smoothed away, and the workhorse text version most of us have actually set a book in without knowing which decisions we inherited. None of them lied about being Caslon. They simply each told a different truth about what "the same typeface" is allowed to mean.
