Nicolas Jenson cut his roman in Venice in 1470, printed a handful of books with it, and left behind no notes on why any letter takes the shape it does. What survives is the type itself: a roman so evenly colored across the page, so free of the calligraphic hesitations that still marked most early printing types, that four centuries of type historians have treated it as the moment printed letters stopped apologizing for not being handwriting. Jenson never explained the achievement. He simply set it, sold the books, and died a few years later, leaving the type to be admired by everyone who could get access to a surviving copy and reinvented by nobody for a very long time.

William Morris got his access in 1888, at an exhibition in London, and by his own account decided within the hour that he would build a type from it. He did not have Jenson's punches, his matrices, or his working drawings — nobody did, and nobody does now. What he had was a photograph of a single printed page, enlarged, from which his collaborator traced the outline of each letterform by hand before Morris redrew it again himself, correcting what he judged to be the wear of the individual impression rather than a feature of the design. Golden Type, the result, is therefore not a copy of Jenson's roman in any strict sense. It is Morris's theory of what Jenson's roman must have looked like before four hundred years of printing history got in the way of one surviving page.

That theory is visible everywhere you look for it once you know to look. Morris's revival is heavier than Jenson's original — partly a considered choice, suited to the handmade paper and dense ink of the Hammersmith press, and partly, historians now generally agree, an artifact of tracing a worn impression and unconsciously thickening every stroke that ink spread had softened. The serifs are blunter. The color of the page is darker. None of it is wrong, exactly; all of it is Morris, filtered through a single Venetian page and arriving somewhere Jenson himself never printed.

Every twentieth-century Jenson revival since has had to choose which layer to answer to: Jenson's actual, elusive 1470 metal, or Morris's confident, well-documented, and quietly inaccurate 1890 theory of it. Most choose Morris, because Morris left a complete working font and Jenson left a rumor. The roman most designers mean when they say "Jenson-inspired" today is, more honestly, Morris-inspired at one further remove — a revival of a revival, still called by the earlier name because the earlier name is the one that carries the weight.
