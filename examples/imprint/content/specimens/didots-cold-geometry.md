Walk from a page of Caslon to a page of Didot and the first thing you notice is not the contrast, though the contrast is real — hairlines thinned nearly to the point of disappearing, stems thickened to compensate, serifs flattened into bracketless slabs. The first thing you notice is that the page has stopped looking handmade. Every curve answers to the same rule. Every counter is the same shape at every size. It is a typeface that has been argued into existence rather than drawn, and that was exactly the point.

<!-- more -->

The Didot foundry worked through the second half of the eighteenth century inside a broader French project to rationalize measurement itself — the same intellectual climate that gave the country the metric system gave typefounding the point system and, not long after, a letterform built to the same standard of abstraction. Where Caslon's punchcutters worked from the eye and the graver, refining a shape across years of cutting the same sorts over and over, the Didot approach started from a geometric skeleton and cut the metal to match it. The result reads less like handwriting reduced to type and more like a mathematical proof rendered in lead: elegant, exact, and faintly hostile to the idea that a letter should carry any trace of the tool that made it.

That hostility is worth sitting with, because it is easy to mistake Didot's contrast for mere fashion — the neoclassical taste for hard edges, arriving in type the way it arrived in furniture and architecture. But the more interesting claim buried in the foundry's practice is a claim about authority. A hand-cut letter is answerable to the punchcutter who made it; you can see his hand in it, and a different punchcutter would have made a defensibly different letter. A geometrically constructed letter is answerable only to the construction. Nobody's hand is legible in it because the whole point was to remove the hand from the equation, to make the letterform an argument that any sufficiently careful geometer would arrive at independently.

{{ alert(type="note", body="The point system Didot helped standardize is still the reason a 12pt line in most page-layout software measures roughly 4.23mm rather than a tidy 4mm — a small daily reminder that the eighteenth century's rationalism never quite finished the job.") }}

Modern revivals of Didot's romans tend to soften exactly this quality, adding warmth back into curves the originals worked hard to keep cold, because a typeface that reads as pure argument is exhausting at book length and unreadable at small sizes on anything but excellent paper. But the display sizes still carry the original ambition intact, which is why Didot's descendants remain the house style of fashion mastheads and perfume labels a quarter of a millennium later: nothing communicates that a claim has been thought through, rather than felt, quite like a hairline serif that refuses to vary by a hundredth of a millimeter.

The English contemporary who reached the same conclusion by a completely different route, engineering rather than geometry, is the subject of [Baskerville's Quiet Machine](/specimens/baskervilles-quiet-machine/) elsewhere in this volume.
