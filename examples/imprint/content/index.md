Every revival is an argument dressed as a typeface. Somebody looked at a worn punch, a foxed specimen sheet, or a single surviving proof and decided which parts of it were the letterform and which parts were just what four hundred years does to metal and paper — then cut new steel to defend that decision.

This volume follows five of those arguments: Caslon recut for Monotype composing machines it was never drawn for, Didot's cold geometry, Jenson's roman carried a false four centuries by William Morris, Baskerville's obsessive machine of paper and ink and letter together, and the Garamond that turned out to be signed by the wrong hand entirely. None of the five agree on what a revival owes the original. Read them in that disagreement.
