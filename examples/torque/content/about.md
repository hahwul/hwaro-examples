Torque is seven people, one converted rail depot outside Two Harbors,
Minnesota, and — for a few more months, until the second machine arrives —
one three-axis mill doing all of the cutting.

## How it started

Mira Solheim spent eleven years machining flight brackets and fixture
tooling for an aerospace supplier before she got tired of parts she'd never
see finished leave the shop on a truck. Andres Kade wrote keyboard firmware
as a hobby for a decade, mostly for boards he'd bought and gutted himself,
and had strong, specific opinions about switch travel that no case on the
market seemed to share. They met in 2018 through a mutual friend, and Mira
machined a one-off case for a PCB Andres had already designed, mostly to see
if she still remembered how. It fit on the first try. By early 2019 they'd
signed a lease on the depot and ordered the mill.

## The shop today

The building was a Great Northern Railway freight depot before it was
anything else, and the original loading dock still runs along one side of
the machine floor — we use it to stage aluminum bar stock, which is either
a nice bit of continuity or just the closest flat concrete to the door,
depending who you ask. The Anvil, our original three-axis mill, has been
rebuilt twice and still does every finishing pass on every case that ships.
A second mill is on order to handle roughing separately, which should push
weekly output past the forty-board ceiling we've been running against for
the better part of a year.

## What we believe

We publish tolerances instead of adjectives, and we'd rather a board arrive
a week late than arrive with a plate that doesn't sit flush. We don't do
seasonal colorways or limited drops timed to a hype cycle — Charcoal, Bone,
and Rust are the finishes, and they're the finishes because the anodizing
tank runs those three colors well, not because a fourth would sell out
faster. And every board is designed to be opened: four screws, no glue, no
plastic clips that snap once and never seat the same way twice. If a switch
dies in six years, you should be able to fix it yourself with a switch
puller and a spare from the parts store, not a reason to buy a new board.

## What's next

The current experiment on the bench is a split variant — two TQ-60-sized
halves on a single low-profile stack, wired through a shared controller in
the right half — that Andres has been typing on daily since late last year
to see whether the shoulder relief is worth the extra machining complexity
before we commit tooling to it. No release date yet, and we're not going to
publish one until the drawing is actually final; see
[what's shipping today](/keyboards/), and [get in touch](/support/) if
you'd like to be notified when that changes.
