Most keyboard cases start as pellets. Ours start as a bar of 6063 aluminum
that gets cut off, faced, and bolted to a table before a single tool touches
it. That difference — subtraction instead of injection — is the whole
company, and it shows up in the tolerance, the weight, and the way a Torque
board sounds when you set it down.

### Subtractive by conviction

Molding is cheap at scale because the expensive part happens once, in the
tool, and every unit after that is nearly free. That math works against you
the moment you want to change something: a revised plate geometry means a new
tool, a new supplier quote, a new six-week wait. A milling program is a text
file. When we shortened the TQ-75's front bezel by three millimeters last
spring, the change went from CAD to cutting aluminum in an afternoon, on the
same mill, with the same fixture. We chose subtractive manufacturing because
it keeps the shop able to fix its own mistakes fast, not because it looks
better in a product photo — though it does that too.

### Small batch, on purpose

One shop floor, one three-axis mill we've nicknamed the Anvil, and a second
machine on order for whenever the backlog outgrows patience. That caps
output at somewhere around forty boards a week, which is a real constraint —
we've turned away retail orders to keep lead times honest — and also the
reason every case that leaves here has been checked by the same two people
who checked the last thousand. Scaling past a single shop floor is the
easiest way to stop knowing what "normal" looks like on your own product. We
are in no hurry to find that out.

### We don't outsource the drawing

Every dimension on this page — the 45 grams, the 1.2 millimeters, the
6063-T5 designation — comes off the same machining drawing that runs the
mill, not a marketing rewrite of it. If a number changes, it changes on the
drawing first, and the drawing ships in the box. We would rather a customer
catch us in an inconsistency between what we cut and what we printed than
have that gap exist at all.
