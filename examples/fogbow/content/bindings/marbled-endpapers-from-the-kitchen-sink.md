Every case binding on this bench eventually asks for endpapers, and the shop-bought marbled sheets are either beautiful and ruinous or affordable and dead. So in July, with the windows open and the kitchen commandeered, I floated my first size and found out why marblers speak about weather the way sailors do.

<!-- more -->

## The size

Marbling happens on the surface of a thickened bath — the size — and the size forgives nothing. Too thick and the colours sit in tight sulky dots; too thin and they billow out to ghosts. This is the mixture that finally behaved:

```text
size    : 20 g carrageenan blended into 4 L soft water, rested overnight
mordant : alum, 40 g in 1 L warm water, sponged onto each sheet
paper   : Aldermill laid, 100 gsm, cut to 380 x 280 mm
colours : violet lake and an iron-gall grey, let down with ox gall
tools   : a straw whisk, two pipettes, and a comb of pins at 12 mm
```

The alum is the quiet hero. It is struck onto the paper the evening before, and it is what persuades the colour to stay put when the sheet is lifted. Skip it and the whole pattern slides off in the rinse like a reflection when the water moves.

## Twelve sheets, two keepers

The first drops of violet hit the size and bloomed to the width of a hand — a small astonishment I recommend to anyone. Then the grey went down, then more violet into the grey, and the comb pulled the whole surface into a nonpareil: rows of feathered arches, tight as sewing.

Ten of the twelve sheets went wrong in instructive ways. Dust landed. A drip of plain water blew a pale crater in the pattern. I sneezed. The two keepers dried on the airer over the bath, and they now sit pressed under the lexicon waiting for a book worthy of them — one of them eventually sided the [violet quarter-goatskin journal](@/bindings/quarter-goatskin-journal-in-violet.md).

Marbling is bookbinding's gambling problem: every sheet is a pull of the lever, and two wins in twelve felt like riches.
