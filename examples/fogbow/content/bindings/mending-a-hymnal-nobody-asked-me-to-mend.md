It cost one pound fifty and it rattled. A pocket hymnal, 1911 by the flyleaf inscription, sewing gone to powder, half the sections loose in the case like teeth in an old comb. Nobody asked me to mend it. Nobody was going to. That is, increasingly, my favourite kind of commission.

## Pulling the text block

A resew starts with unmaking. The case came away whole, and then each section was pulled in order — old thread snipped at the kettle, lifted out, the spine folds cleaned of glue residue with a dull knife and a little methylcellulose. I keep the pulled thread in a saucer until the end of the job. It is no use to anyone, but throwing away a century-old sewing before its replacement exists feels like bad manners toward the first binder.

The folds themselves were the real damage: forty of the ninety leaves had split at the spine from acid and enthusiasm. Each split fold got a guard — a strip of toned kozo tissue, pasted thin, folded over the break — and then an hour under light boards. Guarding is the most monotonous work in repair and the most honest: one leaf at a time, no shortcuts available at any price.

## Sewing it back

The original had been sewn on cords, cheaply, two stations doing the work of four. I resewed on three narrow linen tapes with an unbleached 25/3 thread, finer than my usual, because a pocket book with ninety guarded leaves swells like bread if you sew it fat. Kettle stitches at head and tail, tension checked against the brick — snug, never strangled.

The original case went back on over new mull and a fresh hollow, with the old spine strip pasted over the new like a saved postage stamp.

> Repair is just attention with tools. The book tells you what it needs; the discipline is not doing more than it asked.

It sits on the shelf now between the field guides, closing with that soft, definite sound a sound book makes — a small congregation of pages, back in order, ready for another century of Sundays.
