The book arrived in a biscuit tin, which tells you how it was loved: a 1958 cloth-case cookery book, spine split at both joints, the case hanging on by the mull and by habit. My grandmother's pencil is in the margins — *more butter than this* beside the pastry, a firm *no* across an entire chapter on aspic.

## Lifting the old cloth

A reback keeps everything original that can be kept. The old spine cloth is lifted with a lifting knife, slowly, the way you peel a label you intend to keep — a millimetre of patience at a time, dry where possible, with the barest whisper of moisture where the old animal glue argues. Both cover cloths get lifted back from the joints about 20 mm, opening a pocket for the new material.

The text block itself was sound. Sewing intact, sections tight; only the spine linings had failed, gone to brittle amber flakes that brushed away like autumn. New linings went on in wheat-starch paste: a kozo tissue first, then an airplane linen, then a paper tube — a hollow — so the spine can breathe when the book opens.

## What the stains keep

The new spine piece is a dyed cotton toned three shades darker than the faded case, because a repair should agree with the book, not with the book as it was in 1958. It slid into the lifted pockets, the old spine cloth was pasted back over it, and the whole book went into the press overnight between clean boards.

The one thing I would not touch: the shiny patch on the back cover where it sat too near a stove for thirty years, and the ring where a hot pan once rested. Conservators talk about reversibility — never do what cannot be undone — but there is a homelier rule underneath it. The damage is the biography. Mend the function, keep the story, and return the book able to be cooked from for another fifty years of Sunday afternoons.
