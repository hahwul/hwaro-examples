February in Greyhaven is one long fog, and fog weather is sewing weather: the paste dries too slowly for casework, so the bench turns to structures that need no glue at all. This notebook is the first entry in the journal because coptic sewing is where I would tell anyone to start — the whole structure is visible, every mistake is legible, and the finished book opens dead flat, which is everything a notebook is asked to do.

## The cutting list

Everything begins with the grain running head to tail. Paper folded against its grain fights the fold forever; folded with it, the sheet goes quiet.

```text
signatures : 6 signatures of 4 folios, A5 folded to A6
paper      : Aldermill laid, 120 gsm, grain long
boards     : 1.5 mm millboard, cut 3 mm proud at head, tail, fore-edge
thread     : 18/3 linen, waxed twice over the block
stations   : 4, pierced at 12 / 38 / 64 / 90 mm from the head
needles    : two, curved, size 18
```

The piercing template is a strip of scrap card, folded once, holes punched through the crease. One template for all six signatures, or the chain wanders and the spine tells on you.

## What the chain teaches

Coptic is sewn with a needle at each end of the thread, and the link is made by hooking under the stitch of the signature below — a chain of small hands, each one holding the wrist of the last. There is no swell to knock down, no spine to line, nothing to hide behind. If your tension drifts, the chain leans; if it holds, the spine reads like a row of braids.

Mine leaned, slightly, between the fourth and fifth signature, where the kettle came out of rhythm. I left it. A bench journal that hides its errors is a catalogue, and this is not a catalogue.

The boards got a coat of slate-violet cloth and nothing else. Six evenings, start to finish, most of them spent listening to the foghorn out past the estuary and pulling thread until it sang.
