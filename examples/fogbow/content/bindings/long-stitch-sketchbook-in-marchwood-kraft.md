A friend who draws outdoors asked for a sketchbook that could be dropped in a rucksack, rained on, and opened one-handed on a stile. That is a long-stitch brief if I have ever heard one: a single folded cover of heavy kraft, slots cut where the sewing passes through, and the thread itself doing all the decoration the book will ever need.

<!-- more -->

## Slots, not holes

Long-stitch covers are pierced with slots — narrow windows the width of the signature's swing — rather than round holes. The slot lets the signature hinge without tearing the cover, and it turns the spine into a little loom: each pass of thread lies flat across the window, and the pattern of passes is the visible face of the book.

I cut the slots with a scalpel against the brass triangle, four rows for four signatures, and pierced the head and tail kettle stations with the straight awl. The cover is Marchwood kraft, 300 gsm, folded twice at the fore-edge for a doubled flap that stiffens the whole wrap without a single board.

## The sewing

The paper is a 150 gsm cartridge, grain long, folded into four signatures of five sheets. Heavier paper means fewer sheets to a signature — fold too many and the inner pages creep forward, and the plough is not welcome on a sketchbook with deckle pretensions.

The thread is the same 18/3 linen as always, but run in a dark plum that sits against the brown kraft like a hedgerow in November. Long-stitch shows every decision, so I sewed a simple ladder with a chain link at head and tail and resisted the urge to be clever. The stitch pattern on a spine is like handwriting on an envelope: it should say *someone made this*, not *look at me*.

Finished in an afternoon, which is the other virtue of the structure. Some books want a fortnight of paste and press. Some want to be sewn before the kettle boils twice.
