Fogbow is kept by Nell Farrier, who learned book repair the honest way — by ruining three paperbacks under the supervision of a retired library binder named Mrs. Okonkwo, who said *"paste, not haste"* so often it now plays in my head at the exact tempo of a brush crossing a spine.

<!-- more -->

The bench is a fire door laid across two trestles in the small room that was supposed to be a study. It holds, at any given moment:

- a bone folder worn to the shape of my thumb, inherited and irreplaceable;
- two awls — one straight for piercing stations, one bent for coaxing thread through stubborn holes;
- 18/3 linen thread, run twice over a block of beeswax before every sewing;
- a litre jar of wheat-starch paste in the fridge, labelled so nobody puts it on toast;
- a brass triangle and a shoemaker's knife, both older than me;
- whatever book is currently in pieces, held in a folded towel like a patient.

## Why "fogbow"

A fogbow is the ghost of a rainbow — the same physics, with the colour scattered out of it by droplets too small to sort the light. It is my favourite kind of result: correct, quiet, and almost invisible. A well-repaired book should be a fogbow. If you can point at the mend from across the room, I did too much.

## How the pages are set

The journal is arranged like the books it describes. Entries carry folio numbers and running heads; the sewing pattern in the spine margin is the same four-station layout I use on most of my coptic work, and each heading sits against a cross-stitch, the way a kettle stitch catches at the head and tail of a section. Titles are set in Crimson Pro, the text in Figtree, and the violet throughout was matched to a scrap of dyed goatskin that has lived on the bench since the [quarter-leather journal](@/bindings/quarter-goatskin-journal-in-violet.md) was finished.

Everything here is hand work, done slowly, and written down the same evening while the paste brush is still drying.
