A page is a small building. The headline is the lintel. The paragraphs are the rooms. The negative space is the courtyard. Walk through enough pages and you start to feel which ones are well-built and which ones are houses of cards.

The lintel matters most. Choose it carelessly and the building collapses.

## The display face

We set every headline in [Archivo Black](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Archivo+Black). It is heavy, condensed, sans-serif, and uncompromising. It was designed for posters and signage — for the kind of typography that has to hold its weight from across a room.

There are alternatives, and we considered each:

- **Inter Black** is softer. Reads as polite, not heavy.
- **Roboto Black** is industrial in a different way — too even, too round.
- **Space Grotesk** has weight but introduces geometric quirks we wanted to avoid.
- **Pragmatica Extended** would have been our second choice if not for its complicated licensing.

The choice is not arbitrary. It is the choice of a load-bearing material.

## The body face

The body is set in Inter. Inter is the closest the open-source ecosystem has to a perfectly neutral text face. It reads as transparent on screen, which is what we want — the body must disappear so the lintel can do its work.

## The mono face

JetBrains Mono is reserved for the metadata. Dates, section labels, byline strips, navigation. It is the steel rebar of the building — visible only when the architecture is intentionally exposed.

## Sizes

We do not believe in modest type. The display face appears at sizes ranging from 40px to 220px, depending on the context. The body sits at 16px–18px. There is no in-between. Either type is announcing itself or it is delivering content. Mid-sized type is a sign that the publication has lost its conviction.
