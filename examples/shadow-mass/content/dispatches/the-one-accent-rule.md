Most publications maintain a color palette of three to five colors. We maintain a palette of two: ink and void. To this we permit one accent, and only one.

That accent is sulfur yellow.

## What it does

Sulfur is not a decoration. It is a verb. It marks the parts of the page the reader is not allowed to ignore. The headline of the issue. The current section in the navigation. The blockquote that carries the thesis. The link the reader is expected to follow now, not later.

When everything is colored, nothing is colored. When everything is loud, nothing is loud. By restricting ourselves to one accent, we force every appearance of color to carry a verdict.

## What it refuses

Sulfur is not used for:

- Decorative borders on cards.
- "Visited link" colors.
- Hover states that exist to entertain.
- Logo treatment.
- "Brand consistency."

The accent serves the reader, not the brand. It is a tool, not a flag.

## A useful test

Before adding a colored element to a page, ask: *does this color carry meaning that the type alone cannot?* If the answer is no, the color does not earn its place. The page stays in ink and void.

This is not a stylistic preference. It is a rule. And like all good rules, it produces work that looks effortless and is, in fact, the result of constant refusal.
