The grid is the load-bearing wall of any honest layout. Strip away the decoration, the gradients, the rounded corners, and what you are left with is the grid — or its absence.

Brutalist publications begin, almost without exception, on a twelve-column grid. Twelve is the number that lets you divide cleanly into halves, thirds, quarters, and sixths without surrendering to the tyranny of a single rhythm. You can stack two equal columns, three equal columns, or four — and you can break the rhythm precisely *because* the rhythm is so explicit.

## The trap of prettiness

The trap that most designers fall into is treating the grid as an alignment device. They snap their elements to the columns, then add padding, then add rounded corners, then add subtle gradients to "soften the look." By the time they are done, the grid has become invisible — and so has the publication.

A brutalist grid is *visible*. The rules between columns are drawn. The negative space is measured and labeled. The reader is invited to understand the architecture of the page, not just live inside it.

### How we keep it honest

1. **One internal rule.** A single hairline color, used consistently for all internal divisions. We use a 1px line in deep grey.
2. **No padding wars.** Every cell uses the same padding. If the design calls for a larger cell, it spans more columns — it does not get a different gutter.
3. **Numbers visible.** Every grid cell on the front page carries an ordinal. The reader can count.
4. **Color used sparingly.** A cell only acquires color when it acquires meaning. Otherwise it stays in the void.

The result is a page that admits its own bones. Concrete admitting it is concrete. The grid admitting it is a grid.
