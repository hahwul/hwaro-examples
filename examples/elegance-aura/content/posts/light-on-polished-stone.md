Polished stone is, optically, a partial mirror. A slab of honed marble returns perhaps fifteen percent of the incident light at the specular angle; a slab brought to a high polish returns thirty percent or more, with a measurable image of the source. The remainder is scattered diffusely through the body of the stone, picking up the colour of the matrix and the veining, and emerging at the surface as a soft glow whose angular distribution is broader than that of the specular return.

The implication for interior layout is straightforward. A polished stone surface lit by a hard source &mdash; a downlight, an unshaded lamp, a window with direct sun &mdash; will produce two distinct visual artefacts: a sharp reflection of the source itself, and a halo of diffuse scatter around it. Both compete with the visual content of the surface. The veining of the stone, which is the reason for choosing it, is washed out by the scatter and crossed by the specular streak.

The remedy is not to dim the source but to soften it. An indirect cove, a fabric shade, a frosted diffuser &mdash; any of these spreads the source across a larger solid angle, which lowers the peak intensity of the specular return below the threshold at which it competes with the diffuse field. The veining recovers; the surface reads as stone rather than as mirror.

A second remedy, complementary to the first, is to reduce the polish. A honed finish at four hundred grit returns roughly half the specular component of a polished finish at three thousand grit, with no perceptible loss of colour saturation. Most surfaces in the present layout are specified at the honed finish for this reason. The polished finish is reserved for vertical panels in shadow, where the specular return cannot find a hard source to mirror.
