A glass panel set into a frame is a small problem in optics. The frame is opaque; the panel is transparent; the join between them carries the load and the eye both. The temptation, in the contemporary work, is to thicken the frame to the point where the glass becomes an inset, a window cut from a wall. The older convention, which this layout follows, treats the frame as a thin restraint &mdash; a line drawn around the panel rather than a wall set behind it.

The thin frame is the harder discipline. It must hold the panel under load without bowing; it must absorb the small movements of the surrounding structure without cracking the glass; and it must do so in a section narrow enough not to compete with what is shown through the panel. The standard solution is a milled aluminium section with an internal rebate for a silicone gasket. The gasket carries the panel; the aluminium carries the gasket; the eye sees only the line.

A frame of this kind is finished in one of two ways. The first is a brushed natural anodise, which takes the colour of the surrounding metal and disappears against it. The second is a matt black, which reads as a drawn line at any distance. The polished or coloured anodise, common in the catalogues, has no place in the work of restraint.

The panel itself is float glass, between four and eight millimetres thick depending on the bay. The edges are ground and polished; the corners are radiused to two millimetres to prevent chipping at the gasket. No coating is applied to the surface. The transmission is left to the glass.

What the panel shows is, properly, no concern of the frame. The frame's only task is to hold the glass without being seen.
