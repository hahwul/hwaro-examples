A marking gauge's fence rides against the reference edge of a board while a wheel cutter scores a line at a fixed distance from it — simple, except that on nearly every gauge on the market the fence bore is centered on the beam, which means the fence itself blocks the line of sight to the reference edge exactly when you need to watch it track. Gauge bores the fence off-axis, low on the beam, so the top edge of the fence sits below eye level through the whole stroke.

## Why the wheel matters more than the beam

Most of the redesign effort actually went into the cutting wheel, not the fence everyone notices first. The wheel is beveled on one face only, like a proper marking knife, so it scores a clean line and steers itself into the reference face rather than wandering — a double-beveled wheel, common on cheaper gauges, has no such bias and needs constant hand correction to track straight. The wheel is also removable without tools, since a wheel this small dulls fast in end grain and the studio would rather someone swap it than stop using the gauge.

The beam is brass rather than the usual beech or maple, chosen for the weight: a heavier beam wants less push force to keep the wheel engaged, which matters most on long crosscut layout lines where hand fatigue is what causes the line to wander at the far end.

## Field notes

Six units are out with joiners for a season of layout work before the studio commits to a production run. The open question is the thumbscrew: the current blackened-steel screw locks reliably but is slow to adjust one-handed, and a cam-lock alternative is being tested in parallel on two of the six units.
