Most block planes are sold with a mouth opening set at the foundry and never touched again, which is fine for softwood and wrong for anything figured or interlocked. Sole ships with the mouth cast slightly closed, then hand-filed to final width on the bench against a test cut in curly maple — the wood in the shop most likely to tear out if the opening is wrong.

## The knob problem nobody mentions

The front knob on a block plane exists so the palm can push straight down the length of the tool without the thumb sliding off the front edge. On most planes it's centered on the sole for casting convenience, not palm convenience — thumbs land forward of center on a tool this size. Sole's knob is offset 6mm forward, and testers stopped noticing the knob was there at all, which for a control surface counts as high praise.

The lever cap uses a cam rather than a screw, so changing the blade's depth of cut is a lever flip, not a screwdriver trip back to the tool bag. The cam was the one change that took three prototypes to get right — too little clamping force and the iron chatters, too much and depth adjustment gets stiff.

## Field notes

The A2 iron holds an edge roughly twice as long as the O1 iron used in the first two prototype rounds, but it's slower to hone freehand — testers without a honing guide reported rounding the bevel on the first sharpening. Batch 3 now ships with a honing guide compatible with the iron's 32mm width, included at no extra cost.
