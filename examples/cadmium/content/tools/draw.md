A spokeshave's two wings are supposed to register the sole flat against the wood on both sides of the cut, but on a tapering curve — a chair spindle, a tool handle, a bicycle fork blade in wood — one wing rides higher than the other for most of the stroke. Draw's wings are sprung independently instead of sharing one leaf spring, so each wing self-adjusts to the surface instead of rocking the whole tool off true.

## Casting the body in bronze

Bronze was chosen over the cast iron used in nearly every historic spokeshave for two reasons: it doesn't rust from hand sweat over a long carving session, and it's soft enough to true the sole by hand-scraping instead of requiring a surface grinder, which matters for a shop that makes twenty of these at a time rather than twenty thousand. The sand-casting is done in-house in a crucible that holds enough bronze for four bodies per pour.

The blade sits at a 45° pitch, steeper than the 40° common on vintage shaves, because the studio's testers — mostly chairmakers working green wood — reported cleaner results on end-grain-heavy curves at the steeper angle, at the cost of slightly more push force on long straight strokes.

## Field notes

Batch 2 is a field-test run of fourteen units, most placed with chairmakers who agreed to log blade adjustments over a full spindle-turning project. The independent wing springs are the item under closest watch — if the springs prove to need more than an annual retension, the next revision will move to a stiffer bronze leaf rather than the current steel one.
