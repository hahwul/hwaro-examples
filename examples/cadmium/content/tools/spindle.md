Cone wrenches have to be thin — thick enough to not flex under torque, thin enough to fit between a hub cone and locknut that are sometimes less than a millimeter apart. Spindle holds 3mm of chromoly flat stock through the shaft and tapers to 2.2mm at both jaw tips, which is thinner than most stamped equivalents without sacrificing the torque rating.

<!-- more -->

## Reading the wrench by feel

Both ends of a 15mm cone wrench look identical at a glance, but the jaw opening differs by fractions of a millimeter between the front and rear cone on most hub designs, and grabbing the wrong one rounds a cone fast. Spindle etches the exact jaw dimension into the steel next to each end and anodizes a small window in cadmium yellow around the correct-fit mark, so a mechanic can identify the right end by color in a dim shop or by feel in the dark, since the anodized window sits slightly proud of the surrounding steel.

The shaft carries a shallow flute down its length — not for grip, but to keep the wrench from acting like a sail and skating sideways off the flat when high torque is applied close to the jaw.
