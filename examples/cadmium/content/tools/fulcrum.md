Stock bypass shears put the pivot bolt wherever the stamping die left it — usually dead center of the blade stock, because that's cheapest to punch, not because it's where a hand wants leverage. Fulcrum moves the pivot 8mm back, toward the second knuckle of the grip hand, which shortens the lever arm on the cutting side by the same distance without touching blade length or edge geometry.

<!-- more -->

## What moved and why

Eleven gardeners tested three pivot positions over a season of real pruning, not bench trials. The 8mm-back position won on two measures: less hand fatigue after forty-plus cuts in a row, and a cleaner bypass action on stems near the shears' rated 22mm capacity, where a longer lever arm on the old geometry let the blades twist slightly instead of shearing straight through.

The spring is phosphor-bronze rather than the usual steel coil, because a coil spring that's been rained on for two seasons starts to bind, and a bound spring is the single most common reason a good pair of shears gets replaced instead of resharpened. Phosphor-bronze doesn't rust; it work-hardens slightly with use, which if anything improves the return action over the first year.

## Field notes

Batch 6 corrected one issue from batch 5: the pivot bolt was torqued at the foundry to a spec that was too tight for cold-weather use, stiffening the action below about 5°C. Batch 6 ships with a lower factory torque and a printed card showing the quarter-turn adjustment range, since the pivot is meant to be tuned by the user, not treated as a fixed fit.
