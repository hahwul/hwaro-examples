Cadmium is the studio of Petra Solberg, an independent industrial designer who redesigns hand tools that most people assume were finished being designed decades ago — pruning shears, block planes, bicycle wrenches, marking gauges. The studio runs out of a single bench in a converted sailmaker's loft, with a milling machine, a small foundry crucible for bronze fittings, and a drawer of grip casts taken from the hands of gardeners, joiners, and bicycle mechanics who agreed to squeeze a block of modeling clay so the studio could measure where a hand actually wants a tool to be.

## How a redesign starts

Every project at Cadmium begins the same way: not with the blade, not with the finish, but with the grip. A tool is held before it is used, and most hand-tool ergonomics were set by stamping-die economics in the 1950s and never revisited. Petra's process is to cast the hand first, model the grip second, and let the mechanism — pivot, sole, jaw, wheel — follow from wherever the hand ends up. That is why every [case study in the tools archive](../tools/) opens with an exploded drawing: the grip is drawn first, and everything else is shown coming apart from it.

## Materials and manufacture

Cadmium tools are made in small batches — typically twelve to twenty units — using processes a one-person shop can actually run: forged or machined tool steel for cutting edges, sand-cast bronze or ductile iron for bodies, and hand-finished hardwood or cork for grips. Nothing is chromed, because chrome hides tool marks that matter for regrinding. Every tool ships with the spec sheet shown on its case-study page, so an owner — or a repair shop decades from now — can source a replacement iron or re-cut a worn jaw without guessing at tolerances.

## Commissions and visits

Cadmium takes a small number of commissioned redesigns each year, usually for trade guilds or workshops with a specific tool that has never fit the hands actually using it. The loft is open to visitors by appointment on Thursdays; bring the tool you think needs rethinking, and expect to leave a plaster cast of your hand behind.
