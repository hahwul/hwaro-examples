# Optics of the Mirror Pavilion

The Mirror Pavilion at the centre of the riverside meadow is the most photographed installation on the Kaleidoscope grounds, and also the most misunderstood. This year we sat down with the build crew to walk through how it actually works.

## Three Surfaces, Two Angles

A traditional kaleidoscope uses two mirrors set at a fixed angle to produce its symmetric pattern. The Mirror Pavilion extends that idea to architectural scale by using three first-surface reflectors arranged into a wedge. Two of the surfaces meet at sixty degrees, which produces sixfold symmetry in any object placed between them. The third surface closes the wedge from above, multiplying the apparent depth of the scene.

Visitors walking through the pavilion become the object. The arrangement turns each person into a hexagonal bloom that shifts as they move.

## Why First-Surface Glass

Standard household mirrors carry their reflective coating on the back of the glass, which means light passes through the glass twice and produces a faint secondary image. At the scale of the pavilion this ghosting becomes obvious. We specified first-surface aluminium-coated panels with a magnesium fluoride overcoat, which keeps the reflection sharp and shifts the colour balance very slightly cool.

## Avoiding the Hot Spot

Large reflective surfaces oriented toward the sun can concentrate enough light to scorch grass or damage skin. The pavilion roof tilts forward by twelve degrees to direct any focused beam upward and away from the ground. A radiometer on the apex monitors irradiance during peak hours and triggers a shade screen if readings approach the safety threshold.

## Best Time to Visit

The interior is at its most striking thirty minutes before sunset, when warm light enters at a low angle and the symmetry pattern picks up the colours of the surrounding meadow. Early morning works for a cooler palette. Midday is the least interesting time, although it is the safest if you are bringing small children.
