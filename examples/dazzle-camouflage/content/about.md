## The Observer

This blog is written from inside the problem. I am a design historian with a consuming interest in disruptive visual systems — camouflage, op-art, Vorticism, and every tradition that treats the eye as something to be broken rather than satisfied.

Dazzle camouflage is the model. Not the kind that hides — the kind that **confronts**, that overwhelms the observer's ability to parse motion and form. The ship is not invisible. The ship is *illegible*.

---

## The Subject Matter

The posts here cover a specific intersection:

- **WWI and WWII naval camouflage** — dazzle painting, its inventors, and its contested effectiveness
- **Vorticism and the British avant-garde** — Wyndham Lewis, Edward Wadsworth, and the geometry of aggression
- **Op-art and perceptual disruption** — Bridget Riley, Victor Vasarely, and the science of visual confusion
- **Contemporary applications** — CV dazzle, adversarial patterns, and camouflage in the age of machine vision

> "The object of dazzle painting is not to make a ship invisible but to puzzle the enemy as to her course." — Norman Wilkinson, 1917
