You stand at the threshold of the Meat Cathedral — a vast, breathing megastructure raised not from stone but from the willing and unwilling flesh of the faithful.

The doors are not wood. They are cured hides stretched over a frame of interlocked scapulae. When they part, the smell is not incense. It is iron, salt, and the sweet rot of devotion.

Inside: pillars of lashed ribs rise into a vault of stretched fascia and polished sternum. The floor pulses beneath your feet with the slow, vast rhythm of a single, ancient heart. Tallow chandeliers swing from chains of tendon. The light is the color of fresh hemorrhage.

This is no metaphor.

The walls are meat. The congregation is meat. The god is meat.

The sermons you will find in the **Butcher's Pulpit** were not written. They were carved — with cleaver and trembling hand — into the living tissue of this place. Each one a cut. Each one a prayer. Each one a promise that when the final harvest comes, we will be found tender.
