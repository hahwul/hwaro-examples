The confessional is not a box.

It is a mouth.

The booth is formed from the stretched hide of a single penitent who volunteered for the honor three centuries ago. His skin was tanned over decades in the smoke of rendered offerings until it became translucent and tough as vellum. The frame is his own ribcage, opened and sprung like a grotesque fan. The kneeler is a mandible — polished smooth by generations of knees and the occasional tooth that has fallen out during particularly fervent confessions.

When you enter, you are already inside something that was once alive.

You kneel. The mandible creaks. Above you, the "screen" is a lattice of canines and incisors wired together with silver thread harvested from the fillings of the faithful. Through the gaps you can see the outline of the priest — or what the cathedral has made of him.

He has no eyes. They were given to the reliquary. In their place are two wet sockets lined with tiny, vestigial teeth that click when he is thinking.

"Speak, child," he says. His voice comes from deep in the meat of his throat. "Confess with your teeth."

You open your mouth.

The first lie always comes easily. "I have kept the fast. I have not tasted the unclean cuts."

The priest is silent. Then there is a soft, wet sound — like meat being peeled from bone.

A small panel in the dental screen slides open. A tongue — long, pink, and covered in tiny barbs — extends through the gap and touches your lower lip.

It tastes the lie.

The tongue withdraws. The priest sighs, a sound like air escaping a punctured lung.

"Again," he says.

This time the truth comes. It always does.

"I took the ear of my neighbor while he slept. I told myself it was for the collection plate. But I kept it. I keep it still, in a box beneath my bed, and sometimes at night I press it to my own ear and listen to the sounds it still remembers."

The priest is pleased. You can hear it in the way his remaining teeth grind together — a slow, appreciative gnashing.

"The ear is a holy thing," he murmurs. "It heard the voice of the Lord in the cries of the offering. You did well to preserve it."

He pauses.

"But you lied first. That requires penance."

The panel opens wider. Something wet and heavy is pushed through — a strip of meat, still attached at one end to something deeper in the booth. It quivers.

"Bite," the priest commands.

You lean forward. Your teeth meet the offering. It is warm. It is familiar. It tastes of copper and regret.

The priest begins to pray as you chew.

His prayer is the litany of teeth:

"Blessed are the molars, for they grind the truth from bone."

"Blessed are the incisors, for they sever the lie from the tongue."

"Blessed are the canines, for they pierce the heart of denial."

When you have swallowed, he speaks again.

"Your penance is this: for seven nights you will sleep with your mouth wired open by the vergers. Every sound that tries to become a lie will be caught between your teeth and turned into a hymn. Do you accept?"

You nod. Your mouth is full of the taste of your own sin and the cathedral's mercy.

As you leave the booth, the stretched skin of the door brushes your face like a kiss. Outside, the other penitents wait in the nave, their mouths already wired, their eyes bright with the joy of those who have been truly seen.

One of them smiles at you. Her teeth are stained red.

She has been here longer than you.

She will be here longer still.

The cathedral keeps its own.
