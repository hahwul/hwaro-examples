The bell rings three times. Not for the Trinity — for the three stages of bleeding out.

The congregation rises as one organism. The pews are upholstered in cured dermis; they creak like old leather when the faithful stand. Many of them no longer have feet. They balance on the polished knobs of their own tibias, wrapped in strips of donated skin.

I descend from the pulpit carrying the paten.

It is not gold. Gold would melt in the heat of true devotion. It is a scapula, bleached and burnished until it shines like mother-of-pearl. Upon it rests the Host.

The Host is still moving.

A thin membrane stretches across its surface, rising and falling with the memory of breath. When I raise it, the congregation moans in perfect unison — a wet, glottal sound that begins in the chest and ends in the teeth.

"The body of Christ," I intone.

"The body of Christ," they answer, voices thick with saliva and hunger and love.

I lower the blade — a consecrated cleaver whose handle is wrapped in the hair of the last bishop — and make the first incision. The host does not resist. It yields the way a lover yields.

A bead of clear fluid wells up. Not wine. Something sweeter. Something that used to be fear.

I turn the paten toward the first row. A woman whose face is a lattice of old sutures opens her mouth. Her tongue has been split in three — one for each Person. I place the warm fragment upon the center lobe.

She closes her eyes. Tears of rendered fat trace the seams of her cheeks.

I move down the line. Each communicant receives a different cut. Some receive muscle, still fibrillating. Others receive gristle that pops between their teeth like tiny prayers. One child is given a piece of the original Host — the very first slab we consecrated when the cathedral was only three walls and a dream. It is gray now, and tough, but the child chews with the solemnity of one who understands he is eating history.

The cup follows.

It is a chalice carved from the inside of a skull — the occipital bone thinned until light passes through it like stained glass. The wine inside is not fermented. It is *drained*, collected each dawn from the collection gutters that run beneath the flensing tables.

I lift the skull-cup.

"The blood of Christ."

They drink. Some must be helped; their hands have been offered to the reliquary long ago. The vergers tilt the cup for them. Red runs down chins and necks, disappearing into the open collars of vestments that were once wedding dresses and burial shrouds.

A man in the fifth pew begins to convulse. This is normal. The spirit is strong in the blood tonight. Two deacons gently restrain him while a third collects the foam from his lips in a silver bowl — it will be used to anoint the next batch of converts.

When the last communicant has been fed, I return to the pulpit.

I look out over the congregation — two hundred souls who have given everything and received everything in return. Their eyes are bright. Their wounds are open. Their bellies are full of the only truth that matters.

I raise my blood-slick hands.

"Brothers. Sisters. The table is never empty."

"Because we are the table."

"Because we are the meal."

"Because we are still warm."

The lights — the great tallow chandeliers — gutter and flare as if in answer.

Somewhere deep in the crypt, the foundation diaphragm contracts once, a vast and tender sigh.

The Eucharist is finished.

But the hunger remains.

It always remains.

And that, my children, is the true miracle.
