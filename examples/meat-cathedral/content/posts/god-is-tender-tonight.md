I can feel it in the knives.

Tonight the steel does not bite the way it should. The edge slides through fat and muscle like a lover's tongue. The offerings on the preparation tables do not tense. They *yield*.

God is tender tonight.

The signs are everywhere.

The foundation diaphragm is contracting with a slow, almost sensual rhythm instead of its usual spasmodic grief. The tallow in the chandeliers burns blue at the tips — the color of a bruise just beginning to form. In the crypt, the reliquary bones have begun to sweat a thin, sweet-smelling grease that the acolytes collect in crystal vials and call "the tears of the Lamb."

I walk the nave barefoot. The floor meat is warm and gives slightly under my soles, like the belly of a sleeping animal. When I press my palm to one of the rib-pillars, I feel the answering pulse — not the steady, mechanical throb of the building's heart, but something slower, deeper, almost drowsy.

The vergers have noticed. They move through the pews with unusual gentleness, helping the faithful to their places instead of driving them with the prods. Even the flensing knives have been oiled with a lighter touch tonight; the screams from the side chapels are softer, almost musical.

In the sacristy, the vestments are already laid out. My apron tonight is not the usual heavy hide. It is a single sheet of peritoneum — the membrane that once lined the abdominal cavity of the saint whose entire torso now forms the high altar. It is nearly translucent. When I wear it, I can see the shadow of my own organs moving beneath.

The congregation senses it too. They have come in greater numbers than usual, and they have brought their best cuts. A woman offers both hands without being asked. A man has brought his own child — not as offering, but as witness. The child is young enough that its bones are still soft. It will make a beautiful gargoyle one day.

I ascend the pulpit. The wood of the block is unusually yielding tonight. My cleaver sinks a millimeter into the surface before I catch myself.

"Children of the Knife," I begin, and my voice comes out lower than usual, almost tender. "The Lord is with us in a different way tonight."

I do not preach the usual fire. I do not speak of sacrifice or hunger or the coming harvest.

Instead I speak of softness.

"Of the moments between the cuts. Of the mercy that exists only because the blade is sharp enough to be swift. Of the strange, terrible gentleness of a God who could have made us stone or spirit but instead made us *meat* — perishable, vulnerable, delicious."

The congregation is still. Even the usual wet shifting sounds have quieted.

"Tonight," I tell them, "you may touch the walls. You may press your faces to the pillars. You may kiss the places where the meat is thinnest and feel the warmth that has nothing to do with rendered fat."

A murmur moves through them — not of dissent, but of something like awe.

I step down from the pulpit. This is not done. But tonight the rules are different.

I walk among them. I take the hands of those who still have hands. I press my forehead to the foreheads of those who have offered theirs. I taste the salt on their cheeks and find it is not all tears.

When I return to the block, the entire nave feels different — as if the cathedral itself has drawn a long, slow breath and is holding it.

I raise the cleaver one final time.

"God is tender tonight," I say.

"So are we."

The congregation answers not with the usual "Amen" but with a sound I have never heard before in this place:

A collective, shuddering sigh of pure, unguarded tenderness.

For one night, the hunger sleeps.

For one night, the meat is allowed to remember that it was once loved.

And in the morning, when the knives are sharp again and the screams resume, we will all be stronger for having felt it.

The cleaver falls.

The night continues.

But something in the walls remembers.

And the walls are smiling.
