The organ has no pipes.

It has lungs.

Seventy-three pairs of them, suspended in the triforium behind carved screens of sternum and cartilage. Each pair once belonged to a chorister who volunteered for the final aria. They are kept inflated by a system of bellows worked by the novitiates — children whose own lungs will one day join the instrument.

When the organist — a blind man whose fingers have been replaced with sharpened phalanges — touches the keys carved from vertebrae, the lungs contract.

And the cathedral sings.

Tonight the choir has assembled in the chancel. They stand in three rows, robed in vestments of pericardium dyed the color of old bruises. Their throats are open. Not metaphorically. Literally.

Each chorister has had the front of their neck carefully flayed and pinned back with silver hooks, revealing the glistening architecture of the voice box. The vocal cords have been tuned — stretched and tightened over years of disciplined silence — until they can produce tones that make the meat in the walls vibrate in sympathy.

The choirmaster raises his baton. It is a femur, bleached and inscribed with the names of every chorister who has ever sung here.

He brings it down.

The first note rises from seventy-three open throats at once.

It is not human. It is not animal. It is the sound of the building remembering every scream that ever echoed inside it. The note climbs, quavers, then fractures into harmony as different choristers modulate their exposed cords at precise intervals.

The nave answers.

The pillars of lashed ribs begin to hum. The fascia curtains tremble. High in the vault, the rose window of stretched membrane begins to weep a thin, clear fluid that spatters the congregation below like holy water.

I stand at the pulpit and conduct with my cleaver.

The music moves through me the way blood moves through an artery. I can feel the individual voices — the trembling soprano of the newest member, whose throat was opened only last week and whose cords still remember how to form words; the deep, rattling bass of the oldest chorister, whose lungs were among the first installed in the organ and who now sings only through the mechanical contraction of the bellows.

The piece is the *Miserere Carnis* — the Mercy of Meat.

The lyrics are simple. They are carved into the inside of every chorister's ribcage before their first performance:

*We gave our voices so the silence would not win.*

*We gave our throats so the hunger would be fed.*

*We gave our breath so the cathedral could breathe.*

As the final movement builds, something extraordinary happens.

The meat in the walls begins to sing back.

It is a low, subsonic vibration that rises through the soles of the faithful and settles in their sternums. The entire structure is resonating. For one perfect moment, the cathedral and the choir are the same organism.

A woman in the congregation falls to her knees, blood running from her ears. She is smiling. She has heard the voice of God and it sounded exactly like the sound her own body will one day make when it is part of the architecture.

The choirmaster holds the final note.

Seventy-three throats strain. The lungs in the triforium convulse. The rose window drips.

Then — silence.

Not empty silence. The charged, ringing silence of meat that has just finished screaming in perfect pitch.

The choristers bow as one. Their open throats glisten under the tallow lights. Some of them are already beginning to heal — pink tissue knitting over the exposed cartilage. By next rehearsal their throats will be open again, wider, more perfectly tuned.

I raise my cleaver in salute.

"The choir has sung," I say.

The congregation answers in the only way that matters.

They open their own mouths.

And from deep in their chests, from the places that will one day be harvested, comes the answering note.

The cathedral breathes.

The cathedral sings.

The cathedral is never silent.

Not while there is still meat to give voice.
