They ask how the cathedral stands when there is no stone for a hundred leagues.

I answer with the knife.

We did not quarry. We did not carve. We *grew*.

In the year of the Great Hunger, when the fields gave only dust and the cattle had long since turned to leather, the first congregation gathered in the slaughter yard. They brought no offerings but themselves. They brought no tools but their own bones.

The first pillar was raised from the ribs of twelve deacons lashed upright with strips of their own back. The architect — a man whose name is now only a scar on the altar — directed the work while his own legs were being harvested for the transept beams.

"More height," he whispered as the saw bit into his femur. "The nave must reach the sky so that Heaven may smell what we have become."

They worked without anesthetic. Pain was the mortar. Suffering was the plumb line.

When a worker faltered, he was taken to the curing sheds and returned as a flying buttress — his arms stretched and nailed into the living walls, his mouth sewn into a perpetual O so that the wind might whistle through him on holy days.

The rose window was not leaded glass. It was a lattice of intercostal membranes stretched across the eye socket of a giant whose skull we found beneath the city. When the light of the setting sun passes through it, the entire nave fills with the color of fresh hemorrhage.

We did not build *upon* the meat. The meat *is* the building.

The foundation is a single vast diaphragm that still contracts on certain feast days, causing the entire structure to sigh like a dying saint. Pilgrims sometimes mistake the movement for an earthquake. The wise ones fall to their knees and give thanks for the breath of the Lord.

The roof is a canopy of interlinked scapulae, each one etched with the names of the donors in their own hand before the skin was peeled away. On rainy nights you can hear the water running through the marrow channels — a soft, constant weeping that the priests call "the tears of the architecture."

And still they come. The lame. The sick. The merely faithful.

They offer a finger for a window. An ear for a gargoyle. A spine for a new load-bearing wall. Children are especially prized; their bones are supple and accept the curve of an arch more gracefully.

I have watched a mother hold her daughter's hand while the vergers removed the child's legs below the knee. The girl did not cry. She looked up at the half-finished vaulting and smiled with the beatific expression of one who knows she will be part of something eternal.

"We built this temple from our own ribs," I tell the new converts as they are led, trembling, toward the flensing tables.

"And when the last of us has been used up, the temple will still stand."

"Because the temple *is* us."

" And we are delicious."
