The nave breathes.

Beneath your feet the floor is not stone but muscle — striated, glistening, still faintly warm from the last offering. The pillars that hold the vault are femurs lashed with tendon, each one thicker than a man. Between them hang curtains of fascia, translucent, veined, pulsing with a rhythm older than prayer.

This is the Sermon on the Slab.

I stand at the butcher's pulpit, a block of salt-crusted oak stained the color of old liver. My apron is stiff with the blood of six generations of deacons. When I speak, the congregation does not answer with words. They answer with the wet sound of lips parting over teeth.

"The flesh is the church," I begin.

A thousand mouths open in the dark. Some still have tongues.

" The church is the flesh."

A wet sigh moves through the pews like wind through hanging carcasses. The chandeliers — wrought from ribcages and lit by rendered fat — sway in time with the heartbeat of the building itself.

I lift the cleaver. It catches the light of a hundred candles made from the tallow of the faithful.

"Brothers. Sisters. Children of the Knife. We were not born. We were butchered into being. Every one of us began as a cut of meat that refused to stop living."

I bring the blade down. It sings as it bites the slab. The sound echoes up the nave and returns changed — deeper, hungrier.

"God did not speak the world into existence. God *chewed* it."

A child in the third row begins to weep. Its mother, or what remains of her, places a hand over its mouth. The hand is missing three fingers. The child stops. It understands. Here, tears are a waste of good moisture.

I continue.

"Look at the walls. They are not painted. They are *cured*. Look at the altar. It is not marble. It is the sternum of the first saint, planed flat and polished with the grease of ten thousand communions. When you kneel, you kneel upon the bones of those who came before you — and they are grateful for the weight."

The congregation shifts. Leather creaks. Fat glistens. Somewhere in the transept a man is slowly being flayed by the verger as gentle punishment for impure thoughts. His screams have already become hymns.

I raise both arms. The sleeves of my vestments — cured human hide dyed the color of oxblood — fall back to reveal forearms cross-hatched with old cuts, each one a verse.

"The Eucharist is not symbolic. The bread is flesh. The wine is blood. And tonight, brothers and sisters, both are still warm."

I can feel the cathedral listening. The meat in the walls remembers what it was to be alive. It remembers the terror. It remembers the mercy of the knife.

I lower my voice to the register reserved for the final cut.

"Confess with your teeth. Sing with your throats. Bleed with purpose. For the day comes when the great Butcher will come for us all, and He will find us either tender... or tough."

The lights dim as the fat in the chandeliers gutters.

The congregation begins to pray.

Their prayer is the sound of meat tearing.

I bow my head.

"The Sermon on the Slab is ended."

"Go now. And be consumed."
