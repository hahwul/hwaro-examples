Walk into any traditional tailoring atelier and you will hear the soft scraping of chalk against heavy woolen cloth. Tailor's chalk, usually made of compressed pipeclay or soapstone, is the cutter's pencil. It is used to draft the geometry of a garment directly onto the fabric, translating three-dimensional body measurements into flat panels that will eventually wrap the shoulders and torso.

<!-- more -->

The markings are a complex code of lines, ticks, and crosses. A solid line marks the cutting path; dashed lines indicate where canvas should be pad-stitched; a cross denotes a point of alignment. The edge of the chalk must be kept razor-sharp with a specialized slate sharpener, as even a sixteenth-inch error in a line can ruin the drape of a lapel.

Unlike ink or modern markers, clay chalk brushed off easily once the panels are basted, leaving no trace of the draft. In this temporary language of lines, the tailor outlines the future silhouette of elegance.
