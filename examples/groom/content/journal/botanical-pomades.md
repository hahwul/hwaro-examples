Before the dominance of mineral oils and petroleum jellies in the twentieth century, hair grooming relied on the bounty of orchards and pastures. Nineteenth-century pomade recipes were agricultural affairs, combining purified animal fats with botanical waxes and essential oils. The word pomade itself derives from the French pomme, referencing the apples used to fragrance early formulations.

<!-- more -->

Barbers and chemists of the era would slow-cook beef tallow or lard, filtering it through charcoal to remove impurities. They then infused the purified fat with beeswax, castor oil, and aromatic herbs like lavender, rosemary, and bergamot.

The result was a thick balm that provided both hold and nourishment without suffocating the scalp. Unlike synthetic gels, these traditional dressings washed out cleanly and conditioned the hair fiber. Today, as contemporary makers rediscover these antique formulas, we find that the old ways offer a superior balance of style and scalp health, free from artificial additives.
