To understand the straight razor is to understand the quiet interaction between steel and stone. The modern shaving enthusiast often relies on disposable blades, but the classical barber knows that the finest edge is one nurtured over years on a natural whetstone. The Arkansas stone, prized for its dense novaculite structure, remains the tool of choice for honesters who seek absolute control.

<!-- more -->

Honing a razor is not a task to be rushed; it is a form of active meditation. It begins with the selection of oil or water to float away steel particles. The blade is laid flat on the stone, spine and edge making contact, then swept in a smooth diagonal X-stroke. The pressure must be feather-light, letting the weight of the steel do the work.

Over dozens of passes, the micro-bevel is refined. The barber tests the edge against a wet thumbnail, feeling for a delicate draw that indicates a bite without roughness. Finally, the blade is stropped on shell cordovan leather to align the microscopic teeth. In this ritual, we preserve a century-old standard of grooming.
