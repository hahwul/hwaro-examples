Kornlager occupies a converted grain silo on Malmö's harbor, and Teodor Lindqvist has spent nine winters there developing a line of porridge breads meant less for the table than for the pocket — dense whole-grain loaves enriched with a cooked porridge folded straight into the dough, built to survive a cold commute and still taste like something worth eating at the other end. His base recipe uses steel-cut rye porridge cooled overnight; the variations, which change weekly, run through barley, spelt, and the occasional handful of toasted buckwheat groats.

<!-- more -->

He brings four of Kornlager's standing porridge breads north to Ghent for Sunday's *Cold-Morning Porridge Breads* flight, sliced thick and served plain, since he holds that butter hides exactly the texture the porridge is there to build. The tasting runs early — ten in the morning, deliberately, so the bread is doing the job it was designed for: something dense and a little sweet to eat standing up in the cold before the day's judging gets underway.

Malmö's harbor wind and Ghent's January have more in common than either city admits, which he says is half the reason he keeps coming back.
