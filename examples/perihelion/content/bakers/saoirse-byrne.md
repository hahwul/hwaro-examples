Saoirse Byrne runs The Proof House, a one-room bakery and basketry workshop above a bicycle shop in Cork, where she bakes mornings and coils rattan bannetons in the afternoons — a split practice that started, by her own account, out of frustration with imported baskets that fell apart after a single winter of daily proofing. Every basket she sells has proofed at least one loaf of her own bread before it leaves the shop, on the theory that an unproofed basket is an unfinished tool.

<!-- more -->

Her Saturday workshop at Perihelion teaches the coil-and-lash technique she uses for The Proof House's standard round banneton, working in rattan cane pre-soaked overnight so it bends without splitting. It runs two and a half hours and produces one basket per person, ready to flour and use by the following week — most attendees proof their first loaf in it within days, since the concentric ridges a fresh banneton leaves are shallow at first and only deepen with use, the way Perihelion's own schedule badges are patterned after.

She has taught the class at four festivals now and keeps the group small — eight seats, no more, because coiling rattan evenly is a skill best corrected hand over hand rather than shouted across a room.
