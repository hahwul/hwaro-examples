Mira Delcourt has run Vandaag out of a single storefront on Vlasmarkt for eleven years, baking a rotating line of levains that changes with whatever grain her mill contact is excited about that month. She is the only Ghent baker on Perihelion's roster, which makes her the de facto host — she knows which stallholders need an extra folding table, which fire marshal to call about the courtyard ovens, and exactly how long the market hall's ancient radiators take to warm a room full of judges.

<!-- more -->

As head judge, she chairs both bake-off panels: Saturday's Open Sourdough heats and Sunday's Grand Final. Her scoring method is unusual and, by now, well known on the circuit — she tastes every loaf twice, once warm and once at room temperature, because she has watched too many good bakes get marked down for a crust that firmed up wrong after twenty minutes on the table. Entrants who know this bring a second loaf just for the cold pass.

Off the judging table, Vandaag's own bread is not entered in competition — a house rule Mira set the first year, after a visiting baker joked that the home team had an unfair advantage with the radiators. She still bakes fresh loaves for the tasting bar each morning, unjudged and free, because someone has to feed the queue before the ovens are hot.
