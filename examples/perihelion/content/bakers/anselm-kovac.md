Anselm Kovač has built or repaired eleven wood-fired ovens in and around Ljubljana under the name Ash & Ember, including the two portable courtyard ovens Perihelion trucks in every January. He trained the old way, apprenticed to a baker who refused to install a thermometer in her own oven on principle, and he still mostly works by eye: the color of the flame, the way ash banks against the back wall, the particular hiss a peel makes sliding over a floor that has finally come up to heat.

<!-- more -->

His Sunday panel, *Ash, Ember, and the Long Ferment*, is less a lecture than an argument he has been having with digital-probe converts for eighteen years — that a fire read by hand adjusts to a loaf's ferment in ways a set-and-forget deck oven cannot. He will fire the courtyard ovens live during the talk, narrating the bed of coals in real time, and take questions from anyone convinced a dial would do it better. Most years, somebody is.

Between talks he wanders the courtyard checking on the day's fires the way other people check their phones — a habit his partner has learned to plan around when they travel together, since it means every trip includes at least one detour to look at somebody else's oven.
