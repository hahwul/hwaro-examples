Moinho Novo is a granite-wheeled mill Odalys Ferreira rebuilt from a derelict watermill outside Porto, milling to order rather than to stock. She refuses to bag flour more than a day before it ships, on the theory — now more or less proven by a decade of side-by-side loaves — that the oils in fresh-cracked bran start turning within a week and take the bread's flavor with them. Bakers who work with her plan their orders around her mill schedule, not the other way around.

<!-- more -->

At Perihelion she gives two talks built around the same provocation from opposite directions. Saturday's *Reading the Bran* argues that how coarse or fine you leave the bran changes a loaf more than the starter culture ever will; Sunday's *Fresh-Milled vs. Rested Flour* mills two batches live, an hour apart, and bakes matched test loaves from each so the room can taste her claim rather than take her word for it. The rested batch always loses, but by how much depends entirely on the grain — spelt barely moves, rye falls apart.

She travels to four festivals a year and brings a scaled-down granite wheel to each one, hand-cranked, loud enough to be heard over the ovens. It produces perhaps two kilograms of flour an hour — not enough to sell, but exactly enough to prove a point.
