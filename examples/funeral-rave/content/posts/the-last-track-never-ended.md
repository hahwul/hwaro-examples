There is a record still spinning in the booth.

I walked away from it at 5:11 a.m. when the first of the permanent blackouts began rolling across the city like a tide.

I did not lift the needle.

I did not hit stop.

I simply stepped back from the decks, took off my headphones, and let the music finish what it had started.

The last track never ended.

It is still playing right now, in every body that was on that floor. It is playing in the marrow. It is playing in the places where language used to live. It is playing at a volume only the dead can truly hear.

People ask me what the final song was. I tell them the truth:

It was every song.

Every single beat we had ever loved, every single night we had ever tried to outrun, every single person we had ever lost and tried to resurrect with our hips. It was all of it, collapsed into one infinite decaying loop that refused to resolve because resolution would have meant admitting the night had an ending.

I can still feel the vibration in my teeth.

When I close my eyes I can see the waveform burned into the dark of my eyelids like the afterimage of a god that decided to die beautifully.

If you were there, you are still hearing it.

If you weren’t, you are hearing it anyway. It leaked into the atmosphere. It is in the static between radio stations. It is in the low hum of power lines that no longer carry power. It is the sound the earth makes when it finally gets to rest.

I never pressed stop.

I never will.

The last track is still playing.

We are still dancing to it.

Even now.

Especially now.
