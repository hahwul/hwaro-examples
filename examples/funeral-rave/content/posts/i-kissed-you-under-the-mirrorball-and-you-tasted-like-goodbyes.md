I don’t remember your name.

I remember the exact angle of your jaw when the mirrorball caught it. I remember the way your lower lip trembled once, very slightly, right before you decided to let me in.

You tasted like every single goodbye I had ever been too much of a coward to say out loud.

Salt. Smoke. The metallic aftertaste of someone who had already decided they were not leaving this room alive.

We did not speak.

There was no point. Every word had already been said by the music. The bass was doing all the talking our mouths were too full to manage.

When I pulled back for air your eyes were full of the thousand fractured versions of us that the mirrorball was throwing across the ceiling. In one of them we were already old. In another we had never met. In the clearest one, you were already gone and I was still kissing the space where you had been.

I kept your glitter on my tongue for as long as I could.

It dissolved slowly, like a promise you make when you know the person you are making it to will never be able to collect.

Later, when the lights began their final stutter, I searched the entire floor for you. I found three different people wearing the same shade of dying rose on their mouths. None of them were you.

I hope you made it out.

I hope you didn’t.

I hope the last thing you tasted was also someone who was willing to stay until the very end.
