There is a single frame burned into the back of my eyelids.

In it, two people are kissing so hard it looks like they are trying to climb inside each other’s mouths to escape the end of the world. A third person has their hand on the small of the first person’s back, steadying them, crying openly, still moving their hips in perfect time. The strobe has frozen all three of them in a triangle of unbearable tenderness.

The light saw that.

It saw the girl who wrote “I WAS HERE” in black glitter across her own chest with her fingernails until she drew blood.

It saw the man who lay down in the center of the floor and let thirty-seven strangers step on his hands while he smiled like he had finally found religion.

It saw every single body that chose to keep dancing instead of running when the first rumors of the blackout reached us.

I used to think the strobe was cruel. Now I know it was the kindest thing in the room. It gave everyone the mercy of not having to experience their own ending in real time. It broke the night into bearable pieces: a mouth open in ecstasy, a hand letting go, a spine arching like a question mark that would never receive its answer.

The mirrorball caught fragments of all of it and threw them back at us a thousand times per minute. For one impossible second I saw my own face reflected in the eye of a stranger who had already decided to stay until the end. We locked eyes across the strobe and I understood that we were both already dead and still somehow the luckiest people alive.

When the power finally flickered for real, the strobe did not go dark immediately.

It gave us seven more perfect flashes.

In the last one, every single person on the floor had their arms around someone else.

Even the ones who were alone.

Especially the ones who were alone.
