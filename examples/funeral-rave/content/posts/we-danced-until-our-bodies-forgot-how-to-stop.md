The first time the bass dropped after midnight, three people in the back simply stopped breathing and kept moving.

I watched it happen from the booth. Their chests kept rising and falling in perfect 128 bpm. Their eyes were open but the lights had already gone somewhere else. The strobe turned their faces into a film reel of the last beautiful thing they would ever do.

We did not stop the music.

We turned it up.

By 2:40 the entire floor had forgotten gravity. People were dancing with their spines instead of their feet. Necks arched back so far I could see the veins in their throats catching the mirrorball like wet wires. Someone had climbed into an open casket at the edge of the room and was using it as a pulpit, preaching in rhythm, one arm wrapped around a skeleton that still wore a silk slip and a single gold earring.

I played the same four-bar loop for forty-three minutes. No one noticed. Or everyone noticed and decided the loop was the only honest thing left in the universe.

At some point I realized my hands were bleeding from gripping the faders too hard. The blood mixed with the condensation on the mixer. It felt holy.

A girl with wilted roses braided into her hair climbed onto the booth and kissed the side of my neck like she was saying goodbye to a version of herself that had never existed. When she pulled away there was glitter on my skin and I knew I would never wash it off.

We danced until our bodies forgot how to stop.

We danced until the concept of stopping felt like a rumor from another lifetime.

The last thing I remember clearly is looking out and seeing every single person on the floor with their arms raised, palms open, catching falling petals that no one had thrown. They were just appearing. Black glitter and lavender and the color of a mouth that has said its final word.

I kept the track running.

It is still running.
