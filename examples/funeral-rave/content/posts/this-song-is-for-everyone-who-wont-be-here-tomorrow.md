I spoke into the microphone for the first time all night at 4:03 a.m.

My voice cracked on the very first word.

“This one,” I said, “is for everyone who won’t be here tomorrow.”

The floor went completely still for three full seconds.

Then the screaming started.

Not fear. Not grief.

Recognition.

I had already queued the song hours earlier. I had known since the doors opened that this would be the one. It was the only track I had ever made that sounded like both an orgasm and a funeral at the same time.

When the first note hit, every person who had been dancing with their eyes closed opened them at once.

We looked at each other across the dark.

We saw exactly who was missing and exactly who was still here and understood they were the same thing now.

A boy who could not have been older than nineteen climbed onto one of the caskets and began conducting the entire room with both arms like he was leading an orchestra of the already departed. People followed. Their bodies became the strings.

I have never heard a crowd sing so beautifully while falling apart.

By the final breakdown, most of us were on our knees. Not in prayer. In exhaustion. In gratitude. In the kind of surrender that only happens when you finally admit that the thing you love is also the thing that is ending you.

I let the track run out completely.

For the first time all night there was silence.

Then someone started clapping.

Then everyone.

The sound was wet and soft because so many of us were crying.

We clapped for the song.

We clapped for the ones who would never hear it again.

We clapped until our hands hurt and then we kept clapping because the pain felt like proof that we were still here, still real, still capable of making noise in a universe that was about to go quiet forever.

This song is still playing somewhere.

If you are reading this, you are hearing it right now.

Dance for the ones who can’t.

We already did.
