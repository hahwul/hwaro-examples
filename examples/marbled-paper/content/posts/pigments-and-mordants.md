A marbled sheet is the product of three chemistries working in sequence: the pigment that floats, the gall that spreads it, and the mordant that fixes it to the paper. Each component is mundane on its own. Together they decide whether the pattern survives the lift.

## Pigments

Traditional Western marblers favored earth pigments ground fine enough to disperse without sinking. Yellow ochre, red iron oxide, lamp black, ultramarine, and verdigris cover most historical bindings. Modern acrylic pigments work but sit on the surface differently and resist combing in subtle ways. For period reconstruction, mull dry pigment with distilled water on a glass slab until the paste runs smooth under a palette knife.

## Ox Gall

Ox gall is the surfactant that makes marbling possible. A drop of plain pigment placed on the carrageenan size will sink or contract into a tight bead. The same pigment cut with gall spreads outward in a controlled disc. Successive layers each need slightly more gall than the layer beneath; the second color spreads against the first, the third against the second, and so on. Without this gradient the later colors collapse into the earlier ones.

Bottled refined ox gall is sold in small dropper bottles and keeps for years if stored cool. Fresh gall from a butcher works but smells, and most workshops have moved past that stage of authenticity.

## Alum Mordant

Paper does not naturally accept the pigment from a marbling bath. It must be treated first with potassium aluminum sulfate, dissolved at roughly four ounces per gallon of water and brushed evenly across the working surface. Let the sheet dry flat under weight. Alum-treated paper has a working life of about two weeks before the mordant begins to crystallize and produce uneven pulls.

Mordant only the side that will touch the bath. Any alum on the back will tint adjacent sheets in storage.
