The size is the bath. Without a properly mixed size the pigments will not float, will not hold their shape, and will not transfer cleanly to the paper. Most workshops use Irish moss carrageenan, blended with cold water and left to hydrate overnight before the morning's first pull.

Mixing ratios depend on local water hardness. A common starting point is two tablespoons of powdered carrageenan to one gallon of distilled water. Distilled is preferable because mineral content can flatten the colors and break the surface tension that holds the pigment in suspension.

## Viscosity

The size should resist a stylus the way cold honey resists a spoon. Too thin and the pigment sinks. Too thick and the comb leaves persistent grooves that distort subsequent designs. A practiced marbler reads viscosity by drawing a single drop of color and watching how far it spreads. Three to four inches of even expansion is typical; less indicates the bath needs another hour to rest.

## Temperature

Keep the workshop between sixteen and twenty degrees Celsius. Warmer rooms accelerate evaporation at the bath surface and form a skin that ruins the pull. Colder rooms thicken the carrageenan past usability. If the bath sits unused for more than a day, skim the surface with a strip of newsprint before resuming work; airborne dust settles into the size and shows up as pinholes in the finished sheet.

## Maintenance

A well-kept bath can serve a full week of pulls. Strain the surface after each session, top up with fresh size as the working depth drops, and discard the bath at the first sign of cloudiness. The materials are inexpensive. The hours invested in a single failed pattern are not.
