# Midnight Drive

There is a specific hour when the highway belongs to you alone. The city lights shrink in the mirror, the radio hums with analog warmth, and every song feels like it was written for this exact moment.

## The Playlist

A midnight drive demands a specific kind of music. Not too aggressive, not too mellow. The tempo should match the white lines sliding beneath the car at a steady, meditative pace.

The following tracks define the experience:

- **Kavinsky - Nightcall** -- The track that launched a thousand drives. That vocoder vocal over the pulsing bass is the sound of asphalt under streetlight.
- **FM-84 - Running in the Night** -- Ollie Wride's vocals carry a longing that only makes sense when the windows are down and the air is warm.
- **Timecop1983 - On the Run** -- Pure dreamwave. Layers of arpeggiated synthesizers that build and dissolve like passing headlights.
- **The Midnight - Los Angeles** -- A love letter to a city best experienced from behind the wheel at 2 AM.
- **Gunship - Tech Noir** -- Darker, denser, with a driving beat that suits the empty stretches between exits.

## Why It Works

Synthwave is engineered for movement. The tempo ranges cluster around 100 to 120 BPM, matching the rhythmic pulse of highway driving. The reverb-heavy production creates a sense of vast space. The melodies are nostalgic without being sentimental.

This is music that asks nothing of you except forward motion.

## The Equipment

For the authentic experience, consider the signal chain. A car stereo with adequate low-end response is essential. The bass should be felt in the steering wheel. If your system supports it, a slight boost at 80 Hz brings the kick drum into physical territory.

Avoid earphones. The sound should surround you, not sit inside your head.

## The Route

The best midnight drives follow no particular route. Take the highway out of the city, drive until the exits stop looking familiar, then find your way back. The destination is irrelevant. The drive is the point.
