Ignis Paludis carries no fire, whatever the crofters of the Low Fenlands report to travelers passing through on the causeway road. What crowns its head is a crest of hollow quills, each ridged like a shell, that catch the last low light of evening and throw it back in a soft burnished glow. Riders on the causeway mistake this for a lantern held by someone waiting in the reeds, and more than one has waded in after it.

<!-- more -->

The creature stands motionless for hours at the edge of open water, legs drawn together, watching the surface for the small silver fish that rise at dusk. Its neck folds in three places, unlike the true herons the fenfolk hunt for their feathers, and when it strikes it does so with the whole neck rather than the head alone, a motion the workshop's second illuminator described in her working notes as "unbecoming of a bird, but effective." The strike is fast enough that no plate in this bestiary attempts to render it; the artist chose instead the stillness before, which is the truer likeness of the animal as it is actually seen.

Fenland guides distinguish the marsh lantern from ordinary bitterns by its silence — it makes no boom, no croak, nothing at all — and by the plain fact that it is never seen in company. Whether this solitude is a habit of the species or simply the consequence of there being very few of them left in the deep fen, the guild's naturalists could not agree, and the dispute is recorded, unresolved, in the margin beside this plate.

> *The crest does not burn. It only remembers the sun long enough to trouble a traveler's judgment.* — marginal gloss, hand of the second illuminator
