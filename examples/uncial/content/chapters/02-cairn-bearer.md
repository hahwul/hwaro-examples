Saxifer Montis is known to every drover who has crossed the high passes by the stacks of flat stones it carries balanced on its shoulders and back, four or five at a time, wedged so precisely that a hard shove will not unseat them. Shepherds insist the creature builds the waymark cairns that mark the safe line through scree fields, and rebuilds them each time a rockfall or a careless boot scatters the stones. The guild's naturalists are less certain the cairns are built with travelers in mind at all.

<!-- more -->

What is not disputed is the behavior itself: the creature selects stones by weight and shape with evident deliberation, testing several before lifting one, and will walk some distance to find a match rather than settle for a poor fit. Herders who have watched the process describe it as unhurried to the point of stubbornness — a cairn-bearer disturbed mid-selection will simply set its stone down and start again once the disturbance has passed, rather than resume where it left off.

The horns are short and blunt, of no apparent use in defense, and the creature's only recorded response to a threat is to stand very still until the stack on its back is mistaken for an outcrop of rock, which by most accounts it usually is. Two drovers who lost the trail in fog above Harrow Col report following what they took to be a cairn only to watch it turn and walk away from them, stones and all — the origin, probably, of the passage-guild's standing advice never to trust a waymark that moves.

> *A cairn that walks is not lost; it only knows the path better than you do.* — marginal gloss, hand of the third illuminator
