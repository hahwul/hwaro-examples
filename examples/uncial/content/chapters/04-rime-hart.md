Cervus Pruinae takes its name from the antlers, which grow in flat, angular planes rather than the rounded tines of an ordinary stag, and which hunters who have taken one report ring faintly when struck together, like glass rather than bone. Whether the antlers are truly crystalline or only shaped to resemble ice by the cold at that altitude, no naturalist in the guild has settled to general satisfaction; the two specimens brought down to the lowlands for study lost their ring entirely within a season, which argues for the latter.

<!-- more -->

The rime-hart is sighted only above the point where standing water freezes even in the warm months, on the frost-locked shoulders of the high cirques, and descends no lower than it must. Herders who graze the upper pastures in late summer report it standing motionless on exposed ridgelines for whole afternoons, facing into the wind, a habit some attribute to simple vigilance and others to the animal's evident dislike of anything resembling warmth.

Little is recorded of its diet or its young, since the guild's expeditions to the cirques have been few and brief, but the rime-hart is not thought dangerous; every account describes it moving away from parties of travelers rather than toward them, and no injury from one is on record. The frost motes scattered across this plate are not decoration — the second illuminator insisted on including them after her own ascent, when she reported the animal seemed always to stand at the exact center of a small, private squall of drifting ice that the surrounding air did not otherwise carry.

> *We climbed for three days to draw it standing still. It was worth every hour of the cold.* — marginal gloss, hand of the second illuminator
