Lynx Cinerea is the last plate the workshop completed and, by the compilers' own admission, the least studied. Its coat is the exact grey of weathered granite, banded faintly at the tail in a pattern shepherds of the mid-slopes read as a kind of signal: a lynx sitting with its tail loosely curled, as shown opposite, is considered no threat to a flock, while a tail held stiff and banded bright is taken as fair warning to move the animals on before dusk.

<!-- more -->

The tufted ears are unusually long for the creature's size and are said to turn independently of one another, tracking sound from two directions at once — a claim the guild's naturalists have not been able to confirm directly, since the lynx is markedly shy of observation and the workshop's illuminator drew this plate largely from a single long afternoon spent motionless behind a boulder, sketching by memory once the animal had gone.

Shepherds distinguish the ashen lynx from ordinary hill cats by its habit of sitting bolt upright on exposed rock for long stretches, seemingly indifferent to weather, rather than seeking cover as most cats do. Some read this as simple vigilance over a hidden den; others, more fancifully, as the animal keeping a kind of watch over the pass itself. The compilers note only that no shepherd who has lost stock to a genuine predator has ever named the ashen lynx as the cause, whatever else is said of it around the winter fires.

> *Drawn from memory, behind a rock, in failing light — which is, I think, the only way this animal permits itself to be drawn.* — marginal gloss, hand of the second illuminator
