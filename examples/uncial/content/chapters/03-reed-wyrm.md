Anguis Calamorum is remarkable chiefly for its manner of growth: where most serpents thicken with age, the reed-wyrm lengthens, so that the oldest individuals are reported at eleven or twelve times the length of a young adult while remaining scarcely thicker than a finger. It moves through standing reed beds by a method the workshop's illuminator struggled to render on a flat plate — not a crawl but something closer to a slow, continuous coil, the body passing through the same handful of loops again and again as it advances.

<!-- more -->

Fen trappers who work the bulrush channels for waterfowl treat the reed-wyrm as harmless and largely ignore it, though they are careful never to grasp a stand of reeds without first looking, since the creature's coloring is close enough to dry stalk that a hand can close on it by accident. It is not venomous, so far as the guild's naturalists have determined, and its bite — rarely offered, and only when cornered — is reported as no worse than a sharp pinch.

Its diet is a matter of some dispute. Trappers report finding the wyrm coiled motionless around clutches of marsh-bird eggs for days at a time, which some take as evidence of predation and others, noting that no eggs are ever found broken or missing, take as evidence of the opposite — that the creature is guarding rather than hunting. The plate opposite shows the wyrm mid-ascent through a reed stand, which the second illuminator chose over a coiled pose specifically to avoid taking a side in that argument.

> *It is unclear whether the wyrm gets longer or the fen only gets narrower around it.* — marginal gloss, hand of the first illuminator
