Ardea Coronata is the largest bird described in this bestiary and the only one the tidal-island folk address with a title rather than a name. The crest of five stiff, radiating plumes that gives the creature its common name is present on both sexes, though larger and more upright on birds the fowlers judge to be older, which has led some observers to treat it as a mark of seniority within a loose colony rather than a simple ornament of species.

<!-- more -->

Colonies of the heron-king are small, rarely more than a dozen birds sharing a single reed island, and are said to recognize a single dominant individual whose crest stands highest and who feeds first at any carcass or stranded catch the tide brings in. Island fowlers report that a colony deprived of its dominant bird — by age, injury, or the occasional poacher — will stand unusually still for some days afterward, as though waiting, before the crest of a different bird begins visibly to rise.

The wings, shown spread on the plate opposite rather than folded, are disproportionately broad for a wading bird of its size, and the heron-king is one of the few creatures in this collection observed to glide rather than flap between islands, riding the onshore wind with barely a wingbeat for stretches the fowlers measure in minutes rather than seconds. Whether this economy of motion is related to the crown or simply to a life spent almost entirely over open water, the compilers leave to a later, better-funded expedition to determine.

> *We drew the wings open because no one who has seen the bird glide could bear to draw them closed.* — marginal gloss, hand of the third illuminator
