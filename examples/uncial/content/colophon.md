This bestiary was never finished. Of the twenty-odd creatures the workshop's founding scribe originally proposed to catalogue, six plates survive in a state fit to bind and show — the rest exist only as pencil underdrawing, marginal notes, or a single line in the workshop's daybook naming a creature no illuminator ever got to. What follows is a record of how the surviving six were made, so that whoever eventually continues the work knows what they are continuing.

## Materials

Each plate began as a full-page underdrawing in graphite on prepared vellum substitute, corrected in situ wherever possible — the workshop's second illuminator in particular preferred to sketch from a hide blind rather than from a specimen or a prior sketch, on the conviction that an animal drawn from memory in the field kept a truer proportion than one drawn at leisure from a skin. The gold used for the ruled frames and the creatures' linework is a single consistent ink, mixed once at the start of the project and rationed carefully across all six plates so that no plate would read as more richly illuminated than its neighbors.

## Hands

Three illuminators worked the six plates between them, and each plate's marginal gloss is signed by hand in the manner of the workshop's older records, though the plates themselves are not individually attributed — house style held that the bestiary should read as a single continuous voice, whatever variation existed between the artists who actually held the brush. The scribe who oversaw the whole project, and who is responsible for the scholarly descriptions accompanying each plate, kept a running daybook of disagreements between the illuminators over questions of behavior and habitat; several of those disagreements are preserved, deliberately unresolved, in the text.

## Provenance

The workshop closed before a seventh plate could be started. What is bound here — beginning with [the marsh lantern](/chapters/01-marsh-lantern/) — is presented in the order the six were completed, not in any zoological order the founding scribe might once have intended, since no such order was ever agreed upon before the work stopped. Corrections, when they came, are noted rather than silently absorbed into the description — the compilers preferred a bestiary that showed its own uncertainty to one that pretended to more authority than six plates and a handful of field notes can honestly claim.
