The whole sky had gone to scales overnight. Cirrocumulus in tight ripples from one side to the other, each cloudlet small enough to hide behind a fingertip held at arm's length — the field mark that separates it from the coarser altocumulus. A textbook mackerel sky, and a warm front was already written into the morning observation.

<!-- more -->

Cirrocumulus is high, cold, and short-lived. It forms when a thin layer of ice-crystal cloud becomes just unstable enough to break into regular cells and ripples — the *undulatus* pattern is the sky showing you its own wave motion. It rarely lasts long: within an hour or two it usually thickens into cirrostratus or coarsens into altocumulus. Here it was the leading edge of a warm front sliding up from the south, the classic high-cloud vanguard.

The nearest aerodrome observation that morning read:

```text
METAR EGXH 090650Z 18011KT 9999 CI 5/8 FEW030 14/09 Q1016 NOSIG
BECMG 1012 8000 -RA BKN012
```

Decoded: wind south at eleven knots, visibility better than ten kilometres, high cirriform cloud at five eighths, a few low cumulus at three thousand feet, temperature fourteen with dewpoint nine, pressure 1016 and falling. The *BECMG* group is the giveaway — becoming, between 10:00 and 12:00, visibility down to eight kilometres in light rain with a broken deck at twelve hundred feet.

It ran almost exactly to schedule. The scales thickened to a milky sheet by ten, the sun went to a watery disc behind cirrostratus, and the first rain reached the ness a little after noon.
