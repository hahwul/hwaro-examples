Nobody logs a day like this, which is exactly why I did. A complete lid of stratocumulus over the estuary from horizon to horizon, grey on grey, the kind of sky that gets called *nothing* and waved away. But look for a minute and it is not featureless at all.

<!-- more -->

Stratocumulus is the most common cloud on Earth and by a distance the most common sky over this coast. It forms in a shallow, well-mixed layer of air capped by a temperature inversion — warmer air sitting on cooler air, a ceiling the moisture cannot punch through. So the cloud spreads sideways instead of building up, breaking into rolls, patches, and honeycomb cells.

Today it was the rolled variety, long parallel billows lined up across the wind, darker in the troughs where the layer was thickest. The gaps between rolls let through a paler grey rather than blue — a second, higher sheet behind the first.

Three things worth keeping:

1. Cover was a full eight oktas but the base stayed high enough, around 760 metres, to keep the far shore visible.
2. No rain, despite the gloom. Stratocumulus rarely gives more than drizzle; the layer is too shallow to grow drops.
3. It burned back to six oktas for twenty minutes near noon, then closed again for the rest of the day.

A grey lid is still a sky. It has structure, a history, and a reason. That is enough for the log.
