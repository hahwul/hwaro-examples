It started as an honest summer cumulus at midday, a single crisp turret out over the water, and by three o'clock it had grown into the biggest cloud I have logged from this beach. The top had gone fibrous and flattened into a classic anvil, the incus, streaming downwind for miles.

<!-- more -->

Cumulonimbus is the only genus that spans the whole troposphere in a single cloud, from a base near a kilometre up to a top that here must have been near ten. The tell that it had matured was the head: the hard cauliflower turrets of a growing cumulus congestus had turned soft and streaked — *capillatus*, hair-like — as the summit hit the tropopause, ran out of room to rise, and glaciated into ice. Once the top spreads into an anvil, the storm is fully built.

I logged the sequence as it came:

- **13:10** — single congestus turret, sharp cauliflower top, no anvil.
- **14:25** — top hardening and reaching; a faint pileus cap flickered over the summit for a minute, torn off by the updraught punching through.
- **15:05** — anvil spreading downwind, base darkening, first mammatus pouches sagging under the leading edge.
- **15:40** — first distant rumble; a grey shaft of rain to the south-west, well out to sea.

The storm never came ashore here. It tracked parallel to the coast and dragged its rain along the horizon, and the light under the anvil went that flat bronze colour that makes the shingle look lit from inside. I packed up when the gusts started throwing sand and the visibility began to drop. Respect the anvil.
