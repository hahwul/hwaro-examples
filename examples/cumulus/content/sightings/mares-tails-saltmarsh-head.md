I came down to the marsh before sunrise for the cold, not the cloud, and found the whole eastern sky combed over with hooks. Cirrus uncinus — mares' tails — each strand trailing a little curl at one end, all of them leaning the same way like grass under a slow current.

<!-- more -->

Cirrus lives at the top of the troposphere, above eight kilometres here, where it is cold enough that the cloud is made entirely of ice crystals rather than water droplets. The hook at the end of each tail is the tell: ice forms in a small generating head, then falls into slower air below and gets drawn out into a streamer called a fallstreak. The comma shape is the wind shear made visible.

The old coastal saying is *mares' tails and mackerel scales make lofty ships carry low sails*. It is not superstition. High cirrus thickening and lowering is often the leading edge of an approaching warm front, the cloud deck sloping up and away toward weather that is still a day out. Sure enough, by evening the cirrus had spread to a milky cirrostratus and the barometer had started to sag.

At first light none of that was here yet — just the cold, the hooks turning from grey to pink to white, and the tide a long way out over the flats. The same warm front that these mares' tails announced is the one that later ripened into the [mackerel sky over Gowan Ness](/sightings/mackerel-sky-gowan-ness/) I logged another morning.
