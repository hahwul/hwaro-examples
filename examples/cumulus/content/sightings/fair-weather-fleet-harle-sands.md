The first proper convection day of the year. A whole fleet of small cumulus sailing over Harle Sands from mid-morning on, evenly spaced, flat-bottomed, each one wider than it was tall. Cumulus humilis — the fair-weather cloud, the one children draw.

<!-- more -->

These are the friendliest clouds in the sky and among the most legible. The flat bases mark the lifting condensation level, the exact height at which rising air cools enough for its moisture to condense. Every base sat at the same altitude because every thermal cools at the same rate — so the fleet all floats along one invisible shelf, here about 820 metres up. Count the flat bottoms and you have read the humidity of the whole lower atmosphere at a glance.

*Humilis* means humble, and the name is a promise: these are shallow, wider than tall, and going nowhere in particular. The cap of dry, stable air above them stops the turrets growing, so they stay flat and harmless. Only when that cap weakens do they start to build vertically toward the taller *mediocris* and *congestus* — and then you keep an eye on them.

The rhythm of the day was pure convection. Cloud built through the warmth of the afternoon as the sand heated and threw up thermals, peaked around three, and then dissolved into a clear evening as the ground cooled and the thermals switched off. The fleet simply evaporated, cloud by cloud, until the sky over the sands was empty and blue again.
