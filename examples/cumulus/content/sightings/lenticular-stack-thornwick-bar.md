They looked painted on. Three smooth lens shapes stacked one above the other in the lee of the headland, edges so clean they might have been cut with a knife, and not one of them drifting though the wind on the shingle was hard enough to lean into.

<!-- more -->

Lenticular cloud forms where stable, moist air is pushed up and over an obstacle — a hill, a headland, a distant range — and then oscillates downwind in a standing wave. The cloud sits at the crest of that wave, condensing on the way up and evaporating on the way down, so it appears motionless while the air itself streams straight through it. What you are watching is not the cloud travelling but the wave holding still.

This one built through the middle of the morning and held for the better part of two hours. The stack — pilots call it a pile of plates — means several moist layers were riding the same wave train at slightly different heights. I have only seen a clean triple stack here twice.

A few notes for the log:

- The lowest lens carried the sharpest outline; the top one frayed first as the air dried out aloft.
- Colour shifted from flat white to a faint apricot as the sun dropped toward the sea.
- No precipitation, no lowering, no drama. Wave cloud is the tidy end of the genus.

By four o'clock the westerly had backed a point and softened, the wave collapsed, and the plates smeared into ordinary altocumulus within ten minutes. I stayed until the last lens was gone.
