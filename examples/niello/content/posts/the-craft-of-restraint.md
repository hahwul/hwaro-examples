There is a recurring temptation in design to add one more element, one more color, one more flourish. The assumption is that more communicates more. It rarely does.

The best metalworkers understood this centuries ago. A niello inlay succeeds not because of how much black alloy fills the surface, but because of how precisely the engraved lines guide the eye. Every cut in the metal is deliberate. There is no room for a hesitant hand.

## Subtraction as Method

Restraint is not the absence of ideas. It is the discipline of choosing which ideas deserve space. A page with three elements, each given room to breathe, will always communicate more clearly than a page with thirty elements competing for attention.

Consider the decisions that never reach the viewer:

- The typeface you rejected
- The color you removed
- The section you deleted
- The animation you chose not to add

These invisible choices define the work as much as what remains.

## The Cost of Excess

Every element on a page carries cognitive weight. A decorative border, a gradient background, a subtle shadow — each demands a fraction of the viewer's attention. Individually, they seem harmless. Collectively, they create noise.

Noise is the enemy of communication. When everything calls for attention, nothing receives it.

## Working With Less

The practice of restraint requires confidence. You must trust that the reader will find what they need without being guided by visual spectacle. You must believe that clear typography, consistent spacing, and honest hierarchy are enough.

They are.
