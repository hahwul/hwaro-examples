## ON STAGE DRESSING

A set is not a room. It is a frame for action, and every object placed within it must earn its place. The discipline of stage dressing begins with subtraction: remove anything that does not serve the scene, then remove half of what remains.

### The Working Properties

The working properties are the objects an actor must touch. A telephone that rings, a letter that is opened, a glass that is filled. These items are chosen first, sourced for weight and durability, and rehearsed alongside the performer until they become extensions of the body.

A working property must survive forty performances. It must look correct from the back row of the balcony and feel correct in the hand. The compromise between these two demands is the central problem of property design.

### The Decorative Field

The decorative field is everything else: the books on the shelf, the painting above the mantel, the rug beneath the table. These items establish the world of the play without being noticed. If the audience remarks on the rug, the rug has failed.

The decorative field is built in layers. The largest pieces are placed first, then the medium objects, then the small accents. The final pass is dust and wear: a stage that looks too new looks false.

### The Strike

When the production closes, the set is struck. The working properties are catalogued and stored for future use. The decorative pieces are returned to stock or, more often, dispersed: to other productions, to charity, to the skip behind the loading dock.

A theatre accumulates objects faster than it can use them. The discipline of strike is the discipline of letting go.

---

*The set is a frame. The frame is a discipline. The discipline is the work.*
