## ON THE PROMPT CORNER

The prompt corner is the small desk in the wings, traditionally on the stage-left side, where the deputy stage manager runs the show. From this position, the DSM calls every cue: lighting, sound, scenery, performer entrances. The corner is the nervous system of a live performance.

### The Book

At the centre of the corner sits the book: a marked-up copy of the script, annotated through rehearsal until every cue is recorded with precise timing. The book is the most valuable object in the building during a performance. Lose it and the show stops.

The annotations follow a strict notation. Lighting cues are marked LX, sound cues SQ, fly cues FLY. Each cue has a standby and a go. The standby is given thirty seconds before the cue; the go is given on the exact word, gesture, or beat that triggers the change.

A well-prepared book reads like a musical score. The right margin holds the cues. The left margin holds the warnings, the alternates, and the notes from the director. Between them runs the dialogue, underlined and circled wherever a cue intersects a line.

### The Headset

The DSM speaks into a headset connected to every operator backstage. The voice is calm, steady, and precise. There is no room for ambiguity. A cue called too early is a mistake the audience can see. A cue called too late is the same mistake, in the opposite direction.

The discipline of the headset is the discipline of restraint. The DSM hears everything that goes wrong and says nothing about it until the curtain falls.

### The Tradition

Computerised cue systems have not eliminated the prompt corner. They have changed its tools but not its function. Someone still sits in the wings with a book and a headset, watching the stage, holding the show together one cue at a time.

*The corner is small. The work is enormous. The audience never sees it.*
