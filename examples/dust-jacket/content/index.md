<p class="lead">The dust jacket is the only part of the book the reader encounters twice &mdash; first in the shop, when the book is chosen, and last on the shelf, when the book is put away. Everything else is read; the jacket is <em>seen</em>.</p>

This is the ordinary disposition of a jacket &mdash; flap, back, spine, front, flap &mdash; unfolded and laid out flat, as it comes from the press before it is wrapped. Most readers never see the jacket in this state; they meet it folded around a book. The present site offers the jacket in its unwrapped form, so that the whole of the design may be read at once.

<hr>

## What a jacket does

A jacket performs three offices. It protects the boards from abrasion, soiling, and light. It carries the marketing apparatus of the book &mdash; the title, author, price, publisher, and the short selling-prose of the flaps. And it presents the book to the world, at a distance, upon a shelf, as a single image.

The three offices do not always cohabit easily. A jacket designed for protection may be too plain for marketing; a jacket designed for sales may be too fussy for the library. The designer's task is to reconcile the three, to make a wrapper that is practical, persuasive, and beautiful at once.

<blockquote>A book is sold by its cover, shelved by its spine, and remembered by its title page. All three fall to the jacket.</blockquote>

## The front cover

The front cover carries the title, the subtitle, the author, and usually the publisher. Everything else &mdash; illustration, colour, pattern, ornament &mdash; is at the designer's discretion. The present house favours a single heavy display face set at maximum scale, with the subtitle in italic and the author in small caps. The rest of the panel is air.

## The spine

The spine must be legible from across a room. It is read vertically, either top-down or bottom-up according to national custom &mdash; bottom-up in continental Europe, top-down in Britain and America &mdash; and the present house observes the bottom-up convention, with the title rotated accordingly.

## The flaps

The flaps are where the jacket does most of its talking. The front flap carries the selling-prose: a few hundred words that argue for the book. The back flap carries the author's biography and the designer's credit. Both are set in a face small enough to fit the space, plain enough to be read in a bookshop's dim light.
