<p class="lead">Of all the faces a book presents to the world, the spine is the one most often seen and least often studied.</p>

## The shelf, as a typographic surface

A filled bookshelf is a horizontal sentence, composed by accident. The spines make a band of vertical compositions, most of them unlegible at a distance, a few legible by the force of their design. The legible ones come to govern the shelf &mdash; the eye returns to them; the hand reaches for them; they are, effectively, the headings.

A spine that cannot be read from across a room has, in the language of this imprint, failed. It may be a beautiful text face, a well-set title, a careful composition; if it cannot be read at five paces, it has failed.

## The direction of the text

<p>A spine may be set in one of four orientations &mdash; top-down, bottom-up, horizontal, or stacked &mdash; and each has its conventions.</p>

- **Top-down.** The head of the letters points to the head of the book. To read, the viewer tilts the head to the right. Standard in Britain and America.
- **Bottom-up.** The head of the letters points to the foot of the book. To read, the viewer tilts to the left. Standard in continental Europe.
- **Horizontal.** For thick books only, where the spine is wide enough to set a full line. Common on reference works.
- **Stacked.** The letters are set one above the other, horizontally. Rare; used on very thin books.

<blockquote>A book without a spine is a book without a shelf. A shelf without readable spines is a wall.</blockquote>

## The information on the spine

The spine usually carries the title, the author, and the publisher. Sometimes it carries the series, the volume number, and the price. Sometimes, on very cheap books, it carries nothing at all &mdash; a regrettable economy.

## The imprint mark

<p>We place our imprint mark at the foot of the spine, at a small scale. It is not a brand; it is a placemark, useful to the librarian more than to the reader. The reader who recognises it has already opened the book.</p>

## Setting practicalities

A spine should be set with generous bearing &mdash; the space between the letters and the edges of the spine. A crowded spine is unreadable at any distance. The present house reserves at least one line height of bearing at head and foot, and at least half a line height at the sides.

The display face should be the one used on the front cover, at the largest size the spine can accommodate. The author's name is set below in small caps, at a size no more than a third of the display. The publisher's mark is set at the foot, smaller still.
