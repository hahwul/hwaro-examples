<p class="lead">There exists, at the margins of the trade, a small literature on the unprinted jacket &mdash; the wrapper that carries no title, no author, no design, only its paper and its colour.</p>

## A definition

The unprinted jacket is a sheet of paper, cut to the dimensions of the bound book, folded around the boards, and tucked at the flaps. It carries no ink. The title, the author, the imprint, and the price are printed on the boards beneath, or on a paper label glued to the spine. The jacket is, in this case, a protection against the dust of the shelf and the oil of the hand. It is not a poster.

## The papers

The unprinted jacket is most often set in a laid paper of moderate weight &mdash; somewhere between ninety and one hundred and twenty grams. The laid lines run vertically, parallel to the spine. The paper is undyed, or dyed in a single bath to a quiet middle tone: a dust grey, a stone, a pale ochre, a deep cream. Bright colours are avoided. The wrapper is not meant to draw the eye; it is meant to protect the binding while drawing as little attention as possible.

<blockquote>The wrapper that asks to be read has, by its asking, ceased to be a wrapper.</blockquote>

## The trade in the late twentieth century

The unprinted jacket flourished, in the English trade, between roughly 1945 and 1975, as a mark of the serious press. The university imprints favoured it; the small literary presses copied the convention; a few large houses adopted it for their poetry lists. After 1975, under pressure from the bookshop, the unprinted jacket retreated to the limited edition and the private press, where it remains.

## A note on storage

The unprinted jacket fades. The dust grey becomes a paler grey; the stone becomes a paler stone; the cream becomes a yellower cream. The collector who wishes to keep the jacket in its issued state must store the book away from the light, in a closed case, with a sheet of acid-free tissue between the jacket and the boards.
