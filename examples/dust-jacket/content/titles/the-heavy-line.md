<p class="lead">The ultra-heavy didone &mdash; the face of the jacket, the poster, the magazine cover &mdash; has a longer history than its present ubiquity suggests.</p>

## From Bodoni to Fatface

The didone classification, which gathers the work of Bodoni, Didot, and their followers, is defined by its vertical axis, its hair-thin horizontals, and its sharp transition from thick to thin. In its early nineteenth-century incarnations, the didone was a text face, set in the plain weights that modern readers would call book or regular. Only later, in the rising tide of the advertising age, did the weight balloon, the counters narrow, and the face become what the English trade calls a Fatface.

The Fatface was born, by most accounts, at the Thorne foundry in London around 1803. It was not a response to a felt typographical lack; it was a response to the shop window. The printer needed display type loud enough to be read from across a street, and the didone, with its extreme contrast, was the ready material. The foundry exaggerated the thicks, pinched the thins, and sent the results to the poster printers.

## The modern revival

The modern revival of the Fatface dates to the 1990s and the rise of desktop publishing, which put ultra-heavy display faces on every designer's menu. The present author counts more than thirty new Fatface designs issued between 2005 and 2020, of which perhaps eight are technically excellent. The rest are interesting for their failings.

<blockquote>The Fatface is a difficult face. Its weight is so great that a single mis-drawn counter, a single badly-kerned pair, ruins the whole panel.</blockquote>

## On the setting

<p>A Fatface is best set at maximum scale &mdash; forty points or more, on a panel that can absorb the weight. Set at text size, the Fatface is illegible. Set at middle sizes, it is ugly. Set at the sizes for which it was designed, it is magnificent.</p>

The present book treats the setting of the Fatface at length, with examples drawn from eighty jackets of the last century. A fold-out plate at the back gathers a dozen canonical settings for the student's reference.
