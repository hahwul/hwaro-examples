Meridian Loft was a converted garment workshop above a laundromat, and by 1971 it had become the unofficial home studio for a loose community of players moving jazz toward something less interested in chord changes than in mood, repetition, and breath. Nadia Okafor booked three overnight sessions there in August and kept the loft's tall windows open through all three, letting Pico Boulevard traffic bleed onto the tape as a fifth rhythm section voice.

<!-- more -->

## The Session

There was no control room in the conventional sense — the two-track recorder sat on a table in the same space the band played in, run by whichever member of the group wasn't currently needed on an instrument. Okafor treated the sessions more like extended rehearsals that happened to be captured than formal recording dates, and the album's six side-long pieces were assembled afterward from roughly fourteen hours of continuous tape, edited down with a razor blade on a kitchen table.

## The Take

The piece that opens the record began as a warm-up: Sunny Achebe alone on congas for nearly four minutes before anyone else joined, a duration the group initially planned to trim. Okafor overruled the edit, arguing the slow accumulation — Terrence Boyd's electric piano entering almost imperceptibly, then Wolf Iverson's bass, then her own flute line arriving last, well after the piece had already found its center — was the entire point of the record. Cut it short, she said, and you're left with a song. Left whole, it's an entrance.

## Why It Still Matters

"Glass House" sat outside the era's commercial fusion current, too patient for radio and too electric for jazz purists, and circulated for years mostly as a private pressing traded among Los Angeles musicians. Its embrace of ambient street noise as a compositional element anticipated ideas that wouldn't be widely discussed in jazz criticism for another two decades.

> "We didn't close the windows because the room needed the city breathing in it. Silence would have been the fake thing." — Nadia Okafor, from a 2003 radio interview
