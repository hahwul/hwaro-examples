Cardinal Sound's basement tracking room sat four feet below street level, which was fine every day of the year except the three or four when the river came up. On October 3, 1966, it came up during the Desmond Ruo Quintet's booked session, and the studio's owner gave the band a number instead of a warning: ninety minutes before the generator room took on water and the power went with it.

<!-- more -->

## The Session

Ruo had planned a relaxed afternoon of soul-jazz standards and one original. Instead the band tracked with the studio door open so they could hear whether the street outside was still passable, working through material fast and loose, skipping the usual multiple takes per tune in favor of one good pass and moving on. Big Lou Hartwell later said it was the only professional session of his career where the band applauded itself between songs out of relief rather than pride.

## The Take

"Low Water" — the tune, not just the eventual album title — was written on the spot during a break, Ruo vamping on the Hammond while the engineer changed a reel, Hartwell picking up the riff on tenor almost by accident. There was no chart. Fabien Cato's guitar comping across the tune is entirely improvised call-and-response with the organ, both players reading each other's hands rather than any page. The take that made the record is the only complete one; the tape ran out eleven bars after the final note, and the power went out four minutes after that.

## Why It Still Matters

Soul-jazz sessions of the period were often accused of favoring groove over ambition, but the urgency baked into "Low Water" — audible in tempos that push slightly ahead of where the band usually sat, in Reyna Solis's drum fills that clip early because there wasn't time to let them breathe — reads today as a kind of accidental documentary evidence. The record sounds like exactly what it was: five musicians beating a flood.

Cardinal Point Records, the label behind this date, had already put one hard-bop classic to wax nine years earlier in a Chicago studio nowhere near a flood plain — see [The Amber Room Session](/episodes/amber-room-session/) for that, considerably calmer, afternoon.

> "We weren't trying to make history. We were trying to make it to the parking lot with dry shoes." — Desmond Ruo, interviewed for a 1989 reissue
