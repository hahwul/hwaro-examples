By four in the afternoon, Fantom Sound's second-floor room had already eaten two reels of quarter-inch tape. The Ampex kept dropping a half-second of low end every time the transport hit full speed, and engineer Cal Deutsch was down on the floor with a flashlight, convinced it was a worn capstan. Wendell Cato sat at the house Baldwin the whole time, not practicing, just resting his hands flat on the closed lid.

<!-- more -->

## The Session

Cato had booked three hours and used most of them waiting. Cardinal Point's owner, who was paying for the session out of a shoe-repair-shop budget, came upstairs twice to ask how much longer. When Deutsch finally got a clean transport running a little after five, there was time for one full pass at the title tune before the room had to turn over for a radio jingle date at six.

## The Take

There was no second chance, and the trio knew it. Cato called the tempo down from what they'd rehearsed — slower, more space between phrases — because he'd decided a rushed take would sound rushed forever. Otis Rell's bass sits half a beat behind the piano through the whole first chorus, not from sloppiness but because Cato kept leaving gaps for him to fill, and Rell kept filling them a fraction late, on purpose, until the two of them were locked into something closer to conversation than accompaniment. Junie Marsh plays the entire eleven minutes with brushes; there wasn't time to mic a full kit properly, so he never touched sticks.

## Why It Still Matters

"The Amber Room Session" is often cited as the record where hard bop first let a ballad tempo carry the same weight as an up-tempo blowing session. Cardinal Point pressed nine hundred copies. Most of what circulates today are dubs of dubs, tape hiss and all — which is, if you ask Cato's estate, more or less how the room actually sounded that day.

> "We didn't fix the low end. We just played through it and hoped nobody noticed. Somebody always notices, but by then it's on the record." — Cal Deutsch, engineer's notebook, 1957
