Marisol Kade brought one sheet of manuscript paper to Round Table Recording on the longest day of 1964, and it had four chords on it, none of them meant to resolve into the next. The instruction to the rhythm section was simple and, for 1964, strange: stay on this vamp as long as it takes, and do not cadence until I tell you to.

<!-- more -->

## The Session

Round Table's studio B was a converted church hall with a nine-second natural decay, which Kade had specifically requested over the label's preferred, drier studio A. She wanted the tenor to bloom and hang rather than cut. Engineer Frankie Osei ran two ribbon mics at opposite ends of the room instead of close-micing anyone, betting that the hall itself was the fourth instrument on the date.

## The Take

The take that became the title track runs nineteen minutes unedited, though the released version fades at eleven. Href Solano later said the quartet lost track of time entirely — no one was counting choruses, and the take only ended because Two Ton Ibarra's bass string finally slipped out of tune from the sustained tension of holding one droning pedal note for that long. Kade heard the pitch bend as the string gave out, took it as a sign, and brought the tune home in the next eight bars.

## Why It Still Matters

"Solstice Blue" arrived the same season as several better-known modal statements but pushed the form further toward true drone — Solano's left hand barely moves for the first six minutes, holding a single open fifth while his right hand explores increasingly distant scales above it. Critics at the time called it formless. Musicians who heard it in rehearsal tape circulation called it the record that gave them permission to stop resolving things.

> "Everyone kept waiting for the turnaround. I kept telling them there wasn't going to be one." — Marisol Kade, liner notes, 1964
