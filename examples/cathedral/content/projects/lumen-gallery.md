## Design Intent

The Lumen Gallery was commissioned by a private collector to house a growing collection of large-scale contemporary art. The brief called for spaces that could accommodate works up to five meters in height, with lighting conditions that shift throughout the day without ever producing direct sunlight on the art.

The architects responded with a building organized around light. A series of north-facing clerestory windows run the length of the main gallery hall, filtering daylight through deep concrete fins that diffuse and redirect illumination across the walls. The result is an even, shadowless wash of natural light that changes in temperature and intensity with the weather and season.

## Structure and Materiality

The building is cast entirely in board-formed concrete, using rough-sawn Douglas fir formwork that leaves a pronounced wood grain texture on every surface. The concrete mix includes a small percentage of white calciumite aggregate, giving the walls a faintly luminous quality in raking light.

Interior floors are poured concrete, ground smooth and sealed with a matte finish. Their surface is deliberately imperfect --- shallow undulations and aggregate patterns visible underfoot create a tactile counterpoint to the precision of the walls above.

Secondary spaces --- offices, storage, a small library --- are lined in white oak paneling, providing warmth and acoustic absorption. Door hardware and display fixtures throughout are custom-fabricated in unlacquered brass, chosen for the patina it develops over years of handling.

## Gallery Spaces

The main hall measures forty meters long, eight meters wide, and five meters to the underside of the clerestory beam. Its proportions reference the nave of a basilica, a deliberate association that the architects have acknowledged. Artworks are mounted on freestanding concrete walls that can be repositioned along a recessed floor track, allowing the hall to be reconfigured for each exhibition.

A secondary gallery on the upper level is smaller and more intimate, with lower ceilings and translucent polycarbonate panels replacing the clerestories. This room is intended for works on paper and smaller sculptures, where the softer, more diffuse light protects fragile materials.

Between the two galleries, a double-height stairwell serves as a transitional space. A single brass handrail traces the perimeter of a helical stair, and a vertical slot window at the landing frames a view to the adjacent birch grove.

## Environmental Strategy

The building's thermal mass is its primary climate control mechanism. The thick concrete walls absorb heat during the day and release it slowly overnight, maintaining stable interior temperatures critical for art conservation. Supplementary heating is provided by radiant floor loops, and a displacement ventilation system introduces fresh air at low velocity through floor-level grilles, avoiding drafts that could disturb hanging works.
