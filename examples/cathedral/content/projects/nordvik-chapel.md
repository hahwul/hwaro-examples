## Design Intent

Nordvik Chapel sits on a granite outcrop at the edge of a small fishing village, facing the open sea. Commissioned by the local parish to replace an earlier structure lost to fire, the chapel was conceived as a building that belongs to its site as much as to its congregation.

The architects describe the design as a "folded shell." A single continuous surface of pre-patinated copper forms both roof and walls, bending sharply at two points to create a faceted volume that recalls the angular profiles of the surrounding mountains. The form is deliberately non-symmetrical, with one fold rising higher than the other to mark the location of the altar within.

## Structure and Materiality

The copper cladding is fixed to a secondary timber framework of laminated pine ribs, which in turn spans between three concrete portal frames. The ribs are left exposed on the interior, where their curved profiles create a rhythmic pattern overhead --- an abstracted reference to the upturned hulls of fishing boats stored along the shoreline.

The interior is lined entirely in Scots pine, hand-planed and left untreated. Over time, the wood will darken and develop a warm amber tone, while the exterior copper, already started in a verdigris finish, will continue to shift toward the deep green characteristic of coastal copper patinas.

The floor is local slate, split rather than sawn, preserving the stone's natural cleft surface. Underfloor heating ensures the slate remains warm despite its thermal conductivity, allowing the chapel to function comfortably through the long Arctic winter.

## Interior

The chapel seats thirty-two on benches of the same pine used on walls and ceiling, arranged in two rows flanking a central aisle. The altar is a single block of granite, sourced from the same outcrop on which the building stands, its top surface polished smooth while its sides retain their quarried roughness.

Natural light enters through two sources. A narrow vertical slot on the east wall captures the low morning sun, projecting a blade of light across the interior that tracks slowly toward the altar as the morning progresses. Behind the altar, a large panel of handmade glass --- slightly irregular in thickness, with a faint blue-green tint --- filters the western sea light into a diffuse glow that fills the space in the afternoon.

There is no artificial lighting in the main chapel. Evening services are held by candlelight, with simple iron candelabra mounted on the end of each bench row.

## Site

The chapel is reached by a footpath of crushed slate that winds up from the village harbor. No vehicular access was introduced; the architects insisted that the approach on foot is integral to the experience of arrival. A low dry-stone wall, built by local craftsmen using traditional techniques, encloses a small churchyard on the leeward side.
