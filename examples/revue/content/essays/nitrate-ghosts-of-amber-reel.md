*Low Water* arrived at Amber Reel Laboratories in a fire safe, which is the correct way to ship nitrate and also, senior colorist Petra Lindqvist admits, the reason half the staff still flinch when the safe's hinges creak. The 1931 melodrama had not been screened since a rained-out sneak preview in a converted grain exchange, and the print — five reels of hand-tinted nitrate, the amber and rose still faintly visible under decades of amber varnish of its own making — had spent most of that time believing itself abandoned.

Nitrate does not fail quietly. It passes through five recognized stages of decomposition, and by stage three the film base has begun converting itself into something closer to gunpowder than picture stock; archivists who have opened a can at that point describe a smell like nail-polish remover left too long in a hot room. *Low Water* was staged at stage two — brittle, amber-brown at the edges, but still readable under a loupe — which meant Amber Reel had perhaps eighteen months before the decision was made for them regardless.

What restoration actually means, in a case like this, is a wet-gate scan run at a fraction of normal speed, frame by frame, on a machine built specifically because nobody manufactures projectors gentle enough for a print this fragile anymore. Lindqvist's team does not clean the image so much as negotiate with it: dust and scratches lift out cleanly, but the tinting — amber for lamplight, pale blue for the single night exterior — has to be rebuilt by eye, because no reference print survives to check the work against. The colorist becomes, in effect, the last person alive who remembers what the film looked like, even though she has never actually seen it whole.

The finished digital restoration screens at the Amaranth next season, on a programme billed honestly as an evening with the original hand-tinting reconstructed rather than approximated. It is a strange kind of honesty: the film an audience will watch is not the film that existed in 1931, only the best available argument for what that film was. Lindqvist keeps the original nitrate anyway, in a cold vault held at four degrees, still slowly turning to powder on its own schedule. Some ghosts you restore. Others you simply keep company with for as long as the chemistry allows.
