The Amaranth's midnight slot has run every third Friday for six years, and Renzo Katsura's *Nine Ronin* trilogy has anchored it twice, which by house tradition makes it the closest thing the programme has to a mascot. There is no version of this booking that makes financial sense — three subtitled samurai epics from 1962 to 1965, projected back to back for an audience that has to be awake for work in six hours — and that, according to programmer Jonas Merritt, is precisely the point.

<!-- more -->

Midnight audiences are not the same animal as the seven o'clock crowd. They arrive having already decided to be there, which changes what a screening is allowed to ask of them. A three-hour trilogy with two ten-minute changeover breaks is, at 7pm, an imposition; at midnight, sitting in it becomes the evening's whole shape, and the exhaustion by reel twelve starts to feel less like a cost than an ingredient. Katsura's fight choreography, all long static takes and sudden violent cuts, lands differently on a body that has stopped performing alertness for anyone.

The print itself is a 35mm dupe negative struck from a rougher interpositive than anyone would prefer, with a visible reel-change flare eleven minutes into the second film that the projection booth has stopped bothering to hide — regulars applaud it now, the way a crowd applauds a familiar drum fill. Merritt has resisted requests to source a cleaner restoration when one eventually surfaces, on the theory that the flare has become part of what the slot means to the people who keep the seats warm every third Friday. A perfect print, he argues, would be a different, lesser ritual entirely.

What the midnight slot actually sells is duration and consequence — the sense that watching a film all the way through, at a real cost to tomorrow, still means something. Attendance skews younger than the rest of the Amaranth's calendar, which the front office reads as encouraging and Merritt reads as simply obvious: nobody under thirty has been taught that staying up for a movie is supposed to be a sacrifice. He is just the one still willing to schedule it as one.
