Sol Andrade has written a condition report for every print that has passed through his booth in fourteen years, and almost none of them have ever been read by anyone but the next projectionist down the line. The reports are meticulous — reel-by-reel notes on splice count, base shrinkage, soundtrack wear, the exact frame where a scratch starts and the exact frame where it resolves — and they exist entirely outside the version of repertory cinema that audiences experience, which is to say the overture.

<!-- more -->

Nitrate and safety prints alike used to carry actual overtures — short pieces of music or, later, a studio ident and a run of trailers assembled specifically for that print's touring cycle. Almost none of that material survives as intentionally preserved; it survives, when it survives at all, because a projectionist like Andrade declined to cut it off the head of a reel when a house complained the show was running long. What plays before *Low Water* at the Amaranth next season is not a curatorial choice so much as an accident of someone's refusal to throw something away thirty years ago.

Andrade's condition reports are, in their way, the same instinct working in the opposite direction: a record made for an audience of one future projectionist, describing exactly how a print is dying so that whoever inherits it can decide how much longer to run it before the damage becomes irreversible. He writes them in full sentences rather than the standard shorthand codes, which his colleagues find eccentric and which he defends on the grounds that a code tells you what happened and a sentence tells you what to expect.

Nobody claps for an overture, and nobody reads a condition report for pleasure. Both exist in the gap between what a print is showing and what a print is doing to itself while it shows it — the unglamorous administrative layer underneath every restoration essay this magazine will ever run, including, Andrade would point out, this one.
