The Continental Print Exchange keeps its booking ledger the old way — a bound volume, one line per print movement, initialed by whichever projectionist signed a can in and whichever one signed it back out. Priya Anand spent four months this year transcribing eleven years of entries, and what she found was less a shipping record than an accidental history of which repertory houses trusted each other and which did not.

<!-- more -->

A healthy print, the kind that gets booked forty times before anyone notices wear, tends to move in tight regional loops — three or four houses that pass it between them within a week's drive, minimizing the handling that damages sprocket holes and splices. A print that starts appearing on long-haul routes, crossing several regions in a single season, is almost always a print in decline: houses closer to it have stopped requesting it, usually because a prior booking left it visibly worse, and the exchange has to reach further afield to find someone who has not heard yet.

The ledger also tracks something no database migration ever preserved: a column, informal and never mandated, where projectionists occasionally wrote a single word about a print's condition beyond the standard damage codes. *Tired* appears eleven times across the decade. *Nervous*, oddly, appears twice, both times attached to the same print of a wartime aviation picture prone to unexplained breaks at the same splice. Anand has not found an official condition report that would explain *nervous* — only the word itself, passed down through three different projectionists' handwriting, none of whom ever met.

Digital prints do not accumulate this kind of record — a file either plays or it corrupts, with none of the intermediate, personality-adjacent decline of a physical object being slowly worn down by exactly the affection that keeps it in circulation. Anand's transcription project has no formal use yet. She keeps working on it anyway, on the theory that a distribution ledger is, whether anyone intended it or not, the closest thing repertory cinema has to an oral history.
