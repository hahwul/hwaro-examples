Pairing *Low Water* with a documentary about grain-exchange architecture was not, Teodor Vasse admits, the pairing anyone expected when the Amaranth's spring programme went up. The instinct is to double a melodrama with another melodrama, tone matching tone. Vasse's rule runs closer to the opposite: a good double bill argues two films into a conversation neither would have volunteered for alone, and the friction between them is the actual programme, not a side effect of it.

<!-- more -->

The mechanics are simple enough to state and hard to execute. A double bill runs two to three hours with an intermission, which means both films have to justify their own runtime while also leaving room for the other. Vasse pairs by structure more often than by subject — a slow first film followed by something with more forward motion, so the second half of the evening feels like acceleration rather than fatigue. Content pairing is treated with suspicion: two films about the same thing tend to flatten each other, the second becoming an echo instead of an answer.

The house's most requested pairing in three years put *The Widow's Walk* against a fifteen-minute industrial short about fog-signal maintenance on a coastal lighthouse, sourced from a regional archive with no relation to the feature whatsoever. Audiences who complained about the short beforehand were, by Vasse's account, the same audiences who mentioned it unprompted afterward — the incidental fog of a maintenance documentary recasting the deliberate fog of the feature as something an audience had just spent fifteen minutes learning to actually look at.

None of this is written down anywhere the audience can read it, which is deliberate. A double bill that explains its own logic in the programme notes has already lost the thing that makes it work: the sensation, walking out, of having pieced the connection together yourself, on your own schedule, sometime around the second film's third act.
