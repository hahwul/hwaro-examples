Revue started as a photocopied handout at a single repertory house and has stayed close to that footprint on purpose: one issue a month, six essays deep, no news cycle to feed. We are not a reviews outlet. A film does not get coverage because it opened this week — most of what we write about has been in circulation for decades, some of it for the better part of a century — and it does not get coverage because a studio wants it to. It gets coverage because something about how it survives, or how a house programs it, is worth an argument.

## What we cover

Three beats run through every issue. **Restoration** essays follow the labs and colorists making the hundreds of small, undocumented decisions that turn a decaying print into a screenable one — what to trust when the only surviving reference disagrees with itself. **Repertory Programming** essays sit with the people who decide what plays next to what, and why a midnight slot behaves nothing like a seven o'clock house. **Print Culture** essays look at the layer underneath both: the ledgers, condition reports, and booking circuits that move physical prints between houses long after anyone remembers who first struck them.

We do not cover streaming premieres, festival competition slates, or anything whose primary venue is a phone screen. If a piece cannot be traced back to an actual print, an actual lab, or an actual booth, it does not run.

## The desk

Six essayists rotate the monthly issue: Odile Farrant on restoration chemistry and nitrate, Jonas Merritt on repertory programming and the Amaranth's midnight slot, Marguerite Okonkwo-Hale on color science and dye transfer processes, Teodor Vasse on double-bill logic and house scheduling, Priya Anand on distribution ledgers and print circulation, and Casimir Vezey on projection practice and condition reporting. Bylines run in caps because the print under discussion is the subject of the sentence, not the writer.

## Get in touch

Restoration labs, repertory programmers, and projectionists with a print worth an essay can write to the desk with a paragraph of real context — no press kits, no screener links without a physical print behind them. We read everything in the order it arrives and we do not guarantee coverage. For a sense of tone before you pitch, start with [this issue's lead essay](/) on the front page.
