A sewn binding on raised bands is the oldest and most resilient method of holding a gathered book together. The principle is simple: a set of cords, tensioned across the sewing frame, forms the armature around which each signature is sewn in sequence. The cords &#8212; not the spine of the book itself &#8212; carry the weight of every page-turn.

## The Frame

The sewing frame is an upright wooden stand with two pegs at its base and a crossbar at its head. Between them, three or five hemp cords are tensioned vertically, each at the position where a sewing station has been marked on the signatures. The first signature is laid horizontally against the cords; the sewer passes the needle from outside through the first station, around the nearest cord, and back out through the same station.

The thread is always linen &#8212; waxed, never synthetic &#8212; and is drawn taut after each passage around a cord. A loose sewing will produce a sloppy book; too tight a sewing will cup the signatures and make the book refuse to open flat.

## The Kettle Stitch

Between signatures, at the head and tail of the spine, the sewer makes a **kettle stitch** &#8212; a small hitch that passes the thread through the space between the most recent signature and the one below it, locking the two together independent of the band-sewing. The kettle is what prevents the book from coming apart at the top and bottom of the spine; it is the reason one can drop a well-bound book and still find, hours later, that its pages remain in order.

> The bands hold the book open. The kettle holds the book together. A binding without both is not a binding at all.

## Rounding and Backing

Once all signatures are sewn, the textblock is rounded &#8212; gently tapped with a hammer from the fore-edge toward the spine &#8212; and then backed, which is to say, the spine edges of each signature are fanned outward to form a small shoulder along each side of the spine. This shoulder is what receives the boards of the binding, and it is what gives a well-bound book its characteristic slight convex curve at the spine.

A flat-backed book is a machine-bound book. A rounded-and-backed book is a hand-bound book. There is no middle ground.
