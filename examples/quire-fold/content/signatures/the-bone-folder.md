The bone folder is a simple tool: a flat blade of polished bone, typically cow, shaped like a narrow spatula with one rounded end and one tapered. It is the first tool a binder learns to use, and it is the tool that most distinguishes the hand-bound book from the machine-bound one.

## The Discipline of the Crease

A fold made only by pressure of the fingers is an imprecise fold. The paper resists; it rebounds; it does not retain its crease. A fold made with the bone folder is a decisive fold: the flat edge is drawn along the fold with firm, even pressure, compressing the paper fibers along a single line and forcing them to adopt a new orientation. A properly folded signature, pressed overnight under a weight, will never unfold.

Three virtues govern the use of the bone folder:

1. **Evenness.** The pressure must be identical along the entire length of the fold. A fold with a weak spot will crack; a fold with a strong spot will cup.
2. **Direction.** The folder is drawn away from the body, along the direction of the paper grain. Folding against the grain produces a rough crease that will never sharpen.
3. **Restraint.** The paper must not be scored. A scored fold is brittle; it will crack within a year. The bone folder compresses; it does not cut.

## Why Bone

Bone is used, rather than metal or hardwood, because bone is slightly softer than the paper. A metal folder will polish the paper to a glossy strip along the fold &#8212; visible to any careful eye. A bone folder compresses the paper without altering its surface. Hardwood is acceptable but does not slide as cleanly across the paper's tooth.

Modern synthetic folders, made of Teflon or nylon, are sometimes substituted. They perform acceptably. They do not have the weight of tradition, nor do they acquire a polish from a lifetime of use. A good bone folder, inherited from one's teacher, is a meaningful object.

> One can bind a book without a bone folder. One cannot bind a book well without one.

## Upon the Scoring Blade

For heavier papers &#8212; cover stock, or the boards of a pamphlet binding &#8212; a scoring blade is used in place of the bone folder. The blade runs in a slot on a straight edge, depressing the paper into the slot and producing a crease that can be folded without cracking. This is a different craft and belongs to a different article.
