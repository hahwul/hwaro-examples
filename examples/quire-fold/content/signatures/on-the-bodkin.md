Before any thread is passed, before the frame is strung, before the signature is even gathered &#8212; the binder takes up the **bodkin**. A steel point, set in a handle of boxwood or beechwood, honed to a single fine taper. It is the oldest tool in the bindery. It predates the press. It will outlast the press.

## The Pricking Template

One uses the bodkin in concert with a pricking template: a strip of card or wood, cut to the height of the signature, marked at the three sewing stations with small incised notches. The template is laid along the fold of the signature; the bodkin passes through each notch in turn, piercing all four bifolia at once.

The result: a signature in which every leaf is pierced at precisely the same three points. When the sewer comes to sew, the thread passes through pre-cut holes &#8212; never through raw paper. This preserves the paper, which would otherwise tear under the strain of the thread, and it ensures that the sewing stations align across all signatures, which is the first condition of a flat-opening book.

> The bodkin is a promise made from binder to sewer: *I have marked this signature truly; you need only follow me.*

## Upon the Choice of Stations

Three stations are traditional for an octavo. Two stations will produce a weak spine; four stations will cramp the thread. The spacing is not arbitrary: the first station is set at one-sixth of the height from the head, the third at one-sixth from the tail, and the middle station squarely between them. This leaves the head-cap and tail-cap free for the kettle stitch, which binds the signatures to one another independent of the band-sewing.

Some binders, following the German tradition, prick five stations and sew on five bands. This produces a more flexible spine but requires a taller signature and a more patient sewer. We retain three stations as a matter of taste, and because our paper is not tall enough for five.
