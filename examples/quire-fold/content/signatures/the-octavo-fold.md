The octavo is the most common of the gathered formats and the most conservative. A single sheet, printed on both sides, folded three times perpendicular to the last, yields sixteen pages of text arranged in a single quire. The folding is the most delicate operation in the workshop, and the most unforgiving.

## The First Fold

The first fold is along the long axis of the sheet, bringing the top edge down to meet the bottom. This fold is made with the printed side out, so that pages 1 and 16 &#8212; the outermost pages of the finished signature &#8212; face away from the compositor's hand. The bone folder is drawn along the fold with firm pressure; the paper must be creased sharply, but not so sharply that the fibers split.

## The Second Fold

The second fold is perpendicular to the first, bringing the right-hand edge over the left. This fold is made with the previously folded edge &#8212; the head of the signature &#8212; at the top. Register is maintained by aligning the printed rules at the fold: if the compositor has done his work, these rules will meet within half a millimeter.

## The Third Fold

The third fold is again perpendicular, bringing the new right-hand edge across to the left. This final fold establishes the spine of the signature and determines the sewing direction. After the third fold, the signature is a small, thick, eight-page booklet, unopened at three edges.

## Upon Opening the Quire

The binder, having folded the signature, must now open three of its four edges: the head, the tail, and the fore-edge. The spine edge is not cut; it remains a single continuous fold, around which the signature will be sewn. These three cuts are made later, after binding, with a sharp plow-knife on the finishing press.

> A folded-but-uncut signature is a promise: *the book is not yet a book; it is still a sheet, waiting to become one.*

## Imposition and Its Discipline

Because the sheet is folded, the pages must be set in a non-sequential order &#8212; the **imposition**. For an octavo, the eight pages of one side of the sheet are arranged so that folding places them in sequence 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8. The corresponding eight pages of the reverse side fall into sequence 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16. The compositor who sets these pages must hold the finished signature in his mind's eye at every moment &#8212; one misplaced chase, and the entire sheet is a ruin.

This, more than the folding or the sewing, is the true craft of gathering: the compositor's capacity to see the folded signature in the unfolded sheet.
