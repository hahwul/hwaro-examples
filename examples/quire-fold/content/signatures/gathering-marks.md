At the foot of the first page of each signature, the compositor sets a single letter &#8212; A, B, C, D, and onward &#8212; in a smaller type than the body text. These are the **gathering marks**, also called **signatures** in the narrower typographic sense. They are invisible to most readers and indispensable to the binder.

## Why They Exist

A book, before it is bound, is a stack of loose quires. Each quire is an identical object &#8212; eight leaves of paper, folded, indistinguishable from its neighbors except by its content. The binder, opening a crate of freshly pressed signatures, has no means of ordering them except by reading the first line of each, which is slow and impractical when the edition runs to many copies.

The gathering mark solves this. The binder opens each quire to its first page, reads the letter at the foot, and places the quire in its alphabetical position. A stack of forty signatures can be gathered in the time it takes to recite the alphabet twice.

## The Sequence

The alphabet used for gathering marks is traditionally twenty-three letters: the Latin alphabet omitting J, U, and W (or omitting I, V, and W, depending on the period). For works longer than twenty-three signatures, the alphabet is doubled: Aa, Bb, Cc. For works longer than forty-six signatures, it is tripled: Aaa, Bbb. The great Bibles of the sixteenth century sometimes ran to six-fold alphabets.

## Signature Numbers

Within each signature, the first four rectos carry a progressive number: A, A2, A3, A4. The remaining four rectos go unmarked. This permits the binder to verify, before sewing, that each signature contains its full complement of four bifolia &#8212; a signature missing a bifolium is called a **defective signature** and must be discarded or, at best, set aside for a replacement sheet.

> The gathering mark does not help the reader. It helps the binder. It is a conversation between compositor and binder, conducted in a private language across the workshop floor.

## Upon Retention of the Marks

Modern typography generally suppresses the gathering marks, or replaces them with a printed register at the end of the volume. We have retained them, in miniature, in the page metadata at the head of each signature of the present publication &#8212; not because the reader needs them, but because they are part of the craft, and the craft is the subject.
