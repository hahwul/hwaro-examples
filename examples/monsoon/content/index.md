Monsoon is the log of a single north-facing pantry, roughly sixteen degrees
year round, where nothing is hurried. I keep misos that will not be ready
until I have half forgotten them, krauts that finish in a fortnight, and a few
long brews that mark time by the week. Every jar on the shelf is counted from
the day it was packed, so the front page always tells the truth about what is
alive and how old it is.

I am not a lab. There is no sous-vide precision here, no starter cultures
mailed in dry ice. The tools are a kitchen scale, a bag of coarse sea salt,
a few weights cut from a broken plate, and the patience to leave a lid alone.
What follows is what I have learned by tasting, mostly by being wrong, and
occasionally by throwing a whole crock into the compost and starting again.

If you are new to this: weigh everything, keep your hands and your jars clean,
trust salt over luck, and read the smell before you read the calendar. A good
ferment announces itself. The day count is only there to remind me how long
I have been listening.
