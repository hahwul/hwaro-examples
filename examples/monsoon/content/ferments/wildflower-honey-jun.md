Jun is often called the champagne of kombucha, which oversells it a little, but
there is something to the comparison: fed on honey instead of cane sugar and
brewed on green tea, it ferments cleaner and more floral, with a fine soft fizz.
It also prefers the cool, which suits a larder that never sees a warm day.

<!-- more -->

The culture came from a friend's crock as a pale, rubbery disc floating in a cup
of its own liquid. A jun SCOBY is fussier than a kombucha one — it sulks in heat
and refuses cheap honey — but at sixteen degrees on decent wildflower honey it
is perfectly happy, just slow. Slow is fine. Slow is the house style.

## The feeding schedule

I run a continuous brew, drawing off a third each week and topping back up with
fresh cooled tea. Keeping it on a rhythm is what keeps it healthy:

```text
day 0   brew 1 L green tea, cool to room temp, stir in 80 g honey
day 0   add tea to the vessel with the SCOBY and 200 ml starter liquid
day 7   taste; when just off-sweet, draw 300 ml, bottle
day 7   top up with 300 ml fresh honeyed tea
weekly  repeat; second-ferment the bottles 2 days for fizz
```

The one rule I never break: cool the tea fully before it meets the culture. Warm
tea shocks a jun SCOBY, and hot tea kills it outright.

## How it drinks

The first draws were thin and a bit yeasty while the culture settled into the
cold. By the second month it found its footing — dry, lightly sparkling, with
the honey reading as a floral note rather than sweetness. A two-day second
ferment with a bruised sprig of mint turns it into the nicest thing I make.
