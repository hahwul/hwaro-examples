The nukadoko is the most demanding thing I keep and the one I am most fond of.
It is a living bed of fermented rice bran that you bury vegetables in overnight —
a pickle that is ready by morning. But unlike the crocks I can walk away from for
a season, the bran bed wants a hand in it every day. Skip too many and it sulks,
or worse.

<!-- more -->

You build the bed once and then tend it forever. Mine started as toasted rice
bran, salt, and enough kombu-and-water brine to bring it to the texture of wet
sand. For the first two weeks you feed it scraps — cabbage cores, carrot tops,
the ends of things — turning it twice a day so it sours evenly and the yeasts
and lactobacilli settle in. Those first pickles get thrown away; they are just
there to season the bed.

## The daily rhythm

```text
morning   lift out last night's pickles, rinse, slice
daily     turn the whole bed hand-over-hand, right to the bottom
evening   bury tonight's vegetables, salted cut-side down
weekly    add a spoon of salt; taste the bran, adjust
if away   press flat, salt the surface, refrigerate
```

Turning it by hand is not optional and it is not hygiene theater — it moves air
through the bed, keeps any one microbe from taking over, and, honestly, tells you
more about how it is doing than any thermometer could. You learn the smell fast:
sour and nutty is right; sharp and boozy means it is too warm and needs a rest in
the cold.

## Early days

At barely a month old the bed is still finding its balance — the cucumbers come
out clean and lightly sour, the turnips need a longer sit. It gets better every
week as the flavor deepens and the bran learns the shape of my kitchen. This is
the jar I will still be feeding when the barley miso is finally ready.
