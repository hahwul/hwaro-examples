Not everything on the shelf takes a year. This one is a fast, loud, cheerful
ferment — golden beets cut into matchsticks, a good thumb of ginger, and a
bruised bay leaf, fermented in a proper brine rather than dry-salted. It is the
jar I open when the long crocks are testing my patience and I need a reminder
that some things are ready in a fortnight.

<!-- more -->

Beets are dense and low in their own water, so a dry salt rub never draws enough
liquid to cover them. The fix is a submersion brine: mix salt and water to a
known strength, pour it over, and weigh everything under the surface. I use a
three percent brine here — a touch stronger than a kraut, since the pieces are
chunky and I want them to stay crisp.

```text
water .............. 1000 g
sea salt (3%) ....... 30 g
golden beets ........ 600 g, in matchsticks
fresh ginger ........ 30 g, thin coins
bay leaf ............ 1, bruised
```

Dissolve the salt in a little warm water, then top up with cold so the brine
goes into the jar cool. Pack the beets, ginger, and bay in tight, pour the brine
over until everything is drowned, and fit a weight. A cabbage leaf tucked across
the top makes a cheap, effective float-catcher.

## Reading it

Golden beets keep their color where red ones would bleed everywhere, so you can
actually watch the brine turn from clear to faintly cloudy as it takes off. It
was fizzing by day three and pleasantly sour by day twelve, the ginger sharpening
everything and the bay sitting quietly underneath. Once it tastes right it moves
to the cold shelf, where it stays crunchy far longer than a shredded kraut would.
