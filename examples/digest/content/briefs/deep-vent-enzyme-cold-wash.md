Most laundry enzymes are picky: they work best somewhere near body temperature and a mildly alkaline pH, and they denature — permanently lose their folded shape and stop working — well before water gets anywhere near boiling. A dredge survey of a hydrothermal vent field off the Kelso Ridge, run out of the Anders Bay Marine Station, turned up an archaeon that lives happily in water hot enough to cook it under normal atmospheric pressure, and produces a protease built to match.

<!-- more -->

The organism was recovered from a chimney sample where ambient water temperature measured 92°C. When researchers isolated its dominant protease and tested it in the lab, it retained full activity at that same temperature and kept roughly 70 percent of peak activity at pH 10 — conditions under which nearly all conventional laundry proteases have already unfolded and stopped functioning entirely.

To see whether that heat-and-pH tolerance had any practical value outside a vent, the team expressed the enzyme recombinantly in a lab host bacterium and ran it against standard protein-based laundry stains — egg, grass, and blood — in cold-wash simulations designed to mimic a 20°C household wash cycle rather than the enzyme's native boiling environment.

```text
Native activity: 100% @ 92°C
pH 10 activity: ~70% of peak
Cold-wash stain trial (20°C, 30 min): egg, grass, blood
Stain clearance vs. standard commercial protease: ~2x faster, equal dose
```

At that dose, the vent enzyme cleared the test stains about twice as fast as a standard commercial protease used as a comparison. The team's working explanation is that an enzyme built to stay folded and functional under extreme heat has a structural rigidity that also makes it unusually stable — and therefore unusually active — at the comparatively mild temperature of a cold wash, where a conventional enzyme is already operating well below the conditions it evolved for.

The obstacle now is not the enzyme's performance but its production cost. Recombinant expression yields from the lab host are currently low, and nobody on the team is prepared to say whether fermentation can be scaled to industrial volumes affordably before that economics question gets answered — which they note is true of a great many promising extremophile enzymes that never make it past this stage.
