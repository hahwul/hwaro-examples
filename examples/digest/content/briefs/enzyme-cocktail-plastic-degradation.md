Polyethylene is the most common plastic in the world and one of the most stubborn: its carbon backbone has no weak points for ordinary microbial enzymes to grip, which is a large part of why it can sit in a landfill for centuries without meaningfully breaking down. A team at the Calder Institute of Materials has spent three years pushing a wax-degrading enzyme, originally found in a soil bacterium that eats plant cuticle wax, toward something that can do the same thing to a plastic film.

<!-- more -->

The starting enzyme could barely touch polyethylene on its own — plant wax and plastic share a similar carbon-chain structure, but the wax version is shorter and studded with oxygen atoms the enzyme's active site expects to find. Over eleven rounds of directed evolution, the team introduced mutations that widened the enzyme's binding pocket and reduced its dependence on those oxygen groups, each round screened against thin polyethylene film as the sole test substrate.

The current variant, tested at 45°C, converted roughly 60 percent of a low-density polyethylene film's mass to soluble fragments within 21 days. The unmodified starting enzyme, run as a control under identical conditions, showed no measurable degradation over the same period.

```text
Substrate: LDPE film, 40 micron, low crystallinity
Temp: 45°C, pH 8 buffer
Round 0 enzyme, 21d: no measurable mass loss
Round 11 enzyme, 21d: 60% mass converted to soluble fragments
```

The catch is crystallinity. The film used in these trials was deliberately thin and amorphous; thicker or more crystalline polyethylene, the kind used in most rigid packaging, resists the enzyme almost as well as it resisted the original wax-degrading version. The tightly packed crystalline regions simply do not give the enzyme's active site anywhere to attach.

The team's next step is not further enzyme evolution but a mechanical pre-treatment — briefly milling or stress-fracturing the plastic to disrupt crystalline regions before the enzyme is introduced — on the theory that the two approaches solve different halves of the same problem. A pilot batch reactor combining both steps is planned for later this year, sized to handle a few kilograms of mixed film waste per run.
