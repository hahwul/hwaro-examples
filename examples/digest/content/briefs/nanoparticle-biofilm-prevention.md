Most anti-biofilm coatings work by killing bacteria after they land — releasing a low dose of antimicrobial agent that kills or disables cells that attach to the surface. It is a reasonable approach, but any coating that kills bacteria selectively also selects for the bacteria that survive it, which is one reason resistant strains tend to show up on antimicrobial medical hardware over time. A joint project between Fenwick Polytechnic Institute and Braeloch National Laboratory took a different angle: instead of killing bacteria after they attach, stop them from attaching at all.

The coating is built from silica nanoparticles bearing a dense brush of hydrophilic polymer chains anchored to the particle surface. The brush layer creates a physical and osmotic barrier — bacterial cells that approach the surface encounter chains in constant thermal motion that resist compression, and a hydration layer that makes it energetically unfavorable for a cell to displace the water molecules bound to the polymer and get close enough to attach. Nothing about the mechanism depends on the coating being toxic to the bacteria; it works even against cells that never touch it.

In a 21-day continuous-flow model built to simulate a catheter under steady use, coated tubing held biofilm coverage under 4 percent, compared with 78 percent coverage on uncoated tubing over the same period. As a comparison point, the team also ran a standard antimicrobial coating through the same model against both a susceptible strain and an antibiotic-resistant strain of the same species. The antimicrobial coating performed well against the susceptible strain but showed a clear drop in effectiveness against the resistant one. The polymer-brush coating showed no such gap — coverage stayed low against both strains, which is the expected result of a mechanism that never depended on killing anything in the first place.

The flow model is a simplification of a real body, and the team is explicit that a static lab loop cannot capture everything a device experiences in a patient — protein fouling from blood plasma, mechanical flexing, intermittent flow. The next step is a large-animal catheter model, planned to run for at least eight weeks, before anyone on the team is willing to talk about a path toward testing in people.
