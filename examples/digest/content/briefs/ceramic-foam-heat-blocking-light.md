Insulating a window or a greenhouse roof usually means giving something up: foam glass and most rigid insulation panels block heat well because they scatter light of every wavelength indiscriminately, which is exactly what makes them opaque. A ceramics lab at Ostrow University has been working on a foam that scatters selectively — built with pores tuned to the size of the infrared wavelengths that carry conducted heat, while leaving the pore structure mostly transparent to visible light.

<!-- more -->

The material is a silica-alumina foam produced by freeze-casting, a process where a liquid slurry is frozen in a controlled direction so that ice crystals grow into aligned channels, then the ice is sublimated away and the remaining ceramic skeleton is sintered. Because the ice growth can be steered by the freezing rate and mold geometry, the resulting pores come out narrow, elongated, and consistently sized — small enough to scatter mid-infrared wavelengths strongly, but close enough to the wavelength of visible light that most of it passes through with only modest scattering.

At 4mm thickness the foam measured a thermal conductivity of 0.021 W/m·K, roughly a third of typical foam glass insulation at comparable thickness, while optical haze — the fraction of transmitted light that gets diffusely scattered rather than passing straight through — rose only from a baseline 2 percent to 9 percent. That is enough haze to give the material a soft, frosted look rather than true window clarity, but well within range for daylighting applications like skylights or greenhouse glazing, where diffuse light is often preferred anyway because it does not create hot spots or sharp shadows on plants below.

The bottleneck is production speed. Freeze-casting is inherently a batch process — each panel needs a controlled freeze cycle measured in hours, and pore alignment depends on tight control of the freezing front, which does not scale easily to continuous manufacturing. The team is now testing whether a continuous extrusion process, with the pore-forming step handled by a sacrificial fiber additive instead of directional freezing, can reproduce the same aligned pore geometry fast enough to be commercially viable. Early extrusion samples have shown weaker directional alignment and correspondingly higher thermal conductivity, so that tradeoff is still unresolved.
