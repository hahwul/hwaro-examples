Bacteriophages, the viruses that infect bacteria, are usually described as predators of the gut microbiome — they kill their bacterial hosts and keep populations in check. But a phage that integrates into a host genome instead of killing it outright, a process called lysogeny, leaves something behind when it eventually moves on: stretches of bacterial DNA it picked up along the way, sometimes including genes for antibiotic resistance.

<!-- more -->

Researchers at the Tarrant Institute for Applied Microbiology wanted to know how often this actually happens inside a real gut, not a culture flask, so they sequenced phage and bacterial DNA from 640 stool samples and looked for matching gene fragments that had clearly moved between the two. The rate they found — roughly one transfer event per bacterial genome per week of residency, extrapolated from the fragment turnover across samples — was about four times higher than estimates drawn from laboratory co-culture experiments, which tend to use a narrow set of well-studied phage-host pairs under conditions that do not resemble a dense, competitive gut community.

Antibiotic-resistance genes were disproportionately represented among the transferred fragments relative to their overall frequency in the bacterial genomes sampled, which the team suspects is because resistance genes are often carried on mobile genetic elements that are already primed for movement between genomes, not because phages are selecting for them specifically.

The practical concern is for phage therapy, where a cocktail of phages is used to target a specific harmful bacterial strain. If a therapeutic phage is capable of lysogenic integration, and integrates into an off-target bystander species in the gut, it could carry a resistance gene from the target strain to species that did not have it before, even while it successfully suppresses the strain being treated. That is a plausible failure mode that a phage's ability to kill its target does not rule out.

The team's response is not to abandon phage therapy but to screen candidate phages more carefully. They are now cataloguing a panel of strictly lytic phages — the kind that always kill their host and never integrate — specifically for clinical development, on the theory that a phage that cannot lysogenize cannot become a gene-transfer vector, regardless of what else it does well.
