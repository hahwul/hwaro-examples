When a chronic lung infection stops responding to every antibiotic left on the shelf, the remaining options are limited and often unpleasant. A phase 1/2 trial run out of the Anders Bay Clinical Research Unit tried a different tool on eighteen patients with drug-resistant Pseudomonas aeruginosa infections: a cocktail of four bacteriophages, each selected and matched to the specific strain isolated from that patient's own airway.

<!-- more -->

Phage therapy works by using viruses that infect and kill bacteria, chosen for their ability to attack a particular strain. Because resistance to a given phage can develop almost as quickly as resistance to a drug, most modern protocols use a mixed cocktail rather than a single phage, on the theory that a bacterial population is less likely to develop resistance to four attack mechanisms at once than to one.

Patients received a nebulized dose of their matched cocktail daily for fourteen days. Thirteen of the eighteen showed a significant reduction in bacterial load by the end of treatment, and nine reached culture-negative status — no detectable target bacteria in airway samples — by day thirty. No serious adverse events in the trial were attributed to the phage treatment itself, though two patients reported transient fever within hours of the first dose, consistent with an immune response to bacterial cell debris rather than to the phages.

The trial was small, open-label, and not placebo-controlled, which limits how much can be concluded about efficacy versus the natural course of these infections — a caveat the research team states plainly in their own writeup rather than leaving for a reader to infer. The more durable finding may be about resistance: by day sixty, three of the initial responders had detectable rebounds in bacterial load, and sequencing showed those rebound populations carried mutations in the surface receptors the phages had been targeting, a classic phage-resistance signature.

That rebound is prompting the next design change. Rather than treating with one fixed cocktail for the full course, the team is now testing a rotating regimen — swapping in a second, non-overlapping four-phage set partway through treatment — on the premise that a bacterial population that evolves resistance to cocktail A during week one has not yet evolved resistance to an unrelated cocktail B introduced in week two.
