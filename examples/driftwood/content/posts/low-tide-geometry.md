The lowest tides of the month expose a wide flat of sand that is normally submerged. The retreating water leaves behind an intricate system of marks: parallel ripple lines where waves oscillated back and forth, branching drainage channels where water found its path seaward, and smooth depressions where stones or shells once sat.

Seen from standing height the patterns are interesting. Seen from inches away, lying prone on damp sand with a wide-angle lens, they become architectural. The ripple marks cast tiny shadows in low-angle light, creating a rhythm as regular as corrugated metal. The drainage channels fork and merge with a logic that echoes river deltas viewed from altitude.

The best light for this work arrives in the final thirty minutes before sunset, when the sun angle is low enough to rake across the sand surface and give every ridge its own shadow. The colour temperature shifts warm, turning the wet sand into shades of copper and bronze.

Timing is critical. The patterns exist only in the interval between the tide's retreat and the wind's erasure. Within an hour of exposure, the finest ripple marks begin to soften. By the following morning, only the deepest channels remain.
