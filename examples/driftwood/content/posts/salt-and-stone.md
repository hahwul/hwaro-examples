The basalt here is old. Columnar formations jut from the headland at angles that suggest some ancient violence, now long settled into stillness. At high tide the waves reach the base of the pillars and send white spray upward in slow arcs. At low tide the rock stands exposed, its surface pitted and rough, wearing a crust of dried salt that catches the afternoon light.

These photographs were made over three visits during late winter. The conditions favoured overcast skies with occasional breaks --- the kind of light that removes hard shadows and lets the stone's texture speak for itself. A polarising filter cut the glare from wet surfaces and deepened the contrast between dark basalt and pale foam.

The most compelling frames came at the transition between tides, when thin sheets of water still moved across the lower rocks. In those moments the stone appears both submerged and revealed, its colour shifting between charcoal dry and near-black wet. The salt residue left behind as water retreats creates a faint white patterning, like a map of the ocean's last reach.

What holds attention here is not drama but endurance. These formations have stood against the Pacific for longer than anything human along this coast.
