After the January storms the upper beach was littered with fresh driftwood. Most of it was Douglas fir and Sitka spruce, carried down the coast from river mouths to the north. Some pieces were enormous --- full root systems torn from eroded banks, bark still attached in ragged strips. Others were small and polished, tumbled smooth by weeks in the surf.

The older pieces interest me most. Wood that has spent a full season or more on the beach takes on a particular quality: bleached silver-grey on the exposed surfaces, darker where it contacts sand, the grain raised and pronounced by alternating cycles of wet and dry. The cellular structure becomes visible to the naked eye. Knots stand proud of the surrounding wood, harder and more resistant to erosion.

Close work with a macro lens reveals the wood as a landscape in miniature. The grain lines become ridges and valleys. Cracks open into canyons. Bore holes from marine organisms punctuate the surface like craters.

These photographs were made in flat midday light, which suits the subject. The absence of strong shadows lets the texture carry the image. A pale, even illumination across the wood surface emphasises the subtle colour variations --- the warm tans, the cool greys, the occasional flush of amber where resin once pooled.
