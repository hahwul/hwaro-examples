Applying traditional UI principles to a glassmorphism context requires a delicate touch. Contrast and hierarchy must be maintained despite the transparency. One of the primary challenges is ensuring that interactive elements, like buttons and inputs, remain clearly distinguishable from static content panels.

When everything is a frosted glass card, how do users know what they can click? We solve this by varying the properties of the glass. A static card might have a higher blur radius and lower opacity, blending more into the background. An interactive button, however, might have a lower blur, higher opacity, and perhaps a subtle color tint or a stronger border highlight. This creates a clear visual distinction.

Hover states are also crucial. When a user hovers over a glass button, increasing the opacity slightly or brightening the border highlight provides immediate visual feedback. This reinforces the interactive nature of the element and improves the overall user experience, ensuring that the aesthetic choices don't compromise the fundamental usability of the interface.
