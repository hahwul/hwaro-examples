## Can one small heat pump really heat the whole house?

Yes, and that is the entire point of spending the budget on the envelope first. A
tight, well-insulated shell has a low enough heat-loss rate that a single
cold-climate mini-split — the kind rated to keep working at minus-25 — carries a
Kelvin comfortably. The coldest overnight low a delivered house has ridden out on
one unit is minus-27, and the occupants slept fine.

## Won't a tight house feel stuffy or get damp?

The opposite, when it is done right. Airtight is not the same as unventilated. A
heat-recovery ventilator continuously exchanges stale indoor air for fresh
outdoor air while keeping roughly 85% of the heat, so you get constant fresh air
without opening a window to the cold. Because the walls stay warm on the inside
face, condensation and mold have nowhere to form.

## Do I need a special foundation?

Usually not. Most Kelvins sit on adjustable helical piers or a simple pier pad,
which we can spec for your soil. The Larch, on a park-model chassis, can also
stay road-legal if you register the trailer. We will tell you honestly which
option suits your site.

## How cold is "winters that mean it"?

Our envelope standard is drawn for a design temperature down to minus-24, with
houses delivered into places that regularly see minus-30 wind chills. If you live
somewhere colder still, tell us — we can thicken the assemblies. We would rather
over-build the shell than have you fighting the cold with the thermostat.

## Can I customize the layout?

Within reason. We keep the envelope details fixed because they are what make the
house perform, but window placement, interior finish, loft versus ground-floor
sleeping, and storage are all yours to shape. Read [the build](/process/) to see
where the flexible decisions sit in our sequence.
