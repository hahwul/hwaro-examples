Most tiny houses are drawn for a mild afternoon. They photograph well on a
grassy lot in June and then leak heat like a tent the first time the
thermometer drops below freezing. We started Kelvin because we were tired of
towing beautiful little boxes that nobody could actually live in through a real
winter.

The order of operations matters. If you spend the budget on cladding, fixtures,
and a wood stove before you have solved the envelope, you end up with a
handsome house that is expensive to heat and miserable to occupy in a cold
snap. So we do it the other way around. The wall assembly, the airtightness
layer, and the ventilation strategy are settled before anyone picks a
countertop.

A tight, well-insulated shell is quieter, drier, and healthier than a leaky
one. It holds a steady temperature overnight without the heat pump short-cycling,
it keeps condensation off the windows, and it lets a single small heat source
do the work of a furnace. That is the whole idea behind the name: hold a
temperature, and hold it honestly.
