A silkworm cocoon is not woven. It is secreted. The moth larva, near the end of its caterpillar stage, expresses a single continuous filament from a pair of glands beneath its mouth and wraps itself in roughly a kilometer of that filament over the course of three days. The result is a closed shell of fibroin held together by a glue called sericin.

Reeling is the process of recovering that filament intact. Cocoons are simmered in water hot enough to soften the sericin without dissolving it entirely. A reeler brushes the surface with a stiff-bristled implement until a few loose ends catch. Several ends are gathered together—six, eight, sometimes more—and twisted into a single composite thread, then wound onto a reel at a steady tension.

The number of cocoons combined determines the gauge of the resulting silk. Reelers monitor the thread under a magnifying lens and add a fresh cocoon whenever one nears its end, splicing it in by laying the new filament against the running thread. Done well, the join is invisible after weaving.

A kilogram of raw silk requires roughly three thousand cocoons, and each cocoon requires the death of the moth that made it. Pierced cocoons—those left to allow the moth to emerge—yield only short staple fibers and must be spun rather than reeled, producing a coarser yarn known as schappe.

Industrialization mechanized the basin and the reel but did not change the fundamental sequence: soften, find the end, combine, wind. The constraint is biological, not technical.
