The Hanmaru Ridge Trail does not exist. I should say that first, because everything else in this journal is written as if it does: six hundred kilometres of high granite crest, seven marked stages, thirteen shelters, two mountain villages, and a stone gate at either end. The trail is invented; the walking is not. Every ache, every wet boot, every shelter ledger and ramyeon dinner in these pages is borrowed from real ridges I have walked and stitched onto an imaginary map.

The journal is called Gil — 길 — the Korean word for road, path, way. It is the word you see burned into the wooden trail markers here, one every kilometre, each stamped with the distance walked so far. That is why every entry in this record hangs off a dotted line with a number beside it. The line is the trail. The number is how much of it was behind me.

## How the record is organised

I walked south to north, from Dolbat Gate on the coast range to Cheonmun Gate — the Sky Gate — at the northern terminus, between April and June of 2025. The walk broke naturally into seven stages of six to nine days, and each stage became one entry, written in the valley within a day or two of coming down. Fifty-four of the seventy-two days were walking days; the rest were spent eating, sleeping, and convincing myself to go back up.

Each entry records the stage's span in kilometres, its trailheads, and the days it took, along with whatever the ridge decided the theme of that week would be: rain, wind, deer, the arithmetic of food weight. The {{ to(path="tags/", label="tags") }} collect those themes across stages.

## The walker

I sign these pages **Ha-eun Chae**, which is as invented as the trail. Ha-eun carries a 41-litre pack, trusts paper maps over telephones, and believes the second-best thing on any mountain is barley tea at a shelter table. The best thing is the moment, once or twice a stage, when the ridge opens on both sides at once and the road ahead is visible for a day's walk.
