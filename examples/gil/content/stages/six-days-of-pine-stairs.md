The trail begins with a joke at the walker's expense: two stone pillars, a wooden sign reading kilometre zero, and then four hundred pine-log steps straight up the first ridge before the path deigns to level out. By the time I reached the first marker post — a squat wooden stake stamped GIL 1 KM — I had already stopped twice to rearrange everything in my pack.

<!-- more -->

The first stage is the trail clearing its throat. It climbs from the coast range through pine plantation onto open granite, and it spends six days teaching the same lesson: the ridge does not care what your daily average was supposed to be. My plan said fifteen kilometres a day. The pine stairs said eleven, and eleven it was.

## What the pack weighed, and what it should have

I left Dolbat Gate carrying 16.4 kilograms including water, which every book and every forum told me was too much, and which I can now confirm was too much. The camera tripod went home from the first road crossing in a padded envelope, along with a second cooking pot whose purpose I can no longer explain. Nothing I posted home was missed for even an hour.

> Ledger, Oksan Shelter, night six: "Feet: two blisters, taped. Spirit: absurdly good. Pack: lighter by post."

The shelters this far south are simple plank platforms with rain tanks, and Oksan is the best of them, set in a saddle where the pines finally give way to bare rock. From its steps I got the first real look at the road ahead — the ridge unrolling north, crest after crest, until it dissolved into haze somewhere past the ninetieth kilometre. Six days done. Five hundred and twenty-two to go, and I already understood that the number was the least interesting thing about it.
