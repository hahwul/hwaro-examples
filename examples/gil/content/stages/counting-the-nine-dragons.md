Guryong means nine dragons, and the crest north of the pass earns the name: nine granite towers in eleven kilometres, each one climbed by ladder, chain, or an act of faith in one's boot rubber. Old walkers in Cheongseok told me the rule before I left — count the dragons out loud, or the ridge will add a tenth. I counted. Out loud. Alone on a mountain, a person will honour any superstition that comes with a view like that.

<!-- more -->

This was the first stage walked in proper sunshine, and it changed the trail's whole personality. The same granite that had spent stage two trying to break my ankles now glowed like bread crust from six in the morning until the light went copper at dinner. For seven days the walking was hard and the seeing was easy.

## Things with more legs than me

The dragons' shoulders turn out to be excellent habitat. A pair of water deer watched me eat lunch below the fourth tower, chewing in exact synchrony, unimpressed. On the sixth morning a yellow-throated marten crossed the trail with something in its mouth and gave me the briefest audit of my existence before flowing off the edge of the slab. And at the seventh tower the ravens found me — two of them, patrolling the thermals along the east face, following me most of a day for reasons I choose to believe were social.

> Marker post, GIL 213 KM, in someone else's handwriting: "Dragon 7 of 9. My knees have opinions."

The stage closes with a long, gentle descent to Baram Gap — Wind Gap — where the ridge pinches to a grassy saddle and the pines lean permanently northeast, combed by a wind that had, that evening, mercifully clocked off. Two hundred and forty-one kilometres down. The gap's name, I would learn within a day, is not decorative.
