The last stage is the longest, which feels like the trail's final piece of pedagogy: ninety-seven kilometres, nine days, and a finish line you can see — in clear weather — from three days out. Cheonmun Gate sits on the northernmost summit like a keyhole of pale stone, and once it comes into view the whole ridge behind you reorganises itself into one continuous sentence you are about to finish reading.

<!-- more -->

I had promised myself I would not hurry, and mostly I kept the promise. I took the side path to every lookout I had been skipping since April. I let a rest hour become a rest afternoon at Baeknyeong Shelter because the ledger there had an argument going back two seasons about the correct number of ramyeon blocks per day (the answer is four; the ledger is wrong). On the seventh night I walked out onto the slabs at midnight, turned off the lamp, and stood in the middle of six hundred kilometres of dark rock under a sky so loaded it looked structural.

## Arrival

The gate itself is two natural pillars the trail's founders capped with a single lintel stone, and the tradition is simple: you touch the lintel, you say nothing, you walk through. A family of day-hikers up from the northern trailhead applauded politely, which nearly undid me. On the far side there is a wooden post, the last one, stamped GIL 600 KM, and below the stamp someone has carved the trail's whole philosophy in four words: *the road keeps going*.

It does. The distance markers in this journal stop at 600, but I notice I have started reading maps of the coastal path that begins where the ridge ends. Feet: recovered. Pack: 11.8 kilograms and honest. Spirit: already asking about ferries.
