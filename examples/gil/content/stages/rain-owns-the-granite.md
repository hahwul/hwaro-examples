It rained on five of the seven days, and on the other two the granite was still wet, which on this ridge amounts to the same thing. Stage two taught me the trail's first real rule: dry rock is a path, wet rock is a decision. Eighty-six kilometres of decisions, then, most of them made at half a kilometre an hour with both hands on the chain bolted to the slab.

<!-- more -->

The rain was never dramatic. No storms, no thunder, nothing to write a survival story about — just a soft, patient cloud that sat down on the ridge on the second morning and stayed the better part of a week. Visibility shrank to forty metres. The world became an ellipse of wet rock, three marker posts long, sliding north with me at its centre.

## The shelter economy

In weather like that the plank shelters stop being amenities and become the whole architecture of the day. You do not walk to a view; you walk to a roof. I learned to read the shelter ledgers like newspapers: who was a day ahead, who had turned back, which rain tank was running low. At Myeongdo Shelter someone had left half a bag of dried persimmons with a note in pencil — *for whoever is wettest*. I was, and I ate them, and I have thought about that person warmly for three hundred kilometres.

The stage ends at Guryong Pass, where the trail crosses the first paved road since Dolbat Gate. I stood under the highway overhang for twenty minutes watching water braid off the roof edge, then walked down to the village of Cheongseok, ate two dinners in one sitting, and slept eleven hours. The ledger entry for that night reads, in full: "164 km. Everything is drying on somebody's radiator. The ridge can wait two days."
