Every long trail has a stage like this one, and the honest journals admit it: the long middle, where the drama of beginning has worn off, the pull of the finish is not yet felt, and the work is simply to walk well, day after day, through country that repeats itself like a good song. Junmak to Norumok is eighty-two kilometres of high forested crest — oak, birch, then fir as the ridge climbs — with no towers, no chains, and no resupply of any kind.

<!-- more -->

Which makes it the trail's great problem in arithmetic. Eight days of food is eight kilograms, and eight kilograms is the difference between walking and hauling. I spent a full rest day in Junmak with everything laid out on the guesthouse floor, weighing, culling, repacking, and the resulting table went into the back of the map case:

| Item | Days 1–4 | Days 5–8 |
|---|---|---|
| Rice and grain, per day | 180 g | 160 g |
| Ramyeon blocks | 4 | 4 |
| Dried anchovy and kelp stock | 120 g | 80 g |
| Persimmons, dried (morale) | 8 | 8, guarded |

The persimmons were non-negotiable. Stage two taught me that.

What I remember best is the rhythm the stage settled into: up in the blue hour, twelve or thirteen kilometres by early afternoon, then an hour of what shelter culture calls maru time — sitting on the platform edge, boots off, watching light move along the ridge like a slow tide, doing absolutely nothing at the highest possible quality. The shelters here are older, darker, more storied; Norumok's ledger goes back nine years and reads like a village register of a village that is always leaving.

Four hundred and twelve kilometres. The fir trees now smell like the north.
