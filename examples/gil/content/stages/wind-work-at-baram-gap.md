There is walking, and then there is wind work: the stiff-legged, pole-braced, hat-in-the-pack labour of moving a human body along an exposed crest while the air treats it as a sail. The anemometer at the Baram Gap ranger box read a steady 14 metres per second the morning I set out, gusting past 20 in the notches. The ranger stamped my trail card and offered a single word of advice: "Crouch."

<!-- more -->

Stage four is the trail's most exposed, nearly ninety kilometres with the ridge narrowed to a walkway and the land falling away hard on both sides. In wind it becomes a negotiation. I learned to time my steps between gusts the way you time a run between waves, and to trust the trail builders, who bolted galvanised chain along every section where a stumble would have consequences beyond embarrassment.

## An inventory of things the wind took

- One map case lid, unclipped for four seconds at kilometre 259.
- One glove, gone over the east face while I photographed the sunrise.
- One instant coffee, snatched as powder, mid-pour, at Neopbawi Shelter.
- Every conversation attempted outdoors for eight consecutive days.

What the wind gave back was the sky. Scoured, rinsed, unreasonably blue, with visibility so long that on day five I could see both the dragons behind me and the pale hump of Norusan ahead — three stages of trail in a single glance, past and future pinned to one line of rock.

The stage ends by dropping mercifully west into Junmak, a two-street village that has fed ridge walkers for as long as the trail has had a name. At the minbak guesthouse the owner looked at my wind-burned face, said nothing, and brought out a stew whose size I will describe only as corrective. Three hundred and thirty kilometres. Past halfway, and the road now tilts north for home.
