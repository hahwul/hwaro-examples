June arrived on the ridge like a debt collector, and the only way to dodge it was to walk in the dark. Stage six became a stage of headlamps and half-light: up at three, walking by half past, ten kilometres banked before the granite began to shimmer. Saebyeok means dawn, and for nine days I walked toward the pass of that name inside the thing itself.

<!-- more -->

Night walking on a marked ridge is easier than it sounds and stranger than it sounds. The dotted line of the trail becomes literal: reflective bands on the marker posts float up out of the black one at a time, each catching the lamp thirty metres out, so that the whole enterprise reduces to walking from one small moon to the next. I stopped checking the map at night. The posts carried me.

## The ridge's second shift

The hours before light belong to a different management. Owls first — three species by voice, none by sight. Then the water deer, whose eyes throw the lamp back in flat green coins from the scrub. Once, unmistakably and only once, a boar: a low freight-train shape crossing the trail ahead, trailed by three piglets at a trot, none of them granting me so much as a glance. I stood very still for a long time anyway, out of respect and biology.

> Ledger, Cheongun Shelter: "Walkers tonight: 1. Stars: all of them."

Dawn itself, met daily, never repeated a performance. Some mornings it came up as a thin saffron wire under cloud; once as a full furnace door swung open behind Norusan. I reached Saebyeok Pass on the ninth morning at 5:40, exactly as the sun cleared the ridge I had just spent a week crossing, which felt less like coincidence than like a receipt. Five hundred and three kilometres. One stage left, and I have started walking slowly on purpose.
