Autolyse — resting flour and water before adding salt and levain — is supposed
to make dough more extensible and easier to develop. I wanted to know how long
is actually worth it, so I ran the same 78% dough with five different rests.

<!-- more -->

## What autolyse does

During the rest, flour hydrates fully and enzymes begin quietly cutting the
flour's proteins and starches. Gluten starts to organise itself with no
kneading at all, so the dough that emerges is more extensible and needs less
mechanical work. Salt is held back because it tightens gluten and slows the
enzymes; adding it late lets the rest do its job.

## The five rests

Five 300 g doughs, pure bread flour, 78% hydration, identical except for the
autolyse time before salt and levain went in. Extensibility is judged by how
far a 20 g piece stretches before tearing — crude, but consistent within one
session.

| Rest | Dough after mixing | Stretch (cm) | Notes |
|------|-------------------|--------------|-------|
| 0 min | shaggy, tight | 14 | needed the most kneading |
| 10 min | smoother | 19 | clear improvement |
| 20 min | supple, extensible | 26 | best handling |
| 45 min | slack, slightly sticky | 27 | marginal gain |
| 90 min | loose, a touch gluey | 25 | starting to over-relax |

## Reading the curve

The jump from 0 to 20 minutes is dramatic; the dough goes from tight and
tearing to smooth and stretchy. After 20 minutes the returns flatten, and by
90 the dough was actually a little *worse* — over-relaxed and slightly sticky,
as enzymatic activity started to loosen it past useful.

## Takeaway

For a plain bread-flour dough at this hydration, **20 minutes of salt-delayed
autolyse** captured essentially all the benefit. Ten is a good compromise when
you are in a hurry. Ninety is a waste of counter time and, past an hour, can
start to soften the dough more than you want. I have folded 20 minutes into
[the control schedule](@/experiments/seventy-percent-control-loaf.md) as
standard.
