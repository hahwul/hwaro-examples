Everyone says a longer cold retard means more flavour. I wanted to know how
much more, and whether the loaf pays for it in sourness. So I mixed one dough,
split it, and put half back in the fridge for an extra day.

<!-- more -->

## Setup

One batch, 800 g flour total, mixed to 82% hydration and 2.0% salt, developed
to a moderate windowpane, and bulk-fermented at 24 C until roughly 50% risen.
Then it was divided into two 700 g pieces, pre-shaped, and put into the fridge
at a measured 4 C. Piece A came out at 48 hours; piece B at 72.

Everything downstream was identical: same shaping, same 250 C covered bake,
same oven position. The only variable was twenty-four hours of cold.

## The retard schedule

```text
Day 0  18:00  mix, DDT 24 C
Day 0  21:30  bulk ~50%, divide + pre-shape
Day 0  22:00  into fridge @ 4 C
Day 2  22:00  piece A out  -> shape, proof 45 min, bake   (48 h)
Day 3  22:00  piece B out  -> shape, proof 60 min, bake   (72 h)
```

Piece B needed a longer bench proof coming out of the cold — it was stiffer
and colder in the core, and wanted the extra fifteen minutes to relax before
it would take a score cleanly.

## Results

The 72-hour loaf was clearly better, but not for free.

- **Crust:** B blistered heavily — the fine, glassy bubbling that long retards
  are famous for. A was smooth by comparison.
- **Flavour:** B was noticeably deeper, with a nutty, almost cheese-rind note.
  A was pleasant but flat next to it.
- **Acidity:** B was also more sour. Not unpleasantly, but if you dislike tang,
  48 hours is your stopping point.
- **Oven spring:** roughly equal. Both scored ears cleanly.

## Verdict

For this flour and this levain, 72 hours is the better loaf if you like a
little tang and a blistered crust. If you want maximum rise with a milder,
sweeter flavour, stop at 48 and do not feel you have cut a corner. The extra
day buys flavour and crust texture, and charges you in acidity. Worth it, most
weeks.
