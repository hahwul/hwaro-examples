Most sourdough writing chases wetter and wetter doughs. This experiment went
the other way: a firm 60% hydration dough for a tight, chewy crumb, the kind
you want for a sandwich loaf or a bagel. Stiff dough is a different animal, and
it taught me things the wet ones never did.

<!-- more -->

## Why go low

A 60% dough holds shape beautifully, takes deep scoring, and bakes to a dense,
even, chewy crumb. It is also physically hard work — there is far less water to
lubricate the gluten, so it fights back. That resistance is exactly why it is
worth practising: if you can develop a stiff dough by hand, a slack one feels
effortless afterwards.

## The build

Bread flour 500 g, water 300 g, salt 10 g, stiff levain 75 g. That is a dough
you can pick up like modelling clay.

```text
Bread flour   500 g
Water         300 g   (60%)
Salt           10 g   (2.0%)
Stiff levain   75 g   (15% of flour)
DDT            25 C
Bulk          ~5 h at 25 C, slow because there is little water
```

## Handling notes

- **Mixing** took real effort; I switched from folds to a proper knead.
- **Bulk** ran long — around five hours — because low hydration slows
  fermentation. Do not rush it by warming the dough or you will overproof the
  outside before the inside moves.
- **Shaping** was a joy: the dough held every bit of tension I gave it.
- **Scoring** was clean and deep, no dragging.

## Where it went wrong

Buoyed by how well the loaf came out, I tried to push the same dough into
bagels — shaped, boiled 45 seconds a side, baked at 230 C. They were dense and
a little raw at the centre. A bagel wants a different balance: more malt, a
firmer shape set overnight in the cold, and a shorter boil. The stiff dough
was not the problem; my bagel method was. Filed under *failures* honestly.

## Verdict

At 60%, the loaf itself was exactly what I wanted — tight, chewy, sliceable,
long-keeping. Low hydration is not a lesser dough, just a firmer, slower one
with its own rules. The bagels are a separate experiment for another day.
