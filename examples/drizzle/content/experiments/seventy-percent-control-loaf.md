Before you can say a variable *changed* something, you need a loaf that
doesn't change. This is that loaf: a deliberately ordinary 70% hydration bake
I can reproduce with my eyes closed, and against which everything else here is
measured.

<!-- more -->

## The specification

A control has to be written down precisely or it isn't a control. Total flour
600 g, water 420 g, salt 12 g, stiff levain 120 g. Nothing clever.

```text
Bread flour       540 g
Whole wheat        60 g
Water             420 g   (70%)
Salt               12 g   (2.0%)
Stiff levain      120 g   (20% of flour)
DDT                25 C
```

## The schedule

The whole point is repeatability, so the timeline is fixed to the clock, not
to feel. On a 25 C kitchen day this lands right every time.

- **0:00** — mix to rough incorporation, rest 30 min (autolyse with levain in)
- **0:30** — add salt, develop with slap-and-folds
- **0:30–4:00** — bulk at 25 C, four coil folds at 45-minute intervals
- **4:00** — pre-shape, bench 20 min, final shape
- **4:20** — into banneton, fridge overnight at 5 C
- **next day** — bake cold, 250 C covered 22 min, 235 C uncovered 18 min

## Why 70%

Seventy per cent is the hydration where this flour is most forgiving. The
dough is supple but still holds a shape on the bench; it takes a score
cleanly; it springs reliably. It is not the most open crumb I can make, nor
the most flavourful — those live higher and colder. It is simply the most
*repeatable*, and repeatability is what a control is for.

## Results, every time

Golden, evenly domed, a clean ear, a moderately open and even crumb, mild
tang. Boring in the best sense. When a later experiment produces something
better or worse, this is the loaf I am comparing it to — so I bake one every
few weeks just to make sure the control itself hasn't drifted.
