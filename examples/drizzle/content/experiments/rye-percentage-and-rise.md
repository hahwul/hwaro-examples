Rye behaves nothing like wheat. It has almost no gluten and a lot of
pentosans — gums that grab water and interfere with structure. I stepped the
rye fraction up in a fixed 90% dough to watch the rise die.

<!-- more -->

## Why rye is different

Wheat builds an elastic gluten network that traps carbon dioxide. Rye can't;
its proteins don't form the same web. What rye *does* have is pentosan gums
that bind enormous amounts of water — which is why rye doughs feel slack and
sticky at hydrations where a wheat dough would be firm. More rye means more
water bound into gum and less gas held into structure.

## The five doughs

Same 90% hydration, same 27 C, same rye levain at 20%. Only the flour blend
changed. Each was 400 g total flour.

| Rye % | Feel | Rise (relative) | Crumb |
|-------|------|-----------------|-------|
| 10 | supple, wheaty | 100 | open, springy |
| 20 | tacky | 88 | slightly tighter |
| 40 | sticky, slack | 61 | close, moist |
| 50 | paste-like | 38 | dense, gummy edge |
| 60 | batter | 22 | brick |

Rise is eyeballed against the 10% loaf, measured by final height over a common
banneton, and cross-checked against
[the 70% control](@/experiments/seventy-percent-control-loaf.md). It is not
laboratory-grade, but the trend is unmistakable.

## Where the cliff is

Between 40% and 50% rye, the loaf stops behaving like a risen bread and starts
behaving like a dense, moist, close-grained rye — which is a fine thing if it
is what you want, but a failure if you were expecting oven spring. Above 50%
you should treat the dough as a batter, bake it in a tin, and forget about an
open crumb entirely.

## Practical takeaways

- Up to **20% rye**, treat it like a wheat dough with a touch more water grip.
- At **30–40%**, drop hydration by a few points and expect a tighter crumb.
- Above **50%**, switch to a tin, lower your expectations for height, and let
  the flavour — deep, malty, sour — be the point.

The pentosans win in the end. Plan the loaf around them rather than fighting
them.
