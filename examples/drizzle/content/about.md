Drizzle is a home kitchen-science log kept by Marta Vellinga, a former lab
technician who now spends her Saturdays chasing one question: what actually
changes when you change one thing in a sourdough?

Every entry here is a controlled experiment, not a recipe. I hold as much
constant as a domestic kitchen allows — the same stone-milled flour, the same
levain lineage started in 2019, the same oven with its known cold spot in the
back-left corner — and move a single variable: hydration, fermentation
temperature, rye percentage, autolyse time. Then I write down what happened,
including the bakes that were flat, gummy, or simply dull.

## How to read an entry

Each experiment opens with a specification block: flour blend, levain, dough
temperature, and the one-line outcome. Methods are always in grams and
degrees Celsius, because "a handful" and "warm-ish" are how results become
impossible to reproduce.

## What the pills mean

The coloured pill beside every title is the dough's **hydration** — the
weight of water as a percentage of the weight of flour. A stiff 55% bagel
dough gets a pale pill; a slack 100%-plus ciabatta-adjacent dough gets a deep
one. The wetter the dough, the darker the label. It is the fastest way to
scan the notebook and know, before reading a word, how the crumb probably
behaved.

## A note on failure

Roughly a third of these bakes did not work. I keep them because a failure
with good notes is more useful than a success you cannot explain. If a loaf
collapsed, you will find out why, in grams. The best place to start is
[the control loaf](@/experiments/seventy-percent-control-loaf.md) — the boring
reference every other bake is measured against — and then
[the full notebook](@/experiments/_index.md), newest first.

Corrections and quibbles are welcome by post: the kitchen is always listening.
