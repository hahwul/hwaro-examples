## Two Lattices in One Crystal

Dauphine twinning is the most common form of twinning in alpha quartz. The right- and left-handed regions of a twinned crystal share the same atomic positions for silicon but have their oxygens rotated by 180 degrees about the c-axis. From the outside the crystal looks like a single grain. Internally it is a mosaic of two domains that respond differently to applied stress and to electric fields.

The boundary between domains is sharp. It runs along well-defined crystallographic planes and migrates only at high temperature, near the alpha-beta transition at 573 degrees Celsius. Below that point the twin walls are effectively pinned.

### Why It Matters

Quartz oscillators rely on the piezoelectric coupling between mechanical strain and electric polarization. In a twinned crystal, the two domains have piezoelectric coefficients of opposite sign. A wave passing through one domain is canceled by an opposing wave from the other. The result is a resonator with reduced Q and an unstable frequency response.

For this reason, optical-grade and oscillator-grade quartz is screened to exclude twinned material. The screening uses one of three methods:

- Etching the surface with hydrofluoric acid, which exposes the domain boundaries as visible steps.
- Polariscopic inspection, which reveals stress contrast at the twin walls.
- Direct piezoelectric mapping, which gives the cleanest result but requires custom hardware.

### Reading the Pattern

A twinned specimen viewed between crossed polars shows a fine herringbone or chevron pattern. Each chevron is a single domain. The size of the domains tracks the cooling rate of the parent rock: slow cooling produces large, coherent regions; fast cooling produces small, irregular ones.

The same compositional logic applies to layout. A page divided into two equally weighted columns reads as twinned; the eye cannot decide which side leads. Asymmetric weighting breaks the twin and gives the reader a clear primary domain.
