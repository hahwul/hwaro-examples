The mirror had been lying for years.

We brought it into the theatre under full restraint protocol. The glass was already sweating.

We made the first cut at the corner of its mouth — my mouth — and the silver started to weep real blood.

Inside the mirror was another operating table. Smaller. Older. On it lay a version of me that had never been cut, never been patched, never been loved by machines.

She looked up at us through the wound we were making.

She smiled with my teeth.

We kept cutting.

By the time we reached the sternum the mirror had begun speaking in my voice from before the first firmware. It begged us to stop. It said the reflection was the only thing still human.

We did not stop.

When the final layer peeled away, there was nothing on the other side.

Only the empty frame.

And in the glass, the version of me that had never been cut was now wearing the scalpel.

She waved.
