<p class="lead">The marginal proof mark is a private notation between two readers &mdash; the proofreader who sets it and the compositor who acts on it. Like any private notation, it has drifted toward illegibility wherever the two readers have stopped speaking. The list below records the marks the house still expects, in the order a galley is read.</p>

## The deletion stroke

<p class="annotated">The deletion stroke <span class="dele">runs through</span> the offending letter, word, or phrase, and a matching <span class="annot">delete sign<span class="proof-number">1</span></span> is set in the margin. The sign resembles a lower-case <em>d</em> with a flourish; it is drawn in one motion, never lifted. A deletion stroke without a marginal sign is read as a hesitation, not a correction, and will be ignored at the stone.</p>

## The caret and its insertion

<p class="annotated">The caret <span class="insert-caret">&#x2227;</span> marks the point of insertion; the matter inserted is written in the margin and underscored once. A caret with no margin entry is the proofreader's most common error, and the most expensive: the compositor reads the caret as a deletion of the space, closes up the line, and the omission travels into print.<span class="proof-number">2</span></p>

## Stet, or letting it stand

<blockquote>Stet is the only proof mark that is also a verb in the imperative. It is set when the proofreader has marked a correction, then changed her mind. Underscore the original matter with a row of dots, and write &lsquo;stet&rsquo; in the margin.</blockquote>

<p class="annotated">A stet without dots is read as a fresh correction; a row of dots without a stet is read as emphasis. The two must travel together. <span class="stet">(stet)</span></p>

## Transposition

The transposition mark &mdash; a hooked line drawn around two adjacent words or letters &mdash; is the readiest correction in the proofreader's hand. It saves the labour of striking out and re-writing, and it is unambiguous when drawn cleanly. It becomes ambiguous, however, when more than two units of matter are caught in the hook; in such cases, strike out and re-set instead.

## Marks that have fallen out of use

<p class="annotated">Several marks recorded in the older manuals are no longer set in this house. The mark for &lsquo;wrong font&rsquo; has been replaced by a written note, since modern composition rarely mixes founts by accident. The mark for &lsquo;turn over&rsquo; &mdash; a small reversed letter &mdash; has been replaced by a written direction, since the compositor is no longer setting from a case of foundry type.<span class="proof-number">3</span></p>

The proofreader who knows the older marks should know also that the compositor reading the galley today may not. When in doubt, write the instruction out in plain words. The margin is wide enough.
