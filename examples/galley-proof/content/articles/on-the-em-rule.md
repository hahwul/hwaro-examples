<p class="lead">Of all the punctuation marks the copy-editor inherits, the em rule is the most pliant, the most abused, and the most misunderstood. It performs at least four distinct grammatical offices, and in each it competes with a rival &mdash; the comma, the colon, the parenthesis, the bullet. A habit of the em rule, once acquired, is difficult to break.</p>

## The four uses

<p class="annotated">The em rule may stand &mdash; first &mdash; for a <span class="annot">parenthetical<span class="proof-number">1</span></span> aside, in the manner of two commas or a pair of parentheses. It may stand, second, for a colon, introducing what follows. It may stand, third, for an abrupt break in thought. And it may stand, fourth, as the bullet of a list or the sign of a speaker's turn in dialogue.</p>

<p class="annotated">In the first use, the pair of em rules must be balanced &mdash; two dashes, one opening and one closing, with the parenthetical matter between them. A solitary em rule in the middle of a sentence is almost always a typographical error, or a sign that the author has abandoned the sentence mid-thought.<span class="proof-number">2</span></p>

<blockquote>The em rule is the most intrusive mark of punctuation, and it is the mark most likely to be used in place of a thought. Spare it; and when you use it, use it like a strike of the gong.</blockquote>

## On spacing

<p class="annotated">The most quarrelsome question in the house style sheet is whether to set spaces around the em rule. <span class="dele">Everyone</span> The American house prefers the close em rule &mdash; no space on either side &mdash; while the British house has historically used the <span class="insert-caret">&#x2227;</span>thin-spaced<span class="insert-caret">&#x2227;</span> en rule in the same office. Our house, after a decade of argument, has settled on the close em, but admits a thin space where the line-break risks ambiguity.<span class="proof-number">3</span></p>

## Common abuses

The em rule is abused when &mdash;

- It is used in place of a semicolon or colon <span class="stet">(stet)</span>, to avoid committing to a subordinate relationship between clauses.
- It is used to join two sentences that the author could not quite decide whether to join.
- It is used as a visual device, strewn through the paragraph for rhythm alone.

The corrective is the copy-editor's oldest gesture: replace the em rule, where possible, with the mark it has displaced. Restore the semicolon; restore the comma; restore the honest full stop.

## A final note on hand-setting

<p class="annotated">In hot-metal composition, the em rule is a separate sort, cast to the full width of the em for the face in use. The compositor who runs out of em rules in the middle of a galley will substitute two en rules, and rely on the proofreader to correct the join. The present galley notes one such substitution on folio twelve; see the query list.<span class="proof-number">4</span></p>
