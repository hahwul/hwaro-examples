Coopering is the ancient art and craft of making wooden staved vessels, bound together with hoops and possessing flat ends or heads. The word is derived from the Middle Dutch *kūper*, meaning tub or cask. Historically, coopering was divided into several distinct branches: wet coopers made casks for holding liquids under pressure, dry coopers made barrels for dry goods, and white coopers made tubs, buckets, and churns.

This handbook, *The Cooper's Craft*, focuses on the high art of wet coopering. The craft requires a deep understanding of timber selection, particularly white oak (*Quercus alba*), which must be straight-grained and free of knots. Staves are dressed with a drawknife on a cooper's horse, then assembled inside a temporary hoop, fired to allow bending, bent with a windlass, hooped, and finally grooved to receive the heads.

Today, while modern industrial manufacturing has largely replaced hand-made barrels, the principles of wood mechanics, stave angles, and wood-sugar caramelization remain vital to the spirits, wine, and brewing industries. This book serves as both a historical record and a practical guide for those who still appreciate the tactile beauty of wood-shop craft.
