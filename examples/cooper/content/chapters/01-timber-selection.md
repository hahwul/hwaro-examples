Selecting the right timber is the critical first step in wet coopering. The wood must be white oak (*Quercus alba*), valued for its strength, water-tightness, and chemical compounds that enrich spirits. Traditional coopers reject red oak, which is too porous due to the lack of tyloses in its wood vessels.

<!-- more -->

The selection process begins in the forest. Coopers search for slow-growing trees, typically eighty to one hundred years old, which offer tight growth rings. The timber must be straight-grained, free of knots, and harvested during the winter dormancy when sap levels are lowest. 

Once harvested, logs are split radially rather than sawn. Radial splitting ensures that the medullary rays of the oak run parallel to the flat surface of the stave. This is crucial because medullary rays are impervious to liquids; if they run diagonally across a stave, the barrel will leak. After splitting, the rough timber bolts are stacked in open-air wood yards for seasoning. Air seasoning takes between two and three years, during which wind, rain, and fungi leach out harsh tannins and soften the wood structure, preparing the oak for the high stresses of stave dressing.
