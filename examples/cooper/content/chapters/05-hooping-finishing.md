With the staves bent and set, the cask has its permanent shape. However, the temporary, thick truss hoops must now be replaced with permanent hoops, and the ends of the barrel must be sealed to make the vessel watertight.

<!-- more -->

The cooper begins by preparing the permanent metal hoops, made from flat iron bands cut and riveted to the exact size of the barrel at various positions. Using a hammer and a hardened steel hoop driver, the cooper drives the permanent hoops onto the barrel: the bilge hoops near the center, the quarter hoops in the middle, and the head hoops at the ends. The hoops must fit tightly, as their pressure alone holds the staves together once the temporary trussing is removed.

Next, the cooper must prepare the chime and cut the croze. The chime is bevelled using a cooper's adze, and the inside of the stave ends is smoothed. Using a specialized croze plane, the cooper cuts a deep, clean groove — the croze — around the inner circumference of the barrel ends. The flat, circular barrel heads, assembled from oak pieces dowelled together and sealed with dried river reeds, are then fitted into these croze grooves. The head hoops are driven back on, compressing the staves tightly against the head to seal the cask.
