Once the oak bolts are seasoned, they must be transformed into individual staves. This process of shaping is known as dressing the stave, requiring precision, a steady hand, and a suite of specialized wood-shop tools. 

<!-- more -->

A rough stave is clamped into the cooper’s horse, a foot-operated workbench that holds the wood firm. Using a heavy, two-handled drawing knife, the cooper shaves the inner and outer surfaces of the wood. The outer side must be backed to create a smooth convex curve, while the inner side is hollowed to form the concave interior of the future cask. 

Next, the sides of the stave must be jointed. This is the most demanding step, as each stave must be wider in the middle (the bilge) and narrower at the ends, with the edges bevelled toward the center of the barrel. The angle of this bevel is determined by the total number of staves and the desired diameter of the cask. To achieve this bevel, the cooper slides the stave down the jointer plane, a massive, six-foot-long wooden plane propped at an angle. The jointer plane must slice the edge clean and true; even a fraction of a degree off will result in a barrel that leaks under hydraulic pressure.
