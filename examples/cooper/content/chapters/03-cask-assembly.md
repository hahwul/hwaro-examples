With a full set of dressed staves prepared, the cooper is ready to assemble or "raise" the cask. This step brings the flat staves together into a circular form, held in place purely by friction and mechanical tension.

<!-- more -->

The cooper begins by placing a thick, heavy iron ring called a truss hoop onto the workshop floor. The staves are stood upright inside this hoop, arranged side-by-side. The cooper must select staves of varying widths to distribute them evenly around the circumference, ensuring the barrel remains perfectly balanced. As the final "key" stave is driven into place, the top of the barrel resembles a rose, with the staves splayed outward at the unhooped end.

At this stage, the staves are straight and stiff, and cannot yet be bent to form the closed bilge. The assembly is held together only at one end by the temporary truss hoop. To prevent the staves from slipping or collapsing, the cooper drives the truss hoop down with a heavy cooper's hammer and a wooden driver. Raising the cask requires quick, decisive movements, as any instability can cause the entire splayed structure to burst apart, throwing the staves across the shop floor.
