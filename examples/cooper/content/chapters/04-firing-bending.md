To bend the straight, rigid oak staves into the classic curved profile of a barrel, the wood must be heated and softened. This process relies on a combination of moisture, high heat, and mechanical leverage.

<!-- more -->

First, the raised cask is wet thoroughly with water. The cooper then places it over a cresset — an open iron basket filled with burning oak shavings and scraps. The fire heats the interior of the staves to around three hundred degrees Fahrenheit. The combination of heat and water softens the lignin, the natural organic polymer that binds the cellulose fibers in the wood, making the oak staves flexible.

As the wood heats, the cooper loops a thick rope around the splayed ends of the staves, connected to a heavy wooden windlass. By slowly turning the windlass, the cooper draws the splayed ends together, bending the staves gradually into a tight circle. During this bending process, the cooper must keep the fire hot and the wood wet; if the staves cool or dry out too quickly, the wood will crack or split under the immense bending tension. Once the ends are drawn close, another temporary truss hoop is slipped over the top to lock the staves into their permanent, bent shape.
