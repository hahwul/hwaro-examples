This table follows the principles set out by Ettore Sottsass and his collaborators in Milan during the early 1980s. Inexpensive industrial materials are used in place of the hardwoods and stone that signal value in conventional furniture. The result is a piece that resists the usual hierarchies of domestic design.

### Construction

The top is a particleboard disc, faced with a pink terrazzo-pattern laminate and edged with black PVC banding. The disc rests off-center on a triangular plywood frame, which gives the surface a slight tilt of about four degrees from horizontal. Cups and bowls placed near the high edge will slide.

- Top: 80 cm diameter, 22 mm thick.
- Frame: 18 mm birch plywood, painted matte black.
- Legs: three, each different — one cylindrical in yellow lacquer, one square in a black-and-white grid laminate, one a flat triangle in red.

### Intent

The mismatched legs are not a mistake. They are the most direct statement the piece makes. A conventional table treats its three or four supports as identical, hidden, or at least subordinate to the top. Here the legs claim equal weight and refuse to match. The eye cannot settle on a single dominant element.

The table was first shown at a small gallery in Milan and later reproduced in a short run of forty pieces. Each was finished by hand, and no two are identical in laminate pattern. Surviving examples appear occasionally at auction, usually with some chipping along the PVC edge banding that is consistent with the materials and the period.
