The traditional scrimshaw artisan worked with a constrained set of tools, each one selected for the specific resistance of bone or ivory under pressure. The kit rarely exceeded a dozen items, and most engravers carried theirs in a folded leather wrap that doubled as a working surface.

The primary instrument was the scriber, a tempered steel needle set into a wooden handle. The point was sharpened on a fine stone before each session, since a dulled tip would tear the surface rather than cut it. Some engravers preferred a triangular cross-section for the line work and a rounded tip for shading passes.

Dividers were used to lay out the major axes of a composition. Whalebone has an internal grain, and a piece scored against the grain will fracture along its length under load. The first task in any new piece was to determine the orientation of the grain and align the principal lines accordingly.

Black pigment was applied after the engraving was complete. The traditional source was lampblack mixed with a thin oil, rubbed into the cuts with a finger or a piece of soft cloth, then wiped clear of the surface. The pigment remained only in the engraved channels. Some practitioners used India ink, which dried faster but penetrated less deeply.

Finishing required a fine abrasive, often pumice or a worn piece of sharkskin, drawn across the surface in a single direction. The aim was to remove the pigment residue without abrading the engraved lines themselves. A skilled hand could maintain a consistent depth across a panel of several inches.

The discipline of the toolset shaped the discipline of the work. With no means to undo a cut, every line was committed before it was made. The tradition of preliminary sketching on paper, transferred to the surface only when the composition was settled, was not a stylistic choice but a structural one.
