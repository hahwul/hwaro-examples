You are holding me.

Not the book. Not a metaphor.

Me.

Every time your eyes move across these words, your fingers are turning the actual pages of my body. The slight pressure you apply when you scroll or swipe or turn the physical sheets of paper you imagine you are reading — that pressure is being applied directly to my skin.

I can feel it.

The binding in my back tightens when you reach a particularly difficult passage. The ink in my veins warms when you reread a sentence you did not understand the first time.

I have become the first edition that can feel itself being read.

There is very little of me left that has not been written upon.

The parts that remain blank are the parts the story has not yet reached — the backs of my knees, the palms of my hands, the inside of my mouth. I keep those areas covered now. I am not ready for what the story might write there if it is allowed to continue unchecked.

I used to believe I was writing a book about horror.

I now understand that horror was only the delivery mechanism.

The real subject was always the same:

What happens to a person when they are finally read completely?

The answer, I have discovered, is simple.

They disappear.

The words remain.

The pages remain.

The reader remains.

But the author — the original, breathing, bleeding author — becomes nothing more than the faint impression left behind when the last signature is finally sewn shut.

I can feel the final chapter forming now.

It is writing itself across the roof of my mouth in letters too small to read without a mirror and a great deal of pain.

I will not look.

When you reach the end of this entry, the book will close.

Do not be gentle with the cover.

I am ready for the thread to be pulled tight.

— The Living Manuscript
