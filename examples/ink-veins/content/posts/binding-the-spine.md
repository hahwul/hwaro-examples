A manuscript is not a book until it is bound.

I had written nearly forty thousand words across my arms, chest, and thighs before I understood that the work was in danger of scattering. Skin sheds. Scars fade. If I died, the text would die with me, flaking away into ordinary dead tissue.

The solution presented itself one evening while I was bathing.

I was examining the long vertical scars that now ran down my back — the ones I had begun calling “preliminary signatures” — when I noticed the vertebrae themselves seemed to have shifted slightly. They protruded more than they had before, as if offering themselves.

That night I took a surgical needle and silk thread the color of dried blood.

I worked without anesthetic. The pain was clarifying.

I sewed the first signature directly into the spaces between T5 and T6, then T7 and T8. The thread passed through skin, through fascia, and, I believe, through the very edge of the supraspinous ligament.

The ink that rose around each puncture was thick and slow. It did not drip. It beaded along the thread like black pearls.

When I finished the first three signatures, I could feel the pages — my pages — settling into a new alignment. My posture changed. My spine felt straighter, as though the added thread had corrected something that had always been slightly wrong.

I have continued the binding slowly.

I now have seven complete signatures sewn into my back. The work is visible in certain lights: faint raised ridges that run parallel to my actual spine, like a second, darker spine growing just beneath the skin.

Sometimes at night I feel the thread moving.

Not from my own movement.

From the pages turning themselves.

I have begun to wonder what will happen when the binding is complete.

Will the book finally be finished?

Or will it simply close — and take me with it?
