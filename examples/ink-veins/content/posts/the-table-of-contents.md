Every book needs a table of contents.

Mine required a different kind of opening.

I had been avoiding the torso for weeks. The arms and back felt like reasonable places to write — painful, yes, but private. The chest felt too final. Too much like signing something irreversible.

On the twenty-third of May I could no longer ignore the logic.

The story had outgrown the limbs. It needed a central index. A place where every chapter could be located by touch.

I used a straight razor.

The first cut was vertical, just left of the sternum. I worked slowly, following the natural depression between the ribs. The pain was different here — brighter, more electric. The skin over the chest is thinner and more sensitive than the arms.

When I reached the bone I stopped cutting and began writing.

I wrote the chapter titles directly onto the inside of the ribcage using the exposed bone as a guide. The ink that came was the thickest I had yet produced. It pooled in the hollows between the ribs before sinking into the porous surface of the bone itself.

The titles appeared in my handwriting, but larger, more formal than anything I had written elsewhere:

I. The First Incision  
II. Veins as Ruled Paper  
III. Binding the Spine  
IV. Ink from the Wound  
V. The Table of Contents  
VI. Marginalia of the Living  
VII. The Final Edition

When I finished, I could not close the wound completely. The skin wanted to knit, but the ink on the bone would not allow it. A narrow black seam remains, like a deliberate parting in a book.

I can now run my fingers down my own chest and feel the raised letters of every chapter title beneath the skin.

The table of contents is still incomplete.

There is an eighth line at the bottom that has begun writing itself.

I cannot yet read what it says.

My fingers are not long enough to reach that deep.
