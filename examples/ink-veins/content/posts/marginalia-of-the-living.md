The first note appeared on the back of my left hand while I slept.

It was written in the same black ink, but smaller, tighter, almost cramped. The letters were not in my usual hand. They slanted slightly left, as though written by someone sitting on the opposite side of the page from me.

It read:

> He is taking too long with the ending. The reader is waiting.

I had not told anyone I was writing an ending.

Since then the marginalia has proliferated.

They appear on the undersides of my forearms, behind my ears, in the soft skin of my inner elbows. Sometimes they are single words. Sometimes entire paragraphs that continue arguments I only half-remember starting.

One particularly vicious note appeared across my left shoulder blade three nights ago:

> This is not literature. This is taxidermy. You are stuffing a dead story with living meat.

I did not write that.

I know I did not write that.

The handwriting is too confident. Too cruel. It uses abbreviations I abandoned years ago and a particular way of crossing the letter “t” that I have never used.

I have begun leaving the pen uncapped beside the bed.

Last night I woke to the sound of scratching — not on paper, but on skin. The sound a nib makes when it is being pressed just hard enough to break the surface but not hard enough to draw a scream.

I did not turn on the light.

I lay perfectly still and listened to my own body write in the dark.

When I finally looked in the mirror this morning, there was a new line across my throat, just below the Adam’s apple:

> Stop checking the margins. You are already in the text.

I have decided to stop looking at the notes.

If the book wants to speak to itself about me, it can do so without my participation.

I will continue writing only on the surfaces I can see.

The rest belongs to whoever — or whatever — is now holding the pen while I sleep.
