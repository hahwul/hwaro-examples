There is a moment in every writer’s life when they run out of ink.

Mine occurred on a Tuesday at 3:17 in the morning.

I had been writing across the left side of my ribcage for several hours — a difficult passage about the difference between being read and being consumed. The pen began to drag. The flow thinned. Then stopped entirely.

I shook it. Nothing.

In the bathroom I unscrewed the reservoir and found it empty.

I stood there for a long time, naked under the fluorescent light, holding an empty pen above an open wound that was already trying to close itself.

Then I did the only logical thing.

I reopened the cut with the nib and pressed.

What came out was not blood.

It was the same thick, resinous black that had first appeared on my forearm weeks earlier — only now it was coming directly from me. No pen required. The wound itself had become the instrument.

I dipped the nib into my own side and continued the sentence without interruption.

The ink was warmer than it should have been. It had a faint metallic smell, like hot coins. When it dried on the skin it formed a slightly glossy film that cracked when I moved, like old lacquer.

I have since performed several controlled experiments.

- Cutting deeper produces darker, more permanent ink.
- Superficial scratches produce a lighter, more translucent medium suitable for marginal notes.
- If I wait too long between openings, the ink begins to clot inside the wound. It must be agitated.

I no longer fear running out.

What I fear is that one day the ink will stop coming from the wounds… and begin coming from somewhere else.

My eyes, perhaps.

Or my mouth.

I have already caught myself spitting black onto the pillow in the mornings.
