I used to think the human body was poorly designed for writing.

Then one night, while examining the inside of my right forearm under strong light, I saw them.

The veins were not random.

They ran in nearly perfect parallel lines, spaced approximately eight millimeters apart — the exact distance I prefer between lines in a notebook. At certain angles the smaller capillaries formed the faint horizontal rules I had always wanted but could never find in commercial stationery.

I took the pen and, without breaking the skin this time, simply traced along one of the blue lines with the dry nib.

The skin parted on its own.

Not a wound. An opening. As if the vein had been waiting for permission.

Inside the opened line was not blood, but the same black ink that had first appeared on my left arm. It rose in a perfect meniscus along the channel I had traced, like a living ruled line being inked by the body itself.

I wrote an entire paragraph that night without ever pressing hard enough to draw real blood. The ink simply… arrived.

By morning the lines had closed again, but the words remained, raised slightly like old scar tissue. When I ran my fingers over them I could feel every letter as a tiny ridge.

I have since mapped seventeen distinct ruled surfaces across my body:

- Both inner forearms (primary composition surfaces)
- The left pectoral (for notes and corrections)
- The inside of both thighs (for longer, more private passages)
- The soles of my feet (for text I hope never to reread)

The back is difficult. The shoulder blades keep trying to become an appendix.

I no longer buy paper.

I am the paper.

And the lines were already drawn inside me before I ever picked up the pen.
