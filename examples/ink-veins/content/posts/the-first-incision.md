I bought the pen three weeks before I understood what it was for.

It was an ordinary fountain pen — heavy, black, with a gold nib that caught the light like a scalpel. I told myself I was buying it to take notes for a novel. That was a lie I told the version of me who still believed stories lived on paper.

The first night I used it properly, I was sitting at the kitchen table with my left arm extended under the lamp. I had already sterilized the skin with vodka. The alcohol made the hair stand up.

I pressed the nib into the soft place just below the wrist, where the veins are close to the surface but not yet dangerous.

The resistance was less than I expected.

Ink — or what I thought was ink — welled immediately, darker than any pigment I had ever seen. It did not bead. It sank into the cut as if the skin were thirsty for it.

I wrote the first sentence across my own flesh in my own blood-ink:

> "The book began the moment I stopped being its author."

The letters were shaky at first. Then the ink itself seemed to guide the nib. The sentence straightened. The words became elegant.

When I finished the final period, the cut sealed itself into a thin black scar that looked like it had always been there.

I have not stopped writing since.

The strange thing is that the sentence has continued to grow. Every morning the scar is a little longer. New words appear overnight, written in a hand that is almost, but not quite, my own.

I have begun to dream that someone else is holding the pen now.

I wake with fresh ink under my fingernails and no memory of having written anything at all.
