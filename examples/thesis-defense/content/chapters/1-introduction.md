The isolation of graphene by Novoselov and Geim in 2004 inaugurated the era of two-dimensional materials research. In the two decades since, the van der Waals heterostructure platform has grown to encompass hundreds of layered materials that can be stacked and twisted with atomic precision.

The pivotal discovery by Cao et al. (2018) that twisted bilayer graphene (TBG) at a "magic angle" of approximately 1.1 degrees hosts both correlated insulating states and superconductivity transformed the field. This finding demonstrated that the twist angle between two graphene layers acts as a tuning parameter for electronic correlations, creating a new platform for studying strongly correlated physics in a highly controllable setting.

## Motivation for This Thesis

Despite intense experimental and theoretical activity since 2018, several fundamental questions remain unresolved:

1. **What is the nature of the correlated insulating states at integer fillings of the flat bands?** Competing proposals include Mott-like Hubbard physics, charge-density-wave order, and symmetry-broken quantum anomalous Hall states.

2. **Is the superconductivity conventional or unconventional?** The pairing symmetry, the role of phonons versus electronic mechanisms, and the relationship between superconductivity and the nearby correlated insulators are all debated.

3. **How robust are topological properties to disorder and strain?** Real devices inevitably contain twist-angle inhomogeneity and lattice relaxation that are difficult to account for in idealized models.

This dissertation addresses these questions through a combination of analytical theory, large-scale numerical simulations, and scanning tunneling spectroscopy experiments performed in close collaboration with the Watanabe group's STM facility.

## Thesis Outline

- **Chapter II** develops the continuum model for twisted bilayer graphene and derives the conditions for flat-band formation.
- **Chapter III** presents Hartree-Fock calculations of the correlated insulating states and their topological classification.
- **Chapter IV** reports scanning tunneling spectroscopy measurements on magic-angle devices and compares them with theoretical predictions.
- **Chapter V** synthesizes the findings and discusses implications for the broader field of moire materials.
