Bioluminescence is nature's own neon sign. From the abyssal depths of the ocean to firefly-lit summer meadows, living light has captivated humanity for millennia.

## Nature's Palette

The most stunning bioluminescent organisms produce light across a spectrum that reads like a designer's dream palette:

- **Deep blue** — the most common oceanic bioluminescence
- **Electric green** — found in fireflies and certain fungi
- **Vivid magenta** — rare deep-sea jellyfish species
- **Warm amber** — railroad worm lanterns

## From Ocean to Screen

Translating bioluminescence to digital design means embracing several key principles:

### Glow Over Fill

Natural bioluminescence isn't about solid blocks of color — it's about radiance emanating from a point source. In CSS, this means favoring `text-shadow` and `box-shadow` over flat `background-color`.

### Dark Environments

Bioluminescence only works in darkness. The deep black backgrounds in Electric Bloom serve the same purpose as the midnight ocean — they let the light be the star.

### Organic Gradients

Real bioluminescence rarely presents as a single flat color. It shifts, pulses, and fades at the edges. CSS gradients and animations help capture this living quality.

## The Digital Garden

Electric Bloom brings these principles together. Each element on the page is designed to feel like a living, glowing organism — beautiful, bold, and impossible to ignore.
