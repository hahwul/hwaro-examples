## The leak is usually invisible

Most retention dashboards report a single churn percentage and call it done. That number hides three very different problems: card declines that quietly time out into a cancellation nobody chose, customers who downgrade instead of leaving outright, and trials that never convert because nobody followed up. Flywheel splits the aggregate into the paths revenue actually takes out the door, then ranks them by dollars recovered, not by how loud the alert is.

### Involuntary churn is the fastest win

A failed renewal charge looks identical to a deliberate cancellation in most billing exports. Flywheel tells the difference in real time, retries the charge on the schedule your processor recommends, and only counts an account as lost after every retry and every dunning email has failed. Most teams recover between eighteen and forty percent of what they had assumed was gone the moment they turn on [recovery workflows](/features/).

### Then the slower leaks

Once involuntary churn is under control, the same cohort model surfaces the slower problems: seats that go quiet for sixty days before anyone cancels, plans that get downgraded twice before they're finally dropped, and trial cohorts that stall two days before a card is ever charged. Each pattern gets its own workflow, its own owner on your team, and its own line in the revenue-recovered ledger — so the win is never anonymous.
