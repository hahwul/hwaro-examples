Before paper became widely available in Europe, the written word was preserved on animal skins. Parchment, made from the hides of sheep, goats, or cattle, and its finer variant vellum, prepared from calfskin, served as the primary writing surface for over a thousand years. The quality of these materials shaped the character of medieval books in ways that paper never could.

The preparation of parchment was a labor-intensive craft. Fresh hides were soaked in a lime solution for days to loosen the hair, then stretched on wooden frames and scraped with a curved blade called a *lunellum*. The parchment-maker worked both sides of the skin — the hair side, which retained a faintly yellowish tone, and the flesh side, which was whiter and smoother. The finest vellum, known as *uterine vellum*, was said to come from stillborn or very young calves and achieved an almost translucent quality.

## The Physical Book

A gathering of parchment leaves, folded and nested together, formed a *quire* — typically four folded sheets yielding sixteen pages. The scribe arranged the quires so that hair sides faced hair sides and flesh sides faced flesh sides, creating a visual consistency across each opening. This attention to the material's natural characteristics distinguished the medieval codex from the modern printed book, where every page looks much the same.

The durability of parchment is extraordinary. Manuscripts written on well-prepared vellum have survived a thousand years and more, their text still legible, their pigments still vibrant. The Dead Sea Scrolls, written on parchment and hidden in desert caves, endured for two millennia. Paper, by contrast, grows brittle and acidic within a few centuries.

## The Economics of Parchment

A single large manuscript might require the skins of an entire flock. The Codex Amiatinus, a great Northumbrian Bible completed around 700 CE, is estimated to have consumed the hides of over 500 cattle. This material cost alone made books precious objects, owned by institutions rather than individuals, and chained to lecterns to prevent theft.

The expense of parchment also led to the practice of palimpsesting — scraping an existing text from a sheet so that it could be reused. Some of the most important lost texts of antiquity, including works by Archimedes and Cicero, survive only as ghostly traces beneath later writing on palimpsest manuscripts, recovered through modern imaging techniques.

## From Skin to Screen

The transition from parchment to paper, and from paper to screen, represents a progressive dematerialization of the written word. Each step has made text cheaper and more accessible, but something is also lost. A parchment leaf has a presence that no digital display can replicate — a warmth, a texture, a faint scent of age. To hold a medieval manuscript is to hold the physical labor of many hands: the farmer who raised the animal, the tanner who prepared the skin, the scribe who shaped the letters, and the illuminator who made the page glow.
