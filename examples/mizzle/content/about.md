I began studying chanoyu, the way of tea, on a wet February afternoon when I could not tell a chawan from a soup bowl. My teacher, whom I call Sensei here because she would not want her name on a screen, has practiced for forty years in a school that traces its manners back through the Sen family. She teaches four of us in a borrowed room above a stationer's, on tatami that has gone the color of weak barley tea.

This journal is my homework, made public in case it is useful to another beginner. It is not instruction. I get things wrong constantly, and I leave the wrong things in, because the record of a mistake corrected is more honest than a clean description copied from a manual. The [sessions begin here](@/sessions/_index.md), newest first.

## How to read it

Each entry hangs from the vertical rule like a scroll in an alcove. In the margin to its left you will find the date and the season, named by the old microseasons the calendar still keeps. To the right is the session itself: the **temae**, or procedure, I practiced; the **utensils** that passed through my hands; the **sweet** set out to mark the month; and, at the end, the **one lesson** I carried home.

## A few words you will meet often

- **Temae** — the choreographed procedure of preparing and serving tea. There are dozens; a beginner learns two or three in a year.
- **Chawan** — the tea bowl. Wide and shallow in summer to cool the tea, deep and narrow in winter to hold the heat.
- **Fukusa** — the silk cloth used to purify the utensils, folded and handled with a precision that took me months to stop dreading.
- **Usucha** and **koicha** — thin tea and thick tea. Thin is whisked to a froth; thick is kneaded to the texture of warm honey and shared from one bowl.
- **Wagashi** — the seasonal sweet eaten before the tea, made to name the month without a single word.

If a term is unfamiliar, follow its tag; every session filed under it will help triangulate the meaning better than a definition can.
