*Robiraki* is the tea year's new year. The furo is put away, the sunken hearth is opened for the first time since spring, and the new season's tea leaf, sealed in a jar since May, is finally ground. Sensei had swept the room until it smelled of nothing but clean straw and charcoal.

<!-- more -->

## The temae

Today I made *koicha*, thick tea, for the first time. It is a different animal from thin tea. There is no froth; there is barely any water. You add three scoops of matcha, then a careful thread of hot water, and *knead* rather than whisk, folding the paste against the side of the bowl until it turns glossy and slow, the color of a wet pine needle. Too little water and it clumps; too much and it thins into something ashamed of itself. Mine was a little thin. Sensei drank it anyway, turned the bowl, and said the color was honest.

Koicha is shared. One bowl passes from guest to guest, each taking three and a half sips and wiping the rim. After a year of making single servings, watching one bowl carry the whole room was the first time the practice felt less like a skill and more like a manner.

## Utensils

- **Ro** — the sunken winter hearth, opened today for the season.
- **Chawan** — a deep, dark Raku bowl that holds the heat and hides nothing.
- **Chaire** — the ceramic caddy for thick tea, in a brocade pouch.
- **Fukusa** — folded now on the winter side, a small seasonal change I nearly missed.

## One lesson

> Thick tea is kneaded, not whisked, and the difference is patience made visible. The bowl tells you at once whether you were willing to wait: gloss means you waited, dullness means you did not.
