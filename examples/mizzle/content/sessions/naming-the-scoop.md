Sensei brought a scoop I had not seen before, dark and slightly bent at the node, and set it on the mat between us before we began. Today's lesson, she said, was mostly one object. The tea would be an excuse to hold it properly.

<!-- more -->

## The temae

The usucha itself I could nearly do without thinking now, which is precisely when mistakes move in. So Sensei slowed one moment to a standstill: the placing of the *chashaku* across the lid of the *natsume* at the end. There is a correct angle, a correct distance from the edge, a correct silence in which to do it. I did it four times before the scoop lay down as if it had chosen the spot itself.

Then she told me the scoop had a name. Its maker had carved it, cut a length of bamboo to house it, and inscribed a word on the case — *Tomorrow's Wind*. A named scoop, she said, is treated as a guest, not a tool. You do not toss a guest onto the mat.

## Utensils

- **Chashaku** — the tea scoop, bamboo, named *Ashita no kaze*, tomorrow's wind.
- **Natsume** — a lacquered caddy with a single sprig of autumn grass in gold.
- **Chasen** — replaced this month; the old whisk had lost three tines.
- **Chawan** — a bowl banded in the russet of a turning leaf.

## One lesson

> An object with a name asks to be met, not used. I set the scoop down more carefully after learning it was called something, which tells me the carelessness was never about the bamboo.
