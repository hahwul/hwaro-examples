Between my last lesson and this one the room had changed its geometry. The sunken hearth, the *ro*, had been closed and covered with a fresh tatami, and in its place sat the *furo*, a portable brazier drawn back toward the guest. Summer had entered the room before it entered the street.

<!-- more -->

## The temae

*Hakobi-date* means carrying in: no utensils are waiting when you enter, so the temae begins with a procession of trips, each one a small temae of its own. Water jar first, then the tea things on the mat, then the waste-water bowl and ladle. Sensei made me carry the *mizusashi*, the fresh-water jar, at the exact height of my navel, elbows soft. I sloshed it on the first trip and she sent me back to the mizuya to start again.

With the furo further from the guest, everything is a touch smaller and cooler in feeling. The bowl is broad. The kettle sits lower. The whole arrangement leans away from heat, toward the idea of a breeze.

## Utensils

- **Furo** — the portable brazier, in use from the fifth month through the tenth.
- **Mizusashi** — the fresh-water jar, its lid a glazed lily-pad green.
- **Chawan** — a shallow, pale summer bowl that shows the tea's color.
- **Kensui** — the waste-water container, carried in last and out first.

## One lesson

> The season is not decoration laid on top of the tea. It is the reason the utensils sit where they sit. Move the brazier back a hand's width and the guest feels cooler without knowing why.
