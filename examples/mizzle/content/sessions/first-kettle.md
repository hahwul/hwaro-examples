*Hatsugama*, the first kettle, is the most festive gathering of the tea year, and the closest a beginner's journal like this one comes to an occasion. Sensei wore a formal kimono. There was a willow branch curled into a ring in the alcove, and the sweet was *hanabira-mochi*, its pale disc folded over a thread of sweet burdock and white bean, a New Year's confection older than the school itself.

<!-- more -->

## The temae

I made koicha again, and it was better — glossy this time, kneaded slow, the color even to the rim. But the lesson of the day was not in my hands. It was in the kettle.

While the water came up to heat, Sensei told me to stop and listen. The iron kettle, packed with small bits of iron on its floor, does not simply boil. It moves through voices as it warms: a first faint hiss, then a sound like distant rain, then a low roar the old texts call *matsukaze* — wind in the pines. A host learns to read the water's readiness by ear, never lifting the lid. I closed my eyes and, for the first time, actually heard it: the room going quiet enough that a kettle could sound like weather.

## Utensils

- **Kama** — the iron kettle, set into the ro, singing its way to the boil.
- **Chawan** — a red New Year's bowl, warm in both color and hand.
- **Chaire** — the thick-tea caddy, its brocade changed for the season.
- **Hishaku** — the ladle, its handle a fresh, pale green cut of bamboo for the new year.

## One lesson

> A year in, I finally understood what Sensei meant on [the first day](@/sessions/first-fold.md) about not hurrying to the good part. I sat listening to water heat, making nothing, and it was the best tea I have attended. The kettle sounded like wind in pines, and I had gone quiet enough to hear it.
