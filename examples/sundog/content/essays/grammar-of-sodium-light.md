Low-pressure sodium light has one job, and it does not do it for photographers: it renders almost nothing but itself. Everything under it goes some version of amber-brown, as if the whole street had been developed in the wrong chemical. I used to fight this. Lately I have started reading it as a kind of grammar instead — a set of rules the city agreed to after dark that daylight never had to follow.

<!-- more -->

The technical reason is almost embarrassingly simple: low-pressure sodium lamps emit light at essentially one wavelength, 589 nanometers, the same doublet that colors a struck match of table salt. There is no red in that light, no blue, no green — nothing for a sensor's other two channels to record but a wash of the same signal at slightly different intensities. Point a camera at a sodium-lit underpass and you are not photographing color. You are photographing a monochrome scene that the file format insists on saving in RGB anyway, which is why sodium exposures always look faintly counterfeit, like a hand-tinted print where the hand wasn't paying attention.

I spent the better part of two winters shooting the underpass on Vantage Street trying to correct this — white-balancing against a gray card, dragging the orange out in post until the concrete looked like concrete again. Every version was worse than the sodium original. What I was erasing, it turned out, was the only information actually present in the scene: not the true color of the underpass, which nobody standing there at 1 a.m. could see either, but the specific, dumb, insistent color of *this* light, doing the only thing it knows how to do. Correcting it was like translating a pun and losing the joke.

So the essays that follow don't correct for sodium anymore. When a streetlamp is doing the sodium thing — and in this city, in the older wards especially, it usually still is — I let the whole frame go amber and write down what that meant for the exposure instead of apologizing for it. The meter, for what it's worth, hates this. A single-wavelength light source confuses reflected-light metering in ways full-spectrum light doesn't, because the meter is built to average a scene it assumes has red, green, and blue information to average. Sodium gives it one channel and static. Every exposure under a sodium lamp is a small negotiation with an instrument that thinks something has gone wrong.

Nothing has gone wrong. This is what the city looks like at the hour when only the oldest fixtures are still running the old bulbs, before the retrofit trucks get around to that block. It has a grammar — one vocabulary word, repeated at every noun — and once you stop trying to translate it, the sentences underneath it get much easier to read: a fire escape, a hydrant, two men waiting for a bus that stopped running four hours ago, all rendered in the single tense sodium has for everything. I no longer bring a gray card to Vantage Street. I bring a notebook and let the meter argue with itself.
