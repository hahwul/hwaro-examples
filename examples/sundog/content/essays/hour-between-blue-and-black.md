Everyone talks about blue hour like it's the destination. It isn't, or at least it isn't for the kind of picture I keep trying to describe here. Blue hour is the on-ramp. The essay I actually want to write is about the forty minutes after it ends, when the last violet drains out of the sky and the city has to start lighting itself.

<!-- more -->

There is a specific, unglamorous middle period between "the sky is doing something" and "the sky is simply gone" that most people pack up before reaching, because it photographs, for a while, as almost nothing — a flat charcoal gradient with a skyline silhouetted in front of it, competent but inert. I used to leave then too. What changed my mind was staying on the roof deck above Farrow Hill through an entire failed sunset and watching what actually happens to a skyline's exposure balance during that stretch, which nobody had described to me accurately: it isn't a smooth fade. It's a handoff.

For the first twenty minutes after civil twilight ends, the sky is still brighter than the city's own lights, so buildings read as dark shapes against a paler background — the classic blue hour silhouette, reliable and a little overfamiliar. Then, somewhere around minute twenty-five, the sky's brightness drops below the aggregate brightness of every window, streetlamp, and illuminated sign in the frame, and the whole relationship inverts. The buildings stop being silhouettes and start being the brightest thing in the picture. The sky goes from foreground character to backdrop. Nobody rings a bell when this happens; you only notice in retrospect, looking at four frames shot ten minutes apart, that somewhere in there the picture's whole logic flipped.

I now treat that crossover, rather than the pretty ten minutes right after sunset, as the actual target. At f/5.6 and four seconds I'm exposing for the city's own light output, which by then is doing all the real work — the sky is present but demoted, a dim gray field instead of a light source, and every window in the skyline is finally bright enough to register as itself instead of as a bright point lost against equally bright sky. It's a duller-sounding recipe than "shoot blue hour" and a genuinely more difficult one, since the good ten minutes of crossover are harder to predict than sunset and vary by fifteen or twenty minutes depending on cloud cover, humidity, and how much ambient light the city itself is throwing back up into the remaining sky.

What I've stopped doing is treating blue hour as an event with a start and a finish that I need to be positioned for, like a parade. It's a gradient, and the frame worth keeping is usually not at either end of it but in the crossover nobody schedules a viewing for, where the sky and the streetlights briefly agree on how bright the world should be before the sky gives up the argument entirely.
