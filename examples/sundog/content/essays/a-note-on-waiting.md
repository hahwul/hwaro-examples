Almost none of the actual work in this kind of photography is exposure. The shutter is open for four seconds, or thirteen minutes, or thirty; the waiting that makes the exposure worth taking runs to hours, spread out over weeks, and none of it shows up in any file's metadata.

<!-- more -->

I want to try to describe what the waiting is actually for, because five years of essays in this exact form — title, exposure line, description of a photograph you will not see — have not once addressed it directly, and it is by a wide margin the part of this practice that people ask about most. Not the sodium color science, not the metering tricks. How do you know when to stand still.

The honest answer is that I don't, in advance. What I have instead is a small, stable set of places — the Vantage Street underpass, the Kessler Viaduct, Portage Interchange, Farrow Hill — that I return to on a rotation with no fixed schedule, in weather that seems promising for reasons I can rarely articulate ahead of time, and I stand at each one for anywhere from ten minutes to two hours doing nothing that would look, to a passerby, like photography. Most of that time the camera is on the tripod but the shutter never opens. I am watching a specific piece of the scene — a lamp, a puddle, a gap in traffic — waiting for it to say something it wasn't saying five minutes earlier.

This sounds mystical and I don't intend it to. It is closer to the kind of pattern recognition anyone develops doing a repetitive task in variable conditions: a line cook reading a pan, a sailor reading a swell. After enough nights at Portage Interchange I can tell, within a minute or two, whether the fog currently forming is going to thicken into the narrow band that makes halos legible or thin back out into nothing, and I make that call from cues I would struggle to list if you asked me to write them down in the moment — a particular quality of stillness in the air, a smell, the way the nearest lamp's glow has or hasn't started to soften at its edge. None of that is in any essay's exposure line. It's the actual craft, and it is almost entirely invisible, which might be the real reason I started writing these instead of just posting the pictures: a photograph shows you the ten seconds the shutter was open and hides the two hours of watching that told me when to open it. A sentence can at least gesture at the two hours.

What I can offer, in place of a method, is a kind of permission: it is fine, useful even, to stand at an overpass at midnight for forty minutes and take nothing. That forty minutes is not wasted time adjacent to the work. For as long as I've been doing this, it has been most of the work — the part that never survives into the file, and the part every one of these essays has secretly been trying to write around, one exposure line at a time.
