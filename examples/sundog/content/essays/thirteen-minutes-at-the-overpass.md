Thirteen minutes is a strange unit of time to spend doing nothing on purpose in public. It is long enough that people notice you twice — once when you set up, once again when they pass back the other way and you are, unaccountably, still standing there, hand on a cable release, staring at a bridge.

The Kessler Viaduct carries six lanes of a road that used to matter more than it does now, over a rail yard that matters less than it used to. At night the traffic thins to something you could count on your fingers, which is exactly the condition a thirteen-minute exposure needs: enough cars to write light across the frame, not so many that the writing turns to solid paint. I chose f/11 for depth across the whole span and ISO 100 to keep the sensor's noise floor as far below the exposure as possible, which meant the shutter had to stay open a long time to let in enough light — thirteen minutes, in this case, on a night with a three-quarter moon doing quiet, unglamorous work as a second light source across the concrete.

Almost nothing happens in the first four minutes. This is the part of long-exposure work nobody mentions, because it photographs as nothing and reads as nothing: you are simply present, next to a tripod, while a bridge does what bridges do, which is hold still. I have come to think this stretch is the actual subject, more than the light trails the exposure eventually produces. A car does not know it is being photographed for thirteen minutes; it experiences the bridge for four or five seconds and is gone. Only the camera — and, at the tripod, only me — is doing the longer version of the crossing, accumulating the whole viaduct's evening the way sediment accumulates a river, one indifferent car at a time.

By minute nine a freight train had come through the yard below, invisible to the exposure except as a faint red smear where its rear marker lights sat still at a signal for eleven seconds before the train reasoned its way forward again. That smear is the only part of the image that tells you anything about *duration* rather than just *motion* — the taillights write a stripe, but the stalled marker writes a stop, a pause the length of an actual decision being made somewhere in a signal box. I did not know that decision was happening until I got home and stared at what thirteen minutes had done with it.

I have shot the viaduct in five-minute exposures and in twenty-minute ones. Thirteen is not a magic number so much as the length of time it takes for a moon-lit, moderately trafficked overpass to say a complete thought — long enough for the passing to become writing, short enough that the writing does not smear into a single, meaningless band. Anything past twenty minutes on this bridge starts erasing itself; anything under eight reads as an accident rather than an argument. Thirteen is where the bridge finishes its sentence and stops.
