Fog does to light what rain does to pavement, except upward and in every direction at once: instead of one clean reflection, you get a halo the size of a room, sometimes the size of a whole overpass, built out of a lamp's own light scattering off a million suspended droplets that have no business being that photogenic.

<!-- more -->

Portage Interchange gets fog maybe eight or nine nights a winter, and of those, only two or three arrive with the particular density that makes the halos read as distinct shapes rather than a general soupy glow. Too thin and the sodium lamps just look slightly soft, a minor haze with no real structure to it. Too thick and the halos merge into an undifferentiated murk that swallows the lamps themselves along with everything else — you lose the point source that the halo needs in order to look like a halo at all, rather than fog that happens to be lit. The good fog sits in a narrow band between those two failures, thick enough to scatter but thin enough that each of the interchange's eleven sodium fixtures still resolves as its own separate, structured bloom, distinguishable from its neighbors thirty meters off.

What surprised me the first time I shot this properly is how much the halo's shape tells you about wind that you cannot otherwise perceive. Still fog produces a halo that is nearly a perfect radial gradient, soft and round, centered evenly on the bulb. The moment there's even a faint drift of air, the halo elongates and skews downwind, and if you shoot a sequence of frames over a few minutes you can watch the elongation change angle as the air shifts — an anemometer built entirely out of scattered light, legible only in long lens compression that flattens the eleven lamps into a shallow, layered field where their halos visibly overlap and interact.

At 85mm and f/2 I'm working wide open specifically to let the lens's own imperfections contribute — a touch of coma at the corners, some softness that a stopped-down aperture would have corrected out. This is one of the only times I deliberately want the glass to be a little worse than it's capable of being, because a technically perfect rendering of a sodium halo looks fake in a way that a slightly aberrated one doesn't; real atmospheric scattering has a messiness to its edges that a clinically sharp lens tends to iron out. ISO 3200 handheld, no tripod, because fog nights are cold enough that I want to be moving between setups rather than waiting at one for a long exposure, and because the halos themselves are bright enough — brighter than the ambient scene around them, sometimes by two full stops — to expose adequately at a shutter speed I can hold steady by hand.

I check the forecast for fog more often than I check it for anything else, including whether I'll need a coat. Three good nights a winter is not a lot to work with, but the interchange has eleven lamps and the fog is different every time, which has turned out to be enough.
