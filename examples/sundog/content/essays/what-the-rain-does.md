Rain does one enormous favor for night photography and then takes almost all of it back. The favor is that wet asphalt turns into a second sky, doubling every lamp, every window, every stopped taillight into a pair — the real one and its longer, wobblier twin stretched out along the ground. The theft is that the window for shooting it is short, wet, and actively trying to ruin your equipment.

<!-- more -->

I have learned to want rain the way farmers want it, which is to say: specifically, and not too much of it. A downpour is useless — the surface never settles enough to hold a reflection, just churns it into a thousand small interruptions. What you want is the fifteen minutes after a storm has broken and moved on, when the street has taken on a thin, undisturbed sheet of water and stopped being troubled by new rain falling into it. That window is the entire subject of this essay, and it does not announce itself. You learn to recognize it by ear before you see it — the specific hush that comes when rain stops but gutters are still running.

Portage Interchange is where I go for this, mostly because its sodium lamps line a slight camber in the road that pools water reliably along one edge, giving me a consistent strip of reflection to work with instead of hunting for puddles block to block. The reflected lamp is never a clean copy of the real one. Moving water breaks it into a soft vertical smear, closer to a brushstroke than a mirror image, and the smear's exact character — tight and rippled, or loose and almost liquid-gold — depends on wind, on residual rain, on how recently a car went through and disturbed the sheet. None of this is controllable, which is the entire appeal after months of exposures I could plan to the minute.

Handheld at 1/4 second is a compromise I make peace with every time and regret every time. A tripod would sharpen the real lamp, but the reflection doesn't need sharpness — it needs the slight tremor of a hand-held shutter, which reads, honestly, as more true to what standing in ankle-deep runoff at midnight actually feels like than a locked-off tripod shot would. I brace against a signal box or a rail instead, which steadies the frame without freezing it entirely. ISO 1600 buys me enough shutter speed to keep the real lamp from smearing into its reflection, though on the best nights I let that line blur slightly on purpose, because the moment when the object and its reflection start to merge is, as far as I can tell, the whole point of chasing wet pavement in the first place: two lights that were never going to meet, meeting anyway, for as long as the water stays still.
