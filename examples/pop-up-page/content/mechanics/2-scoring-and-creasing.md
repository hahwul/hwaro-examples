# Scoring and Creasing

<p class="lede">A fold without a score is a guess. The score is the act of weakening the paper along a precise line so that the subsequent crease lands exactly where the design demands.</p>

## What a score does

<p>Scoring crushes the fibres of the paper along a thin channel without cutting through them. The crushed channel becomes the path of least resistance, and the sheet folds along it in preference to any other line. On thick cardstock, scoring is essential. Without it, the paper resists the fold, the crease wanders, and the surface cracks unevenly along the spine.</p>

## Tools

<p>A bone folder, traditionally cut from cattle bone and now more often moulded from polished plastic, is the standard hand tool. It produces a smooth, rounded channel that compresses the fibres without breaking the surface. A scoring wheel, mounted on a pivoting handle, draws a deeper channel suited to heavier stocks. A blunt knife held against a metal ruler can substitute for either, but it tends to score unevenly when the angle of the blade shifts during the stroke.</p>

## Mountain and valley

<p>Every score must be assigned a direction before the sheet is folded. A mountain fold raises the score line away from the viewer; a valley fold pushes it toward. In a pop-up, the two directions alternate across the structure: the gutter is a valley, the central crease of a V-fold is a mountain, the glue tab folds are valleys, and so on. Marking the directions on the back of the sheet before folding eliminates a class of errors that are difficult to undo once the adhesive is applied.</p>

## Grain direction

<p>Paper folds more cleanly along its grain than across it. The grain runs in the direction of the fibres laid down during manufacture. A score made parallel to the grain produces a soft, even crease. A score made perpendicular to the grain produces a slightly ragged crease that may show small fractures along its length. When the orientation of the spread is fixed, the stock must be cut so that the gutter score runs with the grain.</p>
