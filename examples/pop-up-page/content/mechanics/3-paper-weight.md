# Paper Weight and Memory

<p class="lede">A pop-up depends on the paper remembering its folds. Too light, and the structure collapses under its own weight. Too heavy, and it refuses to fold cleanly. The window between is narrower than it appears.</p>

## Grammage as a starting point

<p>Paper weight is conventionally measured in grams per square metre, abbreviated gsm. Office stock sits at eighty gsm. Cover stock for paperback books runs around two hundred and fifty gsm. The structural elements of pop-up books typically use stocks between two hundred and three hundred and fifty gsm, with two hundred and seventy as a common compromise between rigidity and foldability.</p>

<p>Grammage alone does not determine behaviour. A two hundred and seventy gsm sheet of newsprint is soft and absorbent. A two hundred and seventy gsm sheet of bristol board is stiff and smooth. The fibre composition, the surface treatment, and the calendering pressure during manufacture all contribute to how the paper holds a fold.</p>

## Memory and recovery

<p>When a pop-up is opened, the structural elements bend through a range of angles and then return. The paper must hold the new shape without permanent deformation along any score that was not designed to crease, and it must spring back when the spread is closed. This combination of stiffness under load and elastic recovery is sometimes called the paper's memory.</p>

<p>Stocks with high alpha-cellulose content tend to have stronger memory than stocks blended with mechanical pulp. Acid-free archival papers, treated with calcium carbonate to neutralise residual acidity, retain their memory for decades. Newsprint, by contrast, embrittles within a year and loses the ability to recover from repeated openings.</p>

## Testing before commitment

<p>The reliable test is empirical. Cut a strip of the candidate stock, score it, fold it through ninety degrees fifty times, and inspect the score line. A surviving sample shows a clean, narrow channel with no surface fractures. A failing sample shows whitening, cracking, or a visible widening of the crease. Run the test before the final cutting plan is committed, not after.</p>
