After years of accumulating hammers, tongs, files, and measuring tools in buckets and drawers, I finally built a dedicated tool wall for the shop. The backing is quarter-inch plywood mounted to the studs behind the primary workbench, painted with flat black to keep the visual focus on the tools themselves. The layout was planned by tracing each tool onto kraft paper first, then arranging the outlines until every piece had a logical home within reach of the anvil and vise.

The holders are a mix of welded steel hooks, bent rod cradles, and slotted bar stock for items like chisels and punches. Each hook was forged from 3/8-inch round stock, tapered and scrolled at the ends to prevent tools from sliding off during use. The tong rack is a length of one-inch square tube welded to angle-iron brackets, with slots cut at regular intervals to hang tongs by their reins. Heavier items like sledges and swage blocks sit on a welded shelf below the main board.

The result is a visible inventory of every hand tool in the shop, which eliminates wasted time searching through drawers mid-project. Everything is within two steps of the anvil. I added a strip of rare-earth magnets along the top edge for holding layout tools, drill bits, and small files that would otherwise roll off hooks. Keeping the wall organized requires discipline, but the efficiency gain during forging sessions has been significant. Each tool goes back to its outline after every use, and missing items are immediately obvious.
