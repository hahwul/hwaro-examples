Every story on this site starts the same way: a document nobody meant to be read closely. A budget line, a deed, a docket entry filed because the law requires it, not because anyone expected a reporter to line it up against a thousand others. Nora Kessler has built her career on that unglamorous middle step — the part between a hunch and a headline where a claim either survives contact with the underlying data or it doesn't.

## How the work happens

Every number that appears in a margin note on this site was pulled from a named public record, not estimated or rounded for effect. When a dataset has known gaps or a model makes a judgment call, the story says so in plain language, and the underlying query is published alongside it. A reader who wants to check the math should be able to, without asking permission.

Nora's rule for a housing or budget story is simple: if the finding can't survive being explained to a city council member who disagrees with it, it isn't ready. That means showing work, not just conclusions — which is also why this CV reads less like a list of job titles and more like a chain of evidence.

## What makes a good tip

- A specific document, filing, or dataset — not just a feeling that something is wrong
- A pattern that repeats across more than one parcel, ward, or budget cycle
- Something checkable against a public record, even if the record is hard to get
- A willingness to be on the record, or a clear reason why not, discussed up front

## Outside the beat

When she isn't cross-referencing recorder filings, Nora teaches a night-school session on public-records requests at the county library where she used to work, restores a rowhouse stoop one weekend at a time, and keeps a running, mildly obsessive list of every acronym used in a Rourke County budget hearing.
