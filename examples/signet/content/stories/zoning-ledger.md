Rourke County's Planning Commission keeps a clean, well-organized map of every rezoning decision back to 2010. The District Court keeps an equally clean, entirely separate docket of every eviction filing over the same period. Nora Kessler spent four months matching the two, parcel by parcel, block by block, a task the county's own planning staff confirmed had never been done in-house.

The pattern held even after controlling for a block's baseline rent level and the age of its housing stock, two variables the county's own consultants had used to argue that rezoning alone does not drive displacement. It does not prove that rezoning causes eviction; correlation of this kind rarely does. What the data does show, and what the Planning Commission's own five-year housing plan does not currently track, is that the blocks the county has prioritized for redevelopment are disproportionately the same blocks where renters are already losing their leases.

The commission's chair called the finding "worth a closer look" at a public meeting two weeks after publication and directed staff to begin cross-referencing eviction data in future rezoning impact statements, a change advocates had requested for three years without success. A commission spokesperson noted that displacement pressure has many causes and that rezoning decisions also aim to expand the county's housing supply over the long term.

Every parcel match behind this story, along with the matching methodology and its limitations, is published in a public spreadsheet linked from the story's original publication, so any reader can audit the join itself.
