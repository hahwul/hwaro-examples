Property tax bills arrive as a single number, with no explanation of how the county decided a house was worth what it says it's worth. Nora Kessler spent the better part of a year building a model to answer that question at scale, comparing the county assessor's valuation of every residential parcel sold between 2020 and 2025 against its actual sale price, a check known in the assessment trade as a sales-ratio study.

The pattern was consistent and directional: homes in majority-renter precincts were routinely assessed well above what they sold for, inflating tax bills for landlords who, in nearly every case Nora reviewed, passed the cost to tenants through rent. Homes in the county's wealthiest, most homeowner-heavy precincts showed the opposite error, assessed noticeably below sale price, a quiet discount repeated year after year. Assessment officials attributed the gap to outdated comparable-sales data in fast-changing rental markets, not to any deliberate bias, and pointed to a modernization project already underway.

Following publication, the assessor's office adopted a public commitment to publish annual sales-ratio studies by precinct, the same analysis Nora had to reconstruct from raw parcel data because no such report previously existed. Two council members have since proposed tying the assessor's budget to measurable improvement in assessment equity, a bill still in committee as of this writing.

This story anchors the ongoing data desk at Groundwork News; the assessment-equity model built for it is rerun and republished every tax cycle.
