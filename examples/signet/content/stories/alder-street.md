Alder Street looks the way it has for a century: a row of brick two-flats a block from the county courthouse, half of them rented, half owned outright. What has changed, invisibly, is who signs the deed. Nora Kessler cross-referenced every recorded transfer on the block since 2015 against Secretary of State business filings and found that a single limited liability company, registered in a state nine hundred miles away, now holds title to one in four of Alder Street's rental units, most of them acquired from small local landlords who sold within eighteen months of a rent increase notice.

None of the tenants Nora interviewed knew the name on their deed had changed; their lease renewal notices still arrive under the name of a local property-management company that turns out to be a registered agent, not an owner. The LLC's formation documents list a registered-agent service address shared by more than four hundred other entities, a common structure that makes it difficult, though not impossible, to trace the people actually collecting rent.

What the recorder's data does show clearly is timing: every purchase on Alder Street by this LLC followed within a year of the county's most recent rezoning study, which flagged the block for potential higher-density redevelopment. Whether that is coincidence or strategy is the subject of Nora's next records request, filed the week this story published, seeking any correspondence between the LLC's registered agent and the county planning department.

Two of the block's remaining independent landlords have since joined a tenant-organized effort to request historical ownership disclosure at the point of sale, an ordinance change now scheduled for a first reading in January.
