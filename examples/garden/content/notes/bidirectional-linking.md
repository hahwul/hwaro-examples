Bidirectional links (or backlinks) are a key feature of modern knowledge management tools. When note A links to note B, note B automatically knows about the link and can display it as a reference.

## Why Backlinks Matter

Traditional hyperlinks are one-directional. You click a link and arrive somewhere new, but the destination page has no awareness of where you came from. This creates dead ends in your knowledge graph.

Backlinks solve this by making every connection visible from both sides. When you read a note, you can see not just where it points, but also which notes point to it.

## In Practice

Consider a note about the [Zettelkasten Method](../zettelkasten-method/). If several other notes link to it, those incoming links provide context about how the concept is applied across different domains.

This is particularly valuable in a [digital garden](../digital-gardens/), where notes accumulate connections over time, gradually revealing clusters of related ideas.

## The Compound Effect

The value of backlinks increases non-linearly as your collection grows. Early on, connections are sparse. But past a certain threshold, every new note you add creates multiple touchpoints with existing knowledge, making the whole system more than the sum of its parts.

## Tools That Support This

Most modern tools for thought -- including Obsidian, Roam Research, and Logseq -- support bidirectional linking as a core feature. In a static site context, this can be approximated through tagging and explicit cross-references.
