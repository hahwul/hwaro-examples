A tool roll sized for six bench chisels, hand-stitched from 4-ounce vegetable-tanned cowhide. Designed to protect edges during transport and look better with age.

## Process

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 01 -- Pattern and Cutting</div>
  Made a paper pattern first, testing the pocket spacing with actual chisels. Cut the main body panel (12" x 16") and the pocket panel (12" x 6") from the hide using a rotary cutter and steel ruler.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 02 -- Edge Work</div>
  Beveled all exposed edges with a #2 edge beveler. Burnished with water and a wood slicker until glossy. This step takes patience but defines the quality of the finished piece.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 03 -- Pocket Stitching</div>
  Marked pocket dividers at 1.75-inch intervals on the pocket panel. Punched stitching holes with 4mm pricking irons. Saddle-stitched each divider line with waxed linen thread using two needles.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 04 -- Assembly</div>
  Aligned the pocket panel to the main body and glued with contact cement for alignment. Punched the perimeter stitching holes and saddle-stitched the entire border. Set a brass snap for the closure flap.
</div>

<div class="process-step">
  <div class="process-step-num">Step 05 -- Finishing</div>
  Applied neatsfoot oil to condition the leather, then buffed with carnauba wax for surface protection. Attached a leather tie strap through a punched slot for the roll closure.
</div>

## Notes

The 1.75-inch pocket spacing works for chisels up to 1.5 inches wide. For wider chisels, 2-inch spacing would be better. The veg-tan leather will darken significantly over the first few months of use -- it's part of the appeal.

Saddle stitching is slow but produces a much stronger seam than machine stitching. If one thread breaks, the other holds. Worth the time for something that will see daily use.
