A carnival midway at night is one of the most carefully lit spaces in everyday architecture. Every booth fights for attention, but the lighting is deliberately constructed so the visitor can still navigate the chaos.

## Three Layers of Light

- **Ambient string lights** — overhead bulbs hung in continuous lines. Warm-white, low intensity per bulb but high coverage. They define the *path* without competing with individual booths.
- **Booth signage** — saturated, often blinking. Each booth runs at a higher intensity than the ambient layer, so the eye can pick it out. The blinking is a coordination problem; if every booth blinks at the same rate, the visitor stops registering it.
- **Ride spotlights** — the brightest layer, reserved for the largest rides. They function the way a cathedral's stained glass does: orienting the visitor toward the most important structures from anywhere in the field.

The hierarchy is intentional. Strip out any one layer and the midway becomes either a flat light field (no booths stand out) or a frenzy (no path through). Old fairgrounds figured this out in the 1920s, and the formula has barely changed.
