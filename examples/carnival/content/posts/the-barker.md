A carnival barker is a vocal performer working at the boundary between performance and sales. The job has remained nearly unchanged for a century: stand at the edge of a booth, project across ambient noise, and convert passing foot traffic into paying customers.

## What Works

- **A repeating phrase, rhythmically spaced.** The phrase becomes a beacon — the visitor's ear locks onto it from a hundred meters away and the booth becomes orientable in a noisy field.
- **A tonal range, not a constant volume.** A monotonous shout is filtered out by the brain in seconds. A barker who modulates between intimate and announced volumes keeps re-engaging the listener.
- **A specific, falsifiable promise.** "Three rings or your money back" is more compelling than "great prizes inside." The brain processes a constraint as a quality signal.

## What Stops Working Quickly

Pure novelty. A barker who tries a new pitch every visitor exhausts themselves and confuses the crowd. The classic pitches survived because they balance attention with predictability — loud enough to find, repetitive enough to remember.
