A radio astronomer picks up a signal from deep space that causes anyone who hears it to relive their worst memory on loop. The premise is terrific. The execution is not.

<!-- more -->

## Great Concept, Poor Execution

Jordan Peele's track record has earned him the benefit of the doubt, which makes *Dead Frequency* all the more disappointing. The central idea -- a cosmic signal that weaponizes personal trauma -- is rich with thematic potential. Unfortunately, the film abandons its most interesting threads in favor of conventional horror set pieces.

Lupita Nyong'o does her best as Dr. Amara Cole, the radio astronomer who first detects the signal. She brings genuine pathos to the early scenes as Amara grapples with the implications of her discovery. But the script gives her less and less to work with as the film progresses, reducing her to a series of reaction shots in the final act.

## Style Over Substance

The production design is impressive. The radio telescope facility, set in the New Mexico desert, is a striking location, and the visual representation of the signal -- a pulsing, geometric pattern that fills every screen it touches -- is genuinely unnerving the first time you see it. By the fifth or sixth time, it has lost its power.

Pedro Pascal is wasted in a thankless role as the skeptical colleague. Jenna Ortega, as a graduate student who becomes the signal's first victim, is the only cast member who seems to understand what kind of film this should have been -- her scenes have an emotional rawness that the rest of the movie lacks.

## The Verdict

*Dead Frequency* is a film at war with itself: half ambitious sci-fi parable, half generic horror movie. The ambitious half loses. Peele has earned the right to a misfire, but this one stings.
