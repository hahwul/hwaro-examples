A multinational submarine crew faces an impossible moral dilemma when their orders contradict the evidence before their eyes. Park Chan-wook brings his signature precision to the geopolitical thriller.

## Under Pressure

Park Chan-wook has always excelled at placing characters in confined spaces and watching them crack. *Iron Meridian* may be his most claustrophobic film yet -- nearly the entire runtime takes place inside a submarine, and the walls seem to close in with every scene.

Mads Mikkelsen plays Captain Brandt with characteristic restraint. His face reveals almost nothing, which makes the rare moments of visible emotion land with tremendous force. Zhang Ziyi, as the intelligence officer whose loyalty is questioned, delivers a performance of controlled fury.

## Technical Excellence

The sound design is extraordinary. Every creak of the hull, every ping of sonar, every muffled footstep contributes to an atmosphere of suffocating tension. The score by Cho Young-wuk is sparse -- long stretches of silence broken by single, held notes that set your teeth on edge.

Park's visual storytelling remains unmatched. A critical plot point is conveyed entirely through the reflection in a periscope lens. Another pivotal scene plays out in a single, unbroken four-minute take that follows Washington through three compartments of the submarine.

## Where It Stumbles

The third act introduces a twist that, while technically well-executed, feels borrowed from lesser thrillers. The film recovers quickly, but for a few minutes the spell is broken. The political resolution also feels overly tidy for a film that spends two hours arguing that nothing is simple.

## The Verdict

Despite a slightly conventional ending, *Iron Meridian* is a masterclass in sustained tension. Park Chan-wook proves that a confined setting and a great cast can outperform any amount of spectacle. One of the year's best thrillers.
