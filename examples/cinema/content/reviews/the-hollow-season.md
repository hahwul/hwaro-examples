A detective suspended for misconduct is drawn back into a cold case that connects to the highest levels of 1970s London society. McQueen builds a world you can almost smell, but the story takes too long to find its footing.

## London, 1974

Steve McQueen's recreation of mid-1970s London is extraordinary. The production design is so meticulous -- the stained pub carpets, the nicotine-yellow office walls, the rain-slicked cobblestones -- that you can practically feel the damp. This is London before gentrification, before glass towers, and McQueen renders it with a level of detail that borders on obsessive.

Daniel Kaluuya plays DI Marcus Thorne, a detective whose investigation into police corruption has left him suspended and isolated. When a woman's body is found in circumstances that mirror an unsolved case from a decade earlier, Thorne begins his own unofficial investigation.

## Performance and Atmosphere

Kaluuya is superb. He plays Thorne as a man who has been ground down but not broken -- there is a stubborn core of decency beneath the exhaustion. Olivia Colman, as a Home Office bureaucrat who may be ally or obstacle, brings her usual intelligence to a role that could have been one-dimensional. Ralph Fiennes, in a small but crucial role, delivers a single monologue in the third act that is the finest acting in any film this year.

The atmosphere is the film's greatest strength. McQueen uses long, patient shots of empty corridors, rain on windows, and the play of streetlight on wet asphalt to create a mood of pervasive unease.

## The Length Problem

At 152 minutes, the film tests patience. The first hour is almost entirely atmospheric setup, and while it is beautifully executed, several scenes feel redundant. A 130-minute cut of this film would be tighter and more effective. The middle act in particular wanders, following Thorne through a series of interviews that establish character but do little to advance the plot.

## The Verdict

*The Hollow Season* is a well-crafted period thriller with outstanding performances, let down by a reluctance to edit. McQueen's eye for detail is unmatched, and Kaluuya anchors the film with quiet authority. Worth seeing for the craft alone, but temper expectations for pacing.
