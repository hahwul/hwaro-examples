The burner port cracked in late December, a slow failure I noticed only when the kiln stopped reaching temperature evenly, and by the time I traced it to the refractory itself the practice was on hold for six weeks over the turn of the year — the longest gap in the log since it started. Rebuilding the port meant relining part of the kiln, which meant I could not trust the first results to tell me anything about glaze or weather. I needed a control.

<!-- more -->

So the fiftieth opening is deliberately small: a single footed chaire, plain form, a copper matte I have fired dozens of times before, moderate reduction in dry pine needles, nothing experimental about the process at all. The point was to find out whether the rebuilt kiln behaved like the old one before I trusted it with anything I actually cared about losing. It does, as far as I can tell — the matte came up the expected bottle-green, the reduction took evenly, and the light onshore breeze that morning left only the faintest warm cast along one edge, well within the range I would call normal.

I am keeping this one as a reference rather than a finished piece, a marker to compare future firings against as the kiln settles back in. It is not the most interesting object in the log, and that is exactly the job I built it to do.
