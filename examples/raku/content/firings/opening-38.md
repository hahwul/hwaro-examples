January mornings here are usually the stillest of the year, which sounds like good news for a firing and rarely is. No wind means no salt carried in off the water, which means the variable I have come to rely on for surprise simply does not show up, and the glaze does more or less what the recipe says it should. I fired this chawan expecting exactly that kind of quiet, uneventful opening, and got it — until I looked at the crackle.

<!-- more -->

The base glaze is a feldspar crackle clear I have used for six years without much variation, brushed thick under a matte made from ash I sieve myself out of bracken and gorse cut off the headland in autumn. In still air the crackle usually forms in wide, lazy plates, a centimetre or more across, because the glaze cools slowly with nothing moving the surrounding air. This time it split into a web so fine I mistook it for a flaw in the clay body until I turned the bowl in the light and saw the pattern was continuous, unbroken, running edge to edge.

I still do not have an explanation. The kiln atmosphere log shows nothing unusual — a clean, oxidising fire, standard reduction time in the bin, dry bracken as the combustible. My only guess is that the ash itself, milled slightly finer than my last batch, changed the glaze's expansion rate just enough to fracture differently on cooling. I have started sieving to a consistent mesh size since, more out of curiosity than any real plan to repeat this. Some firings you document and let go.
