I should not have fired this one. The forecast had a gale warning up by midday and I lit the kiln anyway, on the theory that a covered lean-to and a stubborn streak would be enough. They were, barely. Wind was strong enough by the time I pulled the jar that the tongs shook getting it the eighteen inches from kiln mouth to reduction bin, and some of that unsteadiness is baked into the piece itself.

<!-- more -->

The jar was hand-built rather than thrown, a bulbous form with a shoulder meant to taper to a narrow neck, and even before the reduction it already carried the slight irregularity that hand-building leaves. The gale added to it rather than erasing it: one shoulder took a hard scorch from ash blown straight out of the bin as I sealed the lid against the wind, leaving a matte black patch where the straw-ash glaze elsewhere fired a pale oat colour, and the rim — soft still when I set the piece down too fast — settled unevenly, so the jar now sits with a visible tilt if you view it straight on.

I kept it anyway, and it is the one piece in the log I have not offered to anyone. Most of what the coast does to a glaze is a surface effect you can admire and move past. This one changed the actual geometry of the object, which feels like a different kind of record — proof not just that the weather touched the glaze, but that it touched the hand holding the tongs.
