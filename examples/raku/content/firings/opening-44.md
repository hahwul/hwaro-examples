Twice a year the tide drops far enough at dawn to expose a stretch of shore I cannot otherwise reach, and I have learned to plan a firing around it when the date lands on a day I am free. Fresh bladderwrack packed straight into the reduction bin behaves nothing like the dried bracken or dune grass I use the rest of the year — it is wetter, it smoulders instead of catching, and it throws far more smoke into the bin than usual.

<!-- more -->

The dish is a wide, shallow kohiki form, dipped in a white slip before a clear feldspar glaze with no ash addition at all — I wanted the base clean enough to register whatever the seaweed reduction actually did, without another variable competing for the surface. What came out of the bin was a soft, uneven grey mottling across the slip, heavier toward the rim where the wet seaweed sat in closest contact, fading to near-white at the centre where less smoke reached. It is a quieter effect than the copper flashing gets, more like a watermark than a glaze reaction, and slip crawls at the very edge where the seaweed's moisture cooled the glaze fastest.

A potter visiting from inland was at the kiln that morning and asked, mostly as a joke, whether I'd trade the dish for a look at her sketchbook of glaze tests. I gave it to her outright. The tide will not be that low again until winter, and by then the log will have moved on to something else.
