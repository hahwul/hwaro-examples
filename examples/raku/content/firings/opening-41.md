I fire on the equinox most years, more as a personal marker than for any technical reason, and this one delivered the kind of weather the kiln was built for. A squall came through off the water about twenty minutes into the firing, sheeting rain sideways across the yard and driving spray up over the headland edge in a way I could taste from six feet away. The yunomi in this load was turned so one wall faced the water directly.

<!-- more -->

Copper matte glazes are the ones most sensitive to what the coast adds, and this piece shows it as cleanly as anything I have pulled. The wall that faced the squall carries a broad pink-to-salmon flash where chloride in the driven spray landed on the still-molten glaze and reduced the copper differently than the dune grass alone would have. The far wall, sheltered by my own body as I worked the tongs, fired the flat bottle-green the recipe is supposed to produce. Stand the cup on a table and rotate it and you can watch the firing's weather pass across the surface like a record of the wind itself.

I did not plan the orientation. I loaded it the way I load everything, by habit, and only understood what had happened once the bin was open and the piece had cooled enough to handle. I have since started noting which way each piece faces before I light the kiln, on the chance that I can learn to do this on purpose. So far the results have refused to cooperate.
