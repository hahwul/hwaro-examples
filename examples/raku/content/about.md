I trained as a studio potter inland, on an electric wheel in a shared workshop where the only variable that mattered was how long I left something in the kiln. I moved to Gullscar Point in 2014 and built a small propane raku kiln in the lean-to behind the cottage, forty feet above the tideline, and I have not fired anything indoors since.

## Process

Bowls and cups are thrown or hand-built and bisque fired in a standard electric kiln to cone 08. Glazing is simple on purpose — a feldspar-based crackle clear, a copper matte that runs green to black depending on how hard the reduction goes, and an ash glaze made from bracken and gorse cut off the headland itself. The raku firing runs to around cone 06, fast, in a small gas kiln I can watch from three feet away. When a piece is ready I pull it with long tongs while it is still glowing and drop it into a metal bin lined with whatever combustible material the season has given me: dune grass in summer, bracken in autumn, pine needles in winter. The bin is sealed, the glaze crazes audibly as it cools, and twenty minutes later I find out what actually happened.

This is Western-style raku, not the Japanese tradition it borrows its name from — Raku-yaki chawan are typically cooled slowly and never touch a reduction bin. What I do is closer to a technique developed in America in the 1960s, prized for exactly the instability I have come to depend on.

## What the coast adds

The kiln has no windbreak because a windbreak would defeat the point. Salt-laden air moving off the water settles on a glaze surface mid-firing and interacts with copper and iron in ways I can characterize but not schedule — sometimes a blush of pink across a matte green, sometimes nothing at all, sometimes a crawl in the glaze that ruins the piece outright. I keep a weather note with every firing log for exactly this reason: wind direction and a rough sea state tell me more about why a glaze did what it did than any recipe change would.

## Documentation

Every kiln opening in the log gets a date, the glaze and reduction material used, an outcome, and a single-line drawing of the vessel's outer profile, made at the bench the morning after. No photographs. The drawing is not decoration — it is the fastest way I have found to record a vessel's actual shape without a camera getting between me and the object, and it keeps the record consistent across eleven years of firings that otherwise share very little. The full record runs in [the firing log](/firings/), newest opening first.

## Commissions and visits

I take a small number of custom orders each year, mostly chawan for tea practice, and the waiting list is usually six to nine months. The kiln is not open to the public, but visits by potters and students can be arranged by post — write care of the Gullscar Point postal exchange and I will reply, eventually, once the weather allows.
