Every bowl here begins on the wheel and ends in a bin of smouldering bracken, forty feet from the water. The kiln sits on a headland with no windbreak, which is by design: the same gusts that make a February firing miserable are what put a fugitive blush of pink and grey into a glaze that would fire flat and matte indoors. I did not choose this. The coast chose it, the third year I fired here, when a wet nor'easter rolled through mid-firing and left a bowl half-clouded in a way I could never plan for and have never been able to fully repeat since.

So the practice now is documentation rather than control. Each kiln opening gets a date, a weather note, and a drawing — a single line tracing the vessel's outer profile, the only record besides the pot itself of what that firing made. No two glazes come out the same, and the record is built to prove that rather than hide it.

What follows is not a shop front. It is a log: firings kept in order, each with the bowl or cup it produced, drawn once, and left alone. More on the kiln, the glazes, and how the record is kept is on [the about page](/about/).
