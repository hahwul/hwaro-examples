She brought the scissors from her mother’s sewing box. The ones with the mother-of-pearl handles that were never allowed near paper.

“I’m sorry,” she said, which was the first time anyone had ever apologized to me.

She cut a thin curl from the back of her head, just above the nape, where it would not show. The strands were the color of weak tea and smelled faintly of the lavender water her grandmother kept on the dressing table.

She worked by the light of a single candle. Each time the wax popped she flinched and the crack in my shoulder widened by the width of a single hair. She threaded the hair through the fissure with a needle meant for lace. Then she sealed it with the clear glue that came in the little glass bottle with the red label.

When she was finished she kissed the place where the hair disappeared into me.

“You won’t come apart again,” she promised.

I wanted to tell her that I had never wanted to come apart in the first place. That the coming apart had been the only honest thing about me in years.

Instead I let the new seam hold. It itches when the weather changes. Sometimes, when the house is very quiet, I can feel one of the hairs still moving, as if it is trying to grow back toward the girl who gave it away.
