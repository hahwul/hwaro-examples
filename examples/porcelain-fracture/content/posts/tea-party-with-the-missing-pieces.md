The missing hand arrived first.

It had been gone since the spring, when the smallest boy tried to see whether porcelain could float. It could not. The hand was recovered from the pond three weeks later, still holding the tiny china teacup it had been reaching for when it fell.

At the table it sat in its usual place, cup raised in a toast that never quite finished. The fingers were graceful. The wrist ended in a clean, terrible edge.

The missing left ear came next, wrapped in a handkerchief that smelled of the attic. It listened very politely while the others spoke. It never answered. Ears are better at that sort of thing.

We were five that afternoon, if you counted the crack that had detached itself from my hip and was now pretending to be a guest. It had grown a voice the color of old sugar. When it laughed, the sound came from inside the table.

The girl who sometimes visits did not come that day. She never comes when the missing pieces gather. I think she is afraid they will ask her to stay.

Before the light changed we all agreed, without speaking, that we would not mention the girl again. Some names are too heavy for a table that already has too many empty chairs.

The hand poured for everyone. The cup, of course, remained empty. Porcelain does not drink. It only remembers the shape of thirst.
