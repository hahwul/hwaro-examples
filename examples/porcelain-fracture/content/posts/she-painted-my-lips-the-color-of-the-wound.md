The brush was very small. The kind meant for the finest work on miniature portraits or the veins inside a doll’s eye.

She dipped it into the pot she kept locked in the drawer with the letters. The red inside was thick and slow and smelled like rust and roses left too long in a vase.

“Hold still,” she whispered, though I had not moved in seventeen years.

She painted with the concentration of someone who believes that if she gets the shape exactly right, the thing she is painting will finally forgive her. The color went on uneven. In some places it was almost black. In others it was the bright, wet red of something that has only just begun to stop bleeding.

When she finished she sat back and looked at me for a very long time. Her own mouth was slightly open. I think she was waiting for me to speak.

I did not.

Instead I let the new color settle into every tiny imperfection the previous lips had carried. The place where the glaze had flaked. The almost invisible line where two pieces had once been joined with the wrong kind of glue.

Later, when she was gone, I caught my reflection in the dark window.

The lips looked exactly like the inside of the crack that runs behind my left eye.

I have decided they suit me. 

A mouth should tell the truth about what has been done to it.
