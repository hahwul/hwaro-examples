Ipomoea alba, commonly called moonflower, is a fast-growing vine in the morning glory family. Unlike its daytime cousins, it opens at dusk and closes by mid-morning. A single vine can produce flowers for three to four months once established, which makes it one of the most rewarding subjects for a small night garden.

## Seed Preparation

The seed coat is hard and water-resistant. Nick each seed with a file or soak in tepid water for twelve hours before sowing. Untreated seeds may take three weeks to germinate. Treated seeds usually emerge within five days at soil temperatures above twenty degrees Celsius.

Sow indoors four to six weeks before the last frost. Use a free-draining mix and a deep cell tray, since the taproot resents disturbance. Transplant once two true leaves are present and overnight temperatures stay above twelve degrees.

## Support and Spacing

Provide a sturdy trellis at planting. Mature vines reach four to five meters and carry significant weight when wet. Space plants forty centimeters apart along a south-facing wall or pergola. Strong vertical support reduces the risk of stem breakage during summer storms.

## Watering and Feeding

Moonflowers prefer consistent moisture but dislike waterlogged soil. A weekly deep watering, supplemented during dry weeks, suits most temperate climates. Excessive nitrogen produces lush foliage at the expense of flowers, so a balanced low-nitrogen feed every three weeks works better than rich compost amendments.

## First Bloom

Expect the first flowers eight to ten weeks after transplanting, often around the summer solstice. Buds open over fifteen to twenty minutes, usually between 19:30 and 21:00. The fragrance carries several meters and is strongest in the first two hours after opening. Each flower lasts a single night, but a healthy vine produces dozens at peak season.

Record opening times and weather for the first ten flowers. The pattern is more consistent than most gardeners expect.
