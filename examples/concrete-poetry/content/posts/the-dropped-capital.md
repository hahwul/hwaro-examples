## A Letter That Announces Itself

The drop cap is not decoration. Do not let the decorators have it.

The drop cap is a **signal at the threshold**: the text begins here, attend, the world of this page is now open. Medieval scribes painted them in gold and blue, sometimes with tiny scenes inside the letter — a hare in the bowl of a *Q*, a climbing figure in the stem of an *I*. These were not ornaments added after the fact. They were the point of entry, the architectural door.

---

## What the Oversized Letter Does to the Eye

When the first letter of a paragraph is set at three or four times the height of the surrounding text, it does several things simultaneously:

1. It anchors the eye — gives it a place to begin without ambiguity
2. It creates a vertical rhythm that the surrounding lines echo as they wrap alongside it
3. It slows the reader, however slightly, at the beginning — which is almost always the right choice

That last point deserves expansion. The beginning of a text is the most delicate moment in the reading experience. The reader is not yet inside; they are on the threshold. A small architectural event — a larger letter, a wider margin, a ruled line — acknowledges this. It says: *here is where you cross over*.

> The dropped capital is the handshake before the conversation. It asks nothing of you except that you begin.

## The Letter as Image

The typefaces we have inherited come from hands. The Roman capitals on Trajan's Column, cut into stone in 113 CE, are still with us: the thin and thick strokes of the serif letterforms derive from the varying pressure of a broad-nibbed brush held at an angle against stone or vellum.

The letter *A* descends, through many transformations, from a Phoenician symbol for an ox head — rotated, abstracted, until the horns became serifs and the animal became a glyph. We write with an alphabet that was once a picture vocabulary.

To make the *A* large is to make the animal large again, if only for a moment. The `::first-letter` pseudo-element in CSS is the digital incarnation of the illuminated manuscript. It is, in the most literal sense, the oldest typographic technology in continuous use.

---

## How Size Confers Significance

There is a rule so obvious it is almost embarrassing to state, and yet it is constantly forgotten in the design of digital text: **large means important**. Not loud. Not aggressive. *Important* — worth attending to, worth pausing at.

We use size to convey hierarchy: the heading is larger than the subheading, which is larger than the body. But hierarchy implies subordination — that some content is less valuable. The dropped capital refuses this logic. The first letter is not a heading. It is not labeled *Section One*. It is simply the first thing, enlarged, so that it can be *seen* as the first thing — acknowledged in its primacy.

---

## A Note on Restraint

The dropped capital should be used at the beginning of a section, not a paragraph. If every paragraph begins with an oversized letter, the effect is noise rather than signal. The eye, overwhelmed by interruption, stops attending.

This is the paradox of emphasis: it depends on the unemphasized field around it. **Bold** text stands out because the surrounding text is regular. The large letter stands out because the letters before and after it are small.

Use the drop cap once per section, or once per piece. Let it mean *beginning*. Let it hold that meaning without competition.

The craft of `typography` is largely the craft of knowing when to stop.
