Sienna was founded in 2014 by Rowan Ashdown, who trained first as a restoration ecologist and only later as a landscape architect — a sequence that still shows in how the practice works. The soil report comes before the concept sketch. A site is read for what it is doing on its own before anyone decides what it should look like.

The practice takes on one kind of commission: land that used to work for a living. Rail yards, quarries, gasworks, dockyards, colliery spoil — ground with a use-history written into its contours, its drainage, and often its contamination. Most landscape practices treat that history as a problem to solve before the real design starts. Sienna treats it as the design brief.

## How we work

Every commission opens with a topographic and soil survey, produced in-house rather than commissioned out, because the two people who do the surveying are the same two who do the grading design. That continuity matters: a contour drawn by someone who has stood on the actual slope reads differently than one interpolated from a flyover.

Grading is worked out in cut-and-fill wherever the contamination profile allows it, keeping material on site instead of trucking it to landfill and importing clean fill to replace it. Planting follows a two-stage logic — pioneer species tolerant of poor or capped soils go in first, and a slower, more diverse palette follows once a season of drainage and settlement has proven the ground stable.

## The practice today

Sienna is five people: two landscape architects, a soil scientist, a planting specialist, and a part-time archivist who keeps the site histories that inform each design. The practice works across the Kell Valley with local authorities, rail operators, port trusts, and the occasional private landowner willing to accept a park instead of a fence.
