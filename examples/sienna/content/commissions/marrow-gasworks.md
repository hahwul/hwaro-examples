Marrow Gasworks manufactured town gas from coal from 1902 until North Sea gas made it obsolete; the two holders on this site were decommissioned in 1996 and stood empty for another twenty years before demolition. What they left behind, once the guide frames came down, was a pair of near-perfect circular footprints sunk slightly below grade — the holder tanks themselves had been set into shallow excavated rings.

<!-- more -->

## Remediation first

Gasworks soil is rarely simple. Ours came back from testing with elevated hydrocarbons and cyanide compounds concentrated in a band roughly a metre below the original tank floors — a signature of manufactured gas production almost everywhere it happened. Rather than excavate and haul the contaminated layer offsite, the remediation strategy caps it under a metre of certified clean fill, engineered to keep the water table from drawing contaminants upward. This is the reason both basins sit as shallow depressions rather than at natural grade: the cap itself becomes the new landform.

## Turning the rings into wetland

Both former holder rings now hold constructed wetland basins, planted with reed and rush selected for stormwater attenuation rather than ornament — the basins double as the ward's surface water management for a much larger surrounding catchment. Boardwalks trace the original holder guide-frame footings, now visible only as a shallow ring of foundation stone left exposed as an edge marker, not reconstructed.

## Elevation

The northern basin (Holder No. 1) steps from 14.6m at its rim down to 11.8m at the wetland floor; the southern basin (Holder No. 2), slightly larger, runs from 15.3m down to 12.1m. Both sets of spot elevations are noted in the site plan margin, referenced to the same benchmark used across the whole Marrow Docks remediation.

## Status

Capping, basin excavation, and boardwalk construction were completed in 2025. The wetland planting has one full growing season behind it and is establishing well in both basins.
