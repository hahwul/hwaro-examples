Saltmarsh Dock imported timber from 1911 until the wharf silted beyond what dredging could keep up with in 1988. The dry dock behind it stayed in occasional use for small repairs into the early 2000s, but by the time we surveyed the site the tidal creek had reclaimed most of the original basin at every high water.

<!-- more -->

## Working with the tide, not against it

Most waterfront remediation in the Kell Valley has tried to hold the tideline where engineers of an earlier century put it — usually behind a dock wall never designed for a rising mean high water. Saltmarsh Dock does the opposite. Rather than raise or extend the dock wall, the design accepts that the lower two-thirds of the old basin will flood on most tides, and designs a park to work with that rather than around it.

## The design

The retained dock wall becomes a public quay and promenade along the landward edge, standing above all but the highest spring tides. Below it, elevated boardwalks follow the old dock footprint out into what is now intertidal salt marsh, engineered to sit just above mean high water so they flood only during larger tides — an experience we want, not a defect. Salt-tolerant planting, largely sea aster and common cord-grass already colonizing the silted basin on its own, is left to continue doing so with minimal intervention.

## Elevation and tidal range

The site's elevation range is small by the standards of our other commissions but the numbers matter more here than anywhere else: the quay sits at 6.4m, stepping down through the boardwalk zone to 1.9m at the lowest accessible point before the true salt marsh begins. These figures, referenced to the same ordnance datum used across the practice's work, are noted in the site plan margin alongside the tidal boardwalk sections.

## Status

The scheme is in detailed design through mid-2026, with a tidal and ecological survey informing the final boardwalk alignment before construction drawings are issued.
