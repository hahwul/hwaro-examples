The Bank is what everyone in the valley still calls the spoil heap raised by Marrow Colliery between 1902 and its closure in 1984 — thirty-eight metres of shale and mudstone waste piled against the hillside, capped and grassed over in the 1990s but left to its own unstable devices ever since. Slippage on the western face had closed the adjoining footpath twice in the decade before we were commissioned.

<!-- more -->

## The problem was stability, not aesthetics

Spoil heap regrading gets discussed as a landscaping problem, but the first year of work on the Bank was geotechnical. Shale spoil compacts unevenly and sheds water fast, which is exactly what had caused the western face slippage. Regrading here meant re-profiling the whole slope to a stable angle of repose before any planting design mattered — in places that meant reducing gradients that had stood since the 1980s cap by nearly half.

## From spoil heap to hill

Once the slope was stable, the design leans into what a thirty-eight-metre mound already offers that flatter sites don't: a view. A spiral path climbs the regraded slope in three long switchbacks, gaining height gradually enough to stay accessible, arriving at a summit viewpoint that is, at 63 metres, the highest publicly accessible point in the Kell Valley. Terraced woodland planting — birch and rowan on the upper slopes, giving way to alder near the base where the ground stays wetter — roots the regraded surface far more effectively than the grass cap it replaces.

## Elevation

The site plan records seven survey levels from the 24.6m base up to the 63.0m summit, each corresponding to a switchback landing on the spiral path. These spot elevations are noted in the plan margin; the full 38.4m of relief between base and summit is, as far as we've found, the largest single elevation change in any Sienna commission to date.

## Status

Regrading, path construction, and the first planting phase were completed in 2025. The summit viewpoint opened to the public in November of that year.
