Foehn is a one-person repair bench in the attic of a former weaving mill in
Trellheim. The name comes from the warm downslope wind that pours over the
ridge every October and detunes everything in the room by a few cents — a
yearly reminder that analog circuits are weather, not software.

The instruments that come through here are mostly European monosynths and
string machines from 1972 to 1984: Vektor, Halbmond, Ostwind, Klangwerk,
Nordlicht. Machines with names like weather fronts, built by companies that
lasted eight years and left behind service manuals printed on paper that now
smells like vanilla. I buy them broken, or they arrive in freight crates from
people who inherited them, and each one leaves with a fresh calibration sheet
taped inside the lid.

## How the log works

Every restoration gets [one entry per major job](/restorations/), written
the same week the work happens. Measurements are quoted from the actual bench sheet, part
numbers from the actual order. I write down the failures too — the recap that
fixed nothing, the fader that snapped in half — because the failures are what
I go looking for when the next dead synth lands on the bench.

## About the faders on every card

Each entry card on this site carries a row of five panel faders. Their
positions are not decoration: they are set by the entry's word count, run
through five fixed multipliers, the way a front panel remembers the last
patch someone played on it. A long, detailed teardown and a short shop note
wear visibly different settings. No two entries hold the same patch.

## The bench

- A 1968 oscilloscope that needs twenty minutes to warm up, same as the synths.
- A frequency counter accurate to a tenth of a cent, the only modern thing here.
- One angled lamp, one magenta LED on the isolation transformer, and no overhead light.
- A drawer of tempco resistors sorted by the year I stopped trusting the previous drawer.

If you have a machine that hums, drifts, or refuses to speak above middle C,
the bench takes bookings two months out. Bring the original lid if you still
have it. The calibration sheet needs somewhere to live.
