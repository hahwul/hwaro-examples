The Halbmond S-200 is a string machine, which means it is really an organ
wearing a scarf. Full polyphony, one master oscillator, a divider chain, and
a keybed with forty-nine J-wire contacts — each one a strip of springy wire
that has spent four and a half decades deciding whether or not to conduct.

<!-- more -->

This one came from a church basement in Vellby. Eleven keys sounded, six of
those intermittently, and the low F-sharp stuck down so firmly it played a
drone through everything like the instrument was practicing for a funeral.

## The autopsy

Someone before me had "fixed" the keybed with an entire can of contact
cleaner, sprayed from above, through the keys. The solvent had dissolved
forty years of dust into a lacquer and re-deposited it exactly where the
J-wires meet the bus bars. Under the microscope each contact wore a little
brown boot of residue. Eleven keys worked because eleven boots had cracked.

So: full disassembly. Every key off the frame, every J-wire unhooked and
bathed in isopropyl, the two bus bars pulled and polished lengthwise with
a strip of untreated paper — never abrasives, the plating is thinner than
the manual admits. The stuck F-sharp turned out to be a swollen bushing,
so all forty-nine bushings were replaced with cut felt while the patient
was open. If you do one you do them all; felt ages as a population.

## Reassembly notes for future me

```text
Key return spring:  hook faces the rail, always
Bus bar torque:     finger-tight plus one quarter turn, no more
J-wire gap:         0.5 mm at rest, checked with feeler gauge
Test order:         chromatic up, chromatic down, then ten-finger chord
```

Final count after reassembly: forty-nine keys, forty-nine clean triggers,
no drones. The divider chain itself never had anything wrong with it, which
is the quiet lesson of string machines — the electronics outlive the
mechanics nearly every time.

The ensemble chorus card has one dull channel, but that is a different
panel and a different week.
