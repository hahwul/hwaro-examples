A multi-tier pagoda holds itself up through a hierarchy of stacked timber brackets known in Chinese practice as dougong, in Korean as gongpo, and in Japanese as tokyo. The bracket sits between the column head and the eave purlin and translates the weight of a deep, overhanging roof into a controlled vertical load on the post below. The cluster is not glued, nailed, or pegged in any structural sense. It is held by interlocking joinery cut to a millimetric standard, then settled into place by the load itself.

Each tier of the bracket complex distributes the roof outward and downward at a slightly different angle. This produces the upward sweep at the corner that is often read as decorative but is in fact the natural geometry of cantilever stacking. A pagoda built without bracket sets, or with bracket sets simplified, will have shorter eaves and a flatter silhouette. The ratio between the column diameter and the first bracket arm, called the cai-fen module in the Yingzao Fashi, governs nearly every other dimension in the building.

Multi-story pagodas add a further constraint. Each story rests on a platform of beams that doubles as the ceiling of the story below, and the bracket cluster on each floor must therefore carry not only its own roof but also the live load of the floor it supports. To prevent progressive sway, master carpenters historically tapered each story so that the upper floors are lighter, narrower, and less laden with bracket arms. The tapering is also what gives the pagoda its receding, telescopic profile.

The result is a structure that resists earthquakes by flexing rather than resisting. When the ground moves, each bracket cluster pivots slightly, dissipating energy through friction at the joinery surfaces. Several thousand-year-old wooden pagodas remain standing in seismic regions because of this behavior alone.
