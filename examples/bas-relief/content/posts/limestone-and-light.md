The color palette of this theme is not arbitrary. It is drawn from limestone --- a sedimentary rock that has been the preferred medium of relief sculptors for millennia.

<!-- more -->

## Why Limestone

Limestone is soft enough to carve with precision but hard enough to endure. Its surface holds detail without crumbling. And critically, its color is warm without being assertive: a spectrum of cream, buff, and pale ochre that makes shadow legible without competing with form.

Egyptian temple walls, Assyrian palace friezes, Romanesque church portals --- all carved from limestone, all still readable after thousands of years. The material does not demand attention. It simply presents.

## Translating Stone to Screen

A screen is not stone. It emits light rather than reflecting it. Colors behave differently. But the principle of material restraint carries across.

The background of this theme uses the warmth of limestone without attempting to simulate texture. The text sits at a contrast ratio that evokes dark carved inscriptions against pale stone. The shadows borrow their color from the material --- they are not pure black but a warm, muted tone that preserves the sense of a unified surface.

## The Absence of Decoration

Limestone carvers rarely painted their work. The material spoke for itself. This theme follows the same logic: no decorative borders, no patterned backgrounds, no ornamental dividers. Structure is expressed through shadow and space alone.

Every element earns its place by serving a function. If it does not help the reader navigate, understand, or engage with the content, it is not present.
