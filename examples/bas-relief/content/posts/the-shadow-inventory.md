Shadow is the oldest tool of visual communication. Before written language, before pigment, humans read the world through light and its absence. A shadow tells you where one thing ends and another begins. It tells you which thing is closer. It tells you the time of day.

<!-- more -->

## Three Shadows

This theme employs three distinct shadow registers, each serving a specific purpose:

**The ground shadow** is the lightest. It sits beneath every elevated element, barely visible, just enough to lift the element a hair's breadth above the surface. This is the shadow of a coin resting on a table.

**The card shadow** carries more weight. It creates the primary sense of depth, the feeling that a panel or section has been pressed upward from the background. This is the shadow of a bas-relief carving under raking light.

**The hover shadow** appears only on interaction. When you reach toward an element, it rises to meet your hand. This is feedback --- an acknowledgment that this surface responds to touch.

## Consistency of Light

In classical bas-relief, all shadow falls from a single light source. The sculptor must commit to one direction and maintain it across the entire composition. If a nose casts its shadow to the left, every nose in every figure must do the same.

This theme follows that discipline. Every shadow is offset to the bottom-right, implying a light source at the upper-left. The consistency creates coherence: even disparate elements feel like they belong to the same carved surface.
