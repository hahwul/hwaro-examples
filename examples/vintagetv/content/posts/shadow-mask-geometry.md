The shadow mask is a thin sheet of low-expansion steel suspended a fixed distance behind the inner face of a color cathode-ray tube. Three electron guns, arranged in a delta or in-line configuration, fire beams that cross at the mask aperture and continue to strike one of three phosphor dots on the screen. Each beam is mechanically restricted by the mask so that, regardless of deflection angle, it can only illuminate phosphor of its assigned color.

### Aperture Pitch

Aperture pitch describes the spacing between adjacent holes in the mask and is the dominant determinant of resolvable detail. Domestic receivers built between 1965 and 1985 typically used pitches between 0.61 and 0.43 millimeters. Tighter pitch demanded thinner mask material, more accurate phosphor screening, and tighter convergence tolerances on the gun assembly.

### Thermal Expansion

The mask intercepts roughly four-fifths of the beam current and dissipates it as heat. Localized heating causes the mask to bulge toward the screen, displacing apertures from their alignment with the phosphor pattern. Manufacturers addressed this through invar masks, internal bimetal supports, and tensioned aperture grilles such as Sony's Trinitron design, which replaced perforations with vertically tensioned wires anchored at top and bottom.

### Convergence

Static convergence aligns the three beams at the screen center and is set by ring magnets at the gun neck. Dynamic convergence corrects errors that grow with deflection angle and is controlled by waveforms applied to auxiliary coils on the yoke. A misconverged tube produces colored fringes along high-contrast edges; the fault is most visible on white text against a black background.
