There is a particular kind of moss that grows on the north side of the stone wall behind my house. It has been there longer than I have. It asks for nothing, announces nothing, and yet it persists with a determination that puts most human endeavors to shame.

## Observation

We are trained to notice the large and the loud. Mountains, storms, declarations of love. But the world is mostly composed of small things: the grain of wood in a table, the pattern of cracks in old plaster, the way dust moves in a shaft of light.

To see these things is not a talent. It is a practice. You do it by slowing down, by resisting the urge to categorize and move on.

## The Ordinary

A friend once told me that the word "ordinary" shares a root with "order." The ordinary is not the dull or the unimportant. It is the underlying pattern, the order beneath the surface of things.

A leaf falls. A door closes softly. Rain gathers on a windowsill. These are not events waiting to become metaphors. They are complete in themselves.

## Accumulation

Small things accumulate. A daily walk becomes, over years, a deep knowledge of a place. A brief note written each morning becomes, eventually, a record of a life. We overestimate the power of single dramatic gestures and underestimate the quiet force of repetition.

The moss on the wall does not grow in leaps. It grows in increments so small they are invisible to the eye. And yet, given time, it covers the stone entirely.
