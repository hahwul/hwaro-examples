## The Components

A down cushion is filled with a mixture of two distinct materials: down clusters and feathers. Down clusters are the soft, three-dimensional plumage that grows beneath the contour feathers of a waterfowl. They have no central shaft, expanding from a small attachment point into a roughly spherical structure of fine filaments. Feathers, by contrast, have a central shaft — the rachis — with paired barbs forming a flat plane on either side. The two materials behave very differently under compression and recovery.

Down provides loft. A single high-quality down cluster can occupy an apparent volume of one to two cubic centimeters at a mass of only a few milligrams. When compressed, it springs back almost completely as soon as the load is removed. Feathers, being structurally flat, do not provide significant loft, but they do provide weight, internal structure, and a certain amount of resistance to migration within the cushion shell.

## The Ratio

A premium European down cushion is typically filled at a ratio of 80 percent down to 20 percent feathers, by mass. A mid-range cushion is 50/50. A low-grade cushion may contain 90 percent feathers and only 10 percent down. The fill ratio determines three independent characteristics of the cushion: its initial loft, its rate of compression over use, and its long-term recovery after each compression.

A 90/10 down-heavy cushion has the highest initial loft and the most pronounced recovery, but it tends to feel unstructured under direct pressure. The cushion compresses fully under a head or an arm, and the user does not feel a stable supporting layer beneath the soft surface. A 50/50 cushion has a noticeable structural layer — provided by the feathers — beneath the soft outer loft of the down. This is the fill ratio used in most reading and seating cushions, where supportive feel is preferred to maximum softness.

## Migration and Re-Lofting

All down-feather mixtures redistribute over use. Down clusters, being lighter and more compressible, tend to migrate toward the corners of the cushion shell, while feathers settle into the center where direct pressure flattens them. After several months of regular use, an undisturbed cushion will show a characteristic pattern: high corners with concentrated down loft, and a lower, denser central region.

Re-lofting is the periodic process of redistributing the fill. The cushion is shaken vigorously for thirty to sixty seconds, with attention to the corners, until the fill is felt to be evenly distributed within the shell. Re-lofting performed weekly extends the working life of a down cushion significantly. A cushion that is never re-lofted will reach an unrecoverable compressed state in two to three years; a re-lofted cushion of the same fill ratio will retain its loft for ten years or more.

## Shell Selection

The shell that contains the fill must be tightly woven enough to prevent down migration through the fabric. The conventional measure is thread count, but a more useful measure for down containment is the air permeability — typically reported in cubic feet per minute per square foot. A shell with an air permeability below 1 CFM is generally considered down-proof. Higher permeabilities allow individual down filaments to work their way through the weave over time, producing the leakage of fine fluff that is the most common complaint about poorly constructed down cushions.
