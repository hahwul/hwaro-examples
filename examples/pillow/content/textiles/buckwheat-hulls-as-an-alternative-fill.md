## The Material

Buckwheat hulls are the dried outer husks of buckwheat seeds, separated from the groat during milling. They are small, irregular, roughly pyramidal shapes about three to four millimeters across, with a hard but lightweight structure. As a cushion fill, they have been used in East Asian and Eastern European traditions for several centuries — most prominently in Japanese sobakawa pillows, which have been documented in domestic use since the Edo period.

The hulls are agricultural waste from buckwheat flour production. They are inexpensive when bought directly from a mill, and the available supply is large enough that price is not a meaningful constraint on their use. Quality control is the primary concern: hulls intended for cushion fill must be cleaned, sieved to remove flour residue and broken fragments, and ideally heat-treated to kill any insect eggs that may be present.

## Working Properties

A buckwheat hull cushion is heavy. A typical pillow-sized cushion fills with two to three kilograms of hulls, against perhaps 700 grams of down fill in a comparable volume. The weight is part of the working character of the cushion. It does not move under the user; it does not require re-lofting; it does not migrate within the shell.

Under load, the hulls behave as a granular medium. They flow around the shape of the user's head or body, conforming closely to the supported surface, and then lock in place under the static load. The behavior is qualitatively different from down or fiberfill, both of which respond to load by uniform compression. The buckwheat hull cushion conforms; it does not compress.

The conforming behavior is also adjustable. A cushion fitted with a zippered shell allows the user to add or remove hulls to change the firmness. A more heavily filled cushion is firmer and conforms less; a more lightly filled cushion is softer and shifts more readily under load. This adjustability is part of the working tradition of the form: a buckwheat pillow is expected to be tuned by its user over the first several weeks of use.

## Limitations

Buckwheat hulls are not silent. The hulls move against each other when the cushion is shifted, producing a fine rustling sound. Most users habituate to the sound within a few nights of regular use, but it is a real characteristic of the material and not a defect in any specific cushion.

The hulls also produce a small amount of dust over time, as the hard outer surfaces wear against each other. The dust collects at the bottom of the cushion shell and can work its way through the seams. A dense, tightly woven shell — typically a 200 to 250 gram per square meter cotton — is required to contain this dust over the working life of the cushion. Replacement of the hulls is recommended every five to seven years, or sooner if the cushion begins to feel less responsive. Used hulls are compostable.
