## The Problem

A standard plain seam in cushion construction has two raw fabric edges exposed on the inside of the cushion shell. These raw edges fray over time. The fraying produces fine fibers that work their way out through the seam and become visible on the exterior of the cushion. Over a working life of several years, an unfinished seam becomes a source of visible lint, structural weakness, and eventually outright seam failure.

The French seam is one of the standard responses to this problem. It encloses both raw edges within a second seam, so that no raw fabric is exposed to the interior of the cushion. The technique is older than the sewing machine — it appears in late medieval European tailoring as a method for joining fine linen — and it remains the construction of choice for cushion shells where shell longevity is the primary concern.

## The Construction

The two pieces of fabric to be joined are placed wrong sides together. The first seam is sewn at approximately 6 millimeters from the edge, on the right side of the assembled work. The seam allowance is then trimmed to approximately 3 millimeters and the work is turned, so that the two pieces are now right sides together with the raw edges on the inside.

A second seam is then sewn, at approximately 8 millimeters from the folded edge. This second seam encloses the trimmed raw edges within a tubular casing of fabric. The finished seam, when viewed from the inside of the cushion, presents a clean folded edge with no exposed fiber. The seam is structurally stronger than a plain seam of comparable thread weight because it carries the load on two parallel rows of stitching rather than one.

## Fabric Selection

The French seam is best suited to lightweight to medium-weight cotton, linen, and cotton-linen blends. The technique requires that the fabric fold cleanly and lie flat under the second seam. Heavy upholstery-weight fabrics — those above approximately 280 grams per square meter — produce a French seam with a visible ridge on the outside of the cushion, which is structurally sound but visually unsuitable for most applications.

For cushion shells, a tightly woven cotton at 140 to 180 grams per square meter is the working range. The weave should be plain or close-twill rather than basket or open weave; open weaves do not contain down fill reliably regardless of seam construction.

## Stitch Length and Thread

The first seam, which carries no load after the second seam is complete, is sewn at a stitch length of approximately 2.0 to 2.5 millimeters. The second seam, which carries all working load, is sewn at a slightly shorter stitch length of 1.8 to 2.2 millimeters for additional density.

Thread is matched to the fabric weight. A 50-weight long-staple cotton thread is the conventional choice for cushion-weight cotton. Polyester thread is mechanically stronger but does not age in the same way as the fabric it joins; a polyester-sewn seam will outlast the fabric around it and eventually pull through a softening shell. Cotton on cotton is a self-balancing system: the seam ages at the same rate as the cushion, and structural failure, when it eventually arrives, arrives uniformly across the construction rather than concentrated at the seams.
