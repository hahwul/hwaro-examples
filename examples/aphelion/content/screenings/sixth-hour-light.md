Three cameras, three fixed positions above a fjord village of forty houses, one long exhale of a day. Lehtola's crew set the tripods before dawn — such as dawn is, this far north in December — and did not touch them again until the last usable light gave out, roughly six hours later. Nothing in the frame is staged. The film is the recording, cut down from eighteen hours of footage to the two hours that hold, with no dialogue and no music beyond what the wind and the water supply on their own.

<!-- more -->

What the compression removes is incident, not duration: the ridge in the background is doing all the acting, and it does it in millimeters. A cloud bank thins for four minutes and the whole valley changes color. A woodsmoke column bends at the eleventh shot and never straightens for the rest of the reel. Lehtola has described the editing process as "waiting twice" — once with the cameras, once again at the cutting table, choosing which stretches of near-nothing to keep and which to let the ridge itself argue for.

Programme two follows the opening film's ferry route with something more stationary: a place that does not move, observed by people who did not move either, until the light gave them something worth keeping.

Seating is unreserved after 17:45. The auditorium's single aisle light stays on through the first ten minutes to let eyes adjust before the house goes fully dark.
