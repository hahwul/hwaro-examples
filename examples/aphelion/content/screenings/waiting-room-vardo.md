The smallest ferry terminal on the smallest fjord in the programme, filmed for a full off-season week when the boat runs twice a day and most of the benches stay empty. Fjellstad mounted one camera in the corner of the waiting room, at bench height, and let the terminal's own rhythm — arrivals, the tea machine, the notice board being updated and not updated — write the film's only structure.

<!-- more -->

Ninety-six minutes is the compressed cut; the uncompressed footage runs closer to sixty hours, and Fjellstad has said the edit kept almost every stretch where somebody was visibly waiting rather than passing through. The result plays like a study of a very specific posture: shoulders down, eyes on the door, a bag held rather than set on the floor. Regulars start to register as characters purely through repetition — the man who always takes the middle bench, the two women who arrive together and sit apart, the terminal worker who checks the same clock at the same intervals without once being shown checking a watch.

It is the quietest film in this year's lineup and, programmed after the wide compressed geography of *Sixth Hour Light*, the most interior. Where that film watches a fjord think, this one watches a room hold twelve people in place until a boat finally lets them go.

Because the terminal setting includes long stretches without any camera movement, latecomers may be seated at any natural pause — check with an usher at the auditorium door.
