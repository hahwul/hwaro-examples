Ingrid Vasstrand boards the coastal ferry at Bergen with three fixed cameras and no crew, and does not cut again until Kirkenes. The theatrical version compresses eleven days of sailing into a hundred and seventy-eight minutes, but nothing on screen is sped up — entire stretches of open water pass at the pace they actually passed, light shifting a few degrees at a time, until a headland or a harbor light arrives to break the held frame.

<!-- more -->

There is a version of this film that plays as endurance. Aphelion asks you to hear it instead as a tuning exercise: the eye recalibrates to the boat's slowness within the first twenty minutes, and after that the smallest event — a gull crossing the frame, a second ferry passing in the opposite lane, the sun finally clearing a ridge at what the timecode insists is midday — reads as incident. Vasstrand has said in interview that she considers the film's true subject not the coastline but the viewer's patience, filmed indirectly.

We open the festival with it because it states the terms plainly. If Aphelion's other four films ask you to wait for something — a light change, an arrival, a shift in weather — this one teaches you how, in real time, at full length, before anything else is asked of you.
