Aphelion closes where it opened: aboard the coastal ferry, with Ingrid Vasstrand behind the same three cameras, sailing the same route from Kirkenes back to Bergen. This is not a re-edit of *[The Hurtigruten Hours](/screenings/the-hurtigruten-hours/)* — it is a second, independent voyage, filmed the following week, in dusk light that runs the other direction across the same headlands. Vasstrand has called the pair "one trip with a hinge in the middle," and the festival plays the hinge four days apart, the way a viewer would actually feel it.

<!-- more -->

At two hundred and ten minutes it is the longest film in the lineup, and the only one with a scheduled interval — a genuine one, fifteen minutes, timed to a harbor stop roughly halfway through the crossing where the original vessel takes on cargo. Vasstrand insisted on keeping the stop in the cut rather than trimming toward it; she has said the film's whole argument is that a return trip is not the outbound trip reversed, and the interval is where that becomes plain, in real time, to an audience that has now spent four days learning to sit with duration rather than against it.

We close the festival here on purpose. If *The Hurtigruten Hours* taught the room how to wait, *The Long Return* asks what waiting adds up to once the light finally starts coming back — which, at these latitudes in the first week of February, it barely has, and only just.

Doors close at 20:00 sharp. The interval is the only re-entry point; there is no late seating after the second half begins.
