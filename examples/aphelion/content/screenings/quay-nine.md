Rasmussen's rule was simple to state and brutal to execute: place a fixed camera on each of nine small quays along one fjord system, roll all nine simultaneously for a full sailing day, and edit so that the film cuts to a new quay only in the instant a ferry actually touches it. Everything between arrivals — and on the smaller quays, that can run past twenty unbroken minutes — stays on screen exactly as filmed.

<!-- more -->

The effect is a kind of enforced patience with a clock built into it. You always know, eventually, that the shot will end, because a boat is always coming; you just do not know when, since the nine quays keep different, unannounced schedules. Regular festival-goers have started calling it "the film that teaches you to read a horizon for a wake," and it is the one screening this year where the audience's collective, unscripted gasp — when a ferry finally noses into frame — has become part of the room's rhythm.

Rasmussen shot on 35mm specifically for the grain: at nine static, half-lit quays, grain is the only thing moving in long stretches of the frame, and it keeps the image from ever reading as a photograph. Programme four plays the 35mm print rather than a digital scan for exactly that reason.

Running time includes a single interval after the fifth quay's arrival, timed to the print's reel change rather than to any dramaturgical need for a break. The [venue page](/venue/) has the projection booth's full print-and-DCP schedule if you're curious which of this year's five films run on film.
