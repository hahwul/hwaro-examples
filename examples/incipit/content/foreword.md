Every city keeps two ledgers: one of what it built, and one of what it quietly agreed to stop mentioning. This book is about the second ledger — the one nobody signs, the one that gets balanced at night by paving crews and rezoning boards and the ordinary forgetting that comes with a new generation not asking where the old market went.

## Why Cities, Not Buildings

A single building can be preserved by decree. A plaque goes up, a facade gets a grant, and the argument is more or less settled. A city cannot be preserved that way, because a city is not an object — it is a set of habits wearing the shape of streets. You cannot landmark a habit. You can only landmark what the habit left behind, after the habit itself has already moved on to somewhere the rent is lower.

That is the premise of the seven essays that follow. Each one takes a single city and asks what happened to a specific piece of civic memory — a market, a demolished quarter, a rebuilt grid, a buried stream — and tries to describe the mechanism of forgetting in enough detail that it stops looking accidental. Cities do not forget the way people forget. People forget by attrition. Cities forget by decision, even when the decision is never written down as one.

## How to Read This Book

The essays run in a deliberate sequence, and I would ask you to read them that way at least once, with the arrows at the foot of each page rather than the table of contents. Istanbul's market resists the argument that erasure is inevitable; Seoul's buried stream, read last, complicates it back. Between them sit five cities where the outcome was less clean than either.

Each essay opens on a faint fragment of its city's own street plan, drawn from memory rather than survey, which is a way of admitting up front that this book is not cartography. It is closer to what a resident would sketch on a napkin, years later, trying to explain to someone younger why a street they have never used still has the wrong name.

If you are looking for a specific city again later, there is a search page in the header. I built the book to be read start to finish, but I did not build it to be searched inconveniently.
