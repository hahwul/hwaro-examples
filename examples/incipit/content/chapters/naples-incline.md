Naples does not so much have a street plan as several, filed one on top of the next like a badly kept archive — a Greek harbor town under a Roman one, a Spanish garrison grid laid over both, and above all of it a nineteenth-century funicular system that was meant to stitch the layers back together and instead became, itself, a record of which parts of the city the twentieth century decided it could do without.

<!-- more -->

## Four Lines, Then Fewer

The funicular network once ran four separate lines up the volcanic hillside, each serving a different quarter with its own rhythm of departures, its own regulars, its own small argument about which line was faster at which hour. One line has been closed for stretches longer than it has run, its upper station repurposed, its lower station used mostly as a landmark for giving directions to a restaurant that has also since closed. Nobody voted to lose it. It simply stopped being maintained past the point where restarting it was cheaper than pretending it might reopen next year, and the neighborhood above it quietly adjusted its sense of how connected it was to everywhere else.

## What Is Actually Underneath

Below the visible funicular grid sits a second network that most residents can point toward but few can enter without a guide: cisterns, quarry tunnels, and a scattering of Greco-Roman passages that once moved water and now mostly move tourists on scheduled visits. The old streets down there do not match the streets up here — a fact residents absorb early and stop finding remarkable, the way you stop noticing that your own basement is not laid out like your living room. The mismatch is the point. Naples was never planned as one city with a single foundation. It was planned four or five times, at different depths, by people who did not particularly consult the plan above or below them, and what survives on the surface is only the layer that happened to still be useful the last time anyone checked.

The funicular that still runs advertises itself, faintly, as scenic. It is more accurate to call it survivorship: the one layer of the plan that nobody has yet found a cheaper way to replace.
