When the reconstruction of downtown Beirut began in earnest after 1990, the excavators found two kinds of ruin in the same ground and treated them as though they belonged to entirely different categories of loss: the Roman and Phoenician remains beneath the street, catalogued, lit, and in places built directly into the new plazas as display cases for the public, and the shelled, sniper-scarred buildings above the street, which were mostly cleared, because nobody had yet decided how to explain rubble from a war people were still related to.

<!-- more -->

## Two Kinds of Ruin

The ancient ruins were safe to love because nobody currently alive had lost anyone in the events that produced them. Two thousand years of distance turns a ruin into an exhibit; it strips the specific grief out and leaves the shape, which is easier to admire than to feel. The war ruins offered no such distance. A shattered nineteen-eighties apartment block was, to someone crossing the plaza, not an artifact but a former address — possibly a former neighbor's address — and the reconstruction company's solution, largely, was to remove the second kind of ruin faster than the first kind could be fully excavated, so that visitors would encounter the tolerable ruin before they had occasion to ask what had stood in its place a decade earlier.

## The Grid That Got a Vote

What returned to downtown Beirut afterward was a street grid that resembled the pre-war one closely enough to be called a restoration, and differently enough that longtime residents describe it as a stage set built over the correct footprint — wider, cleaner, priced for a different city than the one that had lived there. The old souks, dense and mixed-use, came back as a single low-rise retail district with none of their former residential floors above. The plan remembered the streets. It did not particularly try to remember who had walked them, and the district's slow repopulation since has mostly been by people arriving from elsewhere in the city, who know the grid only as it is now — accurate in outline, and largely silent on everything the outline used to hold.
