The Grand Bazaar has burned badly enough, and often enough, that its clearest historical record is a list of fires. It has rebuilt itself after most of them within the same footprint, at the same widths, selling more or less the same categories of goods from more or less the same corners, which makes the fire list also, in a roundabout way, a list of the market declining every opportunity it was given to become something more sensible.

<!-- more -->

## A Plan for Not Having a Plan

Every proposal to rationalize the bazaar's covered lanes — to widen them for fire trucks, to zone the trades into cleaner districts, to run a single wide axis through the middle for circulation — has run into the same objection from the shopkeepers, which is that the crookedness is the inventory. A jeweler on a bent lane fifty meters from another jeweler is not inefficiency; it is the whole reason a buyer walks the fifty meters comparing prices instead of driving to one shop and taking whatever is offered. Straighten the lane and you have not modernized the market. You have made it into a hallway with shops in it, and a hallway does not generate gossip, and a market without gossip is a mall.

## What Survives a Fire

What actually gets rebuilt after each fire is not the timber, which nobody expects to survive, but the width of the lane and the sequence of trades along it — the same order block by block, generation after generation, so that a merchant's grandson can reopen on the ashes of his grandfather's stall without needing a map. That sequence is the part of the bazaar that is genuinely old, older by far than any door or dome you can put a date on. Fire has taken the roof more than once. It has never once taken the order of the streets, because the order of the streets was never made of anything flammable. It was made of habit, passed shopkeeper to apprentice, and habit, it turns out, survives better than cedar.

The bazaar's own maps, sold at the entrances to disoriented tourists, still show it as symmetric, gridded, legible — a diagram of a market that has never existed. The real one only makes sense from inside a single lane, looking two turns ahead and no further, which may be the most honest kind of city plan there is: one that admits, on its face, that nobody has ever seen the whole of it at once.
