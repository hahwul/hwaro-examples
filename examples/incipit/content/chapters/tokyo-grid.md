The instinct after a catastrophic fire is nearly always the same, wherever it happens: widen the roads so the next fire has less to jump across, and straighten them so an engine does not have to negotiate a bend built for foot traffic and ox carts. Tokyo did this twice in twenty-two years, first after the 1923 earthquake and again after the 1945 raids, and each time the replanning treated the previous street pattern less as heritage than as a fire hazard with good publicity.

<!-- more -->

## Two Fires, One Grid

The Edo-period lanes the fires replaced had never been laid out by any single authority — they had accreted, plot by plot, around canals and temple approaches and the boundaries of feudal estates that had stopped meaning anything decades before the first fire — and a grid, however blunt, was easier to defend to a grieving city than a promise to rebuild something nobody could fully explain the logic of in the first place. Wide, straight, and legible, the new streets asked nothing of anyone who had not already lost their bearings along with everything else.

## The Blocks That Refused

A small number of neighborhoods dodged both campaigns, usually because they burned incompletely or because postwar rebuilding money ran out before the surveyors reached them, and those blocks still run on something closer to the Edo pattern: lanes too narrow for two people to pass without turning sideways, dead ends that were never dead ends but property lines nobody bothered to reconcile, a logic legible only if you already know where you are going. Visitors tend to describe these pockets as charming, which is true and also slightly beside the point. They are not charming because they were designed well. They are charming because they are the only remaining evidence that the wide, efficient city around them was ever a choice rather than a default — a single old lane is enough to prove the grid was optional, which is precisely the argument the grid was built to make impossible.
