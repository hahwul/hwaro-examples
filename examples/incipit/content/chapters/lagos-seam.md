Colonial-era Lagos Island still runs on a rectilinear grid platted by British surveyors in the late nineteenth century, and that grid has survived essentially every political transition since partly because it was never asked to defend itself — nobody classified it as informal, so nobody's demolition order ever needed to include it.

<!-- more -->

## A Grid With an Alibi

A few kilometers away, the waterfront settlement of Maroko grew up on reclaimed and unreclaimed swamp through the twentieth century without ever receiving formal land title, which made it legally invisible in exactly the way the colonial grid was legally protected. Both were dense. Both housed working residents going to jobs on Lagos Island every morning. Only one of them had paperwork, and paperwork, in the end, was the entire difference between a neighborhood and a target.

## Three Days in 1990

In July 1990, the state government issued an eviction notice giving Maroko's residents roughly a week, then sent bulldozers in ahead of even that short deadline. Estimates of the displaced run past a quarter of a million people; there was no relocation plan that matched the number of families removed, and many simply moved to the next unrecognized settlement along the lagoon, restarting the same insecure occupancy somewhere the paperwork was equally absent. The cleared land became, over the following two decades, some of the most expensive real estate in West Africa — the neighborhoods now known as Oniru and parts of Lekki, gated, gridded, planned this time from the start.

The new grid there is, by any planning textbook, a better one than what it replaced: wider roads, real drainage, legal titles for every plot. It is also a grid with no memory of what it stands on, because a grid, unlike a resident, cannot be asked what used to be here, and the surveyors who platted it were not required to consult anyone who had lived in Maroko to draw it correctly. The forgetting was not an oversight. It was the plan working as intended.
