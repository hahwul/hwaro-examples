Through the nineteen-sixties and seventies, Seoul covered the Cheonggyecheon — a stream that had run through the center of the city for centuries, by then reduced to an open sewer lined with postwar shanties — first with a road deck and then with a double-decked elevated expressway that carried well over a hundred thousand vehicles a day at its peak.

<!-- more -->

## Paving Over a Stream

The engineering was treated, at the time, as an unambiguous civic win: a polluted eyesore made to disappear beneath a piece of modern infrastructure, the stream's entire watershed rerouted into culverts and, for most residents born after the project, functionally forgotten. Maps stopped drawing it. Most people crossing the interchange every day had no reason to know there was ever a waterway underneath their commute, and for forty years that was simply what the center of Seoul was — an overpass, not a valley.

## Digging It Back Up

In the early two-thousands, over the objections of shop owners along the elevated road and traffic engineers who predicted gridlock, the city tore the expressway down and daylighted roughly six kilometers of the stream, rebuilding it as a stepped, landscaped waterway well below street level, with the old road grid still running along both banks above. The predicted gridlock mostly did not arrive — traffic redistributed onto surrounding streets and public transit absorbed the rest — and the restored stream became, almost immediately, one of the most heavily used public spaces in the city, walked by people who very often have no memory of the highway it replaced, let alone the sewer before that, let alone the working stream before that.

What makes Cheonggyecheon the right place to end this book is that it forgot in both directions. The city forgot the stream for forty years under a road. It has now, with equal thoroughness, mostly forgotten the road. Each forgetting felt, at the time it happened, like the responsible decision for a city that could not survive its own bad memories. Read back to back, they suggest that a city does not really choose to remember or forget — it only ever chooses which version of itself is easier to build on top of this year, and calls that decision progress.
