The judge given the job of replatting Detroit after its 1805 fire had almost nothing to work with — a few hundred residents, a compact French-era grid gone to ash — and drew, instead of a modest replacement, a plan of grand diagonal boulevards radiating from public circles, sized for a metropolis that would not exist for another century.

<!-- more -->

## A Plan Sized for a City Not Yet There

For decades the plan looked absurd: eighty-foot avenues running past farmland, circles with nothing around them to circle. Then the city arrived, almost exactly on schedule, and the oversized plan turned out to be the reason Detroit could carry the traffic of an automobile industry that had not been invented when the streets were drawn. Width, it turned out, ages better than almost any other planning decision — you can always narrow a road that proves too wide, but you cannot widen one that proves too narrow without demolishing whatever grew up beside it in the meantime.

## What the Boulevards Outlasted

The same overbuilt width that absorbed a boom has, since the city's population fell by more than half from its mid-century peak, absorbed the collapse just as calmly. Poletown, a dense Polish and Black working-class neighborhood near the boulevards' eastern reach, was cleared whole in the early nineteen-eighties — houses, two churches, a hospital — to make room for a single automobile plant, over resident objections that reached the state supreme court and lost. The plant still runs. The neighborhood's street names mostly do not survive on current maps, but a few of the wide connecting boulevards that once served it do, running for blocks now past cleared lots and community gardens planted in the gaps, still eighty feet wide, still built for a traffic volume that has not existed on that stretch in forty years. The grid remembers a city; it does not require one to keep functioning as a grid.
