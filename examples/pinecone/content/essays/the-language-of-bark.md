Walk through any forest and you are surrounded by surfaces that most people never read. Bark is not merely a protective shell. It is a biography written in texture, color, and pattern -- a record that spans decades or centuries.

## Patterns of Protection

Bark forms from the cork cambium, a thin generative layer just beneath the surface. As the tree grows outward, older bark cells are pushed to the exterior, where they die and harden. The result is a crust that shields the living tissue beneath from cold, heat, insects, disease, and fire.

But bark is not uniform. Each species has developed its own strategy, shaped by the pressures of its particular habitat. The paper birch peels in thin, papery sheets -- a structure that reflects sunlight and resists overheating in exposed northern clearings. The ponderosa pine, adapted to fire-prone landscapes, builds thick, plated armor that can withstand low-intensity ground fires. The bark of a mature ponderosa may be three inches thick.

## Reading the Record

When you look closely, bark reveals its encounters. Fire scars appear as darkened, triangular wounds at the base of a trunk, where heat pooled and killed the cambium on one side. The tree survived, but the record persists for the rest of its life.

> A tree that has survived fire is not unmarked by it. The scar tissue curls inward year by year, slowly walling off the wound, but the geometry of the injury remains legible for centuries.

Growth rate is visible too. In years of abundant rainfall, the trunk expands rapidly, and the bark develops deep furrows as it stretches. In drought years, the bark compresses, tight and hard. An experienced forester can estimate rainfall history by running a hand along the trunk of a Douglas fir.

## The Texture Catalog

Some bark patterns are so distinctive they serve as field identification at a distance:

- **Shagbark hickory** -- long, loose strips that curl away from the trunk
- **Sycamore** -- a mosaic of cream, olive, and brown where old bark flakes away to reveal new growth beneath
- **Scots pine** -- flaky orange plates in the upper crown, gray-brown fissures below
- **Black cherry** -- smooth and dark when young, developing distinctive horizontal lenticels with age, eventually breaking into rough, burnt-looking scales

Each pattern reflects a different engineering solution to the same set of problems: how to grow, how to breathe, how to resist.

## Lenticels and Breathing

Trees do not have lungs, but they do exchange gases through their bark. Lenticels are small pores -- sometimes visible as horizontal dashes on birch or cherry -- that allow oxygen and carbon dioxide to pass through the otherwise impermeable cork layer.

In winter, when the leaves are gone, gas exchange through lenticels becomes the tree's primary respiratory pathway. The next time you see those small, corky dots on a young branch, you are looking at the tree breathing.

## The Oldest Pages

Some of the oldest living trees on earth -- bristlecone pines in the White Mountains of California -- carry bark that has been weathering for thousands of years. Their trunks are sculpted by wind and ice into forms that look more geological than botanical. The living bark is often confined to a narrow strip, a single lifeline running from root to crown.

These trees remind us that bark is not static. It is a process, a slow conversation between the organism and its environment that unfolds across time scales we rarely have the patience to observe.

The next time you pass a tree, stop. Put your hand on the trunk. Feel the ridges, the plates, the furrows. You are touching a document that has been in continuous production for decades. It is worth reading.
