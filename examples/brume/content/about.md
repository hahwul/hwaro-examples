Brume is the journal of a nine-by-four-metre courtyard behind a former printworks, walled on three sides in soot-dark brick, floored in clay, and shaded so thoroughly that the sun touches the ground for about forty minutes a year, in late June, at an angle of apology.

For two years I tried to grow flowers in it. The courtyard declined. Then a winter of reading about Japanese temple gardens and one long look at what was already volunteering in the drain shadow rearranged the question: this is not a bad garden for plants, it is an excellent garden for bryophytes. Everything since has been cooperation rather than struggle.

## What this journal is

Each moss that has settled here gets a species card on [the wall](@/mosses/_index.md): its growth habit, its appetite for shade and water, the substrate it finally accepted, and an honest account of the failures on the way — the drowned pincushion, the smoothie slurry, the unpinned carpet the blackbirds redistributed. The cards are cultivation notes first and admiration second, though the admiration keeps leaking in.

The techniques are ordinary and cheap: spore and shoot slurries in jam jars, sheet transplants pinned with snapped skewers, plugs cut with an apple corer, stone dressed with a millimetre of sieved soil. No chemicals, no imported specimens, nothing lifted without permission. Mosses are slow; taking them from the wild carelessly is the one way to be fast about ruining something.

## The name

*Brume* is the French word for the low mist that settles in hollows and stays past breakfast. It is the courtyard's best weather — the mornings the whole wall beads over and the greens go saturated and near-luminous — and it seemed right that a journal about a garden of damp corners should be named after the dampest thing that visits.

The journal updates when something roots, browns, recovers, or teaches me a rule. Mosses set the schedule; they are not brisk correspondents.
