The city moss. It grows in pavement cracks, roof tiles, the gap where the gate latch meets brick — anywhere hard, alkaline, and rudely exposed. In a shade garden it takes the one job no other resident will: the bright strip by the gate that gets real afternoon sun.

<!-- more -->

## Why bother cultivating a weed

Because a moss garden with a hard sunny edge has two choices: let the edge go bald, or plant the species that treats sun-baked mortar as a spa. Bryum's tips carry a silvery hyaline sheen — leaf ends without chlorophyll — that reads as frost in July. Against the dark green mass of the courtyard it draws the border like a chalk line.

## The slurry

Bryum colonises on its own, eventually. Painted on as slurry, it colonises where you decide. This is the jar that worked, after two failed batches taught me that more binder is not better:

```text
silver slurry — one jam jar
---------------------------
1 loose handful Bryum, picked clean of grit
350 ml rainwater
1 level tbsp porcelain clay, the binder
blend 10 seconds, no more — torn shoots, not soup
paint into raked-out joints with a 12 mm brush
mist daily for 3 weeks, then leave it alone entirely
```

The two failures, for the record: batch one used tap water in a hard-water district and grew a white crust before it grew moss. Batch two was blended for a full minute into a smoothie, and a smoothie has no shoots left to regrow. Ten seconds. Torn, not pulped.

## Aftercare

None, truly. Bryum shrugs off drought by closing up into silver beads and waits. Watering it in high summer does more harm than the drought does. The entire maintenance calendar is one line: in February, pull out the grass seedlings that think the joints belong to them.
