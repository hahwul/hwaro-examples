The first moss I ever planted on purpose, and still the one guests try to touch. Pincushion grows in pale grey-green domes that look upholstered — a colour so frosted it seems lit from inside, which in this palette of dark greens makes it the garden's quiet lamp.

## The founding patch

The north wall footing was a strip of builder's sand and regret left by the previous owner. It turned out to be nearly ideal: Leucobryum likes acid, likes sharp drainage under its skirts, and likes exactly the light level of a wall base that faces away from the sun — bright enough to read by, never bright enough to warm.

Three cushions came from a licensed woodland clearance sale in February, each the size of a small loaf. Planting was less horticulture than upholstery: scoop a shallow saucer in the sand, line it with a fist of oak-leaf duff, seat the cushion, and press down hard with both palms until it will not rock. A cushion that rocks in the wind never anchors; that single firm press is most of the technique.

## Watering from the side

The mistake that nearly killed cushion one: overhead watering with a can, daily, out of love. The dome held water like a sponge, stayed sodden for a week, and began to brown from the crown outward. Leucobryum needs to dry between drinks — the frosted colour *is* the dry state, and it is healthy.

The correction was to water the ground, not the moss: a slow pour at the cushion's skirt, letting it wick up what it wants. In an ordinary month, rain does the whole job. The browned crown recovered over one autumn and now shows only as a slight dent, the garden's first scar and favourite lesson.

Three years on, the loaves are one continuous bolster half a metre long — the patch every other card on this wall is measured against.
