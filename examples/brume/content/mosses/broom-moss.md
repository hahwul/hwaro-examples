Every leaf sweeps the same way, as if a wind had passed through once and the moss decided to keep it. In the corner the sun never reaches, the swept look reads as motion — the only thing moving back there.

<!-- more -->

## The corner nothing wanted

Behind the water butt is the courtyard's true north: a right angle of brick that has never, in three years of watching, received direct light. Ferns sulked there. Ivy went leggy and left. The corner stayed bare until I stopped trying to plant *into* it and instead furnished it — with a sawn oak round, eighteen centimetres thick, bark still on, already soft at the heartwood.

Dicranum wants exactly this: acid, slowly collapsing wood, deep shade, and air that moves a little. I lifted two fist-sized cushions from a windthrown log on managed forestry land, with permission and a note of the spot, and seated them on the round the way you seat bread dough in a proofing basket — firm, slightly turned, no gaps beneath.

## What it asked for

Almost nothing, which is the point of the species. The cushions took eleven weeks to anchor. I misted them when the top felt like a dry eyebrow, roughly twice a week in autumn, never in the mornings-only rhythm the [sheet moss](@/mosses/sheet-moss.md) gets — Dicranum sulks if it never dries at all. Periodic drought is part of its diet.

The one intervention that mattered: keeping oak leaves off it. A leaf left for a fortnight prints a yellow ghost that takes half a year to grow out. I sweep the cushions with a dry two-inch paintbrush every Sunday, which takes less time than reading this sentence and is the most soothing task the garden offers.

Growth pace: about a knuckle a year, outward, in every direction that stays dark.
