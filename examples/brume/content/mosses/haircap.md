The forest in miniature. Haircap stands up — ten centimetres of it, dark bottle-green, each stem a tiny conifer — and a stand of it around the downspout basin gives the flat mossscape its one piece of vertical architecture.

<!-- more -->

## A moss that behaves like a plant

Polytrichum is the least mosslike moss in the garden. It has internal plumbing — actual conducting tissue — which is why it can afford height, and why it drinks from below like a vascular plant instead of sipping mist like everything else here. That changes the cultivation rules completely: the substrate matters more than the air.

The downspout basin is a shallow saucer of sandy loam that stays saturated from every rain and merely damp between. I soured it with a bucket of collected pine duff worked into the top five centimetres, aiming somewhere below pH 5. Haircap in sweet soil just quietly declines, and takes a year to tell you.

## Plugs, not sheets

You cannot lift haircap as a carpet — the stems connect underground and tear. The transplant unit is the plug: a wine-cork cylinder cut with an apple corer from a donor colony, stems, rhizoids, soil and all. I set forty plugs on a hand's-width grid in June, firmed each one in with two fingers, and topped the gaps with a mulch of the same pine duff.

By September the grid had blurred. By the following June it had closed entirely, and the stand now needs the opposite of encouragement: once a year, in early spring, I edge it back from the [fern moss](@/mosses/fern-moss.md) border with a bread knife, or the taller moss simply shades out the shorter one. A garden of mosses still has politics; they are just slower.

Three of five shade dots, notice — haircap actually likes the basin's hour of morning sun. Full darkness makes it lean and sparse.
