Twice-pinnate, lace-fine, unreasonably elegant for something that grows flat on compacted dirt. Fern moss is the courtyard's ground cover of record: given shade and humidity it knits a carpet that weeds have genuine difficulty entering.

<!-- more -->

## The ugliest ground in the garden

Under the fire escape the soil is builder's clay, packed to pottery by decades of drip-line rain. Nothing rooted in it. But pleurocarps do not root — they lie down on a surface and take it, and a hard damp surface in permanent shadow is close to their natural cliff-bottom brief.

Preparation was one afternoon: scrape the winter's grime and two ambitious dandelions, rake a centimetre of sieved leaf duff over the clay, and tamp it back down with a board. The duff is not soil — it is grip.

## Pinning the sheets

Fern moss transplants as sheets, lifted like pastry and laid the same way. The sheets came from a friend's shaded gravel path where the moss counts as a maintenance problem — one gardener's weed, another's stock plant. Each sheet was laid edge-to-edge with its neighbours and pinned through with bamboo staples: fifteen-centimetre skewers snapped in half and bent to hairpins. Wind and blackbirds will flip an unpinned sheet within a week; pinned, the sheets fuse to the duff in about ten.

Watering, the first summer: a fine-rose can every dry evening, because a transplanted carpet has no anchorage to drink with yet. After that, whatever the fire escape drips is nearly enough — I top it up in August only.

The staples dissolved into the carpet within the year. What remains is a green floor that swallows footstep sound so completely that visitors stop mid-sentence and look down, which is exactly the effect a shade garden is for.
