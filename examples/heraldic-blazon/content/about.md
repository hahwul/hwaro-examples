## Of This Roll of Arms

*Azure, a chevron Or.* Three words, and a herald in any century could draw the shield without ever seeing it. That economy is the whole reason this notebook exists.

**Heraldic Blazon** is a record kept the way a medieval pursuivant might have kept one: shields read aloud, charges named in their proper order, the rule of tincture defended against every well-meaning amateur who wants to paint a silver lion on a golden field. Here you will find the grammar of arms set down plainly — field first, then ordinary, then charges — alongside the history that bound those rules so tightly that they survived the fall of the chivalry that made them.

This is not a fantasy crest generator. The arms discussed here obey the conventions that governed the College of Arms and its Continental cousins: the metals **Or** and **argent**, the colours **gules**, **azure**, **vert**, and **sable**, and the iron logic that keeps them legible across a battlefield.

## Concerning the Herald

I write under the office name **the-herald**, after the messengers and record-keepers who once memorised every shield in a tournament and could recite a man's lineage from the marks of cadency on his arms. By trade I am a student of the medieval record; by inclination I am the sort who corrects emblazonments in museum captions under my breath.

I write about:

- **The blazon as a language** — its word order, its abbreviations, its near-mathematical precision
- **Tinctures and ordinaries** — the flat fields and bold geometric charges that make a shield read at a gallop
- **Cadency and difference** — how a family told its sons apart in paint
- **The discipline of the craft** — why the old rules were rules, and not mere taste

> "Arms are not pictures. They are sentences, written in colour and meant to be read."
