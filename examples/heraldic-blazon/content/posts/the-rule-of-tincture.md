There is exactly one law in heraldry that every herald will defend to the last breath, and it is almost embarrassingly simple. **Do not put metal on metal, nor colour on colour.** That is the rule of tincture, and the entire visual logic of a coat of arms hangs from it.

Break it and you have not committed a crime. You have committed something worse, in a herald's eyes: you have made a shield that cannot be read.

---

## Metals and Colours

Heraldry sorts its tinctures into two classes. The **metals** are light: *Or* (gold) and *argent* (silver or white). The **colours** are dark: *gules* (red), *azure* (blue), *sable* (black), and *vert* (green), with *purpure* (purple) a rarer fifth.

The rule is a contrast rule, stated in heraldic terms:

> Place a metal upon a colour, or a colour upon a metal. Never a metal upon a metal, nor a colour upon a colour.

*Azure, a chevron Or* is sound — gold on blue, light on dark. *Or, a chevron argent* is **false armory** — silver on gold, metal on metal, two pale tinctures with nothing between them.

---

## Why It Exists

The reason is not mysticism. It is visibility.

A coat of arms had one job above all others: to be recognised instantly, at distance, in motion, in poor light, through the dust of a charging line. The only way paint achieves that is through **luminance contrast** — a light shape against a dark ground, or a dark shape against a light one. Put two light tinctures together and the charge dissolves into the field. Put two darks together and it vanishes into shadow.

The medieval eye discovered, centuries before anyone measured it, what modern designers call contrast accessibility. Hue alone is unreliable — a battlefield is no place to distinguish red from green at a hundred paces, and a fair share of men could not tell them apart at any distance. **Light against dark always reads.** The rule of tincture is luminance contrast, codified into law and enforced by professional pride.

---

## Reading a Shield for Legality

You can audit any blazon in seconds. Sort each tincture into *metal* or *colour*, then check that no charge sits on a neighbour of its own class.

```
Argent, a cross gules        ->  metal field, colour charge   OK
Sable, three mullets Or      ->  colour field, metal charges  OK
Gules, a chevron azure       ->  colour on colour             FALSE
Or, a fess argent            ->  metal on metal               FALSE
```

The two failing examples are not ugly by accident. Red on blue muddies; gold on silver glares. Squint at either and the charge swims. Squint at *Argent, a cross gules* and the red cross stays crisp no matter how far you step back. The rule simply formalises what your own eyes confirm.

---

## The Honourable Exceptions

No good rule lacks its escapes, and heraldry names two.

1. **Charges "proper."** A figure painted in its natural colours — a brown stag, a green tree on a hill — is exempt, since nature, not the herald, chose the tinctures.
2. **The arms of Jerusalem.** *Argent, a cross potent Or between four crosslets Or* — gold on silver, a flat violation. The breach is said to be deliberate: arms so sacred they stood **above** the ordinary law, their very irregularity marking them as singular. Heralds call such a coat *puzzle arms*, and they are the exception that proves how seriously the rule was otherwise taken.

---

## The Discipline of Restraint

What makes the rule beautiful is that it is a *constraint*, and constraint is where heraldic design gets its force. With only two metals and four common colours, and a law forbidding half the possible pairings, the herald is pushed toward the bold, flat, high-contrast fields that make arms unmistakable. There is no room for the timid mid-tone, the clever shading, the gentle wash. A field is one flat tincture. A charge is one flat tincture of the opposing class. Nothing blends.

That is why a coat of arms still reads cleanly at the size of a postage stamp or the height of a cathedral window. The rule of tincture is eight centuries old, and it is, quite simply, good design enforced by good law.
