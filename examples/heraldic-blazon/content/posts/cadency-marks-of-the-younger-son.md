A coat of arms belonged to one man at a time. In strict theory, only the head of a family bore the undifferenced arms; everyone else carried a version marked to show exactly where they stood in the line of descent. These small marks are **cadency**, and reading them is reading a family tree painted onto a shield.

The eldest son did not simply inherit. He waited, wearing a mark, until his father died — and only then dropped it.

---

## The Brisure: A Small Mark of Difference

A mark of cadency is called a **brisure** — literally a "breaking" of the plain paternal arms. It is small, placed in chief (toward the top) or over the centre, and it does not change the field or the principal charges. A son's arms are his father's arms, *plus one small mark* announcing his rank among the brothers.

The English system, codified in the late medieval period and still taught today, assigns each of the first nine sons his own brisure:

| Son | Brisure | Blazon term |
|-----|---------|-------------|
| First (eldest) | a label | *a label of three points* |
| Second | a crescent | *a crescent* |
| Third | a mullet | *a mullet* (a star) |
| Fourth | a martlet | *a martlet* (a footless bird) |
| Fifth | an annulet | *an annulet* (a ring) |
| Sixth | a fleur-de-lis | *a fleur-de-lis* |

So if the family bears *Azure, a chevron Or*, the second son bears *Azure, a chevron Or, a crescent for difference*, and the third *Azure, a chevron Or, a mullet for difference*. The chevron never moves. Only the little mark in chief tells the brothers apart.

---

## The Label of the Heir

The most important brisure is the **label** — a narrow horizontal bar with short tabs (points) hanging from it, worn by the **eldest son during his father's lifetime**.

> A label is not a permanent mark. It is a placeholder. When the father dies, the heir removes the label and takes up the plain arms entire.

This is why a label often signals "the next in line" rather than a fixed branch of the family. A label of *three* points marks the eldest son; a label of *five* points, in the older convention, could mark a grandson while the son still lived. The number of points was itself a coordinate in the family tree.

---

## Marks Within Marks

Cadency could recurse, and here the system reveals its quiet genius. A younger son's own younger sons added a second brisure *on top of* the first. The third son of a second son might bear his father's crescent, with a mullet charged upon that crescent — a mark upon a mark, reading "the third son of the second son."

```
Paternal arms:        Or, a lion rampant sable
2nd son:              ... a crescent for difference
3rd son of 2nd son:   ... a crescent, on the crescent a mullet
```

In practice few families pursued cadency to such depths; the shields grew cluttered and the rule of tincture made small marks-upon-marks hard to read. But the logic was sound, and a herald armed with the brisures alone could often reconstruct a man's descent without ever opening a roll of arms.

---

## Why Birth Order Was Painted at All

Land, title, and arms passed by **primogeniture** — to the eldest son, undivided. Cadency was the visible accounting of that law. It answered, at a glance, the questions a medieval society cared about most: *Whose son is this? Where does he stand? Is he the heir, or a younger branch with no claim to the principal estate?*

The marks were therefore never merely decorative. A crescent in chief was a statement of inheritance and exclusion at once — *I am of this house, and I am not its head.* The eldest waited under his label; the younger brothers wore their crescents and mullets and martlets, each a quiet, permanent reminder of the order in which they had been born.

To read cadency is to read a system that turned the cold arithmetic of inheritance into something you could paint, carry into battle, and pass down — a genealogy you could see across a courtyard, written in the smallest marks the shield allowed.
