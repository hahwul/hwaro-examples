A blazon is not a description. It is an instruction. Hand the words *Azure, a chevron Or* to any competent herald, in any century, speaking any European language, and the same shield will appear: a blue field, a gold inverted-V band across it. No picture changes hands. The sentence **is** the picture, compressed into a grammar so disciplined that it travels through time without distortion.

That is the promise of blazon, and learning to read it is learning to hear a shield spoken aloud.

---

## The Field Comes First

Every blazon opens with the **field** — the background tincture that fills the shield before anything is laid upon it. You always name it first, because everything else sits on top of it.

- *Gules* — a red field
- *Azure* — a blue field
- *Sable* — a black field
- *Or* — a gold field (a metal)
- *Argent* — a silver or white field (a metal)

So *Vert* alone is a plain green shield, charged with nothing. The moment you add a word after the field, you are placing something onto that green.

---

## Then the Ordinaries

After the field come the **ordinaries** — the bold geometric bands that are heraldry's oldest and simplest charges. A *chevron* is the inverted V. A *fess* is a horizontal band across the middle. A *pale* is a vertical band. A *bend* runs diagonally from the upper-left.

Their tincture follows their name:

> *Azure, a chevron Or.* Field of blue. Upon it, a gold chevron. Read in order, painted in order.

The word order is not decoration. It is the rendering pipeline. You lay down the field, then the ordinary, then whatever sits on the ordinary — exactly as a painter would, bottom layer first.

---

## Then the Charges, in Order of Importance

Smaller figures — lions, fleurs-de-lis, mullets, crosses — are **charges**. They are named after the ordinaries, and the principal charge is named before lesser ones. Number, then tincture, then any posture:

- *Argent, three mullets gules* — a white field with three red stars
- *Or, a lion rampant sable* — a gold field, a black lion rearing on its hind legs
- *Azure, a fleur-de-lis Or* — the arms of old France in miniature

Posture words tell you how a beast stands. *Rampant* rears up; *passant* walks; *sejant* sits. A herald hears *rampant* and knows the lion's claws are raised before the brush touches the shield.

---

## Position Words and Abbreviations

Heraldry compresses further with a handful of position terms. *In chief* means toward the top; *in base*, toward the bottom; *in fess*, in a horizontal row; *in pale*, in a vertical column. *Dexter* is the bearer's right (your left, as you face the shield); *sinister* is the bearer's left. The shield is described from the knight's point of view, not the viewer's — a small courtesy that trips up every beginner.

A practiced blazon reads almost like a terse markup language:

```
Field:    Azure
Ordinary: a chevron Or
Charges:  three mullets argent, two in chief and one in base
```

Spoken in full, that becomes: *Azure, a chevron Or between three mullets argent.* The word **between** signals that the mullets surround the chevron — two above, one below — without your having to count the positions out loud.

---

## Why the Grammar Holds

The rigor is the point. A coat of arms had to be identified across a tournament field, repainted by a craftsman who had only the words, and recorded in a roll a clerk would copy by candlelight. Ambiguity was not a style choice; it was a failure that could cost a man his identity in the lists.

So the grammar fixed itself: **field, ordinary, charge**, each with number and tincture, each in descending importance. Master that order and you can read a thousand-year-old shield as fluently as a sentence — because that is exactly what it is.

Name the field. Lay the ordinary. Place the charges. The shield assembles itself in the order you speak it.
