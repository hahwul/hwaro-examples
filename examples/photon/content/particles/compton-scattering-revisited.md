## The 1923 Result

Arthur Compton's 1923 paper in *Physical Review* reported a measurement that classical electromagnetic theory could not explain. When monochromatic X-rays of wavelength 0.711 angstroms were scattered off a graphite block, the scattered radiation contained two components. One component had the original wavelength. The other component had a wavelength that depended on the scattering angle, shifted toward longer wavelengths by an amount of order 0.024 angstroms at 90 degrees of scatter.

Classical electromagnetism predicts no wavelength shift in scattering. The incident wave drives the electrons in the target into oscillation; those oscillating electrons re-radiate at the same frequency. The wavelength of the scattered radiation, in this picture, must equal the wavelength of the incident radiation. Compton's data falsified this prediction at the level of the angular dependence of the shift.

## The Photon Picture

The shift admitted a clean kinematic interpretation if the radiation was treated as a beam of particles, each carrying energy E = hν and momentum p = h/λ. In this picture, the scattering event is a two-body collision between a photon and an electron. Conservation of energy and momentum, applied to the collision, predicts a wavelength shift of Δλ = (h/m_e c)(1 − cos θ), where m_e is the electron mass and θ is the scattering angle.

The coefficient h/m_e c has units of length. Its numerical value is 0.0243 angstroms. This is the Compton wavelength of the electron. It sets the natural scale for any process that exchanges momentum between a photon and an electron and is one of the few fundamental lengths in atomic physics that can be derived from non-quantum classical inputs.

## The Modern Treatment

A modern treatment of Compton scattering, in the framework of quantum electrodynamics, computes the same kinematic result and adds an angular dependence to the cross-section: the Klein-Nishina formula. The Klein-Nishina formula reduces, in the low-energy limit, to the classical Thomson cross-section. In the high-energy limit, it falls off as 1/E. This high-energy suppression is the reason a 1 MeV gamma ray penetrates several centimeters of lead while a 100 keV X-ray is stopped within millimeters.

The Klein-Nishina formula is not derivable from classical electromagnetism plus the photon picture alone. It requires the full machinery of relativistic quantum field theory, including the antisymmetrization of the electron field and the explicit treatment of the photon as a gauge boson. The 1923 kinematic result is the entry point; the modern treatment is what allows the cross-section to be computed at any energy.

## Implications

Compton's result, more than the photoelectric effect, is what convinced the working physics community that the photon was a real object. The photoelectric effect could be explained by a semiclassical model in which the electromagnetic field is classical and only the atom is quantized. The Compton shift cannot. The angular dependence of the wavelength shift requires that the scattering object — the photon — carry definite momentum, and that requires a quantized electromagnetic field.
