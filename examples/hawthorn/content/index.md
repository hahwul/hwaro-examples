Hawthorn is a working notebook, not a field guide handed down from an institution. Every entry began as a specimen taken home in a paper bag — never plastic, which sweats and hastens rot — photographed from four angles, sliced for a gill check, and left cap-down on a square of dark card overnight for a spore print. Nothing gets a page until the print has dried and been matched to a colour, because spore print colour is one of the few identification traits that does not lie: cap colour fades, size varies with rain and age, but a mature spore print is close to fixed.

<!-- more -->

Each card below carries two dots in its top corner. The left dot is the actual spore print colour, sampled from the dried print itself rather than guessed from a book plate. The right dot is a simple yes-or-no: filled means the species is considered good eating once positively identified by more than one trait, hollow means admire it and leave it in the ground. That second dot is deliberately blunt. Mushroom edibility has more nuance than two states — some are choice, some are edible only with careful cooking, some are merely disappointing rather than dangerous — and the full picture for each species is written out in its own [entry in the field guide](@/species/_index.md), along with the look-alike most likely to get a careless forager into trouble. The dot is a doorway into that entry, not a verdict to forage by.

If you take one rule from this guide, take this: never eat a wild mushroom on the strength of one photograph, one dot, or one confident stranger. Cross-check cap, gills, stem, spore print, habitat, and season against at least two independent sources, and when a species has a documented deadly look-alike, treat that look-alike as the more likely identification until you have ruled it out on every point, not just the obvious one. The full reasoning behind that rule — and what this guide will and won't do for you — is laid out on the [about page](@/about.md).
