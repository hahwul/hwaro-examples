Hawthorn started as a shared spreadsheet between three foragers who kept finding the same six species in the same square mile of mixed woodland and kept disagreeing, gently, about how to tell two of them apart under a headlamp at dusk. The spreadsheet became a stack of index cards, and the index cards became [this guide](@/species/_index.md), because a laminated card survives a wet October in a way that a phone screen does not.

## What "field-verified" means here

Every entry is written from a specimen that was actually collected, sliced, and printed — not copied from a photograph or a forum post. "Last field-verified" on each page is the date that specimen was logged, not the date the page was last edited for spelling. Cap size, spore print colour, and season are all measured ranges from real specimens across at least two seasons, not textbook averages. Where a range looks suspiciously exact, it is because the underlying notebook has that many entries and no more; woodland does not owe anyone a round number.

## Why the edibility dot is deliberately blunt

The corner badge on every card reduces a genuinely complicated question to a single filled or hollow dot, and that simplification is doing real work: it is the fastest possible signal for "open this page before you touch that," not a substitute for opening the page. Species with a documented deadly look-alike get a dedicated warning section regardless of how the dot reads, because the dot cannot carry a warning that specific. Read the full entry. Then read it again next to a second guide.

## What this guide will not do

Hawthorn will not identify a mushroom from a single photograph sent by a stranger, and it will not resolve a disagreement between two confident foragers standing over the same patch. Spore prints take colour from a controlled surface over hours, not a phone camera under mixed light; habitat and season notes describe one temperate woodland and will not transfer cleanly to a different climate or continent. If a specimen does not match every trait listed for a species — not most, every — the responsible conclusion is that it is not that species, however close it looks.

## Corrections

Corrections are welcome when they come with a second specimen, a spore print, or a citation to a source that itself did fieldwork. Anecdote alone does not move an entry; consistent contradicting evidence does.
