Porcini belong to a different structural family than every other species in this guide: instead of gills, the underside of the cap is a dense sponge of tiny pores, and that single trait rules out most of the dangerous look-alikes a new forager worries about before they've learned anything else. What's left to get wrong is subtler, and worth taking seriously anyway.

<!-- more -->

## Identification

The cap runs 8 to 25 cm across, tan to reddish-brown, smooth and slightly glossy when wet, sitting on a thick, barrel-shaped white to pale brown stem with a fine net-like pattern near the top. The pore surface starts white and ages to yellow-green as the spores mature — check this before cooking, since an old, olive-green pore layer has usually gone soft and is best discarded even if the rest of the specimen looks sound. Flesh is white throughout and does not change colour when cut or bruised, which is the key test against several look-alikes.

## Habitat & Season

Porcini are mycorrhizal with conifers, most reliably pine and spruce in this wood, with occasional finds under oak and beech at the wood's southern boundary. They fruit from late summer through autumn, often a few days after a warm rain following a dry spell.

## Look-alikes

{{ alert(type="warning", body="The bitter bolete (*Tylopilus felleus*) is a close structural match — same size, same barrel-shaped stem, similar cap colour — but its pore surface is pink rather than white or yellow-green, its stem net is darker and coarser, and a small taste on the tip of the tongue (spit it out, don't swallow) reveals an intense, lingering bitterness porcini never has. It is not toxic, only unpalatable, but a single specimen can ruin an entire dish.") }}

More seriously, several boletes with red pores and flesh that bruises blue on cutting are toxic and occasionally dangerous; porcini's white flesh and pale-to-olive pores that do not bruise blue are the reliable exclusion. Any bolete with red or orange pores, or flesh that blues rapidly when cut, should be left alone regardless of how close the rest of the specimen looks.

## In the Kitchen

Porcini dry exceptionally well, and drying concentrates the flavour rather than diminishing it — a small handful of dried slices rehydrated in warm water makes a stock worth building a risotto around. Fresh specimens are best sliced and dried within a day of picking, since the pore layer softens quickly and can turn watery if left whole.
