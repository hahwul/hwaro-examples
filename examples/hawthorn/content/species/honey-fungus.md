Honey fungus is less a single mushroom than a stump event — where one fruiting body appears, dozens usually follow, erupting in tight, overlapping clusters from a root system the fungus has often been quietly decaying for years. It is a genuine tree pathogen as much as a forageable species, and this entry covers both roles, because the second one is why it turns up so reliably in the same three or four spots every autumn.

<!-- more -->

## Identification

Caps run 3 to 12 cm, honey-yellow to tawny brown, with fine dark scales concentrated toward the centre and thinning toward the smoother margin. Gills are cream, running slightly down the stem, and darken with age. The stem carries a persistent, cottony ring — a useful trait, since it rules out several unrelated wood-dwelling mushrooms — and often shows black, shoelace-like rhizomorphs threading through the bark nearby, the fungus's own root-like growth structures. Specimens grow in dense, overlapping clusters directly from wood or buried roots, essentially never singly from open soil.

## Habitat & Season

True to its pathogenic habit, honey fungus fruits at the base of living or recently dead broadleaf trees and on stumps, in tight clusters, through early and late autumn. The same stumps tend to produce a flush in consecutive years as the underlying root system continues to be broken down.

## Look-alikes

{{ alert(type="danger", body="The deadly Galerina (*Galerina marginata*) grows in the same clustered, on-wood habit as honey fungus, is a similar tawny-brown colour, and — critically — contains the same amatoxins responsible for the poisonings described on the death cap entry. It is smaller and more slender than honey fungus, lacks the scaly cap centre, and has a thin, non-cottony ring that can be absent entirely, but the single most reliable distinction is the spore print: honey fungus prints white to pale cream, while Galerina prints rust-brown. Never trust a cluster identification on shape and colour alone — take a spore print from every distinct cluster before eating any of it.") }}

Amatoxins are the same family of toxin covered in detail on the [death cap entry](@/species/death-cap.md); the symptom pattern and the complete absence of a field antidote both apply here too.

Because the two species can fruit within arm's reach of each other on the same rotting stump, cross-contamination in a foraging basket is a real risk, not a theoretical one — keep clusters from different stumps separated until each has been individually confirmed.

## In the Kitchen

Honey fungus must be cooked thoroughly; eaten raw or undercooked it causes significant gastrointestinal upset even when correctly identified. Discard the tougher stems and cook the caps well past the point that feels necessary — a hard simmer or a long sauté, not a quick sear. Many experienced foragers who otherwise rate it highly still treat it as a mushroom for confident, careful pickers only, precisely because of the Galerina risk above.
