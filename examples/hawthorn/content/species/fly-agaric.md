The fly agaric is the mushroom most people can draw without ever having seen one — scarlet cap, white spots, straight out of a storybook. It is also a genuine part of this guide's own woodland, fruiting reliably under the pines each autumn, and worth logging carefully precisely because its familiarity makes people careless about it.

<!-- more -->

## Identification

The cap starts as a red egg-shaped button and expands to 8–20 cm, scarlet to orange-red, studded with white to cream warts that are the torn remains of the universal veil the whole mushroom emerged from — they can wash off in heavy rain, leaving a smoother red cap that catches inexperienced foragers off guard. The gills are white and free, the stem is white with a floppy skirt-like ring, and the base is bulbous with rings of scaly tissue rather than the smooth cup-like volva of the death cap. The spore print is white.

## Habitat & Season

Fly agaric is mycorrhizal with conifers and birch and, unusually among the species in this guide, is genuinely common — in this wood it fruits reliably under the pines at the wood's northern edge every autumn, often in a flush that follows the first proper frost.

## Look-alikes

Fly agaric is distinctive enough that it is rarely mistaken for anything else by an attentive forager; the risk runs the other way. Its dramatic appearance and folkloric reputation as a mild psychoactive has led some foragers to underestimate it as merely recreational.

{{ alert(type="warning", body="Fly agaric contains ibotenic acid and muscimol, which cause a genuinely unpredictable reaction ranging from nausea and disorientation to seizures, and dosage is essentially impossible to judge in the field because toxin concentration varies with the individual mushroom, its age, and how it is prepared. It is not the source of amatoxin poisoning that makes the death cap deadly, but it has caused hospitalisations and, rarely, deaths, and has no place in this guide's edible column regardless of tradition.") }}

## Why It Stays in the Ground

This entry exists to make the fly agaric identifiable at a glance, not to endorse eating it. Where its range overlaps with Caesar's mushroom — a genuinely prized edible Amanita relative — the two are told apart chiefly by the fly agaric's white gills and stem against Caesar's mushroom's yellow-orange gills and stem, a distinction worth knowing even if this wood has never produced a confirmed Caesar's mushroom. Its far deadlier relative, the [death cap](@/species/death-cap.md), shares the same white spore print and the same general genus habit of a skirted stem — one more reason white-spored Amanitas as a group are treated with more caution here than any other genus.
