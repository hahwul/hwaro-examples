title = "X-Height and Readability at Small Sizes"
date = 2024-04-02
+++

X-height — the height of the lowercase x relative to the capital height — is one of the strongest predictors of readability at small sizes. A typeface with an x-height around 70 to 75 percent of cap height generally performs better below 12 pixels than one with a cap-to-x ratio nearer 50 percent.

The reason is mostly arithmetic. The eye uses lowercase shape recognition far more than capital recognition during continuous reading. When the x-height shrinks, the differentiating features of letters such as a, e, s, and o compress into fewer pixels. Anti-aliasing then has less raw signal to work with, and adjacent letters begin to blur into a single horizontal band.

Designers working with low-resolution targets, embedded displays, or print at small point sizes have responded with several adjustments:

- Raised x-heights, as seen in faces like Verdana and Roboto, that prioritize clarity over classical proportion.
- Open apertures on c, e, and a, where the terminal stroke pulls back from the bowl rather than curling toward it.
- Increased ink trapping at junctions, particularly where stems meet bowls in n, h, and m, to prevent dark spots when the type is rendered at coarse sizes.

Trade-offs follow. A tall x-height shortens both ascenders and descenders, which reduces the visual rhythm of a paragraph and can make column setting feel monotonous. Display sizes lose elegance because the lowercase dominates the line. Most type families resolve this by shipping separate optical sizes — text and display cuts — that adjust the x-height by a few percent up or down depending on intended use.
