Stone-cut letterforms differ from inked or printed type in one essential respect: every stroke must be physically removable. The chisel cannot retract material once it is gone, so each cut commits the carver to a final width, depth, and angle. This constraint shaped the proportions of Roman capitals more than any aesthetic preference.

Trajan's Column inscriptions, set in stone around AD 113, remain the reference point for monumental Latin lettering. The letters average roughly 11.5 cm tall at the base and shrink toward the top of the column to compensate for foreshortening. Stroke contrast comes from the angle of the V-cut: thicker strokes are wider at the surface and meet at a deeper point.

Three traits distinguish lapidary glyphs from their later printed descendants:

- Serifs in carved capitals are structural rather than decorative. They terminate the cut cleanly and reduce chipping at the stroke ends.
- Stroke endings are slightly cupped because the chisel rotates as it leaves the stone. This subtle concavity reads as crispness at viewing distance.
- Counters are open and generous. Closed apertures collect dust and shadow, which obscures the form.

Modern revivals such as Adobe Trajan, Carol Twombly's 1989 digital interpretation, preserve the proportions but flatten the V-cut into a uniform outline. Some foundries have begun shipping variable fonts with adjustable cut depth, simulating the play of light across an inscribed surface. Whether that simulation belongs in screen typography is an open question. The original glyphs were never meant to be lit from a single direction.
