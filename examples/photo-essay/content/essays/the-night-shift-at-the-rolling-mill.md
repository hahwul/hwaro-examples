## Access

Access to the mill was negotiated through the union local. The mill manager required a single-page liability waiver, a hard hat, steel-toed boots, and FR-rated outerwear. The union steward required only that the workers themselves had the right to refuse to be photographed, that names would not appear in captions without consent, and that prints would be made available to anyone who appeared in them. Both conditions were accepted in writing before the first frame was made.

The shift began at 11 p.m. The reheat furnace was already running; the mill itself had been operating continuously for sixteen days and would continue for another twelve before scheduled maintenance.

## Light

The light inside a hot strip mill is unlike any other working environment. The ambient illumination — sodium-vapor fixtures hanging from steel trusses thirty meters above the floor — is the dim yellow background. Against that background, the steel itself emits light. A slab of carbon steel exiting the reheat furnace at 1,250 degrees Celsius radiates as a bright orange body, dropping through cherry red and dull red as it passes through the roughing stand and the finishing stands.

This radiance is the photographer's primary light source. The exposure problem is one of dynamic range: the steel itself is approximately fourteen stops brighter than the shadows on the gantry above it. The frames in this essay were made on Tri-X 400 pushed two stops to ISO 1600, with the meter biased toward the highlights to preserve the texture of the cooling slab. The shadows fell where they fell.

## Subject

The essay is not about the steel. The essay is about the rolling-mill operator who stands behind a glass partition twelve meters from the finishing stand and reads the steel as it passes. He is sixty-one years old. He has worked at this mill for thirty-eight years, since before the conversion from open-hearth to basic-oxygen steelmaking. He can hear, through the partition, when a slab is rolling slightly off-center. He cannot explain how. The frames at the center of the essay are of his hands on the controls and his face reflected in the partition glass, lit from below by the orange light of moving steel.

## Sequence

The twelve frames are arranged in shift order: arrival, briefing, mid-shift, the long hour between three and four when the body is at its lowest, and the gradual handover to the morning crew. The final frame is made in the parking lot at 6:14 a.m., after the operator has clocked out. He is walking toward his pickup truck. The light is gray and cold. The mill, behind him and out of frame, is still running.
