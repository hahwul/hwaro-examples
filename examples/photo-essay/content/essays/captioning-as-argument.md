## The Default

In most photographic publication, the caption is a service annotation. It identifies the subject, names the location, and dates the frame. This convention is inherited from newspaper picture desks, where caption space was budgeted by column-inch and the caption's job was to anchor the frame to verifiable fact: who, where, when. The default caption is short, factual, and external to the photograph.

In long-form documentary work, the default is insufficient. A photo essay running across twelve to twenty pages cannot rely on the picture editor to supply context that is no longer adjacent in the reader's experience. The caption becomes a working part of the argument the essay is making.

## The Working Caption

A working caption adds information that the frame itself cannot carry. It identifies relationships that are not visible. It names the labor that is happening offstage. It supplies the temporal context — the shift number, the season, the position within an unbroken industrial process — that the single frame cannot.

A working caption does not explain the frame. Explanation is a different kind of writing and belongs in the essay's introduction or in a back-of-book footnote. The caption operates at a closer distance: it tells the reader what they are looking at, in language that respects the reader's eye.

The working caption is also restrained. It does not editorialize. The argument of the essay is made by the sequence and the choice of subject; the caption supplies the facts that allow the reader to follow that argument. When a caption begins to interpret the frame for the reader, the reader stops looking at the frame.

## Length

A working caption is typically two to four sentences. The first sentence anchors the frame: subject, location, time. The second sentence supplies the procedural context — what is happening just before or just after the moment of exposure. The third and fourth sentences, when present, identify the larger pattern the frame illustrates: a labor practice, a material flow, a recurring event in the working life of the subject.

Longer captions are read as authorial intrusion and shorten the reader's attention to the frame. Shorter captions revert to the newspaper default and waste the long-form opportunity. The working caption sits in the middle: substantial enough to do real work, brief enough to leave the reader's attention on the photograph.

## Editing the Caption

Captions are written last and edited heaviest. The first draft is usually too long and too interpretive. Cuts are made on the principle that the photograph is doing most of the work and the caption is supporting it. By the third or fourth draft, the caption has settled into its final length, and the words remaining in it are the words the photograph cannot supply for itself.
