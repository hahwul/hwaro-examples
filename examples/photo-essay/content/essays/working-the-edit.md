## The Take

The shoot produced 240 exposures across three days of fieldwork at a salt evaporation pond on the southern shore of San Francisco Bay. The light conditions varied: morning fog the first day, flat overcast the second, and a high thin cirrus on the third that produced the kind of veiled sun that flattens contrast without removing it entirely. The negatives were processed in two batches and scanned at full resolution into a working folder.

## First Pass

The first pass through the take is fast and ruthless. Each frame is judged on a single criterion: does it contain anything the photographer can recall having intended? Frames that are obvious mis-exposures, focus failures, or accidental shutter trips are rejected. Frames that are merely uninteresting are kept for the second pass. After the first pass, 240 frames have been reduced to 87.

The first pass is timed. It takes approximately six seconds per frame, or about nine minutes total. Working faster than this produces honest first-impression rejections. Working slower than this introduces second-guessing and starts to function as the second pass without the benefit of distance.

## Second Pass

The second pass happens the following day. The 87 surviving frames are printed as small contact prints — three inches on the long side — and laid out on a large work surface. They are now physical objects rather than screen images. They can be moved, grouped, paired, and rejected in physical space.

The second pass identifies redundancy. When two frames depict the same subject from similar positions, the weaker frame is removed. When a frame is competent but adds nothing to the developing argument of the essay, it is removed. The second pass reduces 87 frames to 31.

## Sequence

The remaining 31 frames are arranged into candidate sequences. A sequence is not a chronological record of the shoot; it is an argument about the subject. Some frames work as openings. Some work as closings. Some are quiet middle frames that allow the eye to rest between the more demanding images. Sequencing is the slowest part of the edit. It takes four working sessions across two weeks.

The final sequence is nine frames. The other 22 frames are filed in a folder labeled "outtakes" and kept for reference. They are not destroyed. Some of them will be useful in other contexts — a single image pulled into a different essay, a frame that becomes interesting after the author has thought about the subject for another year.
