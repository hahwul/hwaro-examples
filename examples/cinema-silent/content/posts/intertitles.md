The intertitle is a discipline. A silent film has perhaps eight to twelve seconds to deliver a piece of dialogue or exposition before the audience grows restless and the music drowns the moment. The screenwriter cannot rely on subtext or layered delivery — every word has to land in still text, on a black background, in a room of strangers who are waiting for the picture to resume.

## What Survived Into Sound Cinema

The Hays-era studios kept many of the writers who had cut their teeth on silents, and the brevity habits stayed. Dialogue in the early sound films runs short by modern standards: eight or nine words a line, never more than a sentence per shot. The actors had been trained to land a beat without needing thirty seconds of room to do it.

A long line in a 1935 picture is a *choice*. A long line in 2024 is often default — the writer is afraid the audience will not catch what they meant. The intertitle generation had no such fear. They knew exactly how few words could do the job, because they had spent a decade fitting dialogue into ten seconds of a black card.
