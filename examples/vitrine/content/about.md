In 1973 the Meridian Metro's engineering department opened a small in-house unit with an unglamorous mandate: make sure a rider who could not read the timetable could still find the stairs. The Systems Graphics Unit — two designers, a draughtsman, and a borrowed photostat camera — spent eleven months producing the *Meridian Metro Graphics Standards Manual*, a black binder of construction sheets that fixed every pictogram to a thirty-two module grid, every stroke to a specified width, and every application to a tested minimum size. The manual shipped to sign shops in March 1974, six weeks before the system's first full line opened to the public.

## What survived

Porcelain enamel plates are durable and unfashionable in roughly equal measure, which is the only reason any of this survives at all. When the authority modernized its signage twice over — once in the early 1990s, once again after the millennium — most of the original plates went to scrap. A handful were pulled by maintenance crews who recognized them as something worth keeping rather than something worth melting, and passed, eventually, into the archive this catalog draws from. Fewer than a dozen construction sheets from the original manual survive alongside them. Where a sheet exists, the plate here is redrawn from its measurements exactly. Where only the object survives, the plate is redrawn from careful photography and cross-checked against the grid module of its neighbors, since the Systems Graphics Unit was disciplined enough that a stroke width in one glyph tells you the stroke width in all of them.

## How this catalog is built

Every pictogram in Vitrine is inline vector art — no scans, no photographs of the physical plates, nothing raster. That is a curatorial choice as much as a technical one: a redrawn line is a claim about what the original line was, and inline SVG makes that claim inspectable rather than hiding it inside a flattened image. Each case entry in the [catalog](@/cases/_index.md) carries the plate number, the surviving designer credit where one could be confirmed, the years the plate is known to have been in service, its physical medium, and a provenance note describing how it reached the archive. Entries are tagged by function and can be searched by title or by the curatorial notes attached to them.
