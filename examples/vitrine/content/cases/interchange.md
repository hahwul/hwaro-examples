Three bars meeting a single filled hub: the plainest mark in the whole program, and the one hung highest, since it had to be read from the far end of a concourse before a rider committed to a direction. It told a rider one thing only — a line change is possible here — and nothing about which lines, which platforms, or which direction, on the theory that anyone who needed more detail was already close enough to read the smaller signage beneath it.

<!-- more -->

Halvorsen's working notes, two pages of which survive in the archive folder for this plate, record that the symbol went through one substantial revision before the manual was finalized. The first draft drew three separate arrows meeting at an open point, implying motion into and out of the hub. Test riders at a mocked-up concourse consistently misread it as a warning symbol, something closer to a hazard mark than an invitation. Filling the hub solid and squaring the three bars into blunt rounded ends fixed the misreading within a single round of testing — a small change, but Halvorsen's notes call it "the only fix we needed to make twice as fast as the first time."

The symbol was designed to generalize: an interchange with two lines and an interchange with four both use the identical three-bar mark, because a rider counting spokes on a pictogram gains nothing a proper line map on the wall behind it couldn't tell them better. This plate, recovered from the Meridian Central pylon where it hung for seventeen years, is one of only two known survivors from the original 1974 print run.
