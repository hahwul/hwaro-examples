Two weighted circles and a curved bridge between them, reduced far enough from an actual handset that it reads correctly even at the small size the manual permitted for amenity marks — a category the Systems Graphics Unit treated as secondary to wayfinding proper, and budgeted a noticeably smaller construction grid allowance for as a result.

<!-- more -->

This is one of the few plates in the archive whose construction sheet survives in full, and it survives almost by accident: the 1994 fire that destroyed most of the Systems Graphics Unit's paper archive started in a filing room two doors from where the telephone sheet was stored, in a separate drawer reserved for amenity symbols rather than wayfinding ones. Everything in the wayfinding drawer was lost. The distinction that saved this one sheet — amenities filed separately from navigation — was itself a small piece of the design office's own thinking made visible.

Public telephones were removed system-wide in 1994, the same year as the fire, in what the archive's surviving correspondence treats as an unrelated but suspiciously well-timed modernization program. This plate, pulled from the Cobble Yard alcove during that removal, is the clearest surviving example of the acrylic light-box construction used for after-dark amenity marks, and the handset silhouette redrawn here follows Wren's construction sheet to the module.
