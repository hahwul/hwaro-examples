An open bracket standing in for a doorway, and a bar breaking diagonally out through the opening: the most legible pictogram in the entire program, by Halvorsen's own account, and also the least frequently needed, since a platform-level exit to the street was a comparatively rare condition confined to a handful of shallow stations built into hillsides.

<!-- more -->

Where most of the system required a rider to pass through a mezzanine and a fare barrier before reaching street level, four stations on the original line let a platform stair rise directly to a street doorway with no intervening concourse. This plate marked exactly that condition, and the manual specified it should never be used for an ordinary station exit — a distinction the archive's surviving correspondence suggests was violated almost immediately, since at least two sign shops installed it at conventional mezzanine exits simply because it read more clearly than the standard exit mark at a distance.

Dray Lane, where this plate hung, was one of the four qualifying stations and kept its platform-level exit until the line was rerouted underground in 1991. The bracket-and-bar plate came down with the rest of the original signage that year; the two other known survivors are in private hands and have not been available for this catalog to examine directly, so this redrawing is the only one checked directly against a surviving physical plate rather than against a photograph of one.
