The dot sits noticeably high and the stem noticeably squared, both deliberate departures from a typeset lowercase "i." Halvorsen's brief to the sign shops was explicit: this mark had to survive being cut from sheet vinyl by hand, at small sizes, by contractors with no typographic training, and a stem with sharp printer's serifs would round itself into an unreadable blob after two or three re-cuttings.

<!-- more -->

It marked a genuinely staffed point, never a passive noticeboard, which is why so few plates of this type were ever produced against the size of the system — thirty-one in total, one per staffed booth, each numbered on the reverse in the sign shop's own hand rather than the manual's. This example, plate seventeen of the original run, backlit an acrylic panel above the Falkbridge booth window for two decades before staffing was consolidated to three central booths systemwide in 1994.

The acrylic light-box construction, rather than porcelain enamel, was reserved for a short list of symbols meant to be legible after dark or in the perpetual dimness of a below-grade concourse: information, telephone, and — briefly, in an unrealized second phase of the manual — a first-aid mark that never made it past a single prototype. The glow this plate once carried is long gone; what remains is the silkscreen layer itself, which is what this redrawing is drawn from.
