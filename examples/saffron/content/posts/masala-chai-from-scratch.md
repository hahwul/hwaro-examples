There is a vast difference between chai made from a teabag and the real thing brewed on a stovetop with whole spices. Authentic masala chai is a ritual as much as a recipe: the cracking of cardamom pods, the sharp fragrance of fresh ginger being grated, and the slow simmer of tea leaves and milk together until the color deepens to a warm copper. Every household in India has its own variation, but the principles remain the same.

Start by crushing two or three green cardamom pods, a small piece of cinnamon stick, two cloves, and a few black peppercorns in a mortar. Grate about an inch of fresh ginger. Bring water to a boil in a small saucepan and add the crushed spices along with the ginger. Let this simmer for two to three minutes so the water draws out the essential oils. Add strong loose-leaf black tea -- Assam CTC works best -- and let it boil for another minute.

Pour in whole milk and bring the mixture to a rolling boil, watching carefully as it tends to rise quickly. Reduce the heat and let it simmer for three to four minutes until the chai takes on a rich, tawny hue. Strain into cups and sweeten with sugar or jaggery to taste. The warmth of the spices should linger on the palate long after the last sip, a gentle heat that is comforting rather than sharp.
