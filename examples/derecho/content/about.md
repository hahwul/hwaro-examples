Derecho is the working log of a single mechanical-keyboard bench that lives in the corner of a spare room, lit by one arm lamp and a strip of warm LEDs behind the monitor. The name comes from the straight-line windstorm that arrives without much warning and leaves the desk rearranged — which is roughly what a new switch order does to a weekend.

I am Wren Halloran, a firmware tester by day and a switch hoarder by every other hour. I started this log because I kept relearning the same lessons: which plate kills ping, how much foam is too much, why a stabilizer still ticks after three rounds of lube. Writing it down turned a pile of half-remembered fixes into something I could search.

## What gets logged

Every entry is one board, start to finish. The header of each build names the three things people actually ask about — the **board**, the **switches**, and the **plate material** — because those three decide most of how a keyboard sounds and feels before you ever touch a keymap.

Then the prose covers the parts that don't fit in a spec line: the mod that worked, the mod that made it worse, the solder joint I had to redo, and an honest note on whether I'd build it that way again.

## House rules

- **Sound is described, never scored.** No "thock out of ten." I tell you what it does and let you decide.
- **Every mod is reversible or noted as not.** Tape mods peel; force-breaking a switch does not.
- **Firmware belongs in the log.** If a board runs QMK or VIA, the relevant keymap snippet goes in the entry so future-me can reflash without archaeology.

## The tools

Nothing exotic: a temperature-controlled iron, a solder sucker that has seen better days, a switch opener, two brushes, and a bottle of thin lube I ration like it is single malt. The bench is small on purpose. A cramped bench keeps the builds honest — you finish one before you can start the next.

If you build along, subscribe to the [builds feed](/builds/rss.xml) and press `Ctrl+K` anywhere on the site to jump straight to a board, switch, or plate.
