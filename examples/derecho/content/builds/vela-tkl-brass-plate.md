The Vela TKL sounded thin. Not bad — thin. The stock aluminum plate and the U4T tactiles together landed in a bright, clacky register that got tiring over a workday. Brass is the classic fix: it is dense, it is dead, and it drops the whole board a few notes into something rounder. I did not want to guess, so I had a plate cut and swapped it head to head.

<!-- more -->

## Getting the plate made

The Vela's designer publishes the plate DXF, so I did not have to draw anything. I sent the file to a local waterjet shop with one note: 1.5 mm C260 brass, deburr both faces. A CNC router would have worked too, but waterjet leaves a cleaner switch cutout and no heat warping on a plate this thin.

When it came back I checked every switch cutout against a loose switch before assembly. Two cutouts on the bottom row were a hair tight — brass does not flex to forgive you the way FR4 does — so I took them to size with a needle file.

| Check | Spec | Measured |
|---|---|---|
| Thickness | 1.5 mm | 1.49 mm |
| Cutout | 14.0 mm | 13.9–14.1 mm |
| Flatness | flat | slight bow, pressed out |

## Filming and fitting the U4T

Boba U4T are tactile with a rounded bump and a poppy stem. I filmed them with 0.15 mm films to tighten the housing wobble and lubed only the springs — you never lube the bump or you kill the very thing you bought them for. On brass, the bump reads a touch softer and the bottom-out is noticeably heavier. That is the plate, not the switch.

## The result

Brass did exactly what brass does. The board dropped in pitch, the ping vanished, and the U4T tactility survived the swap intact. Side by side the aluminum plate now sounds like a demo and the brass like the real thing.

One caveat worth logging: brass adds real weight. The Vela went from comfortable-to-carry to stays-on-the-desk. For a TKL I never move, that is a fair trade. For a travel board it would not be.
