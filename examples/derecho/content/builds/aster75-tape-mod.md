Out of the box the Aster75 rang like a tuning fork. Every keystroke carried a metallic tail — the dreaded case ping, where sound bounces around an empty aluminum shell and comes back to you a half-beat late. The fix is not one thing; it is a stack of cheap interventions applied one at a time so you can hear what each one earns. That discipline is the whole point of this entry.

<!-- more -->

## Test one variable at a time

The temptation is to throw every mod at the board at once and declare victory. You learn nothing that way. I recorded the board at each step with the same phone in the same spot, typing the same sentence, so I could actually compare.

### 1. The tape mod

Three layers of painter's tape on the back of the PCB. It sounds like a joke and it is the single highest-return mod in the hobby. The tape reflects sound back up through the switches instead of letting it rattle in the case, deepening the whole board. Ping dropped by maybe half.

### 2. PE foam between plate and PCB

A 0.5 mm sheet of PE foam, cut to the plate and pierced for each switch. This tightens the knock and removes the last of the hollowness. It also makes the board fractionally harder to disassemble, which is the price.

### 3. Plate foam swap

The Aster ships with a thick, spongy plate foam that muddied everything. I swapped it for a thinner Poron sheet. Cleaner, less dead.

## The MX2A note

The MX2A Reds deserve a mention: Cherry finally fixed the scratch. They are smooth enough out of the bag that I did not lube them, which is a sentence I never expected to write about stock Cherry. On a foamed aluminum case they land in a satisfying, muted register.

If you only ever do one mod to a ringing board, do the tape mod. It costs nothing and it kills most of the ping before you open your foam order.
