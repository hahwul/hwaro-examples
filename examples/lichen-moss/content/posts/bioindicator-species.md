Lichens absorb water and minerals directly from the atmosphere across their entire surface, which makes them unusually sensitive to airborne pollutants. They lack the cuticles and stomata that vascular plants use to regulate gas exchange, so contaminants accumulate in tissue at concentrations that track regional emissions over years rather than days.

Sulfur dioxide is the classic culprit. During the industrial era, much of central Europe lost its fruticose lichens entirely, leaving only a handful of acid-tolerant crustose species like *Lecanora conizaeoides* clinging to soot-blackened bark. As clean-air legislation reduced sulfur emissions through the 1980s and 1990s, surveys recorded a slow recolonization, with bushy *Evernia* and *Usnea* species returning to oaks within forty kilometers of city centers.

### Modern Pollutants

The current concern is reactive nitrogen, mostly from agricultural ammonia and vehicle exhaust. Nitrogen-tolerant species such as *Xanthoria parietina* now dominate roadside trees, while acid-loving species retreat upslope. Conservation biologists use these shifts to map nitrogen deposition at scales that direct measurement instruments cannot match without prohibitive cost.

A standardized survey protocol counts species presence on twelve trees within a defined plot, weighting each species by its known sensitivity. The resulting index correlates closely with hourly nitrogen oxide readings averaged across a full season, and the survey itself takes a trained observer about ninety minutes to complete.
