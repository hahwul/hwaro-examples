Sedimentary Choir is a permanent, unattended piece for Draycott's geology wing — a 40-minute loop with no audible seam, built to sit under the room's ambient hum rather than announce itself. It has run continuously since installation, and most staff on the floor say they stopped consciously hearing it within the first week.

<!-- more -->

## Speaker topology

Nine ceiling-mounted line-array modules cover three exhibit bays, each aimed down at a specific case rather than the room in general — the ammonite wall gets its own channel, the ice-core cabinet gets another. Six contact transducers are epoxied to the undersides of the limestone plinths; visitors leaning on the rail feel a sub-20Hz hum through their forearms that never appears on the line arrays at all.

```
BAY-A  ceiling.line[1-3]    -8.0 dB   LPF 6kHz
BAY-B  ceiling.line[4-6]    -8.0 dB   LPF 6kHz
BAY-C  ceiling.line[7-9]    -8.0 dB   LPF 6kHz
STONE  plinth.contact[1-6]  -14.0 dB  LPF 40Hz   (felt, not heard)
```

## Mix notes

The source material is contact-mic recordings of core samples being cut in the museum's own prep lab, pitched down two to three octaves until the saw's whine becomes a slow granular grain. The 90-second crossfade window at the loop point was tuned by ear over four overnight sessions until no listener in a blind walkthrough could name the seam.

Playback sits six decibels under the wing's HVAC noise floor. The brief was never to be heard, only to change how the room feels once a visitor has stood in it a few minutes.
