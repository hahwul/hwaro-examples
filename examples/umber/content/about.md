Umber is a sound design practice working almost entirely in rooms that were not built for sound: planetarium domes tuned for image and airflow, museum halls tuned for climate control and glass cases. Every project on this site began with a walkthrough — a tape measure, an SPL meter, and a list of everything already fighting for the room's attention before a single cue is written.

## Method

Sound design here starts with topology, not composition. A speaker plot gets drawn before a note is written: how many transducers, where they couple to the structure, what the room already does to frequencies above 8kHz. The score for a piece is written into that shape rather than layered on top of it afterward. Reverb is rarely added — most of these rooms already carry more of it than a studio would ever add on purpose, and calibration is as much about subtracting a room's own resonances as adding new ones.

Each of the [five installations](/installations/) documented on this site carries its finished channel map alongside the mix: not as a boast, but because the topology is usually the most useful thing to hand to the next engineer who has to service the room.

## Practice

Umber is run by Teodor Vansen, formerly a systems technician for touring theater sound before moving into fixed installations in 2021. Recent work spans planetarium dome shows, permanent museum loops, and structure-borne pieces played through the objects they are about rather than through conventional loudspeakers. Commissions run twelve to twenty weeks depending on how much of the room needs measuring before the first draft can start.

## Working with Umber

New commissions typically begin with a site visit and an acoustic survey of the room before any music or sound design work starts — domes, halls, and permanent installations all need their topology mapped first, and the mapping shapes everything that follows. Get in touch through the venue or institution commissioning the piece; every project on this site began that way.
