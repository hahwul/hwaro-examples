There is something deeply calming about the ritual of making tea in the middle of the afternoon, when the world outside is still bright but beginning to soften.

It begins with the kettle. The sound of water coming to life, that low rumble building to a gentle roar. You choose your cup -- not the first one your hand touches, but the one that feels right today. Maybe the wide ceramic one with the pale glaze, or the smaller porcelain cup that holds the heat just so.

## The Waiting

The best part of making tea is the waiting. The leaves need time to open, to release their flavor into the water. You cannot rush this. You should not want to.

While the tea steeps, look out the window. Notice the light. At this hour it comes in at an angle, catching dust motes in the air, warming the table where your cup sits.

## A Simple Practice

This is not about expensive tea or rare leaves or having the right equipment. It is about pausing. About giving yourself permission to do one thing slowly and deliberately in a day that probably asked you to do everything quickly.

> Tea is an act complete in its simplicity. When I drink tea, there is only me and the tea. The rest of the world dissolves.

Pick up your cup. Feel the warmth through the ceramic. Take a sip. That is all there is to it.

## What Tea Teaches Us

If you pay attention, tea teaches patience. It teaches presence. It teaches that the best things are often the simplest, and that warmth, offered without conditions, is one of the kindest gifts.

Make yourself a cup this afternoon. You have earned it, even if you have done nothing at all.
