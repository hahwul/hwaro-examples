The multi-column layout is so fundamental to newspaper design that we rarely pause to consider its origins. Yet the decision to divide a page into vertical columns — rather than setting text in a single wide block — was a revolutionary innovation that transformed how information could be organized and consumed.

The earliest printed newspapers of the seventeenth century borrowed their layout from books: single columns of dense text, with minimal visual hierarchy. It was only as newspapers grew larger and competition intensified that editors began experimenting with multiple columns. The advantages were immediate and obvious.

## Economy and Efficiency

Multiple columns allowed editors to fit more text on a single page, reducing paper costs. But the benefits went beyond mere economy. Narrow columns created shorter line lengths, which made text significantly easier to read. Readers could scan headlines quickly and choose which stories to read in full — a form of user experience design centuries before the term was coined.

## The Golden Age of Layout

By the mid-twentieth century, newspaper layout had become an art form. Editors and compositors worked together to create pages that were both information-dense and visually compelling. The front page of a great broadsheet was a carefully orchestrated composition: a dominant headline, a striking photograph, and a mosaic of subsidiary stories arranged to guide the reader's eye.

The vertical column rule — that thin line separating adjacent columns — served as both a visual divider and a compositional element. It created rhythm and order on the page, preventing the reader's eye from wandering across column boundaries.

## Columns on the Web

CSS `column-count` and `column-gap` have finally made it possible to recreate this layout on the web. While responsive design presents challenges that print never faced, the underlying principle remains the same: narrow columns, clear hierarchy, and the judicious use of white space create a reading experience that respects both the content and the reader.
