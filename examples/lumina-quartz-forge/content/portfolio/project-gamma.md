Project Gamma extends the studio's research into refractive geometry. The work pairs a hand-cut quartz lattice with calibrated low-luminance emitters, allowing each facet to operate as both a passive prism and an active light surface. The build began with a structural model derived from triangular subdivision, then iterated through twelve physical mock-ups before settling on a lattice density of fourteen nodes per linear meter.

Each node was machined from optical-grade silica, then bonded to the carrier frame using a UV-cured adhesive selected for its negligible refractive offset. The bonding pass took eleven hours per panel and required a Class 1000 cleanroom to keep particulate intrusion below the threshold of visible scatter. After bonding, every surface was hand-polished to a quarter-wavelength tolerance.

The lighting layer sits two millimeters behind the lattice. We used edge-coupled diodes operating at 470, 525, and 605 nanometers, each independently driven through a sixteen-bit current controller. By staggering the emitter phase, we produced standing interference patterns that read as static lines from a fixed viewpoint but shift continuously as the observer moves. The effect is closer to a holographic plate than a conventional display.

Calibration consumed the final third of the schedule. Each emitter was mapped against a reference photometer, and the resulting offsets were stored in a per-unit lookup table. The tables ship with the panel and survive transport, so installations can be reproduced without on-site recalibration. Field reports from three early installations confirm photometric drift below two percent over six months of continuous operation, which we consider acceptable for the intended gallery context.

Project Gamma has been documented in the studio archive under series QF-G, with full optical traces and fabrication notes available for licensed reproduction.
