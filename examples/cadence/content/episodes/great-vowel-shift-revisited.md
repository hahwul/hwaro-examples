Between Chaucer and Shakespeare, every long vowel in English moved. *Bite*
stopped rhyming with *beet*, *house* stopped rhyming with *whose*, and no
one alive at the time appears to have remarked on it in writing. In the
first episode of Cadence, we open the show's whole premise with the shift
that gave English its notoriously mismatched spelling.

<!-- more -->

Cadence launches with the sound change every English speaker has already
memorized, whether they know it or not: the reason *sheep* and *step* don't
rhyme with anything you'd guess from the spelling. Historical phonologist
**Dr. Wren Ashdown** joins to walk through the traditional account — a
chain shift where each vowel nudged the next one out of its old acoustic
space — and the newer, messier picture that's replaced it.

## What the old story got right

The chain-shift model, first laid out in the early twentieth century, still
explains the basic mechanics well: /iː/ couldn't get any higher without
turning into a glide, so it diphthongized to /aɪ/, freeing up space that
/eː/ moved into, which freed up space for /ɛː/, and so on down the vowel
chart. That much has held up.

## What the newer story adds

What hasn't held up is the assumption that the shift moved as one
coordinated wave across England. Dialect atlas work from the past two
decades shows London, East Anglia, and the north of England completing
different parts of the shift on different timelines — in some northern
dialects, the shift never fully finished, which is part of why "Scots"
vowels still sound conservative relative to Received Pronunciation on
certain words.

## Why it matters for a show about drift

We're opening with the Great Vowel Shift because it's the clearest proof
that a language can change completely, invisibly, generation by
generation, without any single speaker perceiving anything but their own
accent moving slightly away from their parents'. Every later episode of
this show is really a smaller-scale version of this one.

**In this episode**

- The chain-shift model and its main objection: why didn't the vowels just
  merge instead of pushing each other along?
- Spelling as a fossil record — why English orthography still reflects
  pre-shift pronunciation
- Regional completion times, and the northern dialects where the shift
  stalled
- What a "shift in progress" looks like from the inside, which sets up the
  rest of the season

Further reading: Ashdown's *Chain Shifts and Chart Space* (2019) remains
the most readable single-volume account of the competing models discussed
here.
