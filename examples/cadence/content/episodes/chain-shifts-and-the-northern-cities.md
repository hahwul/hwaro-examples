Opening season two, we leave the Great Vowel Shift's distant past for one
happening within living memory: the Northern Cities Vowel Shift, a chain
reaction moving six vowels around Chicago, Detroit, and Buffalo — and
possibly, per the newest fieldwork, starting to move back.

<!-- more -->

If you want to hear a chain shift while it's still in progress, the Great
Lakes region has been running one since roughly the 1960s. Dialectologist
**Marisol Idowu-Kang** returns to open season two with the Northern Cities
Vowel Shift: a rotation of six vowels first documented by William Labov's
research group and still tracked by fieldworkers today.

## The rotation, briefly

TRAP fronts and raises toward where DRESS used to sit; DRESS backs toward
STRUT's old space; STRUT and LOT shift to fill the gap; and LOT and THOUGHT
move to compensate. The net effect: a Chicago speaker's "bat" can sound
close to a conservative "bet," and their "bus" can drift toward "boss."
None of it registers as strange to the speakers doing it — the whole system
moved together, so the relationships between vowels stayed intact even as
every individual vowel's position changed.

## Push chain or drag chain?

The two competing mechanisms named in episode one — a "push" where a
vowel's arrival forces its neighbor to move, versus a "drag" where a gap
left by a departing vowel pulls the next one in — both get invoked to
explain the Northern Cities pattern, and Idowu-Kang argues the honest
answer is that both mechanisms are visible at different points in the
chain, which is itself evidence against treating either as a universal law
of sound change.

## Is it reversing?

The most striking recent finding: apparent-time studies comparing speakers
born in the 1990s to those born in the 1950s show the shift losing ground
among younger urban speakers, particularly in Chicago, while holding
steadier in smaller cities along the chain. Whether that's genuine reversal
or a shift toward a new, unrelated regional norm is still an open dispute
Idowu-Kang expects to occupy fieldworkers for another decade.
