We take the "apparent-time" method — comparing speakers of different ages
to infer change over time — down to the smallest possible scale: one
extended family, three generations, recorded across fifteen years of home
video and new interviews.

<!-- more -->

Most of what we know about sound change in progress comes from comparing
strangers of different ages across a city. This episode narrows the lens to
one family: the Okonkwo-Beaulieu household, whose home movies going back to
the 1990s gave fieldworker **Dr. Priya Ashworth** an unusually intimate
apparent-time dataset — three generations, recorded on the same porch,
twenty-some years apart.

## The merger that passed down

The cot–caught merger — where *cot* and *caught* become homophones — shows
up fully in the grandparent's speech, partially in the parent's, and
completely in the grandchildren's, tracking almost exactly the merger's
documented spread through the region over that period. Within one family,
you can watch a regional change complete itself across three sittings at
the same table.

## The merger that didn't

Not everything transmits. The grandmother's distinctive PIN–PEN
near-merger before nasal consonants — common in her home region — is
audible in her child's speech only in careful, self-monitored moments, and
essentially absent in the grandchildren's casual speech. Ashworth's
explanation: features tied to a *marked*, regionally recognizable accent
are more likely to be leveled out by a generation raised amid more dialect
mixing, even within the same household.

## Why one family is useful data

A single family can't establish a trend — but it can falsify assumptions
that population-level studies smooth over. The Okonkwo-Beaulieu recordings
show change isn't just something that happens "out there" in a city's
aggregate accent; it happens, unevenly, feature by feature, at the kitchen
table, and even siblings raised in the same house can end up on different
sides of a merger.
