Vocal fry gets blamed on podcasts, uptalk gets blamed on Valley Girls, and
both, it turns out, have been recorded and complained about since long
before either of those things existed. This episode traces two features of
contemporary American speech back further than the panic around them
usually goes.

<!-- more -->

Every generation seems to invent a new way to worry about how young women
talk. Sociolinguist **Dr. Priya Ashworth** brings the data on two features
currently getting that treatment — vocal fry (the low, creaky voice
register at the end of phrases) and uptalk (rising intonation on
statements) — and traces both back through the historical record further
than most listeners expect.

## Vocal fry is not new, and not a flaw

Creaky phonation shows up in recordings of American speech from the 1960s,
well before it had a name anyone complained about. What changed isn't the
sound — it's who gets noticed using it, and how it gets read. Ashworth
walks through acoustic studies showing fry distributed roughly evenly
across genders in casual speech, with the perception gap doing most of the
cultural work.

## Uptalk has a real function

Rising intonation on declaratives isn't confusion or insecurity — in
recorded conversation, it consistently correlates with checking that a
listener is following, especially when introducing new information. It's
older in Australian and New Zealand English than in American English,
which is itself a clue that this is ordinary dialect contact and diffusion,
not a recent invention.

## The pattern behind the pattern

What connects fry and uptalk to every other feature on this list — vowel
shifts, case loss, borrowed sounds — is that innovation in casual speech
overwhelmingly starts with younger speakers and gets stigmatized while it's
associated with them, then either fades or becomes invisible as it spreads.
The stigma is the tell that a change is actually happening.
