*Whom* is the last visible piece of a case system English mostly gave up a
thousand years ago, and it's still losing ground. Closing out the season,
we ask what actually happens when a grammatical form dies — and why the
language keeps working fine without it.

<!-- more -->

Old English marked nouns and pronouns for four cases; modern English kept
almost none of it, except in the pronoun system, where *whom* is the last
holdout still visibly losing a fight it's been losing since roughly 1900.
Grammaticalization researcher **Tobias Reyes-Lindqvist** joins to explain
why this kind of loss is orderly, predictable, and not actually a sign that
anything is breaking.

## Case marking, briefly

English pronouns still distinguish subject and object forms — *I* versus
*me*, *he* versus *him* — but *who*/*whom* is the one pair actively
collapsing toward the subject form in casual speech, appearing almost
exclusively in formal writing and fixed phrases like "to whom it may
concern." Reyes-Lindqvist traces the timeline through corpus data: *whom*
was still the expected form in casual nineteenth-century letters and has
receded steadily since.

## Loss is not breakage

The instinct is to hear this as decay, but a grammatical form disappearing
because context already carries its information isn't decay — it's
redundancy being trimmed. Word order in English already marks who did what
to whom (no pun avoidable); the case-marked pronoun became optional
information a century before it became rare.

## What replaces a lost distinction

When a marked form goes, the language usually doesn't lose the meaning —
it relocates the work. Intonation, word order, and default assumptions
about agency pick up the slack that *whom* used to carry explicitly. This
is the same mechanism, Reyes-Lindqvist notes, that let English drop most of
its case system a millennium ago without losing the ability to say who did
what.
