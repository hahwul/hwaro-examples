Every borrowed word arrives with sounds its new language may not have, and
every language has ways of filing those sounds down to fit. This episode
follows loanwords across two generations of the same bilingual households
to see whose pronunciation wins.

<!-- more -->

When English borrowed *café* from French, it kept the accent mark and lost
the uvular /ʁ/. When it borrowed *taco* from Spanish, it kept a plain
English /t/ instead of the unaspirated one Spanish actually uses. Contact
linguist **Tobias Reyes-Lindqvist** joins for the season finale to trace
what gets kept, what gets sanded off, and why — using interviews with
second- and third-generation bilingual speakers recorded over the past two
years.

## Nativization is not carelessness

Every borrowing language reshapes a loanword's sounds to fit its own
phoneme inventory, syllable structure, and stress patterns — not from
inattention, but because a listener's brain maps unfamiliar sounds onto the
nearest sounds it already has. This is why loanwords in different receiving
languages, borrowed from the same source, often end up pronounced quite
differently from each other and from the original.

## The generational wrinkle

In bilingual households, first-generation speakers frequently keep a
loanword's original pronunciation intact, code-switching cleanly between
two phonological systems. Second- and third-generation speakers, raised
primarily in the dominant language, are more likely to nativize the same
word without noticing — not because they've lost access to the original
sounds, but because the word has, for them, stopped being a loanword at
all and become simply a word.

## The pattern, one more time

This is the same story the whole season has been telling from different
angles: vowels rotating together in Chicago, a case marker fading out of
casual speech, creaky voice spreading and destigmatizing — sound and
grammar don't hold still, and the generation doing the changing is
usually the last to notice.
