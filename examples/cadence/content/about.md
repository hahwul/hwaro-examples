## What this is

Cadence is a biweekly linguistics podcast about the sounds and grammar of
English changing while everyone is still using it — the vowel that drifts
half a step over three generations, the pronoun that quietly falls out of
casual speech, the loanword that gets its edges sanded down to fit. None of
this is decline. It's the same process that produced every feature of
English anyone currently considers standard.

The show is recorded in front of a small live audience roughly once a
month and released as two seasons a year, each built around a loose theme:
season one asked what makes a change *start*; season two asked what makes a
change *spread*, region by region and household by household.

## Who makes it

**Dr. Priya Ashworth** is a sociolinguist whose fieldwork tracks prosody
and voice quality — vocal fry, uptalk, intonation contours — across age
groups in North American English. She hosts and produces.

**Tobias Reyes-Lindqvist** works on grammaticalization and contact
linguistics: how grammatical forms erode and how loanwords get reshaped to
fit a borrowing language's sound system. He co-hosts and handles the
show's research notes.

Recurring guests are named in each episode's show notes; Cadence does not
credit guests as regular cast unless they appear across multiple episodes
in a season.

## How episodes are researched

Every claim made on air is checked against at least one peer-reviewed
source before recording, and disputed claims are flagged as disputed on
air rather than smoothed over for a cleaner narrative. Sound change is a
field with real disagreement about mechanisms — push chains versus drag
chains, gradual diffusion versus abrupt reanalysis — and Cadence tries to
represent that disagreement honestly instead of picking a side for the
sake of a tidier episode.

## Reading the transcription line

Every episode title on this site carries a phonetic transcription beneath
it, set small and closely tracked, in muted ink: a broad transcription in
General American English, using standard IPA symbols. This isn't
decoration. It's the same
convention linguists use to note a *specific* pronunciation apart from a
word's variable spelling — the show's whole subject, made visible on every
page rather than just discussed in the audio.

## Getting in touch

Cadence is fully listener-supported and does not run advertising. Episode
corrections, fieldwork tips, and family recordings like the ones featured
in *What Grandma's Vowels Tell Us* are welcome — get in touch through the
show's usual channels, and see the [tag index](/tags/) if you're chasing a
single thread — say, everything the show has covered on vowel mergers —
across every season.
