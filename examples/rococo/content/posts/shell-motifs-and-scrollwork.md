The rocaille—an ornamental shell form—is the defining motif of the Rococo period. Unlike the symmetrical, monumental ornaments of Baroque art, rocaille embraces asymmetry, fluidity, and organic irregularity.

These shell-shaped curves, often interlaced with acanthus leaves, garlands of flowers, and playful putti, adorned everything from palace ceilings to porcelain teacups. The effect was one of ceaseless movement, as if the decorative elements were caught mid-dance.

### Scrollwork as Language

C-scrolls and S-scrolls—sinuous curves that spiral inward—form the grammatical backbone of Rococo ornamentation. They connect larger motifs, frame cartouches, and provide visual rhythm to surfaces that might otherwise overwhelm.

The genius of Rococo scrollwork lies in its restraint within excess. Each curve has purpose; each flourish contributes to the overall harmony of the composition.
