The firn line marks the elevation on a glacier where last winter's snow finishes the slow conversion into glacial ice. Below it, the surface ablates each summer; above it, snow accumulates faster than it melts. Tracking that boundary across years gives a direct readout of mass balance.

## What Firn Records

Firn is intermediate material: denser than fresh snow at roughly 550 to 830 kilograms per cubic meter, but still permeable to air. As successive layers compress the grains, pore spaces shrink and eventually pinch off, trapping atmospheric gas. Those bubbles are the primary archive used in ice cores. A firn core taken near the equilibrium line will show a clear seasonal stratigraphy — coarse summer layers, fine winter layers, and intermittent dust horizons that pin a chronology in place.

## Field Procedure

Surveying the firn line begins late in the ablation season, usually August in the northern hemisphere. A team takes paired measurements: stake heights driven into the ice the previous autumn, and density samples extracted from a snow pit dug at intervals up the glacier tongue. The transition appears as a textural change in the pit wall, where granular firn gives way to coherent ice.

GPS markers anchor each sampling site so the boundary can be retraced the following year. Drone-borne photogrammetry has replaced much of the manual mapping, but the snow pit remains the only reliable way to confirm density profiles.

## Reading the Results

A firn line that retreats upslope across consecutive seasons indicates negative mass balance — the glacier is losing more than it gains. A stable line implies equilibrium; a descending line is unusual outside of cooler regional regimes. The metric is blunt but durable, and it predates satellite altimetry by nearly a century. Long-term records from glaciers in the Alps and the St. Elias range still rely on firn-line measurements to validate remote-sensing models.
