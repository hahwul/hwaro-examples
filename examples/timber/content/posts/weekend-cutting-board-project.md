A cutting board is one of the best first projects for a new woodworker. It is small, useful, forgiving of minor mistakes, and produces something you will actually use every day. This end-grain board can be completed in a weekend with basic tools and a few board feet of hardwood.

## Materials

You need two species for visual contrast. I used **hard maple** and **walnut** --- a classic pairing. The light maple and dark walnut create a checkerboard pattern that looks far more complex than it is.

**Shopping list:**

- 2 board feet of hard maple (4/4 thickness)
- 2 board feet of American black walnut (4/4 thickness)
- Titebond III waterproof wood glue
- Mineral oil (food-safe)
- 120, 180, and 220 grit sandpaper

## Tools Required

- Table saw or bandsaw
- Planer (or a hand plane and patience)
- Clamps --- at least 4 bar clamps
- Combination square
- Random orbit sander or sanding block

## Step by Step

### Day One: Milling and First Glue-Up

**Step 1:** Mill all lumber to 1 inch thick. Both faces should be flat and parallel. Joint one edge of each board so it is perfectly straight.

**Step 2:** Rip the boards into **1-inch wide strips** on the table saw. You need approximately 8 strips of maple and 8 strips of walnut.

**Step 3:** Arrange the strips in an alternating pattern --- maple, walnut, maple, walnut --- on a flat surface. Check that all edges mate tightly.

**Step 4:** Apply a thin, even coat of glue to each mating edge. Assemble the strips and clamp firmly. Use cauls (flat boards) across the top and bottom to keep the panel flat during clamping.

> Do not over-tighten your clamps. You want even pressure that produces a thin, consistent glue line. Squeezing too hard starves the joint.

**Step 5:** Let the glue cure overnight. Do not rush this.

### Day Two: Cross-Cutting and Second Glue-Up

**Step 6:** Remove the clamps and scrape off any dried glue with a cabinet scraper. Run the panel through the planer to flatten both faces, taking light passes.

**Step 7:** Cross-cut the panel into **1-inch slices** perpendicular to the original strips. This is the step that transforms edge grain into end grain. Each slice now shows a checkerboard of alternating squares.

**Step 8:** Flip every other slice 180 degrees. This offsets the pattern so that maple squares sit next to walnut squares in all directions.

**Step 9:** Glue and clamp the slices together, using the same care as the first glue-up. This is your final board. Ensure the top surface is as level as possible during clamping.

**Step 10:** Allow the glue to cure overnight.

### Finishing

**Step 11:** Flatten the board. A hand plane works well here if you are comfortable with end grain. Otherwise, careful sanding with an 80-grit belt on a belt sander will level things quickly. Follow up with 120, 180, and 220 grit by hand.

**Step 12:** Ease all edges with sandpaper or a block plane. A slight roundover prevents chipping and feels good in the hand.

**Step 13:** Apply **food-safe mineral oil** liberally. Let it soak in for 20 minutes, wipe off the excess, and repeat two more times.

## The Result

What you have now is a proper end-grain cutting board. The checkerboard pattern catches the eye, but the real advantage is functional: end grain is self-healing, meaning knife cuts close up rather than leaving permanent scars. The board will serve you well for years. Refresh the mineral oil monthly, and it will only get more beautiful with age.

This is the kind of project that reminds you why you started woodworking in the first place. It is simple, honest work --- and it makes a very good gift.
