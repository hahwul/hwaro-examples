This project started the way most of mine do: with a stack of rough-sawn walnut that had been drying in the shop for the better part of a year. American black walnut is one of those woods that rewards patience. The color deepens as it dries, and the grain reveals itself slowly, like a landscape emerging through fog.

## Design and Planning

The bookshelf needed to hold a serious collection without looking heavy. I settled on five shelves, each 36 inches wide and 10 inches deep, with tapered legs that give the piece a slight visual lift. The overall height came to 72 inches --- tall enough to be useful, not so tall that it dominates a room.

**Key dimensions:**

- Overall: 72" H x 36" W x 12" D
- Shelf thickness: 7/8"
- Leg taper: from 1-1/2" at the top to 1" at the foot
- Shelf spacing: approximately 12" between each

## Milling the Lumber

I started by jointing one face and one edge of each board, then ran them through the planer to bring everything down to a uniform 7/8 inch. Walnut mills beautifully when the blades are sharp. The moment you notice tearout, stop and resharpen. It is not worth the lost material.

> Take your time at the planer. Walnut is too expensive and too beautiful to rush through this step.

## Joinery

The shelves are attached to the uprights with **through-tenons** --- a joint that is both structural and decorative. Each tenon is 3/8 inch thick and extends fully through the upright, with a slight wedge driven in from the outside to lock it in place. This is old-world joinery, and it holds.

Steps for cutting the through-tenons:

1. **Mark the tenon** on the shelf end using a marking gauge set to 3/8"
2. **Cut the cheeks** with a fine-toothed backsaw, staying just outside the line
3. **Pare to the line** with a sharp chisel, working from both faces
4. **Mark the mortise** directly from the finished tenon for a perfect fit
5. **Chop the mortise** with a mortise chisel, working from both sides to meet in the middle
6. **Dry-fit** and adjust until the joint slides home with firm hand pressure

## Assembly and Finish

I assembled the shelves in stages, gluing two at a time to keep things manageable. Walnut does not need much to look its best. Three coats of **Danish oil** brought out the grain without building up a plastic-looking film. The final coat was buffed with 0000 steel wool for a satin sheen.

The finished piece has the quiet confidence that good walnut always carries. The through-tenons add a visual rhythm to the sides, and the tapered legs keep it from feeling like a box. It is the kind of furniture that looks better with age --- and that is exactly the point.
