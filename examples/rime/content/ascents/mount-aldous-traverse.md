Aldous is not steep and it is not technical, but it is long — three summits
strung along four kilometres of granite ridge, with a connecting arete so
narrow in places that you straddle it and shuffle. On a traverse like this the
danger is not the climbing; it is the clock.

<!-- more -->

We slept at the north hut and left at 03:30 to have the first summit by dawn.
Moving together on a shortened rope is the only way to make the distance: the
leader carries a few slings and drapes them over flakes and spikes as running
belays, and you both keep moving, stopping to pitch only the two or three short
steps that warrant it.

The middle summit holds the crux, a **PD** down-climb off its south top into a
notch, loose enough that we abseiled rather than reverse it — a single 40 m
rope, doubled, just reaching the ledge. From there the final arete is the best
part of the day: solid, exposed, and continuously interesting without ever being
hard, the kind of ground where you stop noticing the drop and just move.

We tagged the south summit at 15:00 and reached the south hut at dusk, legs
gone, having drunk the last of the water an hour before. Timings held because
the weather did; on a traverse this committing there is no fast retreat once you
are past the middle top.

Verdict: a classic mountaineering day. Fitness matters more than skill. Carry
more water than you think, and start in the dark.
