The Grey Slab is misnamed. It is not a slab but a shallow, north-facing dihedral
that only comes into condition in the coldest, driest midwinters — the sort of
year when the seeps freeze thin and grey instead of running, and the whole face
turns to a single sheet of hooking and torquing.

<!-- more -->

We had waited two winters for a spell cold enough. The approach in the dark was
the coldest I have been on the mountain, minus twenty at the bergschrund, tools
sticking to bare skin. But cold is what makes the slab possible: it glues the
thin ice to the rock and turns marginal placements into marginal-but-frozen
placements, which is the difference between climbing and not.

The line is protected almost entirely by wires. The dihedral is too shallow for
cams and too featured for screws except in three short bulges; everywhere else it
is offsets and micro-nuts slotted between iced flakes, backed on the crux pitch
by a single knifeblade R. drove into a frozen crack. The **M5** traverse on the
fourth pitch is the psychological crux — a rightward shuffle on hooks above a
long fall onto small wires, no ice at all, just the picks catching on granite
crystals.

We topped out at dusk, 18 hours after leaving the car, and walked off the back
in the dark. Whether it was a first winter ascent I cannot say and do not much
care; the record is a footnote and the day was the thing.

Verdict: a rare-condition testpiece. Watch for a hard, dry cold snap, carry wires
not cams, and be ready to walk away if the grey ice never comes.
