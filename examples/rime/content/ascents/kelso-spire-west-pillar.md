The west pillar is 700 metres of clean grey granite that catches the afternoon
sun and holds it, which is why we climbed it at the end of March rather than in
summer, when the crack lines weep and the belays bake.

<!-- more -->

We fixed the first three pitches on the afternoon we arrived, then hauled the
next morning and committed to the wall. Capsule style: two bivouacs on the
portaledge, moving the camp up once, climbing above it on fixed lines and
hauling the bag behind. It is slow, deliberate work — the opposite of the
single-push on the Grauhorn — and it suits a face this long.

Most of the pillar goes free at around **6b**, hand and finger cracks with the
odd bold slab between systems. There is exactly one aid pitch, a blank seam on
the fourth pitch of day two that takes cam hooks and a few thin pitons at **A1**;
it would likely go free for someone stronger, but not for us, not with a haul bag
pulling on the rope.

The summit chimney is the sting in the tail: wide, flaring, and cold in shadow,
swallowing the single No. 5 and asking for a second we did not have. M. led it in
fading light and we topped out by headtorch, then slept on the flat summit block
rather than commit to the descent gully in the dark.

Verdict: a proper wall. Climb it in the shoulder seasons, carry water for two
days, and do not underestimate the last hundred metres.
