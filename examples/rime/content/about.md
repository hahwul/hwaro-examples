Rime is a climbing log. It records alpine ascents above the frost line — the
elevation where standing water stops and everything is either ice or the promise
of it. There is no gear shop attached, no coaching, no sponsors to thank. The
mountains and the parties named here are invented; the cold, the rope drag, and
the long walk down are drawn from real days.

## How to read an entry

Every dispatch opens with a summit elevation set large behind the title. That
number is metres above sea level at the highest point actually touched — the
cornice, the block, the survey bolt — not the rounded figure printed on a map.
Below it sits the grade, then a short data table, then the account. The
[full log](@/ascents/_index.md) lists every entry, newest first.

Grades follow the usual alpine conventions:

- **Overall** — the French alpine scale, from **F** (walking) through **AD**,
  **D**, **TD**, up to **ED** and beyond, describing the route as a whole:
  approach, commitment, and how far help is from the crux.
- **Ice** — the water-ice scale, **WI1** to **WI7**, for frozen runnels and
  hanging daggers.
- **Mixed** — the **M** scale, for pitches climbed on crampons and tools across
  rock and thin ice together.
- **Rock** — French sport grades or aid ratings (**A0**–**A4**) where the line
  goes free or on gear.

## On the writing

Entries are kept terse on purpose. A route does not care how you felt about it,
and a log that reads like a diary is useless six winters later when you are
trying to remember whether the second pitch took screws or cams. So the accounts
stay close to fact: conditions, timings, protection, what worked, what nearly
did not. Sentiment, where it survives, is left to the last line.
