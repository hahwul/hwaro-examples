Not all snow falls as the familiar six-armed star. Between roughly minus four and minus six degrees Celsius, ice crystallizes into needles—long, slender rods that grow along the c-axis of the hexagonal lattice rather than across it. Lower the temperature a few more degrees and the same axis preference produces hollow columns, sometimes capped at each end by small plates.

## Why the Axis Switches

The morphology of a falling crystal is governed by which crystal face attracts water vapour fastest. Around minus fifteen degrees, the basal face grows slowly and the prism faces accept new molecules quickly, producing the wide stellar plates and dendrites that dominate photographic collections. Around minus five degrees, the relationship inverts: prism faces grow slowly, basal faces extend, and the result is a rod.

This temperature dependence is sharp. A change of two degrees Celsius can shift the dominant habit from columns to plates. Because a falling crystal passes through several temperature regimes on its way down, a single snowflake can carry evidence of its entire descent—a column nucleated at high altitude may acquire plate-like end caps as it drops into warmer, moister air.

## Photographing the Forms

Needles are difficult subjects. Their cross-section is small, often less than a hundred micrometres, and they reflect light along a single axis. Backlit dark-field illumination tends to produce the cleanest results, with the needle catching one bright reflection along its length while the surrounding background remains uniformly black. Capped columns benefit from oblique lighting that reveals the difference in texture between the smooth column wall and the structured cap.

For both forms, focus stacking is essential. Depth of field at the magnifications required is shallow enough that even a one-millimetre crystal cannot be rendered sharp end-to-end in a single exposure.
