# The Summit

The pyramidion -- the capstone of the obelisk -- was the final stone placed and the first to catch the light of dawn. Ancient builders often sheathed it in electrum, a natural alloy of gold and silver, so that it would blaze like a second sun above the temple complex.

## The View from the Top

From the summit, the desert extends in every direction. The Nile is a dark ribbon threading through green fields. The temple below, which seemed so vast at ground level, becomes a pattern of geometric precision.

Perspective changes with altitude. Problems that loomed large at the base become manageable when seen from above. The summit offers not answers, but proportion.

## What the Capstone Represents

The pyramidion was more than architectural necessity. It was a symbol of completion -- the point at which earth meets sky, where human effort reaches its natural limit and accepts the vastness beyond.

In any undertaking, there comes a moment when the work is done. Not perfect, but complete. The capstone is placed, and the monument stands.

## Endurance

The true measure of the summit is not the moment of completion but the centuries that follow. An obelisk does not need maintenance. It does not require updates. It stands in the desert, unchanged, while empires rise and fall around its base.

This is the aspiration: to build something that endures not through continuous effort but through the quality of its original construction.

## Looking Down

From the summit, trace the monument back to its foundation. Every stone is visible, every course accounted for. The journey from base to apex is recorded in the structure itself -- a physical autobiography written in granite.

The summit is not an ending. It is a vantage point from which to survey everything that was built, and everything that stretches beyond the horizon.
