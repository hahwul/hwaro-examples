There is a low corner behind the shed where the ground turns to grey putty after rain and stays that way for days. For two seasons I fought it with raised beds, gravel, and a French drain that clogged by August. Then I stopped fighting the water and dug a rain garden instead: a shallow basin scraped into the clay, ringed by a low berm, planted with the handful of things that can stand a fortnight underwater and a drought in the same summer. **Petrichor** is the running notebook from that corner — percolation tests, plant lists, storms measured in centimetres, and an honest tally of what drowned, what sulked, and what quietly took hold.
