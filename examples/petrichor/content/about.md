Petrichor is the notebook I keep for one waterlogged corner of a small back garden on the edge of a river town. The soil is heavy grey clay over a pan of compacted subsoil — the kind that cracks into plates in July and holds standing water into April. Rather than drain it away, the rain garden is built to catch runoff from the shed roof and the patio, hold it in a shallow basin, and let it soak in slowly instead of sheeting off to the fence line.

## The corner

It is not large: a basin about four metres by two, dished maybe thirty centimetres below the surrounding ground, with a low crescent berm on the downhill side to keep water from running out before it can sink. A shallow swale feeds it from the shed downpipe. Everything is shaped by hand with a spade and a wheelbarrow, because a machine would only smear and seal the clay further.

## How the diary works

Entries are written on the day the work happens, so the mistakes stay in. Each one records the date, the weather, what went into the ground, and — where it matters — a percolation reading so I can watch the drainage change as roots open the soil. I log rainfall in millimetres from a cheap gauge on the fence, and I try to note what died as carefully as what thrived, because in clay the failures teach more. The full run is in [the planting diary](@/plantings/_index.md), newest first.

> The rule I keep coming back to: plant for the wettest fortnight and the driest, not the average. The average never actually happens here.

## The gardener

I am Rowan Ashby — not a landscaper, just someone who got tired of a corner that smelled of pondwater every spring. I garden on evenings and weekends, keep no chemicals, and buy plugs small because small plants settle into clay far better than pot-bound gallons. If a plant earns its place through one full wet winter and one dry August, it stays. If it sulks, it is composted without ceremony and noted here.
