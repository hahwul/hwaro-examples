The autumn's first real storm arrived overnight and gave me exactly the test I had been waiting for since April. I went out in a coat at eleven to find the basin brim-full and mirror-still, the rush standing in it up to the ankle, and water still trickling down the swale from the shed roof. This is the moment a rain garden either works or teaches you something expensive.

<!-- more -->

## The numbers

The fence gauge and the timestamps on my phone give a rough but useful picture of how fast the basin cleared once the rain stopped.

```text
Storm log — north basin, 2025-10-18/19
rainfall (gauge)     41 mm overnight
23:40  basin full to berm lip, ~30 cm standing
02:10  rain stopped
08:00  water down to ~12 cm
14:00  basin damp, no standing water   (~19 hours to clear)
```

Nineteen hours to empty a thirty-centimetre pond into clay that measured 0.7 cm/hour back in February. The floor plants had been submerged for the better part of half a day and did not care in the slightest — which is the whole point of choosing them.

## What held, what drowned

The floor and slope came through untouched: the iris, sedges, and rush might as well have enjoyed it, and the Joe Pye weed on the mid slope shrugged. The losses were all on the rim, where I had pushed my luck when I decided [what went in first](@/plantings/what-went-in-first.md). Two of the wild bergamot on the lowest part of the berm had their crowns sitting in wet soil for too long and have gone black at the base — my mistake for planting a dry-rim plant a hand's width too low.

The lesson is small and exact: on this ground the boundary between "damp slope" and "dry rim" is sharper than the gentle gradient suggests. Next spring the survivors move up ten centimetres, and the bergamot gets replaced with something that does not mind cold feet.
