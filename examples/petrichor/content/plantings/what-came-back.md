A rain garden's first birthday is the only review that means anything, because it has now stood through a full wet winter — the season that kills the passengers and keeps the residents. The basin held water for most of January, froze over twice, and thawed into grey soup in February. In May I finally went out to take stock of what came back.

<!-- more -->

## The survivors

The floor planting has won its place outright. The tussock sedge has trebled in size and its roots come up trailing crumbs of soil that actually hold together now — real structure where there was putty a year ago. The blue flag iris is in fat bud, the rush is thickening into a proper stand, and the marsh marigold flowered in April with the basin still half-full.

Up the slope the Joe Pye weed and cardinal flower both returned strongly. On the rim, the switchgrass and black-eyed Susan sailed through, and the relocated bergamot — moved up ten centimetres after last autumn's drowning — is alive and budding, vindicating the small correction.

## The drainage has changed

The most satisfying number of the year came from repeating [the percolation test](@/plantings/reading-the-clay.md) in the same spot.

```text
Perc test — north basin floor, 2026-04-28
09:00  water at 30 cm
12:00  26.1 cm    (~1.3 cm/hour)
16:00  21.4 cm
verdict: roughly double last winter's rate
```

From 0.7 to about 1.3 centimetres an hour in a single season, purely from roots and worms opening the soil. No sand, no drain — just the right plants left alone to do the work.

## The overflow spur

Buoyed by all this, I have dug a small second basin — a spur off the berm's low point — to take the overflow in the biggest storms and spread the planting into next year. It is the same recipe: dish it, berm it, amend the top spit, plant it dense and small, and then wait, honestly, to see what the clay allows.
