April gave me a dry fortnight, which on clay is the only window worth having — too wet and you glaze the soil into pottery, too dry and it turns to concrete. I marked the basin out with a hose laid in a soft kidney shape, roughly four metres by two, then started taking the turf off in slabs to relay on the berm later.

<!-- more -->

## Shaping the dish

The basin is dished, not boxed: sides sloping in at about one-in-three so nothing slumps and so a frog or a hedgehog that falls in can walk out. The deepest point is a little over thirty centimetres below the surrounding grade. I piled the spoil along the downhill edge and firmed it into a low crescent berm, because the whole point is to hold water long enough to soak rather than letting it run off to the fence.

The inlet is a shallow swale leading from the shed downpipe, lined with a scatter of cobbles so the incoming water spreads instead of scouring a channel.

## Amending without smearing

You cannot dig sand into structureless clay and expect drainage — you mostly get a sump. What actually helps is opening the surface with coarse material and letting roots and worms do the rest. Into the top spit of the basin floor and slopes I forked through a rough mix, keeping the spade shallow so I never polished the clay below.

```text
Amendment mix — top 15 cm only
5 parts  existing clay topsoil
2 parts  6 mm horticultural grit
2 parts  leaf mould (two winters old)
1 part   composted bark
```

Then a loose five-centimetre mulch of composted bark over everything except the very bottom of the basin, which stays bare for now so I can watch how it silts and drains. It looks raw and honest, a brown dish in a green lawn. Now it needs to settle for a few weeks before [anything goes in](@/plantings/what-went-in-first.md).
