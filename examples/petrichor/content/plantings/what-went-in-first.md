A rain garden is really three gardens stacked by how long each part stays wet, and the trick is to plant each zone with things that actually want those conditions. I let the basin settle through May, watched where the water lingered longest after two storms, and marked the zones with sand lines before buying a single plant.

<!-- more -->

## Three zones, three lists

Everything went in as plugs or nine-centimetre pots. Small plants push roots into clay far better than pot-bound gallons, and they cost little enough that losing a few to the first wet winter will not hurt.

| Zone | Stays wet | Went in |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Basin floor | days after rain | blue flag iris, soft rush, tussock sedge |
| Mid slope | damp, drains in a day | Joe Pye weed, marsh marigold, cardinal flower |
| Dry rim & berm | rarely wet | switchgrass, black-eyed Susan, wild bergamot |

## Planting into clay

Each planting hole was roughed up on the sides with a hand fork rather than left smooth, so roots would not circle in a slick-walled pocket. No fertiliser — clay is rich enough, and soft growth just flops and rots. I watered everything in once, hard, to settle the soil around the roots, then left it to the weather.

The sedges and the rush I planted densest, right in the low centre, because their fibrous roots are what will slowly open the drainage over the next two seasons. The showier perennials are really passengers on that work.

## First impressions

By evening the basin looked less like a wound and more like a plan: fine grey-green sedge foliage in the middle, the broad blades of iris, and a loose scatter of perennials up the slope. Now the only honest thing to do is wait for [a real storm](@/plantings/the-storm-test.md) and see which of these choices I regret.
