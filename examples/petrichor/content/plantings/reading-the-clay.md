Before anything gets planted, the corner has to be understood, and the only honest way to do that in February is to dig. I cut three test holes across the low ground with a narrow spade, each a spit deep, and left them open overnight to see what the water table was doing. By morning two of them had a finger of grey water in the bottom that had arrived from nowhere in particular.

<!-- more -->

## The percolation test

The real number I wanted was how fast this ground actually drains. I dug one hole thirty centimetres deep, filled it the evening before to pre-soak the sides, then filled it again in the morning and timed the drop.

```text
Perc test — north corner, 2025-02-14
hole 30 cm deep, walls pre-soaked overnight
09:00  water level at 30 cm
10:00  29.2 cm    (0.8 cm/hour)
12:00  27.9 cm
16:00  25.4 cm    (~0.7 cm/hour average)
verdict: drains slower than a bathtub with the plug still in
```

Under about 1.3 centimetres an hour is the rough line where conventional wisdom says a plain soakaway will disappoint you. At 0.7 I was well under it. That reading settled the argument I had been having with myself all winter, and led straight to [cutting the first basin](@/plantings/cutting-the-first-basin.md).

## What the clay is telling me

A profile scraped from the side of the deepest hole showed maybe fifteen centimetres of decent dark topsoil, then a hard grey-brown clay that squeaked against the spade and rolled into a ribbon between my fingers without cracking — high clay content, very little structure. The pan below that is where the old builders' traffic compacted everything.

The lesson is that I cannot make this drain like sand and I should stop trying. What I can do is give the water somewhere to sit that is planted, shaded, and slowly being opened by roots. So the plan is set: a shallow basin, a low berm, and a two-year experiment in patience.
