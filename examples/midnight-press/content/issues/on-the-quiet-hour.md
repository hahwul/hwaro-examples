There is a moment, somewhere between three and four in the morning, when the city has finished its work and not yet started its day. The buses have not begun their first runs. The convenience-store clerks have not yet been relieved. The newspaper trucks are still on the third ring road. The hour is, by any measure, useless — too late to finish anything, too early to begin anything.

It is the only hour at which I can write.

I came to this hour late and reluctantly. For most of my twenties I tried to write in the mornings, like the books said to. I read about Hemingway standing at his desk before the sun came up. I read about Murakami running for ten kilometers and then sitting down at six. The implication, in every interview I read, was that great work happens early, and the writer who fails to capture the morning fails to capture anything.

It is not true. The morning belongs to other people. The morning belongs to the city's first commuters, to the bakery deliveries, to the schoolchildren standing at the bus stop. There is too much *life* in the morning to write into.

The quiet hour is different. The quiet hour is the moment when the world has temporarily forgotten you. Nothing requires your attention. Nothing arrives in your inbox. Nothing rings. The hour is a pocket — small, lined with felt, hidden behind a coat the day has hung up and forgotten.

> The quiet hour is not a time to be brilliant. It is a time to be present.

I do not believe in inspiration. I believe in attendance. The writer who shows up at the quiet hour, regardless of mood, becomes a writer. The writer who waits to feel inspired becomes someone with a desk.

What you write in the quiet hour is rarely your best work. What you write in the quiet hour is *next* work. The next paragraph. The next page. The next stretch of sentences that gets you from where you were to where you are going. It is rarely beautiful. It is occasionally true. It is always, *always*, a small advance against the silence.

## On the practice

I keep a single notebook. I write longhand for the first thirty minutes of the hour, regardless of subject. I do not edit during this time. I do not cross out. I do not start a fresh page if the previous one is unfinished. I do not allow myself to write *about* writing, which is a particular vice of writers, and a particularly tempting one in the dark.

After the longhand, I move to the typewriter. I keep an old Olympia at the side of the desk. The Olympia is loud — too loud, my neighbors would say if they were awake — but the noise is part of the practice. The Olympia is the editor that the longhand has not yet earned. The Olympia tells me, with the resistance of its keys, when a sentence is wasting my time.

At four o'clock I stop. I do not stop because the sentence is finished. I stop because the hour is finished. The discipline is the hour, not the sentence.

## On the question of the dark

People who do not write at this hour ask me, regularly, whether it is depressing. They imagine the hour is full of melancholy — the great writer alone at his desk, the lamp burning, the rain (it is always raining, in the imagination of people who do not write at this hour). The truth is that the hour is the least depressing part of my day. There is no email. There is no traffic. The dog is asleep. The neighbor's television is silent.

The dark is a feature, not a bug. The dark makes the page brighter than the room, and the page becomes the only thing that exists. I have written, by now, two books at the quiet hour, and the second one was easier than the first only because I had stopped resisting the discipline of the hour.

I will write a third. It will take me another three years. I am in no hurry. The hour returns every night.

— *J. Y. Lee*
