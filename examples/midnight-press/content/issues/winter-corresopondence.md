A reader in Sapporo wrote to a reader in Glasgow. The reader in Glasgow wrote back. They continued for one hundred and four days, from the first snow of November to the first thaw of February. They had never met. They have agreed to print a selection of their letters, with names withheld.

## I. November 3rd, Sapporo

> I have just read your letter in the autumn issue and I would like to write to you. You said something about the hour at which it is permissible to admit that you are alone. I would like to know which hour you meant.

## II. November 11th, Glasgow

> I meant three in the morning. The hour at which the city has finished its night and not yet started its morning. The hour at which lying about your circumstance becomes too much work.

## III. November 19th, Sapporo

> In Sapporo at three in the morning the snow has usually been falling for six hours. The streetlights are still on but they are pointless — the snow has eaten the air between the light and the ground. I am at my window with tea. I do not have anyone to lie to.

## IV. November 28th, Glasgow

> Then you have already done the difficult part. The hour does not require courage from someone who has chosen it. It only requires courage from someone who has been *given* it.

## V. December 14th, Sapporo

> My mother died in December. I am not telling you this to make you sad. I am telling you because the quiet hour, in December, is the hour at which my mother's voice is most likely to arrive. I do not believe in ghosts. I do believe in the way the brain rehearses, at three in the morning, the things it cannot say out loud during the day.

## VI. December 22nd, Glasgow

> I believe in ghosts. Not in the literal sense. I believe in the imprint people leave in the rooms they used to occupy. My grandfather has been dead for thirty-one years and there is still a chair in my mother's house that nobody sits in. The chair is the ghost.

## VII. January 5th, Sapporo

> The snow stopped today. The city is wearing its winter shape now — the buildings smaller under their hats of snow, the streets narrower for the plowing. I went out for the first time in a week. The pharmacy was open. The pharmacist nodded at me. It is the most communication I have had in eleven days.

## VIII. January 18th, Glasgow

> Glasgow has had no snow this year. The rain has been constant. There is a kind of cold rain that is more isolating than snow because it does not invite anyone outside to admire it. Snow is an event. Cold rain is an erasure.

## IX. February 2nd, Sapporo

> The first thaw started this morning. The sound of water under the snow is the loudest sound in winter. Everywhere I walk I can hear the snow becoming water, drop by drop. My mother used to say that the thaw was when the year began. February, she said, was the only honest month — the month that admits the year is starting, instead of pretending the year started on the first.

## X. February 14th, Glasgow

> Today the first crocus came up in the small park near my flat. I went and stood in front of it for ten minutes. I was not the only one. A man with a small white dog stopped beside me. We did not speak. We watched the crocus together. He nodded at me when he left. The hour was eleven o'clock in the morning. The quiet hour, it turns out, can also happen in daylight.

— *D. Han, Editor*
