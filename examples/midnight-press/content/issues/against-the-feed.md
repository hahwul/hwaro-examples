The dominant publishing format of the last fifteen years is the feed. The feed is an infinite sequence of posts, ordered by recency or algorithmic preference, presented one after another without distinction of importance. The feed has displaced the issue, the chapter, the section, and — in the most aggressive cases — the article itself.

The feed has many virtues. It is fast. It is cheap to produce. It rewards casual reading. It allows the reader to enter and leave at any point. It is, for the producer, a more flexible relationship with the audience than the bound issue ever was.

But the feed has costs. The first cost is the loss of the *issue* — the bounded collection of work that the reader is expected to consume as a unit. An issue is a curated frame. An issue makes a claim: *these pieces belong together; reading them together produces meaning that reading them apart does not.*

The feed makes the opposite claim. The feed says: every piece is independent. Every piece is interchangeable. The next piece will be different from this one, and that is fine, because nothing connects them anyway.

> The feed says: every piece is independent. Every piece is interchangeable.

For some kinds of writing — news bulletins, social posts, brief notes — this is acceptable. For other kinds of writing — essays, criticism, sustained thinking — it is corrosive. The essay was invented to be read in sequence. The essay assumes the reader has read the previous essay and will read the next one. The feed strips this assumption away.

The second cost is the loss of *closure*. An issue ends. The reader closes the magazine and feels something — satisfaction, exhaustion, a small irritation that the next issue is three months away. The feed does not end. The reader scrolls until the reader becomes tired. The reader closes the tab without finishing because nothing is finished. The reader retains nothing because nothing was bounded.

The bound issue produces a different kind of reading. The reader sits down with a magazine and reads it. The reader puts the magazine on the bedside table. The reader returns to it. The reader finishes it. The reader notices that they have finished it. The reader carries the issue, mentally, for a few days. The reader looks forward to the next one.

## What we are doing

The Midnight Press is bound. We publish four issues a year. Each issue is a discrete object. The web archive is organized by issue, not by feed. There is no infinite scroll on this website. There is no recommendation algorithm. There is no "you might also like." The archive is a library, not a feed.

When you read an essay from issue 217, you are reading an essay from issue 217. The essays from issue 216 are available, in the archive, if you wish to read them. They are not pushed at you. They wait.

This is not a posture. This is a different theory of how reading should work. We believe the reader's attention is finite and valuable. We believe the writer who respects the reader's attention will produce better work. We believe the bound issue is the format that best preserves the contract between writer and reader.

The feed is here to stay. We do not pretend otherwise. We are not arguing against its existence. We are arguing that there should also continue to be bound publications, printed at unfashionable hours, distributed by post, with a public archive that does not auto-play.

We are betting that some readers will continue to want this. So far the bet has held for twenty-eight years.

— *R. Kim*
