The bindery is on the second floor of a narrow building in Mapo, above a laundry and below the apartment of an old man who plays cassette tapes of Korean folk music at low volume. I have visited the bindery once a week for fourteen years. The first time, I was a graduate student writing a thesis on typography. I have not changed the day of the week.

The press itself is older than the building. It was manufactured in West Germany in 1962, shipped to a printer in Suwon in 1964, sold to a press in Incheon in 1981, and bought by the bindery's founder, Mr. Han, in 1993. It has been continuously operated for sixty-two years. The chase is the same chase. The bed is the same bed. The rollers have been replaced seventeen times.

## What hand-setting actually means

Outside the bindery, the phrase *hand-set type* is mostly a romantic gesture. People use it to mean "the design feels old." Inside the bindery, hand-setting is a series of physical decisions. The compositor pulls each letter from a case. The case is divided into ninety-four compartments. The compositor learns where each letter lives — *e* in the largest center compartment, *q* and *z* in the small ones at the edge — and her hand reaches the correct compartment without looking.

A single page of body text takes between four and seven hours to compose. A page of display type takes longer because the letters are larger and the kerning is more visible. A page of mathematics takes a full day.

> Type wants to be read. The room decides how much light it gets.

This is not nostalgia. The bindery does not produce more beautiful type than a contemporary digital workflow can produce. What the bindery produces is *attention*. The compositor has been forced, by the physicality of the press, to consider every letter. The compositor cannot blur past a kerning error because the press will literally bite through the paper.

## The screen edition

I am asked, more often than I would expect, why the screen edition of this magazine is set in dark colors when the print edition is, of course, light. The answer is not aesthetic. The answer is a question of where the type is being read.

The print edition is read in lamplight. A lamp is a small, warm, finite light. It falls on the page. The paper is warm cream. The ink is warm black. The reading is an intimate exchange between three objects: the page, the lamp, and the eye.

The screen edition is read in different conditions. It is read in bed. It is read on a phone in a dim subway car. It is read at the kitchen table at midnight, the only light in the apartment coming from the screen itself. Under those conditions, a light page is hostile. A light page is a flashlight in the face of a sleeping room.

A dark page, with carefully tuned ink, is generous. It does not push light into the room. It lets the room remain a room. It allows reading to be the quiet thing it is supposed to be.

This is not a stylistic choice. This is a question of respect for the reader's circumstance. We do not know where you are reading us. We do not know how dark the room is. We make a single defensible bet: we assume the room is dark, and we set the type to be readable in the dark. If the room is bright, the type still reads, though perhaps with a small adjustment of the brightness.

## A note for designers

The dark edition is set in Cormorant Garamond at twenty pixels for the body, with a line height of 1.62, against a paper color that is not pure black but a slight warm shift of black — *#0c0a08*, if you want the hex. The ink is a warm cream, *#ecdfca*, set against the warm black to preserve the feeling of warm paper. Pure white on pure black is hostile; warm cream on warm dark is generous.

The same principle applies to the rules, the captions, and the small monospace text in the byline strip. None of them are pure white. None of them are pure black. The screen, like the page, deserves a temperature.

— *I. Suh, Type Director*
