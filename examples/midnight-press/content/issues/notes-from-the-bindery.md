Mr. Han is sixty-eight. He has run the bindery in Mapo since 1993, when he bought the Heidelberg press from a small print shop in Incheon that had gone out of business. He agreed to the interview reluctantly. He preferred to be interviewed while working. I sat on a wooden stool beside the press for six hours and watched him compose half of an issue.

What follows is condensed from twelve hours of conversation across three visits.

## On starting

> I did not want to run a bindery. I wanted to be a poet. I was a poet, for about eight months, in 1981. Nobody bought the book. I went to work for my uncle's print shop in Daegu. My uncle taught me to operate the press. The poetry left and the press stayed. This is, I think, the correct order.

## On the press

> The press is a Heidelberg cylinder, manufactured 1962. It is older than my marriage and almost as patient. It does what you tell it. If you tell it the wrong thing, it will print the wrong thing very beautifully. The press has no opinion about whether your book is good. The press has an opinion about whether your kerning is good.

## On hand-setting

> When I started, hand-setting was the only kind of setting. There was no alternative. By 1980 there was photo-typesetting, by 1990 there was digital. Each new wave was supposed to retire us. None of them did. The presses are still here. The bindery is still here. The kids who came in from Hongik University to apprentice with me are still here, mostly. Two of them have their own presses now.

> The question is never whether the technology is faster. The question is whether the technology *attends* to the type. Hand-setting attends to the type because you cannot help but attend to it. You are physically holding the letter. You are physically choosing where to put it. The faster technologies are wonderful, but they do not require the compositor to attend.

## On progress

> Progress is what people who have never made a thing call the changes they have not had to live with. It is easy to be in favor of progress when you have not lost anything to it.

> I am not against new things. I have a smartphone. I have a website — my niece made it for me. I order ink from a website in Berlin. The new things are useful. But they are not better than the old things. They are different.

## On the dark edition

> I have read the online edition. I think it is good that the website is dark. The website is not the book. The book is the book. The website is something else. The website should not pretend to be the book. The dark color tells the reader: this is a different thing, set for a different time. I approve.

## On the next thirty-two years

> I will run the bindery until I cannot. After that, my apprentice will run it. After that, his apprentice. The press will outlast me. The press will outlast all of us. That is the point of a press. It is supposed to outlast its operators.

> If you write this down, do not make me sound sad. I am not sad. The bindery is full of light. There is a good smell. The press makes a good noise. I am a lucky man.

— *Mr. Han, interviewed by M. Choi*
