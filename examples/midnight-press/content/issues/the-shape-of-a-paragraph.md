A paragraph has a shape before it has a meaning. The reader sees the shape before the reader reads the words. The shape predicts, to a surprising degree, how the words will be received.

I came to this conviction slowly. For most of my career as an editor I treated paragraph breaks as logical decisions — a new paragraph began where a new thought began, and the length of a paragraph was determined by the length of the thought. This is true, in a limited way. But it is also incomplete.

A short paragraph at the top of a column tells the reader: this will be easy. A tall paragraph at the top of a column tells the reader: this will require attention. Neither shape is better. Both are honest, in their way. But they make different promises.

The skilled writer makes the promise the writer can keep.

## Tall paragraphs

A tall paragraph is a paragraph that fills the eye. It is a wall of text. The reader's instinct, when meeting a tall paragraph, is to estimate the labor required. The reader looks for landmarks. The reader notices where the paragraph ends. The reader decides — sometimes before the first word is read — whether to commit.

Tall paragraphs are appropriate for thinking that is genuinely continuous. A long argument. A close reading. A passage of dense description. They are inappropriate for thinking that is, in fact, a series of small claims pretending to be a long argument.

The most common failure mode of the inexperienced essayist is to write tall paragraphs that do not earn their height. The paragraph contains a sequence of unrelated sentences, glued together by the absence of a paragraph break. The reader feels lied to.

## Short paragraphs

A short paragraph is a paragraph that punctuates.

It interrupts.

It says: the next sentence is important enough to stand alone.

The risk of the short paragraph is the inverse of the risk of the tall paragraph: a sequence of short paragraphs becomes a sequence of small announcements. Every sentence is "important." Therefore no sentence is. The reader becomes numb to the punctuation, and the writer has gained nothing.

## The mid-length paragraph

The most useful paragraph length is three to five sentences. It is long enough to contain a small argument — a claim, a development, a qualification. It is short enough that the reader can hold the entire paragraph in mind while reading the next one. It is the working unit of the essay.

The mid-length paragraph is the brick. The tall paragraph is the column. The short paragraph is the keystone. A good essay is a building, and the writer is the bricklayer.

## A practical exercise

Take an essay you have written. Read it without reading the words. Look only at the shape of the paragraphs.

Where are the tall ones? Are they doing the work of tall paragraphs?

Where are the short ones? Are they earning their punctuation?

Where are the gaps in rhythm — three tall paragraphs in a row, with no relief? Where is the rhythm too constant — six mid-length paragraphs in a row, with no surprise?

The shape of your essay will tell you almost everything about what it is to read. Adjust the shape. The meaning will follow.
