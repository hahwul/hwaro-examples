I have been the editor of this publication for twelve years. In that time I have edited around six hundred essays, perhaps fifty of which I would call excellent and several hundred of which I would call competent. Below is an incomplete list of habits I have noticed in the writers whose work I have come to trust. The list is in no particular order.

1. They send their manuscript in the body of the email, not as an attachment.

2. They include a one-sentence description of the piece in the subject line.

3. They do not apologize for the length of the piece.

4. They do not explain, in the email, what the piece is "really about."

5. They do not include footnotes that are not load-bearing.

6. They use the semicolon correctly, or not at all.

7. They do not capitalize the names of their own articles in mid-sentence.

8. They do not begin pieces with a dictionary definition.

9. They do not begin pieces with a quotation, unless the quotation is the *subject* of the piece.

10. They write in scenes when scenes are required and in arguments when arguments are required.

11. They know the difference.

12. They do not confuse cleverness for intelligence.

13. They do not confuse intelligence for wisdom.

14. They cite their sources, even when the citation is awkward.

15. They do not link to their own previous work in the body of the essay.

16. They do not link, period, unless the link is doing genuine work.

17. They do not write the words "as we have seen" unless we have, in fact, seen.

18. They are suspicious of sentences that begin with "of course."

19. They are suspicious of sentences that contain the phrase "needless to say."

20. They notice when they are repeating themselves.

21. They cut the repetition, even when it costs them a paragraph they loved.

22. They do not love their paragraphs more than they love their readers.

23. They believe their readers are smart.

24. They write *to* the reader, not *at* the reader.

25. They have a personal style and they do not believe their personal style is the only correct style.

26. They are kind to other writers in print, even when they are critical.

27. They are private about the kindness; they do not seek credit for it.

28. They keep notebooks, in a form that suits them.

29. They reread books they have already read.

30. They allow themselves to be wrong in early drafts.

31. They allow themselves to be wrong in published work, when the work calls for it.

32. They issue corrections when they have made a mistake.

33. They do not issue corrections that are themselves disguised arguments.

34. They have a routine, even if the routine is unconventional.

35. They are loyal to their routine.

36. They are not slavish about their routine.

37. They take walks.

38. They keep their sentences within reach.

39. They know which writers have shaped them and they can name them.

40. They do not pretend to have shaped themselves.

41. They are not afraid of the dark.

42. They are not afraid of being unread.

43. They write the next sentence.

— *J. Y. Lee*
