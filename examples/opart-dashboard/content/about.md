Optikon is a perception laboratory rendered as a dashboard. It borrows the vocabulary of optical and kinetic art — Bridget Riley's warping stripe fields, Victor Vasarely's nested geometry — and turns it into a working instrument panel for measuring how a flat surface behaves on the retina.

The discipline is simple and strict: the eye-bending patterns stay in the hero and decorative zones, while every number lives on a solid, high-contrast panel where it cannot be lost in the optical noise.

## the rules of the field

1. **Pure black and white.** Every stripe, ring, and wave is a solid-fill shape. Nothing is faked with a gradient; depth comes from contrast, shadow, and offset alone.
2. **One accent.** A single electric vermillion marks the signals that matter — a peak band, an active pattern, a live channel. Used anywhere else it would stop meaning anything.
3. **Data stays still.** Patterns may drift; body text never moves. Motion is gentle and yields entirely to anyone who prefers reduced motion.

## instrument parameters

- **Engine**: Hwaro, a static site generator compiled in Crystal.
- **Display type**: Anton, an extended heavy display sans for headlines.
- **Body type**: Archivo, a clean neutral grotesque for measurements and prose.
- **Patterns**: hand-plotted inline SVG, solid fills and strokes only.

> A pattern is only hypnotic until you have to read it. Optikon keeps the surface restless and the data perfectly calm.
