The simplest structure I weave and the one I go back to first each season, because a straight twill tells you the truth about a dye lot faster than anything else — one diagonal line, no blocks to hide behind.

<!-- more -->

This warp was linen, sett at sixteen ends per inch, threaded 1–2–3–4 straight across and treadled the same way, so the cloth reads as one unbroken diagonal from selvedge to selvedge. The weft was Shetland wool from the first madder bath of the year, the deepest red the root gives before the dye exhausts — brick rather than rose, closer to the colour of the clay the roots grow in than to a flower.

I wove it in March, the week before the ground softened enough to start turning the beds again, using wool spun over the previous winter specifically for this piece. A straight twill this even doesn't forgive an uneven beat, so I kept the shuttle slow and checked the fell line every few picks against a ruler taped to the breast beam. The finished cloth has a slight sheen along the diagonal where the twill floats catch the light — barely visible in a photograph, obvious in the room. It hangs now in the front room at Thornhope, the first thing visitors see when they come in from the yard, which feels right for the piece that taught me the loom.
