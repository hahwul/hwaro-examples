Undulating twill doubles each shaft in the threading run instead of moving through them one at a time — 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, 4 — which widens the diagonal into a soft wave rather than a hard line, especially when the treadling doubles at a slightly different point in its own sequence.

<!-- more -->

I threaded and treadled this one out of phase on purpose, so the wave in the warp direction and the wave in the weft direction don't quite align — the cloth shifts between a tighter and a looser-looking weave as your eye moves across it, the way a tideline shifts across sand between wettings. It's the closest I've come to weaving something that looks like weather rather than geometry.

The colour is a single dye lot, but not a single simmer: after the wool came out of its first pull, I put it back into the same, still-warm bath for a shorter second simmer, just long enough to deepen the red without pushing it toward the brown that a full second boil would give. The result sits between [Coldhope Twill](@/tapestries/coldhope-twill.md)'s brick and [Diamond Point](@/tapestries/diamond-point.md)'s rose, closer to the first than the second but with more give in it. It came off the loom in March, the same week the beds outside were finally dry enough to turn over for this year's cuttings.
