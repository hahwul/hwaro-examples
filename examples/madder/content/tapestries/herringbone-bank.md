Herringbone breaks the straight twill's threading at the peak instead of turning back through it gradually — 1 to 4, then straight back to 1 — which gives the diagonal a hard reversal instead of a soft point, and reads as a chevron rather than a diamond.

<!-- more -->

Nothing about this piece was meant to happen the way it did. I'd planned to weave it in a single flat colour from the exhaust bath, the third and weakest pull off a dye lot, mostly to see how far the root would stretch before giving up its colour entirely. The exhaust alone came out too faint to hold its own against the linen warp, so I plied it together with a strand of leftover first-pull red — the same lot that runs through [Coldhope Twill](@/tapestries/coldhope-twill.md) — before winding the weft bobbins — a decision made with three days' warp left on the loom and no time to re-dye.

The result is a heathered red rather than a flat one, which the herringbone structure makes the most of: the chevrons catch the lighter and darker fibres differently depending on which way the twill float runs, so the reversal at each point of the chevron shows as a faint colour shift as well as a structural one. It's the widest piece I've woven on this warp width, meant for a stair landing where it gets seen from below and to the side rather than straight on — the chevrons read better at an angle than flat.
