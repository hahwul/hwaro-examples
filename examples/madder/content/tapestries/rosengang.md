Rosengång — rosepath — came to Thornhope by way of a Swedish weaver who stayed a fortnight two winters ago and left this draft in my notebook. It's the only structure I weave whose tie-up isn't a simple twill: treadle three lifts three shafts instead of two, which is what breaks the diagonal into blocks.

<!-- more -->

The blocks are the whole point of the structure — instead of one running diagonal, rosepath builds small repeating diamonds and crosses that read, from a few feet back, almost like a hand-worked bead pattern rather than woven cloth. I leaned into that by alternating weft colour block by block: the first-pull red for half the sequence, the softer second-pull rose for the other half, so the blocks separate by hue as well as structure.

It's the narrowest piece I've woven this way, sett at twenty ends per inch on linen, because rosepath wants a fine, dense warp to let the blocks resolve cleanly — anything looser and the crosses blur into the twill lines around them. It took the better part of three weeks at the loom, most of that spent watching the treadling sequence rather than the shuttle; a rosepath treadling error is far less forgiving than a straight twill's, and reads immediately once the cloth is off the beam. I keep meaning to write to the weaver who taught me this and tell her it finally worked.
