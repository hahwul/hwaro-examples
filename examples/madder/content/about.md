I'm Rhona Kessler. I weave at Thornhope, a stone byre above Coldhope Water, on a four-shaft counterbalance loom that came with the house — oak-framed, older than anyone can date exactly, re-tied by me plank by plank the first winter I lived here.

## The garden before the loom

The dye garden is the actual start of the work, not a backdrop to it. Two long beds of *Rubia tinctorum* run along the south wall, planted from root cuttings a neighbour gave me nine years ago. Madder wants three seasons in the ground before the roots hold enough alizarin to be worth pulling — the first two years teach patience more than craft. I lift a bed in October, wash the roots in the burn, and dry them slowly in the loft through winter so they don't mould before spring.

Dyeing happens in April, once the roots have rested. Wool is scoured and mordanted with alum first — always alum, sometimes with a little cream of tartar to soften the red toward rose — then simmered with the ground root well under a boil, because madder scorched hot turns brown, not red. A single dye bath rarely gives one colour: the first pull runs deep and brick-dark, the second softer and pinker, the third barely a blush. I keep all three, and I plan a piece's palette around which pull I'll need for which yarn.

## Threading the loom

Every tapestry starts as a draft before it starts as cloth: a threading order across four shafts, a tie-up linking shafts to treadles, and a treadling sequence that becomes the weft. I draft these longhand in a notebook before I ever wind a warp, the way you'd write out a melody before playing it — which is why I've started drawing them out properly for these pages, so the structure of a piece is as visible as its colour.

Most of what I weave are twill structures — straight twills like [Coldhope Twill](@/tapestries/coldhope-twill.md), point twills that fold into diamonds, and [rosepath](@/tapestries/rosengang.md), a rose-patterned structure I learned from a visiting Swedish weaver two winters ago and have not stopped using since. Each structure catches the madder differently: a straight twill lets a single dye lot run in a clean diagonal, while rosepath breaks it into small repeating blocks that read almost like beadwork from a distance.

## Visiting

Thornhope isn't a shop. I sell finished pieces a few times a year and take on the occasional custom commission for weavers who want a specific draft turned into cloth. The [full archive](@/tapestries/_index.md) of what's currently on the wall is the best sense of what a commission might look like. If you're passing through the valley and want to see the garden or the loom, write ahead — the byre keeps odd hours, mostly dictated by whatever is currently in the dye pot.
