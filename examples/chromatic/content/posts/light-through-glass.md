## The Setup

A single beam of afternoon sunlight. A collection of hand-blown glass vessels arranged on a matte black surface. The camera positioned low, almost parallel to the table, with a vintage 50mm lens stopped wide open at f/1.4.

## What Happens When Light Bends

Glass slows light. Different wavelengths slow by different amounts. Red barely notices the glass is there. Blue drags its feet. The result: a single white ray unfolds into a ribbon of pure color.

This series captures that unfolding at the moment it happens. No post-processing color separation. No digital trickery. Just physics, rendered visible by the camera.

## Technical Notes

The key to this series was finding glass with enough dispersion to produce visible chromatic separation at the edges, while maintaining enough clarity to preserve the subject in the center of the frame. Old soda-lime glass -- the kind used in bottles from the early twentieth century -- proved ideal.

**Lens**: Helios 44-2 58mm f/2 (1972)
**Aperture**: f/2 -- wide enough for visible aberration, controlled enough for a readable subject
**ISO**: 100
**Light source**: Direct sunlight through a west-facing window, late afternoon

## The Color Channels

When you examine the edges of each glass object in the final prints, you can see the RGB separation clearly. The red channel extends slightly beyond the green, and the blue trails behind both. This fringing is typically corrected in software. Here, it is the subject itself.

## Reflections

There is something meditative about watching light decompose. We think of white light as simple and color as complex. The truth is the reverse. White light is the most complex thing in the room. Color is what remains when you strip away everything except a single frequency.
