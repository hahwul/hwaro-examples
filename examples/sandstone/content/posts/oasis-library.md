## Background

Ghardaia, the principal town of the M'zab Valley, is a UNESCO World Heritage site recognized for its Ibadi urban planning principles --- a thousand-year tradition of compact, climate-responsive settlement. The Oasis Library was commissioned by a local education foundation to provide a public reading space and digital archive for the surrounding communities.

The architects, all trained in both contemporary practice and traditional M'zab construction, chose to work entirely within the local building tradition while addressing modern programmatic needs.

## Plan

The building is organized around a central courtyard in the manner of a traditional M'zab house. This courtyard, planted with a single mature date palm and bordered by a shallow water channel, functions as the library's environmental core --- cooling air through evaporation before it enters the surrounding reading rooms.

Four wings surround the courtyard. The north wing houses the main reading hall, a double-height space capable of seating forty readers. The east and west wings contain smaller study rooms, a children's section, and administrative offices. The south wing, which receives the harshest sun, is given over to storage and mechanical services, its thick walls acting as a thermal buffer for the occupied spaces beyond.

## Construction

All walls are load-bearing adobe brick, formed on site from a mixture of local clay, sand, and chopped palm fiber. The bricks were dried in the sun for twenty-one days before laying --- a traditional curing period that ensures dimensional stability in the extreme heat.

Roofs are constructed from palm trunk beams supporting a deck of split palm fronds, topped with a thick layer of compacted earth. This traditional roof assembly provides excellent insulation and can be maintained using only locally available materials and skills.

The exterior is finished in lime wash, renewed annually by community volunteers during a collective maintenance event. The lime wash is tinted a pale ochre using natural pigments, matching the color palette of the surrounding historic fabric.

## Light

Natural light enters through carefully sized openings. The main reading hall receives light from a clerestory running its full length, oriented north to avoid direct sun. Deeper in the plan, light wells --- vertical shafts piercing the roof --- deliver focused beams of light to reading tables below. These light wells are fitted with adjustable reed screens that can modulate brightness throughout the day.

A distinctive feature is the south wall of the main hall, constructed from recycled glass bottles embedded in the adobe matrix. These bottles, collected from the town's cafes, transmit a filtered, colored light into the otherwise austere interior. The effect is subtle --- not decorative, but functional, providing ambient illumination to a wall that would otherwise be entirely opaque.

## Community

The library operates without air conditioning. Its environmental strategy is identical in principle to the M'zab houses that have sustained comfortable living conditions in this climate for a millennium: massive walls, courtyard ventilation, evaporative cooling, and careful orientation.

Opening hours follow the desert rhythm. The library closes during the hottest afternoon hours and reopens in the evening, when the courtyard becomes a gathering place for readings, discussions, and film screenings projected onto the lime-washed south wall.
