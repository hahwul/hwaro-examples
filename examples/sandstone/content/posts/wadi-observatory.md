## Site

Wadi Rum's sandstone canyons offer some of the darkest skies in the inhabited world. The observatory occupies a narrow side canyon, its entrance invisible from the main valley floor. Visitors approach on foot along a gravel path that follows a dry streambed for four hundred meters before the building reveals itself --- a low concrete form pressed against the canyon's east wall.

The site was chosen for its natural shielding. The canyon walls block ambient light from all directions except directly overhead, creating an ideal aperture for astronomical observation.

## Architecture

The building is organized as a linear sequence of spaces cut into the canyon floor. A long, partially buried corridor connects three principal rooms: an entrance lobby with orientation displays, the primary observation chamber, and a smaller photographic darkroom.

The observation chamber is the spatial climax. Circular in plan, twelve meters in diameter, it is roofed by a retractable steel dome that opens to expose a telescope mount at its center. When the dome is closed, the room is lit only by a ring of recessed floor lights that emit a deep red glow --- preserving the dark adaptation of observers' eyes.

## Materials

The concrete is pigmented with iron oxide and crushed local sandstone, producing a surface color that closely matches the canyon walls. This was a deliberate decision to minimize the building's visual impact. At midday, when the canyon fills with reflected light, the observatory is nearly invisible.

Cor-ten steel is used for the retractable dome mechanism and exterior cladding of the mechanical plant room. Its rust patina complements the red-brown palette of the landscape. Copper flashing at roof junctions will eventually develop a green patina, providing the only color contrast against the monochrome canyon.

Internal flooring is cut basalt, sourced from volcanic deposits thirty kilometers south. Its dark, dense surface absorbs stray light within the observation chamber and provides thermal mass that moderates the extreme temperature swings between desert day and night.

## Environmental Systems

The building generates no external light. All illumination is internal, shielded, and red-filtered. Mechanical systems --- ventilation, telescope tracking motors, data servers --- are housed in a buried plant room beneath the entrance lobby, acoustically isolated to prevent vibration from reaching the telescope mount.

Power comes from a solar array installed on the canyon rim, connected by buried cable. Battery storage provides twelve hours of autonomous operation, sufficient for a full night of observation without grid connection.

Water is delivered by tanker and stored in an underground cistern. Consumption is minimal --- the building has no permanent residents, hosting visiting researchers for stays of typically three to seven nights.
