## History

The agadir --- a fortified communal granary --- has served Amazigh communities in the Anti-Atlas for centuries. This particular structure, perched on a rocky promontory above the village of Tafraout, dates to the early eighteenth century. By the 1990s, it had fallen into disuse as commercial grain storage supplanted the communal system.

The restoration, commissioned by a regional cultural trust, aimed to preserve the agadir's spatial logic while adapting it for contemporary use as a village archive, exhibition space, and meeting hall.

## Restoration Strategy

The architects' approach was forensic. Each of the 114 storage cells --- small, barrel-vaulted chambers stacked four stories high --- was surveyed and catalogued. Sixty-eight cells were structurally sound and required only repointing with traditional lime mortar. The remaining forty-six were rebuilt using adobe bricks formed from the same clay deposits used by the original builders, identified through soil sampling.

The original juniper timber lintels, darkened and hardened by centuries of exposure, were retained wherever possible. Where replacement was necessary, new juniper beams were sourced from managed groves in the nearby Ameln Valley and left unfinished to weather naturally.

## Adaptation

The ground-floor cells have been opened into a continuous gallery by removing selected partition walls and inserting slender steel columns to maintain structural continuity. This gallery houses rotating exhibitions of regional textiles, pottery, and photographic documentation of Anti-Atlas villages.

The upper levels retain their cellular structure. Individual cells serve as archival storage for documents, oral-history recordings, and agricultural artifacts donated by village families. Climate conditions within the thick-walled cells --- stable temperature, low humidity --- are naturally suited to preservation.

A new rooftop terrace, surfaced in local schist flagstone, provides a gathering space with panoramic views of the surrounding granite peaks. A simple steel-and-canvas shade structure protects the terrace from direct sun without obstructing the view.

## Material Palette

The building's dominant color is the deep red of the local clay, which gives the project its name. This red is not applied but intrinsic --- it is the color of the earth itself, present in every wall surface. Lime plaster, used sparingly on interior vaults, provides a pale counterpoint. The only other materials visible are the grey-brown juniper timber and the dark schist stone flooring.

No paint, no synthetic finishes, no imported cladding. The building's material vocabulary is drawn entirely from a radius of thirty kilometers.
