## Siting

Kasr al-Rimal sits at the boundary where the Liwa date palm groves give way to the open dune field of the Rub' al Khali. The building occupies a deflation hollow --- a natural depression scoured by wind --- placing its roofline below the surrounding terrain. From the access road, only the angled photovoltaic canopy is visible, tilted south at thirty-two degrees to match the local latitude.

The architects chose this position deliberately. By nesting the station in the hollow, they reduced wind exposure on all facades and gained the thermal benefit of earth-sheltered construction on three sides.

## Structure

The primary structure consists of load-bearing rammed-earth walls, each 600 millimeters thick, mixed from site-excavated sand and imported clay binder. The earth was compacted in 150-millimeter lifts, producing a visible stratigraphy in the finished walls that records the slight color variations between sand layers.

A steel portal frame spans the central laboratory bay, where clear spans of nine meters were required to accommodate monitoring equipment. This frame is left exposed internally, its oxidized finish reading as a deliberate material contrast against the warm earth walls.

The roof is a composite assembly: structural steel decking supports a planted substrate layer of native desert scrub, providing additional insulation. Above the laboratory, a separate canopy of photovoltaic glass panels generates electricity while filtering harsh overhead light into a diffused, workable illumination.

## Climate Response

Temperatures in the Liwa region exceed 50 degrees Celsius in summer. The building's environmental strategy relies on thermal mass, earth sheltering, and a buried cooling labyrinth --- a network of underground concrete ducts through which intake air passes before entering the building, arriving at approximately 28 degrees even when external temperatures peak.

Water is the scarcest resource. All grey water is treated on site and directed to the rooftop planting. A fog-collection array on the north facade harvests atmospheric moisture during the brief winter humidity season, supplementing a rainwater cistern that receives runoff from the photovoltaic canopy.

## Interior

The main workspace is a single open volume divided by freestanding earth partitions that stop short of the ceiling, allowing air to circulate freely. Floors are polished concrete, tinted with iron oxide to approximate the color of the surrounding sand.

A library and reading room occupies the quietest corner of the plan, its walls thickened to 800 millimeters to attenuate sound. A single slot window, 200 millimeters wide and three meters tall, frames a view of the dune crest to the south.

Staff quarters are arranged along a covered colonnade on the east side, each room opening onto a small private court planted with a single ghaf tree. These courts function as thermal chimneys, drawing warm air upward and inducing cross-ventilation through the sleeping rooms.
