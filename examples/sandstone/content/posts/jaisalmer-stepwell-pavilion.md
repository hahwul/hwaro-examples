## Context

Jaisalmer --- the Golden City --- is built almost entirely from the yellow sandstone quarried from the Thar Desert plateau on which it sits. The city's medieval stepwells, once vital civic infrastructure, fell into disrepair as piped water systems expanded in the twentieth century. This project restores a fifteenth-century stepwell discovered during road construction and builds a new public pavilion at its threshold.

## The Stepwell

The original structure descends twelve meters through five levels of stepped terraces to a water table that still holds seasonal reserves. Its walls are constructed of precisely fitted sandstone blocks without mortar --- a dry-stone technique that has survived five centuries of seismic activity and flash floods.

The restoration involved clearing decades of debris and rubble, consolidating fractured stone courses, and rebuilding two collapsed terrace sections using salvaged blocks from a nearby demolished structure of similar age. New interventions are distinguished from original fabric by a thin reveal line cut into the replacement stones.

## The Pavilion

Above ground, the new pavilion occupies the footprint of a demolished colonial-era warehouse. It is a single rectangular volume, open on three sides, roofed by a deep sandstone lintel structure that references the stepwell's own post-and-beam construction.

The pavilion's columns are monolithic sandstone shafts, each cut from a single quarry block and finished with the same chisel texture used on the medieval original. The roof is a grid of sandstone beams supporting thin sandstone slabs, with gaps between slabs calibrated to admit narrow bands of light that track across the terrazzo floor as the sun moves.

## Material Continuity

The architects insisted on material continuity between old and new. All stone is sourced from the same Jaisalmer formation, a fine-grained golden limestone that has defined the city's appearance for eight centuries. The stone weathers uniformly, meaning the pavilion will become indistinguishable from its surroundings within a generation.

The terrazzo floor of the pavilion is made from crushed sandstone offcuts mixed into a white cement matrix, producing a warm, speckled surface that reads as an abstraction of the desert floor. This floor extends to the stepwell's edge, where it meets the original stone coping without a joint --- a deliberate ambiguity between new surface and ancient threshold.

## Public Use

The pavilion functions as a shaded public gathering space. No permanent program is assigned. Vendors set up in the mornings; students study in the afternoons; community meetings occur in the evenings. The stepwell below is accessible via a restored stone staircase, and its lower terraces remain cool enough to sit comfortably even in peak summer.

A simple brass railing, anchored into the stone coping, is the only safety intervention. Its minimal profile preserves the spatial drama of the open descent.
