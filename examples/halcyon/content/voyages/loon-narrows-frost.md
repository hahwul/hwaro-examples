The Loon Narrows trip closes our season most years, three days and forty-one kilometres run solo in whatever window opens between the last mild week and the first ice on the eddies. This year that window landed on the eleventh of October, with frost thick enough on the tent fly at dawn that it had to be scraped off before packing, not just shaken.

<!-- more -->

Solo travel on the Narrows is a different trip than the group voyages we usually log. The two portages — Narrows Sill and Cutbank Rapid, both short — go faster alone because there is no argument about the line, but the camp chores go slower without a second set of hands, and by the second night I had rediscovered the old truth that a solo canoeist eats dinner an hour later than a group does, every single time, for reasons that never quite make it into the log.

What the Loon Narrows offers in October that it does not offer in July is silence. By the second week of the month every other party has pulled their canoes for the season, and the loons themselves — the ones the river is named for, still working the open channels before their own migration — were the only other voices on the water for three days straight. Frost smoke stood off the current at dawn both mornings, thick enough to lose the far bank in it until the sun cleared the ridgeline.

Cutbank Rapid, the second portage, is a straightforward carry around a ledge that most trips run loaded in summer water but that gets a harder look this late in the year, when a cold swim carries real consequence and there is nobody behind you on the river to help if the line goes wrong. I carried it, as I do most years, and made Cutbank Landing by early afternoon on day three with the truck already rimed with frost in the shade of the boat ramp. The season closes here every year, and every year it is worth the cold hands to close it properly.
