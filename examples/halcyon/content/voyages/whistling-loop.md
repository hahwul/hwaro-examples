Loop routes are rare enough on our map to log carefully, and the Whistling is the only one that returns you to your own put-in without a shuttle vehicle anywhere in the plan. Eight days out and back, a single height-of-land carry at the top of the loop, and a river that earns its name on any afternoon with wind out of the northwest.

<!-- more -->

The outbound leg follows the Whistling's main branch up to a chain of small lakes, paddling against a gentle current that is more nuisance than obstacle. The Height of Land Carry — 1,050 metres, the longest on this loop — crosses into a headwater creek that feeds the Whistling's own upper branch, which is the trick of the whole route: you are still on the same river system the entire time, just approaching your own beginning from a different direction. Old trip logs at the ranger cabin near the put-in call this "closing the circle," and the phrase has stuck with everyone who has run it since.

Echo Narrows, on the descent, is named for what happens when the wind is up: the walls funnel it and amplify it, and on our second-to-last day it did exactly that, a steady twenty-five-knot whistle that made conversation in the canoe pointless for most of an afternoon. We carried the narrows rather than run them in that wind, more for comfort than necessity, and made camp early at the pool below to let it blow itself out.

The Landing Return carry, the last of the three, drops back into the lake system just above our original put-in — close enough on the last morning that you can hear vehicles on the access road before you see the dock. Camp lore says the loop is best run clockwise, against the prevailing wind on the outbound leg so it pushes you home on the last day instead of fighting you the whole way back. We have never tested the counter-clockwise version and, honestly, do not intend to.
