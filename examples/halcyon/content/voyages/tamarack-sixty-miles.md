Eight days for ninety-seven kilometres is slow by our usual pace, and the Tamarack in late September is exactly the kind of river that deserves it. The tamaracks the river is named for turn gold before anything else in the watershed, and by the last week of September the whole valley is doing it at once — a corridor of yellow needles over water so calm most days that we paddled without a bow wake.

<!-- more -->

Only two portages in sixty miles is unusual on our map, and both of them are short. Split Rock Falls, three days in, is a clean six-metre drop with a well-built trail on river left that the local outfitters maintain every spring — the best-kept carry we log anywhere, benched and graded like a proper footpath rather than a rooted scramble. Old Mill Dam, a relic of a logging operation nobody alive remembers running, is more curiosity than obstacle: a low timber crib that has silted in on the upstream side and left a clean lift-over on the right bank.

What the Tamarack lacks in portages it makes up in camping. The river runs through a band of old-growth white pine for its middle third, and the flat sand terraces under those pines are, by unanimous vote of everyone who has camped there, the best sites in the whole log. We took a full layover day at the terrace above Heron Bend just to sit under the colour and let the fire run long into a cold, clear night — the kind of night where frost forms on the canoe hulls by morning and the coffee gets poured before anyone has said a word.

Camp lore on the Tamarack is mostly about the light. Paddlers who have run it in July say it is a pleasant, unremarkable river. Nobody who has run it in the last week of September says that. The difference is entirely the colour, and entirely worth planning a trip around.
