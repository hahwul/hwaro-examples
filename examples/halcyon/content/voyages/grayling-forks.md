The Grayling Forks Traverse is not a river trip so much as a river-to-river trip: seven days spent leaving one watershed for another, with the Saddle Height portage as the hinge between them. It is the shortest crossing on our map by distance and the longest one by effort, and every year somebody in the group forgets that before they remember it.

<!-- more -->

The East Fork is easy, braided water, good for working the stiffness out of shoulders that have not carried a canoe since last September. We fished it lazily on day one and caught grayling on nearly every cast — small, quick, and so eager that after an hour it stops feeling like sport and starts feeling like a formality you owe the river for the privilege of camping on its bar.

Saddle Height is the trip's whole argument in one carry: 1,240 metres over a height of land with two false summits, a bog crossing in the middle that never fully dries even in a dry July, and a view from the top worth every step of it — both forks visible at once, the East catching morning light and the West still in shadow. We double-carried, which on this trail means four trips per canoe party and a full day accounted for before lunch.

The Fork Connector and Millstone Rip that follow are almost restful by comparison, short carries around a beaver dam complex and a rock sill that has claimed at least one canoe hull we know of by reputation. Camp at the confluence pool below Millstone is the traditional last-night site, and the tradition includes a fire large enough to dry three days of bog mud off every piece of gear in camp, portage packs included. The West Fork carries us out easy the next morning, current doing the work the height of land made us earn.
