The Sable gauge read forty centimetres below its August average when we put in at Sable Lake, and the trip log for this stretch of river got longer for it. What is usually three portages becomes five in a dry year, because rapids that carry a loaded canoe at normal flow turn into rock gardens with no clean line through them, and the honest choice at that point is to walk.

<!-- more -->

Kettle Ledge is a portage in any water level, so that one changed nothing. The two we added — Dry Riffle No. 1 and No. 2 — are usually a scrape-and-line affair, standing in the current and walking the canoe down through the shallowest channel. This year there was no channel deep enough to bother, just exposed cobble from bank to bank, and we carried both rather than grind the hull down to the gelcoat on rock that does not forgive impatience.

Barrenground Chute earned its name honestly this trip: the usual run drops through a slot on river right that was, in August, a dry crack in the bedrock you could stand in. We carried the height of land route instead, an old trail that climbs away from the river entirely and rejoins it below the worst of the ledges — longer, but it does not care what the gauge reads.

The camp lore on the Sable in a low year is about patience with the boat, not the weather. We spent more time this trip reading rock than reading water, walking sections before running them, and got to Kettle Narrows a day ahead of schedule anyway, because portaging a known trail is faster than lining a boat through water too shallow to trust. The last gravel carry into Kettle Narrows itself is barely worth the name — ninety-five metres over smooth stone — but by day six it felt like the longest ninety-five metres on the river, which is its own kind of camp lore, retold at every take-out since.
