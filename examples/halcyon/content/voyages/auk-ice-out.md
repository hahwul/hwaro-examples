The put-in at the Auk headwaters was still rimmed with candle ice on the eighteenth, and we broke camp before the sun cleared the black spruce so the crust would still bear a boot. Nine days later the same river was running brown and full at Cormorant Bay, twelve kilometres of tide flat between us and the truck. That is the whole story of an ice-out trip on the Auk: the river changes character faster than you do.

The first three days are quick water and no portages — the Auk drops fast off the height of land and a loaded canoe wants a bow paddler who reads eddy lines, not scenery. We made Widgeon Camp on day three with daylight to spare and paid for it that night: a north wind came up off the lake basin and held us there an extra day, tent guys doubled, the tarp cracking like a sail all afternoon. Nobody minded much. Widgeon Camp has the best gravel bar on the river for drying gear and a stand of old jack pine that breaks the worst of it.

Below Widgeon the river narrows into Blackwater Ledges, a boulder garden that is a clean Class II at this water level and an unrunnable maze by August. We ran it loaded, scouted the second drop from river left, and were glad of the extra freeboard from nine days of eaten food. The two short carries below — Two Rock and the mosquito-choked portage around a logjam nobody bothers to name — are more about balance than distance.

Tidewater Cutoff, the last portage, is the one that catches people: 640 metres of soft muskeg that floods at the top of the tide and turns the trail into a wade. We hit it three hours before high water and walked it dry, then paddled the last stretch on a rising tide that did half the work for us. Camp lore holds that the Auk gives you the current back if you carried honestly upstream of it. I do not know if that is true. I know we made Cormorant Bay by noon with the flood still pushing, and that is the kind of ending this river rewards.
