The terms grotesque and gothic both refer to sans-serif typefaces developed in the nineteenth century, but the labels carry different meanings depending on the foundry tradition. British and continental European sources use grotesque for the entire category. American sources use gothic for the same broad family and reserve grotesque for a narrower subset. The result is a long-standing terminological confusion that the working designer should be aware of without being trapped by.

## Structural Characteristics

The early grotesques and gothics share a set of structural properties: monoline or near-monoline strokes, closed apertures, modest contrast between vertical and horizontal weights, and a slightly squared treatment of curves. Compared to the later neo-grotesques such as Helvetica and Univers, the early designs feel more idiosyncratic, with quirks in the lowercase a, e, g, and t that the rationalised twentieth-century revisions deliberately smoothed out.

## Display Use

Heavy gothics excel at display sizes precisely because their large x-height, closed apertures, and uniform stroke weight remain legible in the brief fixation times typical of poster reading. The reader does not parse the letters individually at sixty or ninety points; the silhouette of each word does the work, and the gothic's even colour produces a stronger silhouette than a contrasted serif at the same size. This is why nineteenth-century broadside printers preferred wood gothics for the largest lines and reserved fatface serifs for secondary display.

## Modern Practice

Contemporary revivals such as Klim's Untitled Sans, Commercial Type's Druk, and Berton Hasebe's Druk-adjacent designs lean on the heavy display gothic tradition. The defining move is to push the weight axis far past the conventional black, into compressed and ultra-condensed cuts that recover the impact of the wood-type originals while remaining usable in digital workflows. The trade-off is the same as it ever was: extreme weight buys impact at the cost of legibility below display sizes.
