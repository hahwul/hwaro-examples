Type designed for body text begins to misbehave above thirty-six points. Letterspacing opens up into visible gaps, kerning pairs that read tightly at ten points read loosely at sixty, and tracking values that produce comfortable text become almost cartoonishly wide at display sizes. The designer who sets a sixty-point headline using the same parameters as the body text produces a headline that looks unintentional.

## Negative Tracking

Most display work requires negative tracking. The exact value depends on the typeface, but a starting point of minus ten to minus twenty thousandths of an em removes the spacing slack that body settings build in. The goal is not to crash letters into each other but to restore the visual rhythm that the typeface holds at its design size. Many display-specific typefaces are drawn with tighter sidebearings to begin with and require less manual correction.

## Optical Adjustments

Letter pairs that look fine at body size show their seams at display size. The cap-T over a lowercase r leaves a visible gap. The cap-W next to a lowercase a leans away from the next letter. Manual kerning of display lines is unavoidable, and the typographer should expect to adjust five to fifteen pairs in a typical headline. The corrections are small, often two or three thousandths of an em, but they accumulate into a more cohesive line.

## Line Spacing

Default leading at display sizes opens too far. A sixty-point headline set on seventy-two-point leading reads as two disconnected lines rather than a single block. Tightening the leading to a value close to the cap height of the typeface, often around eighty-five percent of the point size, knits the lines together visually. Care is required with descenders: a tight leading that crashes a y or g into the cap of the line below is worse than the original loose setting.

## Optical Sizes

Type families that ship optical-size variants make most of these adjustments unnecessary. The display cut is drawn with the corrections built in: tighter sidebearings, sharper terminals, more contrast in serif designs, less in sans-serif. When an optical variant is available, use it and reserve manual adjustment for the residual cases the variant does not cover.
