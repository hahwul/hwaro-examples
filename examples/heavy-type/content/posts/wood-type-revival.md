Wood type was developed in the 1820s to solve a specific problem: metal type at display sizes was too heavy to handle, too expensive to cast in volume, and too brittle to survive the impression pressures required for poster work. End-grain hardwood, cut on a router controlled by a pantograph, produced large-format letters that were lighter, cheaper, and more durable. By 1850, wood type dominated the broadside and theatrical poster trades.

## Decline and Persistence

Photolithography displaced wood type for commercial poster work through the twentieth century, and most foundries closed by the 1950s. Hamilton in Wisconsin continued limited production and held the bulk of the surviving inventory in trust. Studios such as Hatch Show Print in Nashville kept the equipment in daily use for music and entertainment posters, preserving operational knowledge that would otherwise have been lost.

## Why It Returned

The current revival rests on three properties of the medium that no digital workflow reproduces cleanly. The first is impression: a heavy strike from end-grain maple produces a debossed letterform with ink density that registers physically on the paper. The second is wear: every block carries dings, soft corners, and ink-fill patterns specific to its history, producing variation that designers find interesting and clients are willing to pay for. The third is constraint: a job case offers what it offers, and the designer composes within the available characters rather than specifying anything desired.

## Practical Limits

Wood type rewards typographers who plan around its limitations. Letter spacing is fixed by the body width of each block. Mixing faces across the same line requires careful makeready to bring the tops of the letters into common alignment. Long runs are not feasible: the type wears visibly after a few hundred impressions. The medium suits short runs of large posters and resists almost everything else.
