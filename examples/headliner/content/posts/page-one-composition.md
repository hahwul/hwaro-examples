The front page of a daily newspaper communicates a hierarchy of importance before the reader has parsed a single word. The size of the lead headline, the position of the main image, the depth of the columns, and the use of rules and white space together produce a map of editorial judgement that the reader absorbs at a glance.

## The Lead Story

The lead occupies the top right of the page in most Western broadsheet traditions, with a headline running across two to four columns and body text dropping below in a deeper measure than secondary stories. The lead headline carries the largest point size on the page and uses the heaviest weight available in the masthead family. A reader who sees a forty-eight-point lead next to a thirty-point sublead understands without being told which story the editors consider more important.

## Above and Below the Fold

The fold divides the broadsheet into two zones with different attention values. Stories above the fold are visible in the rack or on the newsstand and earn the strongest display treatment. Stories below the fold rely on the reader having already picked up the paper. Editorial conventions reserve the above-fold space for the lead, the second story, and a strong image; below-fold space carries the third and fourth stories and the index to inside pages.

## Rules and White Space

Vertical rules between columns separate stories in dense compositions. Horizontal rules above headlines reinforce hierarchy. White space carries weight equal to the rules: a generous margin around a single-column item draws the eye more reliably than a heavy rule around a wider one. Modern broadsheets use rules sparingly, relying on the grid and on consistent vertical spacing to guide the reader through the page.
