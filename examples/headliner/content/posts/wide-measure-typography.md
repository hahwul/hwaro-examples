The traditional broadsheet uses a measure of twelve to fourteen picas per column, set in roughly nine-point text on eleven-point leading. The narrow column compensates for the size of the page and keeps the eye from losing its place across long lines. When a newspaper widens its measure beyond fifteen picas, readers complain of fatigue and editors complain of stories running short.

## Line Length and Reading Speed

Reading studies through the twentieth century converged on a comfortable line length of sixty-six characters, including spaces. Below forty-five characters, the eye is forced into too many returns and reading becomes choppy. Above seventy-five, the return to the next line becomes unreliable and the eye drifts. Headline text follows different rules because it is read in fewer fixations and tolerates much wider measures, but body copy hews close to the sixty-six character target.

## Multi-Column Layouts

Most editorial pages divide a broadsheet into five or six columns and combine them in different proportions for different stories. A lead feature might run across two columns at fourteen picas each. A short news item might use a single column. The grid imposes a discipline that the reader perceives as authority, even when the reader is unaware of the underlying structure.

## Display Typography

Above the body, headline typography exploits the wide measure that text cannot. A four-column headline at sixty points produces an immediate visual hierarchy and signals to the reader where the most important story sits on the page. The contrast between the wide display measure and the narrow body measure is the primary tool of front-page composition. Lose the contrast and the page reads as undifferentiated grey.
