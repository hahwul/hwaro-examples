A jump is the continuation of a front-page story onto an inside page. Editors use jumps to fit more stories on page one than would otherwise be possible and to give important stories the depth they require without surrendering the entire front page to a single piece. The jump introduces a small typographic problem: the reader must follow the story across a discontinuity and find the continuation reliably on a different page.

## Jump Lines

The jump line at the bottom of the front-page section names the inside page where the story continues. Standard practice sets the jump line in italic at body size, prefixed by a continuation symbol or the word "continued." The corresponding pickup line on the inside page restates the headline in a smaller display size and notes the page from which the story jumped. Readers locate jumps by scanning headlines on the inside page, so the pickup line must be visible without requiring the reader to read the body text.

## Length Control

A well-edited jump runs no more than three quarters of the original front-page length on the inside page. Longer jumps suggest the editor underestimated the space the story would need and split it arbitrarily. Shorter jumps suggest padding. The discipline of running tight jumps forces the editor to make decisions about which paragraphs belong on page one and which belong inside, which in turn improves the front-page version.

## Reader Behaviour

Surveys of newspaper readers show that fewer than half follow a jump to its conclusion. The implication for the front-page editor is straightforward: the most important information must appear above the jump. The jump itself carries supporting detail, background, and the closing material that benefits the engaged reader without being essential to the casual one.
