A typeface is not a font. A font is a file. A typeface is a system of visual decisions: stroke width, x-height, aperture, contrast, rhythm. Choosing a typeface means choosing all of those decisions at once.

## The structural role

Type does not sit on a page. It builds the page. The weight of a heading establishes visual gravity. The width of a paragraph determines reading rhythm. The space between lines controls pace.

Get these wrong and no amount of color, imagery, or layout will compensate.

## Pairing as counterpoint

Good type pairings work like counterpoint in music. One voice leads, the other supports. They share enough to feel related, differ enough to feel distinct.

The classic pairing of serif headings and sans-serif body text works because:

- **Serif** brings authority and formality to headings
- **Sans-serif** brings clarity and efficiency to body text
- The contrast creates natural hierarchy without additional styling

But the reverse can work equally well. Sans-serif headings feel modern and direct. Serif body text feels literary and considered.

## Monospace as punctuation

A third voice, monospace, serves as typographic punctuation. It signals precision: dates, code, metadata. Used sparingly, it adds texture. Used heavily, it becomes noise.

```
2025.05.15 — 4 min read
```

That line is monospace doing its job. It is data, not prose, and it looks different because it *is* different.

## The IBM Plex system

This site uses the IBM Plex family: Serif for headings, Sans for body, Mono for metadata. Three faces from a single design system. They share skeletal proportions and differ in surface treatment.

That shared skeleton is what makes the pairing feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

---

Choose type like you choose materials for a building. Not for appearance, but for how it bears weight.
