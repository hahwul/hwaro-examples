The rule is simple and old: gather your wood before dark. Not during dark. Not after. Before. Because once the light goes, the forest changes. The fallen branch you could see from thirty paces becomes invisible. The ground, which was legible in daylight, turns into a uniform black surface that your feet must interpret alone.

I learned this from a man named Earl who ran a canoe outfitter on the edge of the Boundary Waters. Earl was not a philosopher. He would have been embarrassed by the word. But he had the kind of practical intelligence that comes from decades of solving the same problems under slightly different conditions.

Gather your wood before dark. Set your tent before you are tired. Eat before you are hungry. Each instruction was a small argument against the human tendency to wait until need becomes crisis.

There is a right way to gather firewood. You want dead wood, dry and off the ground. Standing dead trees are best --- still upright but no longer living, their bark peeling, their wood gray and light. A standing dead tree has been drying in the wind for months. It will catch quickly and burn hot. Fallen wood has been absorbing water through its underside. It will hiss and smoke and produce more frustration than heat.

The gathering itself is a kind of quiet focus. You walk through the forest with your eyes adjusted to a specific task. You are looking for the particular: the right diameter, the right color, the right sound when you knock on it with your knuckle. A dead branch sounds hollow. A green one sounds dense. Your ear learns the difference before your mind can explain it.

I carry the wood back in armloads, stacking it near the fire ring in order of thickness. Tinder first, then kindling, then fuel wood. The stack is a plan made physical. It represents forethought, the boring and essential virtue that separates a comfortable night from a miserable one.

Earl died three years ago. I think of him every time I gather wood. The light is going. The air is cooling. I have enough for the night, and a little more for the morning. This is preparedness: not abundance, but sufficiency. Knowing what you need and getting it while you still can.
