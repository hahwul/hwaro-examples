The town was smaller than I remembered, which is what everyone says and what is almost never true. Towns do not shrink. It is the person returning who has expanded, and the difference in scale feels like a loss. The gas station where my mother used to let me choose a candy bar is a tire shop now. The candy bar cost thirty-five cents and I always chose the same one, and that specificity is what I remember most clearly. Not the taste. The certainty.

I drove the old roads with the windows down. This is important. You cannot revisit a place through glass. You need the air, the particular mix of dust and pine and cut grass that your body recognizes before your mind does.

The creek was still running behind the elementary school. This surprised me, though I do not know why it should have. Creeks do not close. But there is a part of childhood geography that feels provisional, as though the landscape existed only for your use and might have been dismantled after you left.

We used to build dams here. Terrible dams, made of sticks and mud and the occasional stolen brick, that lasted half an afternoon before the water found its way through. The engineering was beside the point. What mattered was the collaboration --- four or five children working together on something clearly impossible, arguing about placement, getting their shoes wet, achieving a brief and glorious obstruction.

The willows were larger now, their branches trailing in the water with a kind of elderly grace. The creek did not care that I had come back. It had been making this sound for years, for every child and for no child. This is the great democracy of moving water. It offers itself to everyone equally and is diminished by no one.

I did not try to build a dam. That would have been sentimental. Instead I picked up a flat stone and skipped it across the water --- three jumps and a sink --- and then I walked back to the car. The map in my head had been redrawn. Not corrected. Adjusted.
