The summit was never the point. I understood this somewhere around the third hour, when the trail bent away from the tree line and the wind hit me full in the chest like a door thrown open. The mountain did not care that I had come to stand on top of it. It had been here for a hundred million years. My ambition was irrelevant.

I kept climbing anyway. Not out of stubbornness, exactly, but because the body in motion tends to stay in motion, and because the light was doing something extraordinary to the western ridge --- laying itself down in long amber sheets across the rock face, turning every crevice into a line of gold.

By the time I reached the saddle between the two peaks, the sun was a hand's width above the horizon. The valley below had already gone to shadow. Up here, though, the air still held warmth, the way a room holds warmth after someone has left it. I sat on a flat stone and ate the last of my bread and cheese, and for ten minutes I did not think about anything at all. This is rarer than it sounds.

The ridge walk that followed was the best part. There is a particular quality to high places at dusk. The world simplifies itself. You can see the shape of the land without the distraction of detail --- the long curve of the river valley, the dark mass of the forest, the far mountains already turning to silhouette.

I thought about the people who had walked this ridge before me. Shepherds, probably. Surveyors. Soldiers, during one war or another. Each of them had seen this same view, though the light would have been different and the trees younger or older. The mountain holds all of these moments simultaneously. We are the ones who experience them one at a time.

I reached the trailhead in near darkness. I sat behind the wheel for a long time before starting the engine. Not because I was tired, though I was. But because I wanted to hold the last of it --- the light, the ridge, the wind --- before the drive home turned it into a memory.
