The rain started at three in the morning. Not gradually, as rain sometimes does, with a few exploratory drops before committing. This rain arrived all at once, a curtain of water dropped from the full height of the sky, and the sound it made on the tent fabric was enormous --- a sustained, percussive roar that erased every other sound in the forest.

I lay in my sleeping bag and listened. There was nothing else to do. My boots were outside the tent door, which I would regret in the morning.

Rain on canvas is one of those sounds that people describe as soothing, and in the right circumstances it can be. But there is a threshold beyond which the sound stops being a lullaby and becomes a confrontation. This was that rain. It was the sky reminding you that your shelter is a membrane, a few millimeters of coated nylon between you and the full indifference of weather.

I thought about the tent. About how strange it is that we trust our lives to fabric. A house has walls and a foundation and the accumulated authority of architecture. A tent has poles and stakes and a zipper. It is shelter reduced to its most essential argument: you are on this side, and the rain is on that side, and for now, this arrangement holds.

The rain continued through dawn. When light finally filtered through the tent walls, I unzipped the door and looked out at a world that had been remade. Everything was wet --- not just the surfaces but the air itself, thick and silver and close. The creek, which had been modest the evening before, was now wide and hurried and the color of milky tea.

I pulled my boots from under the vestibule. They were full of water. The coffee I made on the camp stove, crouching under a tarp, was the best coffee I have ever had. Not because of the beans. Because of the context. Warmth entering a cold body. A hot cup held in wet hands. Comfort is relative. It requires discomfort to exist, the way light requires shadow.
