The river has been here longer than the town. This is easy to say and difficult to feel. The buildings along the bank are old by human standards --- brick storefronts from the 1890s, a stone bridge that has carried horses and then automobiles and now joggers with headphones. But the river predates all of it.

I come here in the late afternoons, when the light turns the water from brown to amber. There is a bench on the east bank, placed there by someone who understood that certain views require sitting. I bring a book but rarely read it.

What fascinates me is the paradox. The river is the same river it was yesterday and last year and a century ago. People call it by the same name. They point at it on maps. But not a single molecule of water in it today was there yesterday. The river is entirely new, moment by moment, and entirely old in form. It is a pattern, not a thing. A verb disguised as a noun.

This is true of people, too. The cells in your body are not the cells you were born with. Your thoughts today bear little resemblance to your thoughts at twenty. You are, in every measurable way, a different person than you were a decade ago. And yet you feel continuous. The self is a river. It keeps its shape and changes its substance.

The bridge upstream throws a shadow across the water that moves with the sun. The fish know the shadow. I have watched them gather at its edge, holding steady in the current with small corrections of their tails, waiting for insects that the shadow dislodges from the underside of the bridge. Patience, in nature, is almost always rewarded.

A heron lands on the far bank with the mechanical precision of a folding chair, arranges itself, and goes still. It will stand there until something moves beneath it. I admire the heron's clarity of purpose. It knows what it is waiting for. Most of us are not so fortunate.

The river does not remember anything. That is its genius. It carries everything downstream and arrives at each moment clean. Memory is a human burden, and perhaps a human gift. We are the ones who sit on the bank and insist that the water means something. The river just moves.
