A relief block is an exercise in subtraction. You do not draw the image; you remove everything that is not the image. The ink will sit on whatever surface you leave standing, and the cuts &mdash; the channels your tools open &mdash; will print as paper. This inversion is the first thing the hand has to learn, and it never fully stops being strange. You are carving the *negative* and thinking in the *positive*.

## The Block

Tradition gives you two broad families of block. There is the **plank-grain** block, sawn along the length of the log, where the grain runs in long parallel fibers across the face. And there is the **end-grain** block, cut across the trunk so the fibers stand up like the ends of a bundle of straws. Albrecht Dürer worked plank; Thomas Bewick built his reputation on end-grain boxwood, which is hard enough and uniform enough to hold a line as fine as an engraving.

For most relief work I reach for one of three surfaces:

- **Lime (basswood)** &mdash; soft, even, forgiving; the wood that lets a beginner make clean cuts
- **Pearwood** &mdash; close-grained, dense, and willing to hold detail without splintering
- **Battleship linoleum** &mdash; no grain at all, the same in every direction, which is exactly why some carvers love it and some find it dead under the tool

Linoleum is honest about one thing: it has no opinion. Wood has opinions. The grain wants the tool to go one way and resists the other, and that resistance is the whole subject of this post.

## The Tools

The carving tools for relief are few and they are old in their logic.

- The **V-tool** (or veiner) cuts a sharp-bottomed trench and is what you use for outlines and for the thin white lines that describe a form.
- The **U-gouge** scoops a round-bottomed channel; in wide sweeps it clears the large fields that will print as open paper.
- The **flat chisel** levels and cleans the floor of a cleared area so it sits below the printing surface and stays clean of ink.
- The **knife**, held like a pen, makes the first incision around a shape so the gouge has an edge to break against.

A sharp tool is not a luxury here; it is the difference between a cut and a tear. A dull gouge crushes the wood fibers ahead of it and leaves a ragged shoulder that prints as a smear. The single most useful habit at the bench is stropping the tool before you think you need to.

> "The wood will tell you which way it wants to be cut. Your job is to listen before it splinters, not after."

## With the Grain, and Against It

Cut *with* the grain &mdash; in the direction the fibers run &mdash; and the tool glides; the channel comes up clean and the shoulder stays crisp. Cut *against* it &mdash; into the fibers head-on &mdash; and the wood lifts and tears ahead of the blade, pulling out a chip you never meant to lose. The cruelest case is cutting *across* the grain on a plank block, where a long fiber can split well past the end of your stroke and take a printing surface with it.

The craft, then, is constant rotation. You turn the block, not the tool, so that every cut meets the grain at a friendly angle. Carve a curved line and you may turn the block three times inside a single arc. This is why a printmaker's bench has a turntable or a sandbag cushion under the block: so the wood can swing to meet the gouge.

When you genuinely must cut against the grain &mdash; and sometimes the design leaves no choice &mdash; you take it in shallow passes. A series of light cuts removes the same wood as one deep cut, but each shallow pass severs the fiber cleanly before it can run. The deep cut is faster and the deep cut is how you ruin an afternoon's work in one second.

## Carving for the Ink

Everything you cut has to serve the eventual ink. Three rules govern the relief block:

1. **Undercut nothing.** A surface that overhangs its base will catch the brayer, tear under pressure, and crumble within a dozen pulls. Cut your walls sloping outward, wider at the floor than at the printing face.
2. **Clear deep enough.** A field that should read as white must sit far enough below the surface that the roller cannot reach it. Lazy clearing prints as gray fog.
3. **Leave the shoulders bold.** Fine isolated ridges of standing wood snap off or fill with ink. Relief rewards the broad, confident mass over the timid hairline.

Here is the editioning notation I scratch into the margin of the block before the first proof, so I never lose count of an edition once it begins:

```
EDITION: 12/40        block: pearwood, plank
state: II             ink: carbon black + vermilion
12  =  this impression's number in the run
40  =  total prints the edition will contain
```

That fraction &mdash; **12/40** &mdash; is a promise. It says the block will give forty good impressions and then the matrix is done. Cutting against the grain is the part of the craft that decides whether the block can keep that promise, or whether it falls apart at impression thirty.

The grain is not the enemy. It is the material telling you, in the oldest language there is, how it wants to become a picture. Carve in conversation with it and the block will print clean, deep, and black &mdash; forty times over.
