People who are new to prints often ask which one is the *real* one &mdash; as if among forty impressions there were a single original and thirty-nine copies of it. The question is reasonable and the answer is the most important idea in the whole craft: there is no original to copy. Every impression in the edition is an original. A relief print is a **multiple**, not a reproduction, and the difference is not a technicality. It is the entire point.

## The Matrix

At the center of every print is the **matrix** &mdash; the carved block, the etched plate, the drawn stone. The matrix is the thing the artist makes. It is not a picture of an image that exists somewhere else; it *is* the image, held in a form that can give that image up to paper under pressure.

When you pull a print, ink passes from the matrix to the sheet. Nothing is scanned, nothing is photographed, nothing is measured and re-drawn. The mark on the paper is the direct physical descendant of the cut in the wood. That unbroken physical line &mdash; gouge to block, block to ink, ink to paper &mdash; is what makes the result an original. The matrix is the parent and every impression is its own child, not a photograph of a sibling.

## Reproduction Is the Opposite Thing

Contrast this with reproduction. A reproduction begins with a finished artwork and makes likenesses of it: a photograph of a painting, a giclée of a drawing, a poster of a poster. The chain runs *artwork &rarr; capture &rarr; output*, and every step is a translation of something that already existed complete on its own.

A relief print has no such prior artwork. There is no painting the print is *of*. The block was never a finished picture to be photographed; it is unviewable as art in the ordinary sense &mdash; a slab of cut wood, the image reversed and recessed. Only the act of printing brings the picture into being, and it brings it into being *every single time*, freshly, in ink.

> "A reproduction asks how faithfully you copied the original. An edition asks how many originals the matrix can give."

## What the Numbers Mean

This is why the numbers in the margin matter, and why printmakers are fussy about them. You will see a fraction penciled below a print:

```
12 / 40        the twelfth impression of an edition of forty
A/P            artist's proof, outside the numbered run
B.A.T.         bon à tirer — "good to pull," the approved standard
state II       the second cutting-state of the matrix
```

The lower number &mdash; **40** &mdash; is the size of the edition: how many impressions the artist has decided the matrix will yield. The upper number &mdash; **12** &mdash; is *not* a ranking. Impression 12 is no more or less original than impression 1 or impression 40. The fraction is a census, not a leaderboard. It records that this sheet is one of a known, finite family, each member pulled from the same matrix by the same hand.

When the edition is complete, a serious printmaker **cancels the block** &mdash; cuts a deliberate line across it, or planes the surface &mdash; so it can never yield a forty-first. The cancellation is a kind of honesty. It guarantees that the number 40 told the truth.

## Why Relief, Specifically

Relief printing makes all of this especially vivid because the matrix is so blunt and so physical:

- The **surface** prints; the cuts read as paper. You can see the logic of the image just by looking at the block in raking light.
- Every pull **wears** the block a little, which is why editions are finite and why early and late impressions can differ in weight. The matrix is mortal.
- The ink sits in a faint **relief** on the paper &mdash; you can feel the impression with a fingertip. A reproduction is perfectly flat; an original relief print is not.

These are not flaws to be corrected toward some ideal of sameness. They are the evidence that a hand pressed a block to a sheet. The slight differences from impression to impression &mdash; a touch more ink here, the grain of this sheet against that one &mdash; are the signature of the process, not noise in it.

## The Plain Version

So: when someone holds up a numbered relief print and asks where the original is, the honest answer is to gesture at the whole stack. *They are all the original.* The edition is a set of equally legitimate first-generation objects, each born from the same matrix, each carrying the direct mark of the tool.

A copy hides its origins and pretends to be the thing it imitates. An edition does the opposite. It announces, in a fraction penciled in the corner, exactly how it was made and exactly how many of it the world will ever hold. That candor &mdash; **12/40**, and not one more &mdash; is what a print is, and what a copy can never be.
