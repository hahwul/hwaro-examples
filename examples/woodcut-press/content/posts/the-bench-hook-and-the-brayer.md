Once the block is cut, the carving is the easy half. The hard half is the *pulling* &mdash; getting an even, dense, repeatable impression off that block and onto paper, again and again, until the edition is complete. This is bench work, and it lives or dies on two humble objects: the **bench hook** and the **brayer**.

## The Bench Hook

A bench hook is the least glamorous tool in the shop and the one I would replace first if it vanished. It is simply a board with a lip on the underside that catches the front edge of the bench, and a second lip on top that the block butts against. You push the block forward into the top lip, the lip pushes back, and your carving hand is suddenly free of the job of holding the block still.

Before the bench hook, every cut is a negotiation between the tool and a block that wants to slide. After it, the block is anchored and the cut is the only thing you have to think about. It also keeps the hand that is *not* carving out of the path of a slipping gouge &mdash; and a relief gouge slips toward whatever is in front of it. More than one printmaker carries a thin white scar that the bench hook would have prevented.

## The Slab and the Ink

Relief ink is stiff. It has to be: it must cling to the surface of the block without flooding into the cuts, and it must transfer cleanly under pressure without smearing. I work an **oil-based** ink for the depth of its black and the long open time it gives me on a warm day; water-based inks are faster to clean but skin over quickly.

You roll the ink out on a **slab** &mdash; a sheet of plate glass, an offcut of marble, or an acrylic inking plate. The sound tells you when it is ready. Drop a knife-tip of ink on the cold slab and roll it thin; at first it is silent and sticky, then as the film evens out across the slab it begins to *hiss*. That hiss &mdash; printmakers call it the right "tack" &mdash; means the ink is distributed in a film fine enough to charge the roller evenly.

A note on the two inks I hold to:

- **Carbon black** does the work: the figures, the text, the mass of the image.
- **Vermilion** does one thing only, and does it loudly: a catchword, a single ornament, a rule. One spot color earns its place precisely because it is rationed.

## The Brayer

The **brayer** is the hand roller that carries ink from the slab to the block. A good one has a soft rubber or polyurethane roller that flexes just enough to ride the printing surface without dipping into the cleared fields.

Charging the brayer is its own small ritual. You roll it through the inked slab in several directions until the roller wears an even, matte coat &mdash; never a wet, shiny one. Then you bring it to the block and roll in light passes, lifting and turning between each so the roller does not lay a seam of heavy ink along one track. Three or four directions, light pressure, and you stop the moment the surface reads a uniform velvet black. Over-ink and the cuts fill and the image goes muddy; under-ink and the impression comes up gray and starved.

> "Ink the block until it looks right, then stop. The press will find every place you were greedy."

## Registration

If the design uses both inks, the black block and the vermilion block must land in the same place on every sheet. That is **registration**, and the simplest reliable jig is a sheet of card with the block's position and the paper's corner both marked on it &mdash; an **L-shaped registration guide**. You seat the block in its outline, lay the paper to the marked corner, and every pull repeats the same geometry.

For a two-ink print I pull the entire edition in black first and let it dry. Only then do I clean down, ink the second block in vermilion, and run the whole stack through again. Trying to register wet-on-wet is how a clean edition turns into a smeared one.

## The Pull

The pull is the moment of truth, and you can do it with or without a press.

1. **By press**, the platen squeezes block and paper together under even, heavy force. You set the pressure, run the bed through, and lift a finished impression.
2. **By hand**, you lay the paper on the inked block and *burnish* the back &mdash; with a smooth Japanese **baren**, or, in a pinch, the bowl of a wooden spoon &mdash; rubbing in firm circles until the ink lifts to the paper.

Burnishing by hand teaches you something the press hides: you can *feel* the image come up through the paper. Where the burnisher passes, the back of the sheet takes on a faint sheen and the black underneath goes solid. You learn to read that sheen and to lean harder on the broad blacks and lighter over the fine cuts. Then you peel a corner, check the impression against the light, lay it back down, and work the thin spots before committing to the lift.

The first clean pull off a fresh block is the reason for all of it &mdash; the bench hook that held it, the slab that hissed, the brayer that laid the velvet coat. Everything before the pull is preparation. The pull is the print.
