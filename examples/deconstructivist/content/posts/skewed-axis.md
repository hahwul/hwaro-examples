The orthogonal grid is the inherited language of modernism. Walls meet at ninety degrees, slabs stack horizontally, columns drop vertically. Deconstructivism rejects this convention not for novelty but to expose the assumptions buried inside it.

## Two Axes, One Building

A skewed axis is not a tilted axis. Tilting implies a single rotation applied to a fixed frame. Skewing means two frames coexist within the same volume, each refusing to defer to the other. The viewer's eye is forced to track both simultaneously.

Bernard Tschumi's Parc de la Villette demonstrates this principle through three superimposed systems: points, lines, and surfaces. None of the systems acknowledges the others. Their collisions generate the program of the park rather than its violations.

## Implementation in Form

In built work, the skewed axis is achieved through several techniques:

- Structural columns rotated relative to the cladding grid.
- Floor plates that pivot between levels rather than stacking.
- Window mullions running off-parallel to the wall behind them.
- Ramps and stairs that ignore the orthogonal flow of corridors.

Each of these choices has a structural cost. The fabricator must produce one-off components rather than repeating modules. The contractor must hold tighter tolerances. The architect accepts these costs as the price of the formal argument.

## The Reading

A building with a skewed axis cannot be read at a glance. The visitor must move, must compare, must reconstruct the geometry from successive views. The plan and the elevation tell different stories. The section reveals contradictions that neither resolves.

This is not obscurantism. It is an argument that buildings should reward sustained attention, that the gestalt is a reduction rather than a synthesis. The skewed axis enforces patience.
