## Identification

The largest nudibranch species in most reef systems. Body is flattened and oval, typically deep red to orange-red in color. The mantle edge is often lighter with a pale or white margin. Six branching gills (the origin of *Hexabranchus*) are visible at the posterior end when the animal is crawling.

When disturbed or swimming, the mantle unfurls to reveal a broad, ruffled margin that undulates through the water -- resembling the skirt of a flamenco dancer.

## Habitat and Behavior

Strictly nocturnal. During the day, Spanish dancers shelter in crevices, under coral ledges, or within sponge cavities. They emerge after dark to forage on sponges, which form the bulk of their diet.

**Swimming:** Unlike most nudibranchs, Spanish dancers can swim by flattening the body and performing rhythmic undulations of the mantle. This behavior is primarily a predator escape response but may also be used for covering distance between foraging areas.

**Egg masses:** Produces distinctive rose-shaped egg ribbons, bright red-pink in color, deposited on hard substrate. The eggs contain toxic compounds derived from dietary sponges, deterring predation.

## Dive Encounters

Found exclusively on night dives. Search sand flats and rubble zones adjacent to reef structure between 8-20 meters. Their red coloration makes them visible under torch light against pale sand.

Use a red filter when approaching to avoid triggering a retreat. If the animal begins swimming, observe from a distance -- pursuing a swimming Spanish dancer will cause it to expend energy and settle in a less favorable location.

## Notes

Often hosts the commensal shrimp *Periclimenes imperator* (emperor shrimp), which rides on the dorsal surface. Check for these small translucent shrimp -- they are a rewarding macro photography subject.
