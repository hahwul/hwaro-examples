The summit closes where it should: on the question of justice. Every technical
choice in the previous four tracks — which street to defend, which to buy out, who
pays and when — is also a choice about fairness. Coastal adaptation redistributes
risk, safety, and belonging, and pretending otherwise is how good plans lose the
people they are meant to protect.

<!-- more -->

Dr. Lindqvist frames the whole afternoon: adaptation is a distribution question
wearing an engineering costume. Sea walls protect some properties and, by changing
where the water goes, expose others. Buyouts rescue those who can afford to wait
for a fair offer and strand those who cannot. She offers a simple equity screen
that any council can run over a draft plan before it is adopted.

Tomas Berg follows with the practice of real participation. Too many consultations
are held after the decision is effectively made, in rooms and at hours that exclude
the people most affected. He describes how the Fjordside assembly gave residents
genuine authority over the sequencing of their own retreat — and what changed once
they had it.

We close together, quietly. When a place goes under, its maps, its street names,
and its ordinary memories go with it. The final session gathers residents,
archivists, and artists to ask how a community keeps the memory of a home it has
to leave — and sends everyone back to their own coast with that question in hand.
