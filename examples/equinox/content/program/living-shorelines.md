Not every coast should be defended with concrete, and not every coast can be
retreated from tomorrow. The Living Shorelines track is about the middle path:
restored wetlands, rebuilt dunes, and living breakwaters that absorb wave energy
while doing the things concrete never will — filtering water, storing carbon, and
bringing a place back to life.

<!-- more -->

Dr. Vanden Broeck begins with the physics, because nature-based defence is often
oversold. He shows, honestly, how much wave energy a salt marsh of a given width
actually attenuates, where oyster reefs help and where they don't, and why a dune
is only as good as the vegetation holding it together. The message is not that
green defences are magic, but that they are measurable — and measurably good value
when the accounting is done right.

Nadia Fischer takes up that accounting. Grey infrastructure has a seductive
certainty: you can point to a wall. Green infrastructure delivers over decades and
across budget lines that rarely talk to each other. She lays out a costing method
that lets a water board compare a sea wall and a restored marsh on the same page,
including the maintenance costs everyone forgets.

We end at dusk with the people who did it. The stewards of the Sønderstrand
restoration share ten years of field notes — the storms that tested the dunes, the
years the marsh nearly failed, and the summer it finally held.
