Managed retreat is the hardest sentence a mayor ever has to say out loud, and the
afternoon of Day 1 treats it with the seriousness it deserves. Retreat is not
failure; it is a strategy, and often the cheapest and kindest one available. But
it only works if it is done with people rather than to them.

<!-- more -->

Dr. Njoroge opens by reclaiming the vocabulary. "Managed retreat" has become a
phrase councils use precisely so they don't have to say "we are helping you leave
your home." She argues that the euphemism itself erodes trust, and offers language
that is both honest and humane.

The centre of the track is practical. Sofie Karlsen presents Vestby's voluntary
buyout programme — how it valued homes, how it sequenced offers so that early
sellers didn't strand their neighbours, and how it kept a community intact by
relocating households together rather than scattering them across the region. The
numbers are sobering, but the design choices are transferable.

The track ends in a working session. Participants take a real coastal parcel map
and try to draw a retreat line: which streets defend, which streets buy out, and
how to phase the transition over twenty years. The exercise makes vivid how a
line on a map becomes a boundary between the saved and the abandoned — and how
careful sequencing can keep it from feeling that way.
