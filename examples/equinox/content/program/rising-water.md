The first track sets the shared factual ground for everything that follows. Before
a council can argue about buyouts or sea walls, it has to agree on what the water
is actually going to do — and by when. This is harder than it sounds, because the
honest answer is a distribution, not a date.

<!-- more -->

We open with the long records: a century of tide-gauge data from the North Sea
coast, and what it does and does not let us say about the next thirty years. Dr.
Solvang walks through why relative sea-level rise varies so much between towns only
kilometres apart, and why land subsidence is often the part local planners forget.

The middle of the morning turns to forecasting. Compound flooding — where high
tide, storm surge, and swollen rivers arrive together — is where models and
intuition both tend to fail. Prof. Rausch demonstrates a nowcasting workflow that
takes a regional surge forecast down to the level of individual streets, and is
candid about its error bars.

We close with the human problem that recurs through the whole summit: a good
forecast changes nothing if no one acts on it. The panel gathers an emergency
manager, a harbour master, and a journalist to ask why accurate warnings so often
fail to move people, and what a warning has to feel like before a household packs
the car.
