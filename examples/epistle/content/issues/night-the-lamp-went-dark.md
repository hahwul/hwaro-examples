<p class="salutation">Dear Constance,</p>

<p class="opening">I am writing this a full day later than I intended, because for eleven minutes on the night of the equinox low, Verge Light went dark for the first time since Mr. Prynne lit it eleven years ago, and I have needed the day since to be certain of my own account before I set it down for you.</p>

<!-- more -->

The storm came up faster than the glass predicted, which happens on the strait perhaps once a season, and I was in the lens room servicing the clockwork when the flame simply — went. Not guttered, not starved of oil; it went the way a candle goes when a hand closes around it. In the same moment I heard, under the wind, the sound Mire described and I had privately hoped he exaggerated: the Rock, breathing.

I will tell you what I did, because I want it on paper somewhere that is not only my own memory. I relit the lamp — the ninth try again, Constance, I counted without meaning to — and while my hands were still shaking on the striker, Pell went down the tower stair two at a time and out onto the landing rocks with a storm lantern, because he had seen, before the dark took the beam, a ship standing in far too close on the exact bearing Mire's numbers marked as the mouth of the caves. He stood on the landing swinging that lantern like a man signaling a train, in the code from Mire's log, which he had copied out and memorized without telling me, and the ship came about with perhaps four hundred yards of water left beneath her keel.

When I had the main lamp burning steady again and came down to find him, he was sitting on the wet stone with the storm lantern still lit in his lap, and he said, before I could ask anything, "It wanted the light off long enough for one, not for all. I am sorry I did not warn you it might ask." I said, whatever it is, ask it to send its bill to Mire's estate. He almost laughed. It was the first time.

We have not gone down to the caves. We have agreed, without much discussion, that we are not ready to, and that the lamp comes first, every time, no matter what the eleven dark minutes cost either of us to replace.

<p class="signoff">Yours from the light, still,<br>Josiah</p>
