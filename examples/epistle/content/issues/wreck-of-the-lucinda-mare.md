<p class="salutation">Dear Constance,</p>

<p class="opening">I write this with a blanket around my shoulders that is not mine, in a kitchen made unfamiliar by the presence of a second heartbeat in it. Three nights ago the Lucinda Mare came apart on the reef south of the Rock, and I have a survivor drying by my stove who has not yet told me his name.</p>

<!-- more -->

I saw her lights before I heard her — two lamps too low and too close together, the sign old sailors call "walking on her knees." By the time I had the flag up and the bell going, the reef had already opened her along the keel. I will not describe the sound. I have tried three times to write it and each time the pen has stopped of its own accord, which I take as good advice.

I found him in the last of the wreckage the tide would give up, lashed by his own hand to a hatch cover, more rope than sailor by the look of the knots. He is perhaps thirty, dark-haired, with the kind of quiet that is not shyness but arithmetic — a man counting something behind his eyes and not liking the sum. When I asked his ship's business in the strait, so far from any charted lane, he said only: "We were told the light would be out." I have turned that sentence over every hour since. The light was not out. The light is never out. That is the entire of my employment.

He calls himself Pell, no more, and will not be moved to a bed in Fenhollow though I have offered Ruskin's boat twice. He watches the lamp from my kitchen window as though it owes him an apology, or he owes it one, and eats almost nothing, and last night I woke at three to find him on the stair, halfway to the lantern room, and no memory, he swore, of having risen.

I do not entirely believe him. I am not entirely sure what I would prefer to believe instead.

Tell Aldercombe nothing alarming — a wreck is common enough news for the strait, and there is no cause yet to make more of it than that. But keep a corner of your fire for the thought of me, because I confess this house holds a stranger's silence rather badly, and I find I am glad, God forgive me, of the company.

<p class="signoff">Yours from the light,<br>Josiah</p>
