<p class="salutation">Dear Constance,</p>

<p class="opening">The lamp caught on the ninth try last night, which the outgoing keeper told me is a fine number, since the ninth try counts twice — once for the flame and once for the nerve to keep striking after the eighth had failed. I stood on the gallery until the beam had swept its first full circle, and only then let myself feel the cold.</p>

<!-- more -->

You would laugh to see how small the world has become. Verge Light stands on Solitary Rock, a knuckle of black stone some four hundred yards off Fenhollow Point, and in fair weather I can see the whole of the mainland laid out like a map someone left open in the rain — the church tower, the fish sheds, and if I squint, I tell myself, the corner of Aldercombe where you'll be reading this by a fire I can only imagine.

The tower itself is seven storeys of spiral stair, each one smaller than the last, as though the building were narrowing its shoulders against the weather the higher it climbs. I sleep on the fourth, cook on the second, and spend most waking hours on the eighth beside the lamp, which the last keeper — a Mr. Prynne, gone to his daughter's house inland — left in excellent order, save for a logbook he did not leave at all. Odd, for a man so careful with brass and wick, to be careless with paper.

Ruskin brings the boat out from Fenhollow every second Thursday with coal, post, and whatever news survives the crossing, which by the time it reaches me has usually softened into rumor. He tells me the strait has taken eleven ships in memory, and that Verge Light is why it has not taken more. I did not tell him how heavy that arithmetic feels at two in the morning, alone with a flame that must not, under any circumstance I can prevent, go out.

The days have a shape I am still learning: trim the wick at dawn, polish the lenses at noon, wind the clockwork at dusk, and watch, watch, watch. I had thought loneliness would arrive like weather, all at once. Instead it comes in at the seams, the way damp finds a coat.

Write when you can. Tell me what Aldercombe smells like in January, so I can hold both towns in my head at once.

<p class="signoff">Yours from the light,<br>Josiah</p>
