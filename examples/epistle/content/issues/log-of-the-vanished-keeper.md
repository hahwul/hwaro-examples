<p class="salutation">Dear Constance,</p>

<p class="opening">The seam gave way to a hand chisel and forty patient minutes, and behind it, wrapped in oilcloth gone the color of tea, I found the log of Aldous Mire, who kept this light nineteen years before Prynne and vanished from it on a clear night in a calm sea, which the official record — I have since written to the harbor board to confirm — has never explained.</p>

<!-- more -->

Mire wrote a careful hand for the first eleven years. Wick lengths, weather, the ordinary bookkeeping of a lonely trade. Then, in the twelfth year, the entries change character. He begins to note the lamp burning low on nights when he swears he trimmed it full. He begins to note lights answering his from out on the water where no ship's lantern has any business being, out past the reef where the chart shows nothing but depth. He begins, in the thirteenth year, to write about a sound the Rock makes at the extreme low tides of the equinox, which he describes, and I set down his words exactly because I will not improve on them: "not the sea in the caves, which I know, but something using the caves the way a throat uses a room."

The last entries are dated across a single week in March, nineteen years before I was given this post. He writes that he has taken to answering the lights, with the lamp's shutter, in a code he devised and will not record even here. He writes that something has begun to answer back correctly. His final entry, four lines, reads: "The tide is due low again at the equinox. I believe I am meant to go down and see what keeps the light beneath the light. If I do not come back up, tell Fenhollow the lamp will need a steadier hand than mine, and tell them not to look for me in the water, because I do not believe that is where I will be."

He did not come back up. The equinox that year fell on the twenty-second of March. It falls, this year, in six days.

I showed the log to Pell. He read it twice, silently, and then said the numbers on his salvaged page were soundings for the caves beneath the Rock, not the strait at all. Constance, I do not know what I am keeping this light against, only that I am now certain it is something, and that Mire's twelfth year is beginning to look, from where I stand in my own first, uncomfortably close.

<p class="signoff">Yours from the light,<br>Josiah</p>
