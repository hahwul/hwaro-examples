Within fifty years of Gutenberg's first Bible, an estimated twenty million books were in circulation across Europe. The number is hard to overstate — it represents more printed material than had been produced in the previous thousand years combined.

## What Changed

- **Cost.** A manuscript Bible cost roughly the price of a small farm. A printed one cost a craftsman's monthly wage by 1500.
- **Standardization.** Printed books are *identical*. Errors get debated and fixed across editions. Manuscripts varied book to book, and disagreements were taken as scribal noise rather than substantive textual problems.
- **Reach.** Print pushed beyond the universities and monasteries. By 1520, lay readers in cities held private libraries — a category that did not meaningfully exist a generation earlier.

## What Did Not Change

The pace of new ideas. Printing accelerated *distribution*, not necessarily authorship. The first century of print is dominated by classical texts and church works that already existed in manuscript. The genuinely new books — Erasmus, Machiavelli, Luther — came along when the printing infrastructure was mature enough to amplify them.
