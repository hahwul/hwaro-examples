# Decipherment

<p class="lede">Cuneiform died as a living script around the first century of the common era. For eighteen hundred years, no one could read it. The recovery of the script in the nineteenth century stands as one of the great achievements of philological scholarship.</p>

## The Behistun Inscription

<p>The first breakthrough came not from Mesopotamia but from Iran. The Behistun inscription, carved into a cliff face by the Persian king Darius I around 520 BCE, recorded the same text in three languages: Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian, all written in cuneiform. Henry Rawlinson, a British army officer stationed in Persia, copied the inscription at considerable personal risk between 1835 and 1847, dangling from ropes hundreds of feet above the ground.</p>

## The Old Persian Foothold

<p>Old Persian cuneiform was the simplest of the three scripts, with only thirty-six signs functioning largely as an alphabet. Working from royal name lists in Greek sources, Rawlinson and the German scholar Georg Friedrich Grotefend identified the names of Darius, Xerxes, and Hystaspes in the inscription. From these names they extracted the phonetic values of individual signs, then used the values to read connected text.</p>

## The Babylonian Problem

<p>Babylonian cuneiform was harder. It was not an alphabet but a mixed system of syllabic signs and logograms, with hundreds of values and extensive polyphony. The decipherment proceeded slowly through the 1850s, as scholars compared the Babylonian portion of Behistun against the now-readable Old Persian. By 1857, the Royal Asiatic Society conducted a famous test: four scholars were given an unpublished cuneiform text and asked to translate it independently. Their translations agreed in substance, and the decipherment was declared sound.</p>

<blockquote>The recovery of cuneiform doubled the documented history of human civilization. Texts that had been silent for two thousand years began to speak again.</blockquote>
