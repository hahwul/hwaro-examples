# Sumerian Pictographs

<p class="lede">Before cuneiform became a system of wedges, it was a system of pictures. The earliest tablets from Uruk, dated to the late fourth millennium, show signs that are recognisably drawings: a head for the word head, a bowl for ration, a star for sky or god.</p>

## The Uruk Phase

<p>The first writing emerged in southern Mesopotamia around 3300 BCE, in the temple administration of the city of Uruk. Scribes drew their signs onto wet clay with a pointed reed, producing curving lines and rough outlines. These early tablets record quantities of grain, livestock, and labour, organised into ruled boxes that resemble modern spreadsheets more than they resemble later literary texts.</p>

## From Picture to Sign

<p>The pictographs were not yet a writing system in the full sense. They could record nouns and numbers, but they could not represent grammar, syntax, or sound. A drawing of a foot could mean foot, or stand, or walk, depending on context. The reader had to supply the verbal interpretation. This limitation drove the next stage of development: the rebus principle, in which a sign for one word was used to represent a similar-sounding word, allowing scribes to write abstract concepts and grammatical particles.</p>

## The Shift to Wedges

<p>By the middle of the third millennium, scribes had abandoned the curving stylus for a flat-cut reed pressed into the clay. The new technique was faster and produced sharper, more standardised signs, but it also obscured the pictorial origins. A sign that had once been a recognisable star became a cluster of four wedges arranged in a starburst. The pictograph survived only as a memory inside the abstract form.</p>

<blockquote>The earliest tablets are not literature. They are receipts. But within those receipts lies the entire future of written language.</blockquote>
