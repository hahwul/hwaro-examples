# The Stylus and the Clay

<p class="lede">Cuneiform looks the way it does because of the materials used to produce it. A stylus cut from a reed, pressed into a tablet of damp river clay, leaves a wedge-shaped impression. Every characteristic of the script follows from this simple physical fact.</p>

## The Reed

<p>The standard scribal stylus was cut from the giant reed Arundo donax, which grew abundantly along the Tigris and Euphrates. The reed was sliced at an angle, producing a triangular tip with one sharp edge and two flat faces. Pressing the corner into the clay produced a small wedge; rolling the stylus along its edge produced a longer line. The combination of these two motions, repeated thousands of times, produced every sign in the cuneiform repertoire.</p>

## The Clay

<p>Mesopotamian clay was sourced from the riverbanks and prepared by levigation: the raw clay was mixed with water, allowed to settle, and the fine fraction was decanted. The resulting material was smooth, plastic, and free of grit. Tablets were shaped by hand into rectangular or pillow-shaped forms, then inscribed while still wet. Once the text was complete, the tablet was either left to dry in the sun or, for documents of greater importance, fired in a kiln.</p>

## Constraints and Affordances

<p>The wedge shape constrained what scribes could write. Curves were difficult to produce and were generally avoided. Diagonal strokes were rare. The script settled into a vocabulary of horizontal wedges, vertical wedges, and four diagonal orientations, with no other forms permitted. This limitation produced a paradoxical result: cuneiform looks alien to a reader of alphabetic scripts, but it is in fact one of the most regular and standardised writing systems ever devised.</p>

<blockquote>The medium is not neutral. The clay shaped the wedge, and the wedge shaped the mind of the scribe.</blockquote>
