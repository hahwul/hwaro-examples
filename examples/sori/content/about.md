Sori — 소리, "sound" — is a small independent podcast about the tales that used to travel between villages before anyone thought to write them down, and about the songs that carried them from one house to the next. Every episode in the [full archive](/episodes/) retells one story and reconstructs, as closely as we can manage, the tune an elder remembers singing inside it.

## How an episode gets made

Our collector, Yun Areum, spends most weekends on the same circuit of hill and river villages she has been visiting since university: sitting on porch steps with tea, letting an elder talk in whatever order the memory arrives. She records nothing at first — just listens, asks the small clarifying questions, and comes home to write. A story is only ready for the show once she has heard at least two versions of it from two different households, because the gaps between two tellings are usually where the interesting part lives.

From there the recording goes to Baek Ho-yeol, who keeps a janggu and a well-worn gayageum in the equipment shed behind the community hall. He does not compose a new melody; he tries to rebuild the one that is half-remembered, testing phrases against whatever fragment the elder could still hum, until enough of the original shape returns to be sung again on the show.

## The rhythm strips

Every episode page carries a small strip of thick and thin marks above the story. This is not decoration — it is a rough sketch of the jangdan, the named rhythmic cycle a traditional drummer would keep under the telling. A thick stroke is a deep, open beat struck across the wide drumhead; a thin stroke is a light tap from the stick. Slow, flowing tales tend to run on a gutgeori cycle; a chase or a trickster's escape usually wants something faster, like jajinmori or hwimori. Once you know a handful of these patterns, you can often guess how tense a tale is about to get before the first sentence of the summary.

## Get in touch

If your family kept a version of a tale we have already covered, or a song that never made it into any of our episodes, write to us — we read every letter, even the ones that arrive in dialect we have to look up.
