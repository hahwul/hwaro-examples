A mother walks the Chilbawi road home from market with a basket of rice cakes balanced on her head, and a tiger stops her at every bend to ask for one — first for the basket, then for an arm, then for the other, until there is nothing left to bargain with. Grandmother Ha will not soften this part for younger listeners, and she is right not to; the story needs you to understand exactly what is waiting at the door before the children open it.

<!-- more -->

The tiger arrives at the house wearing the mother's clothes and answering in her voice, but a voice can only fake so much. Through the crack of the door the children see a paw where a hand should be, and they run — out the back, up the tallest tree in the yard, praying aloud to whoever in heaven might be listening for a rope, a strong one, to pull them up out of reach. One drops down. They climb. The tiger, close behind, prays the exact same prayer and is given a rope too — a rotten one, which snaps over a sorghum field and explains, in this telling, why sorghum stalks still grow faintly red at the base. The children keep climbing, past the top of the tree, past the sky, and become the sun and the moon.

Grandmother Ha keeps the chase itself in jajinmori — fast, insistent, the same cycle used for a pansori scene when a character is being pursued and the drum has to keep pace with running feet rather than walking ones.

> Up the tree, up past the tree, past where a rope can reach —
> *the sky took what the ground could not keep safe.*

She always pauses after the rotten rope breaks. Just long enough that you notice the silence before the field turns red.
