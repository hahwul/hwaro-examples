Every elder we have ever recorded in Ganjae pass starts a story the same way: in the days when tigers smoked pipes. Ask what that means and most laugh you off. Grandfather Eom did not laugh — he just kept going, straight into the one tale that opening line was built to introduce.

<!-- more -->

A mother is trying to quiet a crying child at night and has run out of patience. Nothing works — not scolding, not singing, not the promise of rice cake in the morning — until she finally says, without much hope, "there's a dried persimmon in the cupboard." The child goes silent at once. Outside the wall, a tiger who has been circling the house all evening, working up the courage to break in, overhears this and freezes. A dried persimmon. Whatever that is, it must be a creature far more terrible than a tiger, if the mere mention of it can silence a screaming child. He turns and runs — straight into a cattle thief in the dark, who has just climbed onto what he assumes is an ox and grips its back for dear life. The tiger, convinced the dried persimmon has caught him, runs faster.

Grandfather Eom kept the pace of this one brisk on purpose — jungmori, the same moderate cycle used for the long narrative middle of a pansori performance, quick enough to carry a chase without tipping into the frantic.

> A dried persimmon, they say, waits worse than any tiger —
> *and the tiger believed it, all the way to morning.*

He still opens every telling the same way, tigers and their pipes, whether or not this is the story that follows.
