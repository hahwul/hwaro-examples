Kongjwi's stepmother works her like a farmhand from before dawn: weeding a stone field with a wooden hoe, filling a cracked jar that will not hold water, husking a mountain of grain in a single afternoon. Every task is set to make her fail. In Songhak-ri, Grandmother Ji tells it, none of these tasks are ever finished by Kongjwi's own hands — and that detail is the whole point of the story.

<!-- more -->

An ox spirit turns the stone field in an afternoon and leaves before anyone can thank it properly. A toad plugs the cracked jar with its own body and holds the shape until the water sits full. A flock of sparrows descends on the grain pile and husks it clean in the time it takes Kongjwi to cry through half a song. Each helper appears exactly when the task looks impossible and vanishes before it can be thanked, which Grandmother Ji says is the correct way to be helped by something you cannot repay.

The wedding itself gets less attention in most retellings, but it is where the story turns hardest: Patjwi tries to take her stepsister's place at the ceremony, banking on the magistrate never having actually seen Kongjwi's face. Songhak-ri keeps a short work-song for the husking scene, sung in semachi — a quick three-beat folk rhythm, the kind used for repetitive chores that need a pace to keep hands moving.

> Small hands, small hands, the grain will not wait —
> *but neither, tonight, will the ones who are watching.*

Ho-yeol plays it fast enough that you can almost hear the sparrows arriving.
