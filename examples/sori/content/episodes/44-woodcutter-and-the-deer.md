In the hills above Deoksan hollow, a woodcutter named Cho spares a deer fleeing a hunter's dogs — and is handed a debt he does not understand until the ridge itself starts singing it back to him three winters later.

<!-- more -->

The deer, once its breath comes back, tells Cho of a pond one ridge over where a woman bathes at dusk and leaves a feathered cloak folded on a stone. Hide the cloak, the deer says, and she cannot fly home; she will stay, and in time she will marry him — but he must not tell her where the cloak is hidden until they have three children between them. Cho does exactly this, and for a few years the story is a happy one, a house with rice steaming and a wife who sings while she works. It is the impatience that undoes it, the way it always does in this family of tales: two children only, and a night when she finally asks the right question in the right voice, and he answers it.

Grandmother Song, who still keeps chickens on the hollow's east slope, hummed the wife's leaving-song for us over a bowl of barley tea, insisting she was singing it wrong and singing the whole thing anyway. Ho-yeol built it back out on the gayageum in gutgeori, the same unhurried twelve-beat cycle used for lullabies and for washing songs — this is not a tense story until its very last minute, and the rhythm never pretends otherwise.

> Deoksan-a, Deoksan-a, keep the ridge till I return —
> *the cloak was never mine to lose, only yours to have hidden longer.*

We recorded the closing verse twice. The second take, Grandmother Song stopped singing halfway through and just said, "that's enough now," and we kept that too.
