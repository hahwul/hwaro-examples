A peddler crossing home late from market meets a stranger at Crooked Bridge outside Baemgol who challenges him to a wrestling match, right leg locked behind the knee, no hands below the waist. He wins, barely, and the stranger vanishes into the reeds. The next night, crossing the same bridge, the same challenge, the same stranger. By the third night the peddler has stopped being surprised and started being curious.

<!-- more -->

He wins the third match too, and this time, instead of walking away, he does what his grandmother always told him to do with a dokkaebi that will not leave you alone: he ties it to the nearest tree with a length of straw rope before it can slip off into the dark. It struggles all night, and at first light, when he comes back to see what he has actually caught, there is no goblin at Crooked Bridge at all — only an old fire-poker, black with soot and something older than soot, lashed to the trunk with his own rope.

Mr. Noh, whose grandfather kept the bridge-side field for forty years, tells this one with the beat slightly off from what you would expect — a ten-count cycle, eotmori, that never quite lands where the ear wants it to. It is the traditional rhythm for stories about things that are not what they first appear to be, and once he pointed that out to us we could not hear the pattern any other way.

> Three nights at the crooked bridge, three even matches —
> *and only the rope, in the end, told the difference between them.*

We left an extra length of straw rope by the recording gear that night, just in case. Ho-yeol insists this was a joke. He also insists on checking the shed door twice before locking up.
