The painting that gave the movement its name was first shown at the Salon d'Automne in October 1905. Henri Matisse exhibited a portrait of his wife Amélie wearing an elaborate plumed hat. The reception was hostile. Critic Louis Vauxcelles wrote that the room felt like a cage of wild beasts, fauves, and the term stuck to the painters whether they wanted it or not.

The portrait itself rejects the conventions of academic likeness. Matisse used green and vermilion to model the face, violet to mark the brow line, and unmixed orange across the hat brim. The brushwork remains deliberate rather than rushed. Each stroke sits beside its neighbor without blending, allowing the surface to read as a constructed field of color rather than a window onto a sitter.

What the critics missed was the underlying logic. Matisse was not painting Amélie as she appeared in lamplight. He was painting the sensation of looking at her, the way the eye registers warmth against cool, the way a hat dominates a composition by sheer mass of pigment. The choice of green for the bridge of the nose was not arbitrary. It anchored the face against the surrounding pinks and made the structure legible.

The buyer was Leo Stein, who hung the work in the Stein apartment on rue de Fleurus. Gertrude Stein later wrote that the family argued over the purchase for weeks before agreeing. The painting now sits at the San Francisco Museum of Modern Art.

Subsequent Fauvist work pushed further into chromatic dissonance. André Derain and Maurice de Vlaminck adopted similar strategies in landscape. By 1907 the movement had largely dissolved, with Matisse himself moving toward the flatter decorative idiom of his later interiors. The portrait remains the document of a single hostile afternoon and the questions it raised about what painting is for.
