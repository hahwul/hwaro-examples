In our workshop the joint is the argument. A soban's legs pass through its top in a through-tenon, wedged from above so that seasonal movement only tightens the joint further. A bandaji's case is dovetailed at each corner, the pins cut a hair proud and pared flush by hand, so that the case is, in a structural sense, one continuous piece of wood that happens to have four sides.

We do not use nails, screws, dowels driven with glue alone, or hidden metal brackets. Every fastening you cannot see is either a peg of the same wood, split along the grain and driven dry, or a joint with enough mechanical purchase that no fastening is needed at all. This is not a purity test. It is what mastery is for: a jangin earns the right to leave nothing behind that a future owner cannot someday take apart, repair, and put back together.

Pieces are made to order, one at a time, in the order the wood tells us it is ready. More on the workshop, its tools, and how a commission begins lives on the [about page](/about/).
