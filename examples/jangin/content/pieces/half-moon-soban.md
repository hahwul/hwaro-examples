The half-moon soban takes its half-round top from trays meant to be carried in one hand and set down at a low table without announcing themselves. This one is ash, chosen for the way its open grain takes an oil finish without going glassy — a soban gets picked up and set down a hundred times a week, and a low-lustre surface hides the small scuffs of real use far better than a glossy one does.

<!-- more -->

The four legs splay outward at a shallow eight-degree cant, both for stability on an uneven ondol floor and because the angle reads, from across a room, as lighter than a leg that drops straight down. Each leg is joined to the apron with a through-tenon that passes completely through the top rail and is wedged from above with two thin splits of the same ash, driven in perpendicular to the grain of the tenon so the wedge spreads the tenon's cheeks against the mortise walls as it seats. No adhesive was used anywhere in the piece. The joint tightens, very slightly, every dry winter, and loosens back by a hair in the humid months — movement the wedge is there to absorb rather than fight.
