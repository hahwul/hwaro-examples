Two low stools, built as a pair and sized so the second nests fully under the first when not in use — a small courtesy for a one-room apartment where floor space during the day matters more than seating at night. Oak was chosen for its stiffness relative to its section size; a stool this low can be built with slender-looking legs and stretchers only if the wood itself is doing real work rather than relying on bulk.

<!-- more -->

Every leg-to-seat and leg-to-stretcher joint is a double mortise-and-tenon, drawbored rather than glued: a hole is bored slightly offset through the tenon and its mortise cheek, and an oak pin driven through pulls the shoulder tight as it seats, the same trick timber framers have used on barn joints for centuries. A soap finish was chosen over oil or lacquer specifically because these are floor stools, handled with bare feet and low tables — soap leaves the surface matte and slightly toothy rather than slick.
