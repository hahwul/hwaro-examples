Bandaji were built to be moved — a household's linens and valuables packed into one chest that two people could carry out of a burning house. That history sets the brief for the joint: the case has to survive being picked up by its own edges. Through-dovetails at all four corners lock the sides to the front and back under load in every direction a chest actually gets handled, which is why the form has used them for centuries longer than screws have existed.

<!-- more -->

This one is elm, cut from a single flitch so the grain runs continuously around three of the four corners. The lid does not use butt hinges of any kind; instead its two rear stiles taper into round pivots that seat directly into matching sockets bored in the case's back posts, the traditional Korean solution to hanging a lid without metal. It is worked and waxed rather than lacquered, so the surface will darken slowly and evenly with handling the way old elm bandaji do, instead of wearing through a film finish at the corners first.
