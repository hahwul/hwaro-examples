A soban built for a reading room rather than a meal, this one sits lower and wider than a tray table and carries a single shallow drawer for brushes, seals, and paper. Paulownia was the obvious choice for the top and the drawer — it is astonishingly light for its size, resists warping better than almost any wood we work with, and has been the standard for Korean writing furniture for exactly those reasons.

<!-- more -->

The frame is oak, mortise-and-tenoned in the usual way, but the drawer box asked for a different joint entirely. A drawer front takes a horizontal pull dozens of times a day, and a dovetailed drawer resists that pull by wedging tighter the harder it is pulled — but a lightweight paulownia box this thin didn't need dovetail's mechanical advantage so much as it needed a joint that was quick to cut cleanly in soft, easily-torn grain. Finger joints — even, interlocking rectangular fingers glued at every face — spread the load over more surface area than a single wedge shape and are far more forgiving to cut in paulownia without splintering the pins.
