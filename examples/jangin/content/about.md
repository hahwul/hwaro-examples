<p class="about-lede">Jangin is a one-room joinery workshop working in the vocabulary of the Joseon cabinetmaker — soban tray tables, bandaji chests, low writing desks — built entirely with cut joints and driven wooden pegs.</p>

The workshop is run by a single maker, trained first in a furniture conservation studio and later apprenticed for six years under a *sogongin* in Wonju who still planes by hand and refuses, on principle, to own a router. Every piece that leaves the shop passes through the same short list of tools: rip and crosscut saws, a set of bench chisels, a plane till, a marking gauge, and a pot of hide glue kept warm on the back of the stove for the rare joint that wants a whisper of adhesive alongside its mechanical fit. There is no pocket-hole jig on the premises and never will be.

## On tools and time

A dovetailed carcass takes roughly four times as long to lay out and cut as a screwed and glued one. That time is the entire argument for hand joinery: cutting a joint slowly enough to get it right teaches you where the wood wants to move, and a joint that respects that movement outlives the maker who cut it. Museum bandaji two hundred years old still open and close on their original wooden pivot hinges. Nothing bonded with modern carpenter's glue has been tested by anywhere near that much time, so wherever a traditional joint will do the job, we let it.

## Commissions

Each piece is built to order after a short conversation about the room it will live in — light, humidity, how it will be used, what wood the client's household already leans toward. Lead times run twelve to twenty weeks depending on the queue and the piece's complexity. Browsing the [current and recent pieces](/pieces/) is the fastest way to see the range of forms, woods, and joints we work in before writing to describe what you have in mind.
