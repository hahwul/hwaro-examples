The treeline ended abruptly. One moment the path was enclosed by spruce and juniper; the next, it opened onto bare rock and a sky so vast it felt like vertigo. The ridge extended ahead, a narrow spine of stone with slopes falling away on either side into blue shadow.

## The Ascent

Above the treeline, the rules change. Wind arrives unobstructed, constant and cold, pressing against you with a force that the forest would never allow. Vegetation clings close to the ground -- alpine grasses, cushion plants, lichens that have taken decades to cover a single stone. Everything here has learned to be small.

The sun was low now, touching the western peaks and turning them into dark cutouts against an orange sky. My shadow stretched long and thin across the rock, pointing east, pointing back the way I had come. The meadow where the morning had started was invisible now, lost behind ridges and forest, existing only in memory.

## Between Day and Night

Twilight in the mountains is not a brief event. The sun sets behind one peak only to reappear briefly in a gap before setting again behind the next. Each disappearance brings a deeper shade of blue to the sky, a cooler tone to the rock. The transition from day to night happens in stages, like walking through rooms of diminishing light.

The colors were extraordinary. The western sky moved through a sequence -- pale yellow to deep amber to a red so saturated it seemed impossible. The eastern sky answered with its own progression, from blue to indigo to something approaching black. Between them, the ridge sat at the exact boundary, half-lit and half in shadow.

## Stars

The first stars appeared while there was still color in the west. They arrived one at a time, tentatively, as though checking whether it was dark enough. Then, as the last light drained from the sky, they emerged in crowds -- hundreds, then thousands, filling the darkness with a cold, steady light that had been traveling for years to arrive at this exact moment.

I sat on a flat stone at the ridge's highest point. Below me, the landscape I had crossed during the day was reduced to dark shapes and outlines. The meadow, the forest, the lower slopes -- all of it folded together into a single dark mass under the stars.

The wind dropped. The temperature fell. The day's journey was complete.

There was nothing left to do but sit and watch the sky turn.
