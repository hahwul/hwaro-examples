By three in the morning the peak-night crowd has thinned to a dozen or so
die-hards, and the field belongs to a quieter kind of watching. This is the
one session built entirely around the eastern sky: a faint, leaning pyramid
of light rising from the horizon well ahead of the sun, made of sunlight
scattered off interplanetary dust and visible only from sites as dark as
this one.

<!-- more -->

While we wait for the zodiacal light to clear the ridge, the last earthgrazers
of the night put on their own show — long, slow meteors that skim the upper
atmosphere near the horizon rather than diving straight in, sometimes taking
several seconds to cross the whole sky. Non talks through what makes them
different from the fast, bright peak-night trails, and why they favour a
radiant sitting low. As the eastern sky begins to grey, we pass round tea from
a trolley wheeled up from the barn and let the stars go out one constellation
at a time — Perseus first, since it rises highest, Orion last, hanging on
until the horizon finally swallows it whole. Sleep is optional before the
[Closing Watch](/sessions/closing-watch/) picks back up that evening.
