The first night is not the peak, and we like it that way. Camp chairs go out
in loose rows facing north-east, spaced so no eyepiece catches a stray beam
from its neighbour, and for the first forty minutes nobody is allowed to say
a word about meteors — dark adaptation takes half an hour to complete and
one careless torch to undo.

<!-- more -->

Once eyes have settled, sky guides walk the rows with green pointers, tracing
out the Double Cluster in Perseus, the pale smear of the Andromeda Galaxy,
and the Ring Nebula hanging faint above the horizon. Perseus itself does not
clear the ridge until around a quarter to eleven, and for the hour after that
the shower is a slow drip rather than a downpour — fifteen or twenty meteors
an hour, mostly faint, worth logging on the tally sheet if you are counting
for the citizen-science log. Warm cider circulates from the fire from
midnight, and the watch winds down by two, deliberately early, so that the
field is rested and ready for the following night's [Peak Watch](/sessions/peak-watch/).
