The rate has fallen back to twenty or thirty an hour by the third night, and
most of the field is glad of the excuse to point their telescopes somewhere
other than the radiant for a change. The core of the summer Milky Way is
still hanging over the western ridge as the sky darkens, so the closing watch
opens as an informal deep-sky marathon — the Veil Nebula's twin arcs, a run
of summer globular clusters, whatever faint smudge someone in the next row
insists is worth a look.

<!-- more -->

Around midnight the whole field gathers for a best-object vote, shouted out
row by row and settled, as always, by acclaim rather than any actual count.
Ceri gives the short farewell address that closes every party: thanks to the
volunteers who kept the counting sheets and the fire fed, a reminder that the
red-light rule holds until the very last car has cleared the gate, and an
invitation to whoever wants one last look before packing down. A handful of
telescopes stay out until first light regardless of what the schedule says —
that has never once been discouraged.
