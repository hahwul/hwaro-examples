The refractor arrived by rail three days ago in a box that still smelled like the previous owner's basement — a 70mm f/6 achromat on an alt-azimuth mount, twelve years old and, according to the listing, "barely used," which turned out to be true in the way that mattered: the optics were clean.

{{ alert(type="note", body="A cheap achromat shows a faint violet fringe around anything bright — Jupiter, a first-quarter Moon, Sirius. It is not a defect to chase away; it is just what a two-element objective does. Ignore it and look past it, not through it.") }}

Ursa Major was already high by the time the mount's legs stopped wobbling, so the Dipper's handle became the test target: Mizar and Alcor, the naked-eye double that has been used to check eyesight since before telescopes existed. Both stars were obvious even to the eye, a tight but clearly separated pair. The refractor's job was to go one step further — split Mizar itself into its own close double, the fainter companion sitting just off the primary's glare at fifty times magnification, holding steady in a Bortle 7 sky calmer than usual for early April.

First light, then, was two doubles resolved with one scope in one hour, on a landing three flights up with a subway grate humming somewhere below. Not a dark-sky result. A working-scope result, which is the only kind this instrument is going to produce from here, and the only kind that matters.
