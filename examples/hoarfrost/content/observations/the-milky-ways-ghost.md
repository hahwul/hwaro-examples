Late July, still ninety at midnight, the air thick enough that the streetlights below wear visible halos. This is the worst kind of night for anything faint and the best kind of night to write down exactly what "worst" means from here.

<!-- more -->

The summer Milky Way is supposed to run straight through Cygnus, thick enough in a dark sky to look like smoke caught between the stars. From the balcony rail tonight there was nothing — not a hint, not a suggestion, just black between the points of light, the same black you would get from a planetarium projector with half its bulbs out. Bortle 9 does not dim the galaxy's band; it erases it completely.

What survived was the cross itself. Deneb held on at first magnitude despite sitting nineteen hundred light-years away, a genuinely absurd distance for a naked-eye star, hidden in plain sight the way a punchline gets buried in a long story. Albireo, at the foot of the cross, split in binoculars into its famous gold-and-blue pair, one of the few double stars where the color contrast survives real light pollution because both components are bright enough on their own.

There is a kind of grief in writing this entry and a kind of stubbornness in finishing it anyway. The galaxy is not visible from this balcony. The five brightest stars of one of its constellations are, and were, tonight, worth the climb out to see.
