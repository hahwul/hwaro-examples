Three nights running, the same ten minutes after dinner: binoculars up, find the sickle of Leo, find Regulus at its base, and check how much closer the ochre point of Mars has crept since the previous look.

<!-- more -->

By the third night the gap was under a degree — close enough to hold both in the same low-power binocular field, a genuine color contrast between Regulus's cold blue-white and Mars's warm rust, unmistakable even with a hazy Bortle 8 zenith behind them. The refractor added little beyond confirming Mars as a disc rather than a point: no polar cap, no albedo markings, just a small orange dot that magnification made rounder without making it more informative. This is one of the honest limits of a 70mm aperture from a city rooftop, and it is worth stating plainly rather than skipping past — some targets are a naked-eye or binocular story with a telescope footnote, not the other way around.

What made the three nights worth logging separately, rather than as one entry, was the motion itself. Mars does not visibly move against the stars in a single evening, but it clearly moves against them across three, and there is something steadying about watching a planet keep its schedule over a light-polluted rooftop exactly as it would over a mountain observatory. The glow changes nothing about the mechanics.
