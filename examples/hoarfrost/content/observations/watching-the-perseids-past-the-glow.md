Peak night, and every published forecast for a dark sky promised sixty to seventy meteors an hour. This was never going to be that kind of night — a waning gibbous moon rose at eleven, and the fire escape landing sits directly under a stairwell light with a broken sensor that has stayed on for three years running.

<!-- more -->

The count from ten to one in the morning: eleven meteors, four of them bright enough to leave a brief afterimage, one genuine fireball around midnight that lit the underside of a cloud bank far to the south and drew a shout from a neighbor's window two floors up. That is not sixty an hour. It is roughly what a Bortle 9 sky under a bright moon should produce, and it matched the forecast's own caveat almost exactly once the moon and the glow were both accounted for.

Perseus itself never fully cleared the water tower until well past midnight, so the radiant was more a direction than a landmark for most of the session — meteors traced backward, roughly, toward where Mirfak and Algol would eventually rise. A thermos of tea lasted the whole watch. The neighbor who shouted at the fireball came down with a folding chair around one, and the two of us counted the last twenty minutes together, which is its own kind of result: not a better sky, just better company under the same compromised one.
