A full year of entries now sits behind this one, and the clearest thing that year taught is that Cassiopeia is the most useful constellation in a light-polluted sky, not the most impressive. It never sets from this latitude. It is always somewhere in the north, always shaped like a W or an M depending on the hour, and it has become the first thing checked before anything else gets found.

<!-- more -->

Tonight's reading was the year's best: 7 at the zenith, close enough to 6 that Ursa Minor's fainter stars were traceable almost to Polaris, an unusual result for January and probably owed to the cold snap scrubbing the air of the usual haze. With that clarity, the W held one extra prize in binoculars — a faint, grainy patch just off Ruchbah that resolved into a loose scatter of a dozen or so points, the open cluster catalogued as NGC 457 and nicknamed for the two brighter stars that sit like eyes above a wider body of fainter ones. It is not a difficult target in decent conditions, but it had stayed just out of reach on every previous try from this rooftop.

One year, six entries, three sites, two instruments, and a sky that never got darker. What changed was the log itself — which targets are reachable at Bortle 8, which need the rare Bortle 6 night, which never will be visible from here regardless. Cassiopeia sat through all of it, unmoved, exactly where a fixed point should be.
