Hoarfrost is a stargazing journal kept by Wren Marsh, who owns no car, no dark-sky permit, and no drive out of the city — only a sixth-floor walk-up, a building with roof access, and a stubborn refusal to believe that a Bortle 8 sky is not worth logging. Most astronomy writing assumes you can get somewhere dark. This journal assumes you cannot, and asks what is left to see anyway.

## Reading the meta line

Every entry opens with the same four facts, in the same order, because a log that changes its own units is not a log:

- **Bortle reading** — a rough, by-eye estimate of that night's sky glow on the nine-step Bortle scale, where 1 is a pristine desert sky and 9 is the inner core of a large city. Readings here run from 6, on the rare dry January night, to 9, on a hazy August one with a gibbous moon overhead. There is no meter involved, just the same three checks each time: can Polaris's companion be split by eye, is the summer Milky Way visible at all near the zenith, and how far into Ursa Minor can the dimmer stars be traced.
- **Date** — the night the observation happened, not the night it was written up.
- **Instrument** — usually a pair of 10x50 binoculars, occasionally a 70mm f/6 achromatic refractor bought secondhand and first used in April 2025.
- **Site** — one of three fixed spots: the east parapet (roof access, best horizon, coldest in winter), the fire escape landing (fastest to reach, worst light trespass from the stairwell fixture), or the kitchen balcony rail (indoor telescope storage, narrowest sky window).

## What does not get logged

No astrophotography — a single 70mm refractor and a phone camera do not resolve anything a sketch and a paragraph cannot say better. No claims about targets that were not actually confirmed; a "probable" open cluster stays out of the entry until it is a certain one. And no complaining about the glow itself past the first paragraph of any entry — the point of the journal is what got seen, not what did not.
