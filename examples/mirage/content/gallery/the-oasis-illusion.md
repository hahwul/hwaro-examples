Every traveler in the deep desert has seen it: a pool of silver light hovering just above the road, receding as you approach, never arriving. The inferior mirage is the desert's signature trick -- heated air near the ground bends light from the sky downward, painting a false lake on the sand.

This series captures that phenomenon from multiple distances and times of day. In early morning, the mirage is faint, a subtle wobble at the base of distant rock formations. By noon, it becomes bold and convincing -- a shimmering expanse that reflects clouds and seems to ripple with wind.

The photographs are a meditation on perception. We trust our eyes, and the desert gently reminds us that light itself is not always honest. What we see is real -- real photons, real refraction -- but the water it promises does not exist.
