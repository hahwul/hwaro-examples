My dearest,

I am writing this during the intermission of the third performance. I know there is no intermission. I know this because I have been here since the first performance and I have never once left my seat.

The ushers are very polite about it. They bring me tea that has long since gone cold and programs from seasons that have not yet been announced. I thank them each time. They never reply. I think they are not permitted to speak while the music is playing.

The soprano is wearing the dress I was married in.

I did not notice at first. It was only during the mad scene, when she turned toward the stalls and lifted her arms in that particular way, that I recognized the lace at the cuffs. I had sewn those cuffs myself in the spring of 1976. The thread was the color of old ivory.

She is singing our song.

I have tried to leave. Twice. The first time the doors would not open from the inside. The second time they opened onto a corridor that led back to my seat. The baritone in the wings watched me the entire walk. He never blinked.

I am not afraid.

I am only tired.

If you find this letter, please do not look for me. I am exactly where I have always wanted to be — in the best seat in the house, listening to the only voice that has ever mattered.

The lights are rising again.

They are always rising.

Yours,  
in the velvet dark,  
— 

P.S. The nightingale has begun to build a nest in the chandelier. I can hear the small sounds of construction between the notes. It is the most beautiful thing I have ever heard that was not singing.
