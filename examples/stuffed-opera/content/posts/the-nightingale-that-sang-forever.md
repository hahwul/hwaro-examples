The house lights never truly dim at the Stuffed Opera. They merely grow more respectful of the silence that precedes the first note.

On the third of November, beneath a proscenium carved from a single ancient yew, the company presented *La Rondine Preservata*. The lead, a common nightingale (*Luscinia megarhynchos*) prepared in 1979 by the late Master Taxidermist V. Luthier, stood upon a branch of lacquered bone.

It sang for forty-seven minutes without drawing breath.

I counted. I always count.

The audience did not applaud at the conclusion. Applause would have implied an ending. Instead we sat in the particular hush that follows when one realizes the song will continue in the dark after we have all gone home.

One patron — a widow in a dress the color of dried blood — remained in her seat until the ushers (themselves remarkably still) gently reminded her that the performance had concluded three hours prior. She claimed the nightingale had been looking directly at her the entire time.

I believe her.

The nightingale has not left the stage since. It continues to sing between the hours of 2:17 and 3:04 each morning, though no one is present to hear it.

No one living, at least.
