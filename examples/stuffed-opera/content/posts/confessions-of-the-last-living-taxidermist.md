I have not slept properly since the winter of 1986. That was the year the mezzo-soprano first opened her glass eyes during the second act and held the note longer than the score allowed.

She is still holding it.

When I began, the work was simply preservation. A beautiful thing should not be allowed to rot. That was the philosophy. Then the founder — a man whose name I have long since sewn into the lining of a swallow's wing — asked if I could make them sing.

I told him the dead do not sing.

He smiled with all his teeth and said, "They will learn."

The hands are the worst part. Human hands, even when expertly mounted, retain memory in the tendons. I have seen fingers curl, very slowly, during the final movement of *Don Giovanni in Amber*.

I no longer use human hands.

The animals are kinder. They forgive the needle.

Last week I discovered a small moth had made its home inside the hollow of our newest soprano's ribcage. It emerged during the mad scene and circled the chandelier three times before returning to its velvet chamber. The audience thought it was part of the lighting design.

I have stopped correcting them.

I will be replaced eventually. Everyone is replaced eventually. Even the dead.

But the performance continues. It must continue.

I have already begun work on my own replacement. The eyes are the most difficult. I want them to look surprised. I have been surprised for forty-seven years. It seems only fair.
