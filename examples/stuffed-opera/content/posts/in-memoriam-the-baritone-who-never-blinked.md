He was a man of extraordinary presence in life. In death he has become something far greater: permanent.

The baritone — we never speak his living name — debuted in our sixth season in the role of Scarpia. The reviews from that night still exist, though the paper has grown brittle and the ink has migrated slightly into the fibers, giving the words a faint halo.

He was praised for his "unblinking intensity."

The critics did not know how prophetic those words would prove.

On the 312th performance, during the final aria of Act III, his eyes ceased to close between phrases. The movement was so gradual that only the stage manager noticed. By the 314th performance, the entire company had accepted the new interpretation.

He has not blinked since.

The company has adjusted around him. New performers are positioned so they never stand directly in his fixed gaze for more than seven measures. The costume department replaces his collar every three seasons; the original fabric has worn thin from the constant, gentle pressure of his unmoving chin.

He is, by all accounts, our most reliable artist.

When the final curtain falls each night, the other performers are returned to their cases. The baritone remains on stage. It was decided, decades ago, that moving him would be disrespectful to the role.

Some nights, very late, the cleaning staff report hearing a single low note echo from the empty auditorium.

It is always the same note.

It is always perfectly in tune.

**The performance continues.**  
**The baritone watches.**  
**The audience is advised to avoid the center-left stalls.**
