## On the volvelle

<p class="lede">A volvelle is a rotating paper or parchment disc, mounted on a page by a central pivot, that serves as a calculation device or information display. Found in manuscripts from the thirteenth century onward, volvelles were used to compute astronomical data, determine feast days, predict eclipses, convert between calendars, and display relationships between categories of knowledge. They are among the earliest interactive devices in the history of information design -- paper instruments that transform a flat page into a mechanical computer.</p>

The volvelle consists of one or more circular discs of graduated diameter, stacked concentrically and attached at the center by a thread, pin, or rivet. The outer disc is typically fixed to the page; the inner discs rotate freely. Each disc bears a ring of data -- zodiac signs, months, planetary hours, letter values -- and the reader aligns the rings by turning the inner discs until the desired relationship appears in a window or at a pointer.

## Chapters

This publication examines the volvelle in five chapters:

1. **The Anatomy of a Volvelle** -- The physical construction: discs, pivot, pointer, and windows.

2. **The Astronomical Wheel** -- Volvelles for calculating the positions of sun, moon, and planets.

3. **The Calendar Wheel** -- Computistical volvelles for determining Easter, feast days, and the golden number.

4. **The Medical Wheel** -- Volvelles in medical manuscripts for bloodletting times and humoral diagnosis.

5. **The Legacy of the Wheel** -- From manuscript instrument to printed ephemera, and the volvelle's influence on information design.
