## Mapping the heavens

<p class="lede">The earliest and most common application of the volvelle was astronomical calculation. Before printed ephemerides and long before electronic computers, the volvelle allowed a literate person to determine the approximate position of the sun in the zodiac, the phase of the moon, the rising and setting times of the stars, and -- with more elaborate instruments -- the positions of the planets. These calculations were not merely academic curiosities; they determined the calendar, the timing of religious observances, the planting of crops, and the practice of medicine.</p>

The simplest astronomical volvelle correlates the months of the year with the zodiac signs. The outer ring shows the twelve months; the inner ring shows the twelve signs. By aligning a date on the month ring with the corresponding position on the zodiac ring, the reader can determine which sign the sun occupies on any given day.

## The lunar volvelle

More complex volvelles track the phases of the moon. The lunar cycle of approximately 29.5 days does not divide evenly into the solar year of 365.25 days, and the relationship between the two cycles shifts from year to year. A lunar volvelle uses two or more rotating rings to model this relationship, allowing the reader to determine the phase of the moon on any date. Some lunar volvelles include a window through which a disc painted half black and half white indicates the visible phase.

## The equatorium

The most sophisticated astronomical volvelles are equatoria -- instruments for computing the positions of the planets. An equatorium uses multiple rotating discs to model the epicyclic orbits of the Ptolemaic system, in which each planet moves on a small circle (the epicycle) whose center moves on a larger circle (the deferent) around the Earth. By setting the discs to the correct initial positions and rotating them according to known rates, the reader could determine the longitude of any planet on any date.
