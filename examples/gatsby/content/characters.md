The tragedy of F. Scott Fitzgerald's masterpiece is driven by a small, tightly knit cast of characters whose lives collide on the shores of Long Island. At the center stands Jay Gatsby, a self-made man of mystery whose entire existence is a theatrical performance designed to recapture the past. Opposite him is Daisy Buchanan, the object of his obsession, whose voice is "full of money" but whose character is ultimately shallow and fragile.

Nick Carraway, our narrator, provides the moral anchor of the tale, observing the excesses of East Egg and West Egg with a mixture of fascination and disgust. Tom Buchanan, Daisy's wealthy and brutal husband, represents the careless power of old money, destructive and immune to consequence. Finally, Jordan Baker, the cynical golfer, embodies the modern, detached attitude of the New Woman of the twenties. Together, these figures play out their roles in a gilded theater, demonstrating the divisions of class, gender, and morality that define the American experience during the Jazz Age.
