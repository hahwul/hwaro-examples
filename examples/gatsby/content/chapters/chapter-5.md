At the core of Gatsby's character is a profound obsession with memory and the past. He refuses to accept that Daisy has built a life without him during their five years of separation, believing instead that he can return to the moment they first met in Louisville. When Nick warns him that "you can't repeat the past," Gatsby replies with his famous, defiant assertion: "Why of course you can!"
<!-- more -->

This refusal to accept the passage of time is the source of Gatsby's greatness and his ultimate ruin. He spends his fortune constructing a monument to a memory, using his mansion, his parties, and his wealth as a theater to stage their reunion. Yet memory is a fragile foundation, and the Daisy he remembers is an idealized figure who cannot compete with the complex, flawed woman of the present. Gatsby's tragedy is that he is anchored to a ghost, unable to move forward because his gaze is fixed on the shore he has left behind.
