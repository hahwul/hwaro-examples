The final chapters of the novel chronicle the collapse of Gatsby's illusions and his tragic death, serving as a powerful commentary on the death of the American Dream itself. Gatsby's dream is built on the belief that anyone can recreate themselves and rise to the highest levels of society through sheer willpower and wealth. Yet, the novel shows that the social divisions of class are rigid and unforgiving, separating those who belong to the old elite from those who merely imitate them.
<!-- more -->

When the truth of Gatsby's past is revealed, his dream crumbles. Daisy retreats back to her husband Tom and the security of their money, leaving Gatsby to face the consequences alone. His death at the hands of George Wilson is a quiet, lonely affair, in contrast to the crowded, noisy parties that defined his summer. Fitzgerald concludes the novel with Nick reflecting on the green light and the tragic nature of human effort: we run faster, stretch out our arms farther, but we are always borne back into the past. The American dream is thus presented as a beautiful but impossible illusion.
