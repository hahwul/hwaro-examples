The spatial organization of F. Scott Fitzgerald's novel is built on a fundamental contrast between two peninsulas on Long Island: East Egg and West Egg. East Egg represents the established aristocracy of "old money," where wealth is inherited, polished, and cold. West Egg, on the other hand, is the domain of "new money," characterized by raw, ostentatious displays of wealth and sudden fortunes made in the post-war boom. Nick Carraway, our narrator, describes the physical proximity and cultural distance between these two landmasses.
<!-- more -->

This geographical divide is not merely physical; it is deeply symbolic of the social barriers that define the American class structure. West Egg is a place of gaudy imitation, where houses are built to look like European villas, whereas East Egg houses are understated, classic, and secure. We can visualize the distribution of wealth using a simple layout script. For instance, here is a Python snippet that models the social distance based on geographical coordinate offsets on the Sound:

```python
def calculate_social_distance(egg_origin, egg_destination):
    # Returns the relative difficulty of social mobility between Eggs
    distance = abs(egg_origin - egg_destination)
    if distance > 10:
        return "Social barrier is impassable"
    return "Social connection possible under exceptional circumstances"

print(calculate_social_distance(5, 25))
```
