Halfway between West Egg and New York City lies a desolate corridor of land known as the Valley of Ashes. Created by the dumping of industrial byproducts from the growing metropolis, it is a grey, dusty wasteland where the working class lives and labors. This industrial purgatory is overlooked by the fading eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg, painted on a billboard to advertise an optometrist who has long since departed.
<!-- more -->

The Valley of Ashes stands in stark contrast to the glittering luxury of East and West Egg, exposing the waste and human cost that supports the lives of the wealthy elite. It is the location of George Wilson's garage and the setting for the tragic death of Myrtle Wilson, demonstrating that those without wealth are the ones who pay the price for the carelessness of others. The billboard eyes of Doctor Eckleburg, fading under the sun and rain, are interpreted by George Wilson as the eyes of God, watching the moral decay and silent suffering of the modern wasteland. This desolate setting underscores the novel's criticism of the industrial era.
