Hanji is run by Seo Da-in from a single bench in a converted net-loft above the fish market in Tongyeong. The bindery produces four to six limited editions a year, never more than forty copies of anything, each one sewn by hand in Korean mulberry paper.

## Why mulberry

Da-in trained as a conservator before turning to binding, and the conversion happened in a conservation lab: a court document from 1478, dunked, flattened, and rebacked, its hanji leaves behaving as if they had been made the previous week. Western rag paper of the same age would have been a lace of foxing and losses. Mulberry bast fibre is simply a different order of material — the strands run to a centimetre in length, they interlock rather than merely mat, and the traditional dip-and-rock sheet formation crosses them in both directions. A sheet of good hanji is closer to textile than to paper, which is why this bindery uses it the way other binders use cloth and board.

## The papers

All paper comes from the Baekun mill in Wonju, made to order in three weights: a 30 gsm text paper lined for printing, a 55 gsm sheet for endleaves and guards, and a 110 gsm double-couched sheet that serves as cover material on every edition. Nothing is bleached. The cream you see on this site is the colour the bark gives.

## The sewing

Every structure used here leaves its thread visible. That is a constraint, not a style: exposed sewing can be inspected, tightened, and repaired without breaking a single fold. The bindery's five working structures are diagrammed on their edition pages, in the margin, where a binder would pencil them: Coptic chain on [The Night Ferry Poems](@/bindings/night-ferry-poems.md), four-hole stab on [A Field Guide to Winter Light](@/bindings/field-guide-winter-light.md), long-stitch on [Letters from the Salt Farm](@/bindings/letters-from-the-salt-farm.md), French link on [The Orchard Ledger](@/bindings/orchard-ledger.md), and buttonhole on [Sijo for the Paper Moon](@/bindings/sijo-for-the-paper-moon.md).

## Commissions and standing orders

Editions are announced to the mailing list first and usually subscribe out within a month. Collaborations with poets, letterpress printers, and small presses are welcome; the bindery takes on two commissions a year, chosen in the winter. Write to the studio with a description of the text, the print run you have in mind, and the way you want the book to open.
