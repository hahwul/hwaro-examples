Hanji is what Koreans call paper made from the inner bark of the mulberry tree, and it is the reason this bindery exists. Most binders treat paper as a surface — something to print on, decorate, and hide inside covers that do the real work. Here the proposition is reversed. Mulberry paper is the structure. Its fibres are long enough to fold ten thousand times without cracking, strong enough to serve as spine, hinge, and cover in one continuous material, and honest enough that a book made from it needs no cloth, no board, and no glue.

That honesty extends to the sewing. Every edition that leaves this bench is bound with its thread exposed — Coptic chains, stab wraps, long-stitch runs — because the stitch is not a fastener to be ashamed of. It is the book's skeleton, and skeletons are worth looking at. Each binding below opens with a diagram of its stitch, drawn the way it was sewn. The papers, the mill, and the binder are all introduced [in the colophon](@/about.md).
