Im Dal-rae wrote these twenty-six poems over a winter of crossings between Tongyeong and Yokjido, and asked for a book that could be read one-handed on a ferry rail in a crosswind. That request decided the structure before any drawing was made: a Coptic chain, sewn through-the-fold at seven stations, is the only binding on this bench that opens dead flat and stays there without being held.

<!-- more -->

## The commission

The manuscript came as a stack of pencil drafts with the ferry timetable printed on the versos — Dal-rae drafts on whatever the terminal kiosk sells. We kept that spirit in the printed book: Jin-ho at Cheolmul Press, who also printed [Sijo for the Paper Moon](@/bindings/sijo-for-the-paper-moon.md), set the poems in a 14-point Fournier revival, one poem per opening, with the title dropped into the fore-edge margin where a timetable column would sit.

## Paper and covers

The text is 30 gsm Baekun hanji, lined against the printing direction so the chain's tension pulls with the grain rather than across it. There are no boards. The covers are four folds of the 110 gsm double-couched sheet, creased wet and dried under weight, which gives a cover that flexes like heavy sailcloth and comes back true.

## The sewing

Seven stations, marked from a pierced template rather than a pricking cradle — the paper is soft enough that a cradle bruises the folds. The chain is sewn with indigo-dyed linen at a deliberate half-twist per link, so the spine reads as a continuous braid from head to tail. On a book this light the chain is the heaviest single element, and you can feel it settle into the palm exactly where a thumb wants to rest.

## Edition notes

Twenty-six copies, lettered A through Z rather than numbered, at the poet's insistence — she wanted no copy to be first. Letter O went back across on the last ferry of the subscription week, bought by the boat's engineer, which both of us have decided is the entire point of making books.
