Gwon O-sun writes sijo the old way — three lines, a turn in the third — and asked for a book the size of a hand and the shape of a full moon's reflection in a rice paddy: square, pale, interrupted. The interruption became the binding. A window is cut clean through the spine wrapper, and the signatures are sewn to its edge with buttonhole stitch, so the book hangs from a hem of indigo silk and the folds of the paper show through the gap like the moon through a door.

## The commission

Twelve poems, one per signature opening, printed letterpress by Jin-ho in a large quiet type with the third-line turn set on its own page. The square format wastes paper and the press complained, correctly, and was overruled, also correctly.

## The sewing

Buttonhole is embroidery vocabulary, not bookbinding vocabulary, and that is the point: the stitch is worked along the cut edge of the window exactly as a tailor finishes a buttonhole, each loop locking the last, building a corded hem that the signatures hang from. Above and below the window the thread runs plainly, so the spine reads as line, hem, line. It is the slowest sewing on this bench — each of the twelve copies took an evening — and the only one where a mistake cannot be eased out and must be cut.

## Paper

The moon window is left unlined, so what you see through it is the actual fold of each signature: 30 gsm Baekun, thin enough to glow when the book is held to a lamp. The cover sheet is the 110 gsm double-couched stock, trimmed with a blade change per copy to keep the window's edge crisp.

## Edition notes

Twelve copies for twelve poems, numbered in the poet's hand with a brush. O-sun kept number twelve and mailed number one to the schoolteacher who first marked her sijo, forty years late, with the third-line turn of the last poem underlined.
