Ha Jeong-min spent a year describing what the sun does between the solstices — to persimmon skins, to frost on vinyl greenhouses, to the insides of north-facing rooms — and the essays arrived already broken into leaf-sized fragments. Single leaves ask for a side-sewn book, and side-sewing done honestly means yotsume toji: four holes, one thread, every pass visible.

<!-- more -->

## The commission

The Dodam Press printed the essays letterpress in a quiet 11-point face with generous leading, one fragment to a page, dated like diary entries. Jeong-min wanted the reader to feel each page as a day: discrete, same-sized, accumulating. A stab binding does that mechanically — the pages turn from the sewn edge like a calendar rather than flowing from a fold.

## Paper

The leaves are 55 gsm Baekun hanji, heavier than our usual text weight because a stab-bound page cantilevers from the spine edge and thin paper droops. The stab margin steals twelve millimetres from every page, so the press set the text block asymmetric — the gutter is part of the design, not a loss to it.

## The sewing

Four holes at uneven intervals, closer together toward the head, a spacing borrowed from a nineteenth-century ledger in the bindery's reference drawer. The doubled ramie thread wraps the spine edge at every hole and carries over the head and tail, so the book's edge is corded in indigo — from across a room the spine reads as a single blue line. One length of thread, no knots visible; the tail is buried between leaves eleven and twelve, and the diagram in the margin shows exactly where.

## Edition notes

Forty copies, numbered on the last leaf in the printer's hand. The first ten went to subscribers with a loose extra leaf: the essay Jeong-min cut at the last minute, about the light in the press's own window, printed but unsewn — a page that never became a day.
