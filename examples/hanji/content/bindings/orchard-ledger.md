The original ledger surfaced in an estate sale in Chungju: a 1936 account book from the Hwangnyong orchard, every page ruled by hand, recording apples by the crate and frosts by the hour. The Muldong Historical Society commissioned a facsimile edition that could be handled by schoolchildren — which meant a spine built for abuse, and a sewing that flexes a thousand openings without loosening.

<!-- more -->

## The commission

The society's photographer shot every opening flat, and Cheolmul Press printed the plates in a warm duotone on our 55 gsm sheet, washed with oak gall to sit near the original's toasted colour without pretending to its age. A facsimile should feel like a healthy sibling of the original, not a forgery of its decay.

## The sewing

French link is the workhorse of exposed spines. The thread runs through each signature, and at every tape it drops out, crosses under its neighbour from the signature below, and climbs back in — a diamond of linked thread over each hemp tape, repeated down the spine. The links share load between signatures, so a child hauling the book open at the centrefold is pulling against the whole text block, not one thread. Three tapes, ten signatures, kettle stitches at head and tail: the diagram in the margin is the one taped to the bench for six weeks.

## Covers

The tapes lace into covers of folded, four-ply hanji — our standard boardless case, but doubled here for the schoolroom. The cover creases were burnished with a boar-bristle brush until they shine faintly, the only polish anywhere on the book.

## Edition notes

Thirty-two copies: twenty for the society, the rest sold to fund the conservation of the original, which now rests, resewn and boxed, in the society's reading room. The bindery's fee was taken partly in dried apples. This is disclosed in the colophon.
