Between 1963 and 1994, Baek Chun-gil wrote a letter every spring to the daughter who had left for Incheon, and every letter opens with the state of the evaporation ponds. His granddaughter brought the bundle to the bindery in a rice sack. The book we made keeps the sack's logic: a single rough wrapper, everything held by thread you can see, nothing precious about the outside and everything precious within.

<!-- more -->

## The commission

The letters were transcribed and printed offset by Dodam Press — the same shop that later set [A Field Guide to Winter Light](@/bindings/field-guide-winter-light.md) — with the granddaughter's annotations set in the margins in a lighter ink. We bound eighteen copies for the family and the regional archive. The wrapper is dyed with green persimmon juice, the traditional gammul treatment, which tans the mulberry sheet to the colour of pond silt and stiffens it — the same treatment salt farmers used on their work clothes.

## The sewing

Long-stitch is the most legible structure a binder can sew: the thread enters a slit, runs the length of a signature in the open, and enters the next. Nine signatures snake between two sewing columns, and the exposed runs alternate left and right down the spine like rows in a flooded field. There is no chain, no link, no crossing — just long stitches doing exactly what their name says. The diagram beside this page is nearly a map of the finished spine.

## Paper

Text on 30 gsm Baekun, chosen because letters deserve thin pages — a thick page makes correspondence feel like literature, and Chun-gil would have objected. The signatures are guarded at the first and last folds with 55 gsm strips so the slits bear on doubled paper.

## Edition notes

Eighteen copies: one for each surviving member of four generations, two for the archive at Sinan, and one that stays at the bench. The family asked that the edition never be reprinted in this form. It will not be.
