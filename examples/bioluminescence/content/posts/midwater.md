Most of the planet's biomass lives in the mesopelagic zone — between 200 and 1000 meters below the surface, where sunlight thins to a faint blue and finally fails entirely. In that zone, bioluminescence is not a curiosity. It is the lighting system.

## Three Roles

- **Counter-illumination.** Hatchetfish and many squid emit light from their underside that matches the dim downwelling light above them. From below, the silhouette disappears against the sky.
- **Burglar alarm.** A startled deep-sea jellyfish releases a ring of light that draws predators *of* the predator that just attacked it. The defense works because nothing in that environment expects sudden illumination.
- **Lure.** The classic anglerfish strategy — a single point of bait light, suspended in front of teeth.

## Why It Works There

In a fully dark environment, even a faint emission becomes visually loud. A flashlight that is invisible at noon is conspicuous at midnight. In the mesopelagic, the rules of contrast favor the photographer over the photographed; light reveals you and gives you control of the scene at the same time.
