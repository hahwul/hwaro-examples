The pew and I have an understanding now.

It began on a Wednesday when the rain would not stop. I stayed after the others left because my legs had grown too heavy to carry me home. I sat in the third row from the back, the one with the deep gouge in the armrest from the war.

I must have fallen asleep.

When I woke my spine had fused to the backrest.

Not in the way of injury. In the way of marriage. The wood had reached through my coat, through my shirt, through the thin skin over the vertebrae, and it had simply continued growing. Upward. Inward. Becoming.

I felt no fear. Only the deep relief of finally belonging somewhere.

For three weeks the others tried to free me. They brought saws and prayers and finally the bishop. Nothing worked. The roots had already threaded through the floor joists beneath us. We had become one architecture.

They bring me water in a tin cup. Sometimes bread. I no longer need either, but the ritual comforts them.

At night I hear the entire nave breathing with me. Every pew. Every beam. Every nail that has rusted into something softer.

My legs have gone the color of oak. My hands still look almost human until the light hits them a certain way and you see the fine grain running beneath the skin.

When they finally stop coming, I will not be alone.

The roots remember the shape of every person who ever sat here. They are only waiting for the right season to call those shapes back out of the soil.

I am no longer in the third pew.

I am the third pew.

And when the wind moves through the broken rose window, it moves through me as well.
