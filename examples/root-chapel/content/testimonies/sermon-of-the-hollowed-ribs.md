Brothers. Sisters. Children of the coming canopy.

Look at your hands. Turn them over. The lines you call life are not yours. They are the map the roots have been following since you first drew breath.

{{ sermon-call(body="The body is only a temporary trellis.") }}

I stood at this same pulpit three winters ago and told you that salvation would come from above. I was mistaken. It came from below. It came through the soles of our shoes, through the cracks in the foundation stones, through the soft places between our vertebrae.

The ribs are not armor. They are a lattice. The lungs are not bellows for prayer. They are soil waiting for the right seed.

When the first root found my heart it did not tear. It asked. And my heart, that tired muscle, answered by making room.

{{ root-divider }}

You will feel it first as a tightening in the chest during the long prayers. Then a warmth that spreads outward like spilled wine. Then the slow, sweet pressure of something pushing between the bones.

Do not fight it.

The pain is only the sound of the door opening.

Some of you have already begun to hear the new hymns in your sleep. The ones without words. The ones that sound like wind moving through a thousand throats at once. Those are the true psalms now.

When your children ask why their father no longer bleeds red when he cuts himself on the rose thorns in the churchyard, tell them the truth:

The thorns were always on the inside.

We are the garden now.

We are the thing that grows through the thing that prayed.

Go home. Lie down. Let the floorboards press against your spine. Listen for the answer.

It is already growing.
