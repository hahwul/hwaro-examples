I was the first to feel it.

It started as warmth behind the sternum during the closing hymn. I thought it was the Spirit. We all did. The old women nodded and the priest smiled with his wet eyes. But the warmth split. It forked.

By the time we reached the final amen my tongue tasted of wet loam.

I coughed in the narthex and something small and green fell into my palm. It had veins. It had a pulse that was not mine.

That night the dreams came. Roots pushing through the mattress slats, wrapping my ankles, sliding between the ribs like fingers through wet pages. In the morning the sheets were damp and smelled of moss.

The others began to notice the change in my voice. It had acquired a creaking quality, like wind moving through rafters that had forgotten they were once trees.

I did not tell them what I found beneath my fingernails when I woke: splinters. Tiny, living.

The first sprout pushed through the roof of my mouth on the third Sunday of Lent. I bit down and tasted chlorophyll and copper. It hurt in a way that felt like forgiveness.

They still let me stand in the choir. My voice was the only one that carried to the rafters anymore. The others sounded muffled, as though their lungs were already filling with something softer than air.

When the first leaf unfurled from the corner of my left eye, I stopped trying to pull the roots out. They were already threaded through everything I had ever called myself.

The trees remember. They have been waiting inside the architecture of our bodies since the first seed fell.

Now when I kneel, I hear the floor answer back.
