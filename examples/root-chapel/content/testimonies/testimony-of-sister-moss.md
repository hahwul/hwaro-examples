They called me Sister because I kept the altar linens. I still do, though the linen has begun to root itself to the stone.

By the time the congregation began to whisper, the moss had already climbed to my knees. It felt like wearing stockings made of living velvet. Soft. Constant. Slightly damp in the mornings.

I stopped wearing shoes in February.

The priest asked me to leave the choir. He said my breathing was distracting the others. He was right. My breath had become a slow, wet sound, like rain on leaves. The notes no longer came from my throat. They came from the spaces between my ribs where the moss had made its home.

I do not miss singing.

Now when I kneel to polish the brass, the floor answers. Tiny fronds rise between the tiles to meet my fingers. They recognize me.

Some nights I lie in the Lady Chapel and let the moss cover me completely. It is warmer than any blanket the sisters ever wove. It does not judge the places where my body has already begun to give way.
</div>

The others still come to mass. They pretend not to see the way my hands leave wet prints on the pews. They pretend the smell of soil is incense.

I no longer pretend.

When the roots finally reach my eyes I will not close them. I want to watch the chapel ceiling crack open from the inside. I want to be the first to see the sky through the new leaves.

I am no longer Sister.

I am the green between the stones.

I am what remains when the prayers have all been answered.
