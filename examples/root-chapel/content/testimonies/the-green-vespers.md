The bells no longer ring at six.

Something in the tower learned a different music.

Every evening, just as the light fails between the trees outside, a low harmonic rises from the floorboards. It is not quite singing. It is not quite wind. It is the sound of a thousand small throats opening at once.

I have stopped trying to lead the responses. The responses no longer need me. They rise from the places where the plaster has fallen away and the lath has begun to bud.

Last week the old crucifix behind the altar put out leaves. Tiny, trembling things the color of new bruises. By morning they had turned toward the broken windows as if the sun were still a thing worth reaching for.

The congregation that remains comes later now. They sit in the dark and listen. Some of them have begun to sway in time with the sound, though they do not seem aware they are moving.

I have started to understand the words.

They are not Latin. They are older. They are the names of every root that ever pushed through a grave. They are the sound of xylem filling. They are the slow exhalation of the world deciding it has waited long enough.

When the Green Vespers end, the silence that follows is not empty.

It is full of the sound of things still growing.

Do not be afraid when your own chest begins to answer the song.

That is only the forest remembering you were always meant to be part of the choir.
