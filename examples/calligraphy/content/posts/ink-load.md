A freshly loaded nib carries more ink than the paper can absorb cleanly. The first stroke after dipping is almost always too wet. Practiced calligraphers dab the side of the nib against the inkpot edge to release the surplus before committing the stroke.

## What Goes Wrong

- **Excess ink** — produces a bleed at the start of the stroke. The serif blooms outward, the line widens, and the rhythm of the letter is broken before it begins.
- **Insufficient ink** — produces a dry start. The nib catches on the paper, and the first quarter of the stroke is broken or skipped.
- **Uneven side load** — produces an asymmetric stroke that thickens on one side. Common when the nib is dipped vertically rather than at an angle.

## The Discipline

Refill on a predictable cadence — every two letters, every word, every line — so the ink load is consistent across the page. The refill cadence becomes part of the rhythm. Erratic refilling shows in the finished page even when the individual letters look correct.
