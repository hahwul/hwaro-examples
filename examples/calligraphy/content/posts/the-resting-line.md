The pause between strokes is not silence. It is the rhythm of the page.

In Western practice, the calligrapher lifts the nib at predictable intervals — between letters, between words, between lines. The lifts are part of the composition, even though no ink is laid down during them. A practiced eye can tell whether a hand is rushing by the inconsistency of those pauses, before noticing anything about the letterforms themselves.

## The Three Pauses

- **Between strokes within a letter.** A momentary lift, just enough to refresh the ink and reset the angle. Usually under half a second.
- **Between letters.** A longer pause that carries the body forward, not just the hand. The page advances by half a glyph width.
- **Between words.** The longest pause, where the calligrapher recenters posture and re-evaluates the next stroke against the line above.

A piece of writing with even, considered pauses reads as composed. The same letters with chaotic pauses read as anxious — even when the individual letterforms are flawless.
