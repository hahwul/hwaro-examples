## A Desk, a Pen, and Five Lines

**The Copyist's Desk** is a small publication about musical notation: how five lines and a handful of symbols came to hold pitch, rhythm, and feeling, and what happens in the gap between the page and the sound.

For most of history, a copyist was the person who turned a composer's draft into clean, readable parts — ruling staves by hand, spacing the noteheads so a player's eye could move without stumbling, deciding where a page should turn so no one had to flip mid-phrase. It was craft as much as transcription. This blog keeps that spirit: it treats notation as *design*, not just shorthand.

---

## What You'll Find Here

- **Notation as a writing system** — how the staff encodes three dimensions at once: which note, how long, how loud.
- **The composed silence** — rests, fermatas, and the rhythm of nothing.
- **Score versus sound** — why two honest readings of the same page can be unrecognizably different.
- **Typography and engraving** — the long argument between handwritten manuscript and printed plate.

Everything here is set in the visual language of a manuscript page: ruled staves, a treble clef at the margin, tempo and dynamic markings standing in for ordinary metadata. The look is borrowed from the thing it writes about.

## About the Copyist

I am a working music copyist and engraver. I spend my days deciding how far apart two noteheads should sit, whether a slur should arc high or low, and which of two correct answers a conductor will actually thank me for. I am fascinated by the moment a smudge of ink becomes an instruction, and an instruction becomes a sound in a room.

> "The score is a letter the composer writes to a performer they will never meet."
