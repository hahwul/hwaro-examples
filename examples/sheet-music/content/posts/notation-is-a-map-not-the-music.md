Hand the same page of Bach to two fine players and you will hear two different pieces. Not different *notes* — the pitches and rhythms will match — but different music: different weight on the downbeats, different shaping of the long lines, a different sense of when the piece is hurrying and when it is holding back. People sometimes find this troubling. If the score is right there, why isn't there one correct way to play it? The answer is that a score was never meant to *be* the music. It is a map of it.

## What the Map Leaves Out

Staff notation is astonishingly precise about some things and deliberately vague about others. It pins down pitch to the semitone. It is fussy about rhythm. But ask it the questions that actually separate a great performance from a dutiful one and it starts shrugging.

- How loud, *exactly*, is *forte*? Louder than *mezzo-forte*. That is all you are told.
- How fast is *Allegro*? Fast and cheerful. Pick a tempo.
- How long is a fermata? Until it feels finished.
- How much should this phrase breathe before the next begins? The page goes quiet.

Even the metronome mark, which looks like hard data, is a starting suggestion the whole tradition agrees to bend. The map gives you the roads. It does not drive the car.

## The Map Is Not the Territory

The phrase comes from Alfred Korzybski, and it fits music better than almost any other art. A map is useful precisely *because* it omits. A street map that reproduced every crack in the pavement would be unusable; its value is in what it leaves out. A score works the same way. It records what must be fixed and stays silent on what must be chosen, and that silence is not a defect — it is the room where interpretation lives.

> The score is a frozen set of instructions. The music is what happens when a living player thaws them in real time.

This is why "just play what's written" is, on close inspection, impossible. The written page does not contain a tempo, a tone, a sense of line, or a single actual sound. Those are supplied by the performer, every time, out of training and taste and the acoustics of the room. The notation constrains the performance; it does not determine it.

## Reading the Composer's Handwriting

If notation is a map, then learning a piece is partly an act of *reading between the marks* — inferring intention from the limited vocabulary the composer had to work with. A flurry of accents suggests urgency. A sudden thinning of texture asks for a lighter touch. A passage marked **sempre piano** through a rising line is asking for restraint exactly where the instrument wants to bloom, and that tension is the point.

Good editors and copyists understand they are in the same business. When I decide whether a slur arcs over four notes or six, I am not transcribing a fact; I am clarifying an intention so the player reads the composer's gesture and not my guesswork. Engraving is interpretation wearing a very disciplined uniform.

## So What Is the Score For?

If it isn't the music, why revere it? Because it is the most durable, most portable form a musical idea has ever had. A symphony can outlive its composer by centuries, cross every border, and be rebuilt from scratch by people who never heard the original — all because someone wrote down enough, and only enough, to make the rebuilding possible.

The score is a letter sent forward in time to performers the composer will never meet. It cannot dictate every detail, so it doesn't try. It fixes the skeleton and trusts the reader with the flesh. That trust is not a weakness of notation. It is the reason the art is still alive: every performance is a fresh reading of an old map, and the territory is remade, in sound, each time someone agrees to play.
