Levante is named for the wind, not the notebook — the easterly that pushes damp air along this coast and, more often than not, flattens the swell enough to make the rocks safe to walk. When it blows through the night, the morning low is usually the clearest one of the month, and that is when these notes get written.

The project is simple: walk the same rocky shelf on the lowest tides of each season, write down exactly what is exposed, and change nothing about the method from one visit to the next. No aquarium collecting, no turned-over boulders left upside down, no specimens taken home. A folding rule, a hand lens, a waterproof notebook, and a wristwatch set to the harbor tide table are the whole kit.

## Method

Each entry corresponds to one low-water visit, timed to start about forty minutes before the predicted minimum and end when the rising water pushes back over the lowest quadrat line. The tide reading logged with each entry is the predicted height at that morning's low, not a measurement taken on site — the site itself has no gauge, only the rock's own barnacle and lichen lines, which are more honest than any printed table by the time a season has passed.

Species are logged by common name first, with the binomial alongside on first mention in an entry. Counts are approximate unless a quadrat is noted directly in the text. Nothing here is a formal survey; it is closer to a diary that happens to keep good company with the tide tables.

## Who keeps it

These notes are kept by Adela Ferran, who trained as a benthic ecologist and now spends most low tides doing exactly what is described above, for no one in particular except whoever reads this afterward. Corrections on identification are always welcome — several entries have already been quietly fixed after a sharper eye caught a limpet misnamed as a top shell.

## Using this record

Every entry lives in the [tide log](/tide-logs/), sorted newest first, with the tags — mostly by the taxonomic group most represented on that visit — usable as a rough index. The [search page](/search/) covers titles and full entry text if you are hunting for a specific species or cove.
