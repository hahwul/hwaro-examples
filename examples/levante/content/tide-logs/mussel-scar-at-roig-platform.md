The concrete groyne at the platform's western edge finally lost a slab over the winter, and the bare patch it left behind has been colonised by nothing yet — a clean rectangle of exposed rock in the middle of an otherwise settled mussel bed, sharp-edged enough to still read as damage rather than habitat.

<!-- more -->

The mussel bed itself (*Mytilus galloprovincialis*) runs thick around the scar's edges, byssal threads visibly reattaching along the fracture line where the slab tore free — a slow patch job that will probably close the gap within two or three seasons if nothing else breaks loose first. A banded dye murex (*Hexaplex trunculus*) was found working the bed's margin, drill hole already started in one mussel shell; these show up here often enough that the mussels along this stretch carry more bore scars than the ones at Cala Ombrina, half a kilometre east.

A single sea hare (*Aplysia depilans*), close to twenty centimetres and a mottled brown-green, sat motionless in a shallow pool near the groyne — sea hares turn up here perhaps twice a year, always solitary, always in early summer. Released the way it was found, undisturbed. Cystoseira thickets (*Cystoseira compressa*) further out on the platform are thickening into full canopy for the season, a good sign after two summers of visibly thinner cover; whatever stressed them seems to have eased.

This was the lowest reading logged at Roig platform since the notebook started, and the shelf stayed dry long enough to walk the whole scar twice.
