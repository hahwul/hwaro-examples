Punta Salobre only gives up its lower terrace a few mornings a year, and the equinox spring tide is the most reliable of them — this year's reading was middling by that standard, which still meant forty extra centimetres of rock nobody sees in an ordinary month.

<!-- more -->

The terrace itself is a shallow step in the platform, always holding a skin of water even at the lowest reading, and it is where the purple sea urchins (*Paracentrotus lividus*) concentrate. Counted along a two-metre stretch of the step edge: twenty-three individuals, most tucked into shallow grazing pits worn into the soft limestone over what must be years, a few sitting exposed on bare rock with no pit at all — juveniles, probably, that haven't started excavating yet.

Marbled rock crabs (*Pachygrapsus marmoratus*) were everywhere the moment the water dropped, moving in that quick sideways scatter they do when a shadow crosses them, and impossible to count with any honesty — dozens, certainly, across every size class from thumbnail to full adult.

A meadow of sea lettuce (*Ulva rigida*) has taken over a shallow pool at the terrace's landward edge since the last visit, bright enough to see from the cliff path above. Snakelocks anemones (*Anemonia viridis*) sit at its margins in full sun, tentacles fully extended and faintly violet-tipped, which they only do in strong light. The tide turned before the terrace's lowest corner was reached; that corner will have to wait for a bigger spring.
