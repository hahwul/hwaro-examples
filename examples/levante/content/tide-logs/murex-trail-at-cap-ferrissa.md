The tide table had flagged this morning months ago — the lowest predicted reading of the whole year, low enough to reach a strip of Cap Ferrissa's outer platform that has come up dry perhaps three times since the notebook began. It was worth setting an alarm for the dark.

<!-- more -->

A trail of banded dye murex (*Hexaplex trunculus*) crossed the outer platform in a loose line, seven shells in various stages of a single slow migration toward what was probably a mussel patch further out than the water allowed this morning to follow. Trails like this are usually inferred rather than seen outright; this one was clear enough to photograph shell by shell along its length, roughly a metre between each.

The purple sea urchin bed on the platform's lower step was denser than the one at Punta Salobre — a rough count along a three-metre stretch came to over forty individuals, tight enough in places that adjacent grazing pits share a wall. A black brittle star (*Ophioderma longicauda*) was found in the open this time, not under a slab, arms partly extended across bare rock in a shaded gap between boulders — unusual enough to note, since every other sighting here has been under cover.

Marbled rock crabs worked the tideline behind the retreating edge the whole visit, the usual scatter. The water was already returning by the time the outer strip was reached a second time; whatever wasn't recorded on the first pass will have to wait another year for a tide this low.
