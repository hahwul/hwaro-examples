No wind, no swell, and a sky the colour of the rock itself — the kind of flat winter morning that makes Bassa flats easy to read, because nothing is moving except what you disturb yourself. This is the shelf's third visit and the first with enough light to photograph properly, not that photographs are kept, but it changes how carefully you look.

<!-- more -->

Grooved chitons (*Chiton olivaceus*) turned up in the highest number logged here so far — nine individuals across the middle shelf, most under loose cobbles rather than fixed to bare rock, each one replaced exactly as found. Chitons are the one group in this notebook that reward turning things over more than any other, and Bassa flats has consistently been the best cove for them, likely because the cobble field never fully compacts the way the rock does at Roig.

The star barnacle band here is thinner than at Cala Ombrina but denser than expected for a flat, current-sheltered platform — a reminder that exposure to swell is not the only thing that decides where this species settles. Marbled rock crabs were scarce today, three seen against the dozens usual for a June visit, which tracks with the season rather than anything wrong with the shelf.

Toothed top shells (*Osilinus turbinatus*) are back in numbers after being nearly absent through most of last year — six counted along the same crack line checked at every visit, the most since the notebook started tracking this particular crack. Whatever moved them off, they seem to have moved back.
