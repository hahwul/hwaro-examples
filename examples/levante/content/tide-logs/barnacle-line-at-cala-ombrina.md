Ten days of levante finally broke the night before this visit, and the shelf at Cala Ombrina looked scoured rather than battered — every loose weed gone, every fixed thing still exactly where it was in November. That is the useful thing about a barnacle line: it either holds through a blow or it doesn't, and this one held.

<!-- more -->

The star barnacles (*Chthamalus stellatus*) run in an unbroken band from about knee height down to the first standing pool, denser on the north-facing rock than the south, which tracks with three winters of watching the same pattern. Rough limpets (*Patella rustica*) sit scattered through the band rather than massed at its edge, each one homed to a scar in the rock it will return to after grazing.

A quadrat at the northern end of the shelf, thrown without looking, came back:

| Species | Count in 0.25 m² |
|---|---|
| Star barnacle | 61 |
| Rough limpet | 4 |
| Toothed top shell | 2 |

Toothed top shells (*Osilinus turbinatus*) were harder to find than usual — only two, both wedged into the same crack — which may just mean they moved higher during the storm and haven't come back down yet. Worth checking again on the next low.

Above the barnacle line, in crevices that only ever see spray, a first flush of rock samphire (*Crithmum maritimum*) has come up fleshy and green after the winter rain. It is not a tide-pool animal, but it marks the top edge of the zone as reliably as anything logged below it, and it is worth noting every January it appears.
