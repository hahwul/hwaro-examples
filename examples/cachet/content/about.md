## What "cachet" means here

A cachet was originally a seal — proof that a document had passed through the right hands and could be trusted without re-checking it. That is the standard I hold a pipeline to: a deploy should be trustworthy enough that nobody has to watch it happen.

Most of my engagements start the same way. A team has a deployment process that technically works, run by one or two people who have memorized its quirks, undocumented and unowned by anyone else. Then that person changes teams, or leaves, and every release becomes an archaeology project. I get called in somewhere between "we're nervous about the next release" and "we already had the incident."

## How I work

I do not arrive with a target stack. The first two weeks are always the same: read every script that runs during a deploy, sit in on a release, and write down what actually happens — not what the runbook says happens. From there the plan is usually staged: stabilize the existing process first so nobody is firefighting during the migration, then replace it piece by piece with something a new hire could run on day one. I hand off with documentation, not just working code, and I stay on as a retainer for the teams that want a second opinion before the next migration.
