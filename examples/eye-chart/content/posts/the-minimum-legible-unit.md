## WHAT SURVIVES THE BLUR

Here is a clinical exercise. Take any interface and apply a Gaussian blur of 8 pixels. Whatever survives — whatever you can still read, whatever structure you can still perceive — that is the **minimum legible unit** of the design.

The blur test is not subtle. It will immediately expose:

- Headings that are not large enough to read at a glance
- Body text that depends entirely on fine details to parse
- Buttons that vanish against their backgrounds
- Navigation that relies on hover states to communicate meaning

The minimum legible unit is the part of your communication that survives degradation. Everything else is detail.

> Good information design is not about adding clarity. It is about removing everything that obscures it.

## THE OPTOMETRIST'S REDUCTION

When I fit a first-time contact lens wearer, I do not start with the final prescription. I start with something slightly undercorrected. The goal is to establish baseline: what is the minimum correction that produces functional vision?

From there, we refine.

This is not laziness. It is *diagnostic rigor*. Starting from the minimum prevents overcorrection, which is its own category of problem. Overcorrected vision creates distortion, headaches, and fatigue. The eye was not designed to be pushed past its optimum.

Interfaces are the same. Overcorrected design — too much visual information, too many clarifying labels, too many guiding arrows — creates cognitive fatigue. The user is exhausted by the effort of filtering signal from the noise added in the name of clarity.

## THREE CATEGORIES OF VISUAL NOISE

After years of examining both eyes and interfaces, I have settled on three categories of visual noise that reliably degrade the minimum legible unit:

### DECORATIVE COMPLEXITY

Textures, shadows, ornamental borders, illustrative flourishes that carry no information. These consume visual bandwidth. The eye must process them before it can ignore them. The processing cost is small but it is never zero.

The clinical term for a lens coated with unnecessary layers is *clouded*. A clouded lens is not merely imperfect. It is actively harmful.

### TYPOGRAPHIC INCONSISTENCY

Using six type sizes, three weights, and two families without a clear hierarchy is the visual equivalent of a patient who has had their prescription changed by three different doctors without a unified record. Every element insists on its own importance. None communicate it.

*Consistency is not limitation.* A consistent type scale is a prescription that works.

### PROXIMITY WITHOUT RELATIONSHIP

Elements placed near each other that have no logical connection. The eye assumes proximity implies relationship — this is Gestalt, this is how human vision evolved. When proximity and relationship disagree, the user must spend cognitive effort resolving the contradiction.

Use space as signal. Separation is information. Closeness is information. Do not spend either carelessly.

---

## THE MINIMUM IS ENOUGH

The Snellen chart's bottom line — the `20/20` line — contains letters that are 8.73 millimeters tall at a distance of six meters. That is the minimum. Not smaller. Not more complex. Not decorated.

Just legible. Just enough.

A useful design heuristic: before you add anything, ask whether the design without it passes the minimum legibility test. If it does, consider not adding the thing.

The minimum legible unit is not a constraint on creativity. It is the baseline below which creativity stops being communication and starts being noise.

**Pass the blur test. Then, and only then, add detail.**

---

The chart on the wall of an optometrist's office contains exactly as much information as it needs. No more. Every element serves the diagnostic. The design is invisible because the design is correct.

That is the standard.
