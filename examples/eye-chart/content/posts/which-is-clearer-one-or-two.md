## THE QUESTION

Every optometrist's appointment reaches the same pivot point. The phoropter is in place. The patient is staring at the chart. And the examiner asks, with clinical neutrality: *which is clearer — one, or two?*

Not: which is more interesting. Not: which is more stylish. Not: which do you prefer in the abstract.

**Which is clearer.**

I want that question asked in every design review, about every single decision on the screen.

---

## ONE

Lens one is your current design. It is familiar. You have been staring at it for weeks. The team has discussed it extensively. There are strong feelings about the color of the secondary button. Someone argued for forty-five minutes about whether the nav should be 14px or 15px.

You cannot see it clearly anymore. That is not a character flaw. It is just what happens when you look at anything long enough. Your visual system adapts. The signal becomes invisible.

This is why fresh eyes exist. This is why optometrists do not prescribe glasses for themselves.

## TWO

Lens two is what you show a new user who has never heard of your product. Watch where their eyes go first. Watch what they click. Watch where they hesitate.

> The hesitation is the diagnosis. Wherever a user pauses and looks confused, there is a refractive error in your design.

A hesitation is not stupidity. A hesitation is a signal that the visual hierarchy has failed to communicate what should happen next. The path forward is not *clear*.

---

## THE REFRACTION PROCESS

A refractive exam does not jump straight to the final prescription. It works through a process of elimination: gross adjustments first, fine adjustments second. Each flip of the lens narrows the range.

Good design iteration works identically:

1. **Gross pass** — is the overall structure correct? Does the hierarchy make sense? Can a user identify the primary action?
2. **Fine pass** — is the type set correctly? Are the sizes, weights, and spacings doing what they should?
3. **Final check** — does it hold up on a small screen? In poor light? For someone with average eyesight, not a designer's trained eye?

Each stage is a flip of the lens. One or two. Clearer, or not. The answer is never "both are equally fine." One is always better. The work is finding which one.

## THE HARDEST PART

The hardest part of the exam is not technical. It is asking the question without loading it. The examiner does not say: "This one is from our new premium lens line and it tested very well in focus groups." The examiner does not say: "Most patients prefer option two."

The examiner asks neutrally and waits.

In a design review, try this. Put two options on screen. Do not explain them. Do not introduce them. Ask a single question — *which is clearer* — and wait for the answer.

**The silence before the answer is where the real information lives.**

---

You will notice that the question strips away every subjective consideration. Not prettier. Not more modern. Not more on-brand. *Clearer.* It forces the criterion back to first principles.

That is the question. One or two. Ask it every time.
