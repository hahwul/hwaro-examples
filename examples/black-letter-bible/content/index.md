<p class="front-lede">This study treats the black-letter Bibles of Western printing - those volumes impressed in Gothic type from the middle of the fifteenth century into the early seventeenth, and in continued tradition upon certain presses into the present day. The chapters that follow attend to the particular types, the pressmen, the formats, and the ornament.</p>

<p class="illum-para">Where the scribes of the monasteries had set the sacred text in the rounded Carolingian minuscule and, later, in the condensed Gothic textualis, the first generation of printers took up the textualis as their model and cut their types to match. The result - the forty-two-line Bible of Gutenberg, struck between 1452 and 1455 - was a book that pretended to be a manuscript, and very nearly succeeded: early owners filled in the rubricated initials by hand, added coloured paragraph marks, and in many cases believed themselves to possess a very fine manuscript of a regular monastic hand.</p>

<p>Within a generation the illusion was set aside and the printed book declared itself as such. The textura type became the standard for sacred text in Northern Europe, and remained so for the better part of a century before the roman types of the Italian humanist presses began their slow advance northward. In England the black-letter Bible persisted longer than elsewhere - the Coverdale Bible of 1535, the Great Bible of 1539, and the Authorised Version of 1611 were all set principally in textura, and it was only in the course of the seventeenth century that roman began to displace black-letter in English sacred printing.</p>

<p>This study gathers ten chapters of commentary upon the principal types, presses, and editions of this tradition. The reader will find on each chapter page a brief specimen of the type, a record of the pressmen, a collation of the format, and a short discussion of the ornamental programme. The intent is neither exhaustive nor definitive; it is a pedagogical sampling for the student of typography and the historian of the book.</p>

<p>The present edition has been typeset in UnifrakturMaguntia for the display type - a twenty-first-century digital cutting after Peter Wiegel's reconstruction of the Schwabacher used in the German Bibles of the sixteenth century - and in Old Standard TT for the body text, a digital cutting after the nineteenth-century Russian scholarly types. The two faces sit well together upon the dark folio ground and permit the dense reading which the subject demands.</p>
