## The Eighteenth-Century Decline

By 1700 the roman type had substantially displaced black-letter in English Bible printing. The only surviving use of black-letter in English sacred books was confined to certain portions of the Book of Common Prayer (the rubrics, set in Gothic against the body text in roman) and to commemorative editions of the 1611 text issued for particular anniversaries or for particular ecclesiastical ceremonies. Continental Bible printing followed a parallel trajectory, though the German-language Bibles retained the Schwabacher and Fraktur types for considerably longer.

In Germany, the Fraktur type - a late-Gothic development cut originally for the chancery documents of the Habsburg Emperor Maximilian I in the early sixteenth century - became the standard for German vernacular publishing in the seventeenth century and remained so until the 1940s, when the National Socialist government banned it by decree in 1941 (ostensibly as "Jewish" in origin, though this was a fabrication) and imposed roman types as the standard. The Fraktur never recovered its former dominance, though it persists in certain commemorative and ecclesiastical contexts.

## The Kelmscott Revival

William Morris's Kelmscott Press, founded in Hammersmith in 1891, took the revival of the textura tradition as one of its principal projects. Morris's Troy Type (1892) is a modern cutting of the fifteenth-century Gothic type as modified for the Kelmscott Chaucer of 1896. The Chaucer is not a Bible, but its typographic programme - two columns, densely set, with historiated initials, ornamental borders, and column dividers - is directly modelled upon the forty-two-line Bible and has shaped every subsequent private-press treatment of the Gothic tradition.

The Kelmscott Press produced its own versions of several Biblical texts (the Psalmi Penitentiales of 1894, the Book of Wisdom of 1897) in Troy Type with full ornamental programme. The impressions are small (three hundred copies or thereabouts) and the books are among the finest productions of the private press movement.

## The Doves and Ashendene Presses

Cobden-Sanderson's Doves Press (1900-1916) and St John Hornby's Ashendene Press (1895-1935) both set the Bible in types explicitly drawn from the early printing tradition. The Doves Bible (1903-1905) is set in the Doves Type - a light roman after Jenson rather than a textura - and represents a different branch of the revival. The Ashendene Bible of 1909-1911 was never completed but produced several gatherings in the Subiaco Type, a close imitation of the types used by Sweynheym and Pannartz at the monastery of Subiaco in 1465.

Neither of these is strictly a black-letter Bible, but they form part of the same intellectual project: the recovery of the typographic traditions which the nineteenth-century industrial printing had displaced.

## The Twentieth-Century Bibles

Several twentieth-century presses produced commemorative editions of the Bible in black-letter. The Oxford University Press issued a 1935 black-letter reprint of the 1611 text to mark the four-hundredth anniversary of the Coverdale Bible; the Cambridge University Press issued a similar 1961 reprint for the three-hundred-and-fiftieth anniversary of the 1611 Authorised Version. Both are creditable productions but are essentially facsimiles rather than fresh compositions.

The Arion Press of San Francisco completed a major new folio Bible in 2000 (the "Arion Press Bible"), set in the Lectern Roman - not a black-letter type, but with a typographic programme explicitly modelled upon the textura Bibles of the Reformation. The Arion Bible is the most ambitious Bible typography of the late twentieth century.

## The Digital Inheritance

The digital font foundries of the last three decades have produced numerous cuttings of the textura, Schwabacher, and Fraktur types, ranging from commercial revivals (Hermann Zapf's Gilgengart of 1941-1953, still in Linotype distribution) to scholarly reconstructions (Peter Wiegel's UnifrakturMaguntia of 2007, used in the display type of the present edition). The availability of these digital cuttings has made the textura tradition accessible to amateur typographers in a way that was not possible in the letterpress era, and a modest community of contemporary digital practitioners continues to produce work in the tradition.
