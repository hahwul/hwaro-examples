## The Commission

The Authorised Version was commissioned by King James I at the Hampton Court Conference of 1604 and produced over the following seven years by a company of fifty-four translators working in six companies at Westminster, Oxford, and Cambridge. The text was ready for the press by 1610; printing began the same year and the first folio copies were ready for distribution in the spring of 1611.

## The Type

Barker's textura for the 1611 Bible is one of the last great cuttings of English black-letter for Bible printing. It is cut at a large body size (the first folio is a lectern Bible, intended to be read aloud in parish churches, and requires a type that is legible at a distance of several feet) with generous line spacing and a clear separation between words.

The type is characteristically English rather than Continental in its design: the minims are somewhat less rigid than in the German textura tradition, the broken bowls are more organically drawn, and the tails of the descenders are longer and more gracefully finished. The result is a type that is at once Gothic in its general character and English in its particulars.

## The Rubrication

The 1611 Bible continues the rubrication programme of the earlier English Bibles, with chapter openings set in red ink (printed from a second impression rather than added by hand, as the technology had long since been mastered) and the chapter initials set in a heavier textura with an ornamental woodcut border.

The chapter initials are not figurative; the Protestant settlement of the English Church had moved away from the figurative illumination of the mediaeval tradition toward a more restrained ornamental programme. The borders around the chapter initials are in a late Renaissance foliate manner, with acanthus scrolls and strapwork drawn from the prevailing Continental printing styles.

## The Textual Apparatus

The 1611 Bible includes an elaborate textual apparatus in the outer margins: variant readings, alternative translations, and cross-references to other passages of scripture. The apparatus is set in a smaller textura at a condensed letterfit, and rewards the patient reader who follows the cross-references across the folio.

## The Persistence of Black-Letter

The 1611 Bible is the last major English Bible to be set principally in black-letter. The roman-type reprint of 1612 (also from Barker's press, intended for private reading rather than lectern use) was the first step in a transition which was substantially complete by 1640. Nineteenth and twentieth century reprints have occasionally returned to a black-letter setting for commemorative editions, but the living tradition of printing the English Bible in Gothic type ended with the Barker press in the middle of the seventeenth century.
