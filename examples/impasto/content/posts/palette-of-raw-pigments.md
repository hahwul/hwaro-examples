Before there were hex codes, there were pigments. Cadmium red. Yellow ochre. Burnt sienna. Ivory black. Each name carries centuries of craft.

<!-- more -->

## Naming Colors

The names we give colors shape how we use them. A designer working with `#c8102e` makes different choices than one working with "cadmium red." The latter carries weight -- a history of studios, of linseed oil, of palette knives scraping across wooden boards.

This theme takes its palette from that tradition:

| Role       | Name           | Value     |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| Background | Raw Canvas     | `#f5f0e8` |
| Text       | Charcoal       | `#2c2416` |
| Accent     | Cadmium Red    | `#c8102e` |
| Highlight  | Yellow Ochre   | `#c49a2a` |
| Muted      | Warm Grey      | `#8a7e6b` |

## Warm Over Cool

Most dark themes lean cool -- blues, purples, cyans. Most light themes lean neutral -- pure whites and greys. This palette is deliberately warm. Canvas backgrounds have a yellow undertone. Text is brown-black rather than blue-black. Even the code blocks carry warmth.

Warmth in a color palette does what impasto does on canvas: it brings the surface closer. Cool palettes recede. Warm palettes advance. They feel present, tangible, real.

## Constraint as Freedom

Working with five core colors may seem limiting. In practice, it is liberating. Every color decision is already made. The designer is free to focus on structure, rhythm, and weight -- the things that actually matter.

> The enemy of art is the absence of limitations.
> -- Orson Welles
