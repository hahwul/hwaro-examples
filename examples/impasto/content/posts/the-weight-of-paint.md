There is a particular satisfaction in watching paint pile up on canvas. Each stroke adds not just color but mass, not just hue but height. The impasto technique is, at its core, an act of accumulation.

<!-- more -->

## Texture as Content

When Van Gogh laid thick curls of cobalt and chrome yellow across his skies, he was not merely depicting starlight. He was building it. The paint itself became the subject -- its ridges catching real light, its valleys holding real shadow.

This is the fundamental insight of impasto: **texture is not decoration. It is content.**

## The Digital Parallel

In digital design, we have lost much of this physicality. Screens are flat. Pixels have no weight. But we can evoke the sensation of depth through deliberate visual choices:

- Heavy borders that suggest layered edges
- Deep shadows that imply relief from the surface
- Bold type that commands the eye with its mass
- Generous spacing that lets each element breathe and assert itself

```css
.card {
  border: 4px solid #2c2416;
  box-shadow: 6px 6px 0 #2c2416;
}
```

The code is simple. The effect is substantial.

## Less Subtlety, More Conviction

The prevailing trend in web design favors the ethereal -- thin lines, airy gradients, whisper-light type. Impasto pushes back against this. It insists that weight has beauty. That boldness is not the same as loudness. That a thick stroke, placed with intention, carries more meaning than a thousand hairlines.

Sometimes the bravest design choice is simply to commit.
