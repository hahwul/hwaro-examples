This month was dedicated to completing a three-legged Windsor stool commission for a local writer. For the seat, I selected a single slab of eastern white pine, carving the saddle profile using a hand adze, a drawknife, and a heavy wooden scraper. Pine is lightweight and carving-friendly, yet holds its shape perfectly when seasoned. The legs and stretchers were shaped from green red oak billets that I had split in the winter.

<!-- more -->

Working the green oak on the shavehorse is a tactile pleasure; the wood curls peel off like butter under a razor-sharp drawknife. Once shaped, the legs were turned on a foot-powered lathe to establish the traditional bamboo-style collars. The joinery is entirely mechanical, relying on through-wedged round mortise and tenon joints.

The oak legs were dried to a low moisture content before assembly, while the pine seat remained slightly green. As the seat dries, it shrinks slightly, locking the legs in place with a vice-like grip. A small wedge of walnut was driven into the top of each tenon to ensure the joint never loosens over decades of heavy use.
