In early November, I set up ten monitoring plots in the watershed to assess the spread of beech scale disease (Cryptococcus fagisuga). This insect, combined with a neonectria fungus, causes beech bark disease, which has devastated American beech stands throughout the northeast. In each plot, I surveyed every beech tree over ten centimeters in diameter, mapping the severity of the scale infestation and checking for the telltale red fruiting bodies of the fungus.

<!-- more -->

I also took tree core samples to see how the growth rates have declined since the initial infection occurred. The results show a clear drop in annual ring growth starting roughly three years after scale colonization. Interestingly, some mature trees seem to exhibit resistance, showing minimal bark lesions despite being surrounded by heavily diseased neighbors.

I have marked these resistant specimens for future seed collection, in hopes of breeding resistant saplings. Documenting these survivor trees is our best chance at conserving the beech component of these forests, which provides critical food and shelter for local wildlife.
