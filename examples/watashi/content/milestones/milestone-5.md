During the cold, quiet weeks of late winter, I focused on building a large trestle dining table from black cherry lumber that had been salvaged from a nearby orchard renovation several years ago. Cherry is a beautiful wood to work with hand planes, releasing a sweet, almost fruit-like aroma as the shavings are sliced away. The top is composed of three wide, book-matched planks, which I joined using hide glue and hidden splines to ensure flat, stable seams.

<!-- more -->

The trestle base is designed to be fully demountable, using tusk-tenon joints where the main stretcher passes through the heavy vertical posts. Each tenon is locked in place by a matching walnut wedge, which can be tapped tight with a mallet if the wood shrinks or swells with seasonal humidity changes.

I spent three days tuning the mortises with a wide socket chisel, ensuring that the wedges fit snugly and seat with a satisfying friction. This traditional joinery is not only strong but also allows the table to be easily disassembled for moving, making it a piece that will survive generations of use.
