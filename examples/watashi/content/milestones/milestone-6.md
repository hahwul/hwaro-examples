In mid-May, as the forest canopy leaf-out neared completion, I conducted my annual understory regeneration surveys in several canopy gaps that were created by winter blowdowns. These gaps are crucial arenas of competition, where young saplings race to capture the sunlight and fill the void in the canopy. In each gap, I laid out circular quadrats to count and measure the height of all tree saplings, paying close attention to the ratio of shade-tolerant beech and sugar maple to shade-intolerant cherry and ash.

<!-- more -->

I also measured the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) at the forest floor using a ceptometer, mapping how light levels vary from the center of the gap to the forest edge. This data helps us model how these canopy disturbances drive changes in forest composition over decades.

The initial counts show a dense carpet of sugar maple saplings, which are poised to take over the understory, although a few fast-growing white birch saplings have managed to outpace them in the center of the larger gaps where light is most abundant.
