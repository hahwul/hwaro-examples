During the first heavy freeze of January, I hiked up the east ridge to inspect a wind-fallen white ash tree that had been damaged in last autumn's storms. It was an ancient tree, positioned at the edge of a small clearing where it had grown slowly under the shadow of taller hemlocks. Harvesting timber in the winter is ideal, as the sap flow is dormant, which reduces the moisture content of the wood and prevents fungal staining during the drying process.

<!-- more -->

With the help of a crosscut saw, I bucked the main trunk into four-foot bolts, which will be hauled down to the workshop on a sled. The ring structure is remarkably dense, showing an average growth rate of less than one millimeter per year. This tight grain is exactly what is needed for split-spindle woodworking.

In the shop, I will use a wedge and froe to split the bolts along the medullary rays into rough billets. By splitting the wood rather than sawing it, I can follow the natural slope of the fibers, ensuring that the final chair parts will have maximum strength. The billets will then be shaved down on a draw-shave horse.
