With the arrival of spring, I began the seasonal fieldwork at Hemlock Ravine. The goal of this campaign is to obtain core samples from forty old-growth Eastern hemlocks. These trees are located on a steep, northeast-facing slope where the soils are shallow and nutrient-poor, making the trees highly sensitive to climate fluctuations. Using an increment borer, a hand-cranked hollow drill, I extracted pencil-thin wood cores from the trunks at breast height.

<!-- more -->

Each sample provides a continuous record of the tree's life, going back over two hundred years. Back in the laboratory, these cores will be mounted on wooden blocks, sanded with progressively finer sandpaper, and examined under a binocular microscope to measure ring widths. By cross-dating these samples with historical meteorological records, we can reconstruct regional drought patterns.

The initial field observations show a significant narrowing of rings during the early 1930s, corresponding to a major dry spell. This scientific data is invaluable for understanding how these slow-growing canopies adapt to long-term climate stressors.
