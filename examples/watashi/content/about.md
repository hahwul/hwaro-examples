I am a forestry research fellow based in the northern hardwood zone, specializing in tree-ring analysis (dendrochronology) and old-growth canopy structure. Concurrently, I run a small woodwork workshop where I craft bespoke furniture using traditional Japanese and European hand tool techniques. My academic research focuses on the response of canopy trees to historical drought patterns, utilizing deep-core boring and ring-width indices.

This scientific exploration of wood anatomy directly informs my studio practice. Knowing how a tree grew—whether it struggled for light in a dense stand or stood alone against the wind—helps me understand how the timber will behave on the workbench. In the workshop, I work primarily with local hardwood logs, splitting them along the medullary rays and using drawknives and hand planes to shape parts. This approach preserves the continuous fibers of the wood, resulting in pieces that are both lighter and stronger than those cut by machine. This website is a repository of both my scientific reports and my hand-built objects.
