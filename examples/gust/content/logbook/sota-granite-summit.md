Solstice morning, and the trail to the summit was still holding a rime of frost
in the shade. Summits on the Air rewards you for suffering a little: carry a
radio to a qualifying peak, make four contacts, and the summit counts. The
suffering this time was 1,100 metres of gain with a squid pole strapped to the
pack like a fishing rod.

<!-- more -->

The station on top fits in a lunch box. A five-watt CW transceiver, a linked
dipole cut for 20 and 40, the mast guyed to a cairn, and a straight key wedged on
a flat rock. No amplifier, no tuner, no mains — just a small LiFePO4 pack and
whatever the ionosphere feels like doing.

I called CQ SOTA on 14.061 at 1730Z and the frequency filled up inside a minute.
Spotting networks mean chasers are watching for you before you have finished
sending your callsign. Twelve contacts in twenty minutes, coast to coast, every
one of them copying five watts without complaint. The one that made the trip
worth it was **WA7SUM**, a summit-to-summit with another activator two states
away — two people on two mountains, forty milliwatts of received signal each,
trading reports over a straight key.

The wind came up, the log hit its four contacts and kept going, and I packed the
lunch box back into the frost. QRP is not a handicap. On a good summit it is the
entire point.
