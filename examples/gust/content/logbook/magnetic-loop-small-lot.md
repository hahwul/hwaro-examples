Not every station has a fir tree and a lot line to hide a sloper behind. When I
helped a friend get on the air from a townhouse with a postage-stamp yard and a
watchful association, the answer was a small transmitting magnetic loop — a
metre of copper tubing bent into a circle, tuned by a fat variable capacitor,
and leaned against the fence looking like garden art.

<!-- more -->

A magnetic loop is a resonant tank circuit that happens to radiate. The loop is
the inductor; the capacitor across the gap resonates it. Because the whole thing
is high-Q, the efficiency can be surprisingly good for its size — but that same
high Q makes it viciously narrow-band. On 15 metres this loop has a 2:1 SWR
bandwidth of only about 25 kHz. Move across the band and you must re-tune the
capacitor every time.

That narrow bandwidth is also a feature. The loop is a strong bandpass filter, so
the local noise that swamps a compromised wire antenna simply is not there. A
valley station or a townhouse full of switching supplies gets a quieter receive
than the size suggests.

The capacitor is the whole game. Use a wide-spaced or vacuum variable — ordinary
receiving capacitors arc over the moment you key up, because the circulating
current in a tuned loop is enormous even at low power. We ran forty watts of FT8,
peaked the tuning by ear on the band noise, and at 0641Z **JA1KTO** came back
from Tokyo on 21.074. A metre of copper tubing, leaning on a fence, reaching
across the Pacific. Restricted lots are a constraint, not a sentence.
