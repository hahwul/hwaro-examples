Saebyeok (새벽) is the Korean word for dawn — not sunrise itself, but the stretch of dark before it, the hour when the sky has not yet decided what color it will be. This journal is written entirely inside that hour: one lamp, one kettle, one page, finished before the window turns any real color.

The rule is simple and has held for a year now. An essay has to be started and finished before first light, in a single sitting, at whatever time the alarm happened to go off that morning. Nothing gets touched after sunrise. If a paragraph is bad in the dark, it stays bad in the dark — the version the lamp saw is the version that gets published, time-stamped down to the minute the first line went down. That minute replaces the usual category label on every entry here; it felt more honest than sorting these essays by subject when the thing they actually have in common is the hour, not the topic.

There is no editorial calendar. Some mornings produce a finished thought about a kettle or a streetlight. Others produce three hundred words about nothing in particular, kept anyway because the hour was spent and something ought to survive it. Readers looking for consistency of subject will not find it here. Readers looking for a consistent hour will find exactly seven mornings so far, each one real, each one written by someone who could, in theory, still have been asleep.

If you keep an hour like this yourself, or are thinking about starting one, [the mornings section](/mornings/) is the place to begin — sorted newest first, filed by date and by the small caps time beside it. The whole record began with [a kettle](/mornings/kettle-knows/) and has since been filed loosely under a handful of [recurring words](/tags/). There is no newsletter, no comment section, nothing asking for your address. Just the record of one lamp, kept honestly, one dark hour at a time.
