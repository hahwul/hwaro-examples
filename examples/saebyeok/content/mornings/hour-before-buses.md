Somewhere down the hill there is a depot, and in the depot there are buses, and at a time I have never confirmed but have come to trust completely, the first of them pulls out onto the main road and the city's private hour is over. Until then, the street outside my window belongs to almost no one.

<!-- more -->

I have learned to hear the difference between the silence at 04:30 and the silence at 05:30, though I could not describe the difference to anyone who has not sat still enough to notice it. The early silence is total, slightly suspicious of itself, the kind that makes you check whether the clock has stopped. The later silence has texture — a compressor humming somewhere, a door two buildings over, the specific quality of air that means the temperature is about to start rising. It is a silence that knows it is ending.

The first bus, when it finally comes, does not announce itself so much as change the register of everything. Its brakes, its weight on the road, are the loudest thing the street has heard in hours, and after it passes the second bus follows within minutes, then a third, and by six the hour I have been keeping is effectively over, absorbed back into a city that has places to be.

I do not resent the buses. I have ridden enough of them at more reasonable hours to know they are only doing their job, delivering people who did not choose to be up this early the way I did. But I notice, every time, a small private mourning when the first one passes — not because the essay is unfinished, though it usually is, but because the specific kind of attention available before six o'clock does not survive contact with the working day. It has to be spent before the buses start, or it is gone until tomorrow, and tomorrow is never guaranteed to feel the same.
