The desk lamp is broken, so this morning I am at the kitchen table, which turns out to change the essay more than I expected. The desk faces a wall. The kitchen table faces a window, and at 05:21 in January the window is a black mirror, showing me the room, the mug, my own face looking mildly surprised to be up this early a year into a habit that no longer requires deciding.

<!-- more -->

A year, as of this week, since [the first genuinely early morning](/mornings/kettle-knows/) — not the occasional insomnia-driven page I had written before, but the deliberate kind, alarm set on purpose, kettle filled the night before so it would not clatter and wake anyone. I did not tell myself at the time it would become a practice. I told myself I would try it for a month and see whether the sentences were any different before six than they were at nine. They were. They still are. I have never quite worked out why, only that something about writing while the rest of the street is dark removes an audience that is otherwise always faintly present, and the sentences get bolder for its absence.

{{ alert(type="aside", body="The mug on the table this morning is the same one from the first week of this — chipped now on the handle, kept anyway, because replacing it felt like it would break something other than a cup.") }}

Winter has been the hardest test of the habit, if I am honest. It is one thing to get up before sunrise in June, when the sky is already doing half the work of waking you. It is another to get up into a kitchen this cold, this dark, knowing the sun will not show any real interest in the day for another three hours. Some mornings this month I have sat here mostly to prove to myself that the habit survives the season, more than because I had anything urgent to say. I think that is allowed. Not every essay needs a reason beyond the kettle having already been switched on.

What I know now, a year in, that I did not know at the start: the value was never really the writing. The writing is what I have to show for it, the thing that makes the hour defensible to anyone who asks why I would choose to be awake at 05:21 in the coldest month of the year. But the actual thing I have been doing, quietly, at this table and the desk before it, is learning to be alone with an unfinished thought without rushing to finish it. That turns out to be useful for far more than essays. It has just been easiest to practice here, at this table, in this particular kind of dark, before anyone else in the house is awake enough to need me to be anything other than still.
