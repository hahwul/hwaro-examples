You will read this later, properly awake, with the window already bright and the coffee already made by someone other than me, and by then it will just be a paragraph on a page, no different from the others. I want to tell you, before that happens, what it looked like from here.

The hallway light is off because I did not want to wake you crossing it. I know the floor well enough now to walk it in the dark without the creak near the bathroom door, which took two winters to learn. The kettle went on quietly. The lamp on the desk is turned down as low as it goes and still throws light, the good kind, warm rather than white, the kind that makes 04:09 feel less like an hour and more like a room you get to visit.

I am not writing this because the day will be hard, or because I have something difficult to say that I could not say to your face at a reasonable hour. I am writing it because there is a version of this house that only exists between about four and six in the morning, and you are never in it, and that has started to feel like something I should account for rather than simply enjoy. You sleep through an entire, separate version of our life together. I have never resented that. I chose this hour; you did not choose to miss it.

So here is what I owe you, in place of a full report: the house is fine. The heater clicked twice around four-thirty, same as always, and I did not get up to check it because I have learned it means nothing. The essay for this morning is not very good, but it exists, which some mornings is the whole victory. And somewhere in the last hour, without deciding to, I thought about how strange and fortunate it is that I get to have this quiet, private version of a life we are otherwise living entirely in the open, together, in full daylight, where you will read this and know, finally, what the room looked like while you were still asleep in it.
