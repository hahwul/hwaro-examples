I do not decide to get up. My hand decides, reaching for the switch on the kettle in the dark, working from memory rather than sight. By the time I am fully awake the water is already climbing toward a boil, and some part of the morning has started without my permission.

<!-- more -->

There is a particular sound a kettle makes in the last thirty seconds before it clicks off — not the rolling boil itself but the quieter, higher hum just before, like the water clearing its throat. I have come to trust that sound more than any alarm. It means the room is warm enough to think in, that the first cup is close, that I have, once again, gotten up before the part of me that argues could stop me.

I keep the kettle on the counter nearest the window, which is a small inefficiency — the cord barely reaches — but it means I can watch the glass go from black to the color of a bruise while I wait. Boricha, most mornings, the barley tea my grandmother kept in an old glass jar with no label because everyone in the house already knew what it was. Some mornings, when the essay is stubborn, something stronger.

What I like about the kettle is that it asks nothing of my judgment. It does not care whether the sentence I am about to write is any good. It boils at the same rate for a bad morning as a brilliant one, and there is a kind of permission in that — the sense that the ritual will hold its shape regardless of what I do with the hour it opens. I have written some of my worst paragraphs at 05:12 and kept them anyway, not because they were good but because the kettle had already done its part, and it felt dishonest to waste the water.

Somewhere around the second cup the sentences start arriving in a more usable order. Not sooner than that. I have tried skipping the tea and going straight to the page, and the page always seems to know. The kettle, more than the notebook, is where the essay actually begins — the first proof, every single day, that I meant to show up.
