There was a streetlight outside my childhood window that switched off every morning at almost exactly the same moment, and for a stretch of years I considered it, without ever saying so to anyone, a kind of company. I do not remember deciding to watch for it. I only remember that by age nine or ten, I had a private theory that the light was aware of me watching it, and that this was a comfort rather than an embarrassment.

The streetlight outside this apartment does the same thing now, on a schedule I have never looked up because I would rather not know the mechanism. At 05:58, give or take, the orange color drains out of it over the course of about four seconds and it goes gray, then invisible, absorbed into a sky that has already started doing the same work more slowly and on a much larger scale. It is such a small event. Nobody else on the street is awake to see it happen, and even if they were, I doubt they would think it worth watching.

I think what I owe the streetlight is the original instruction in noticing something for no reason. Long before I called any of this writing, I was a child at a window keeping track of a light that owed me nothing and would not have missed me if I stopped looking. That is, as far as I can tell, most of what an essay is — attention paid to something that does not require it, offered anyway, on the theory that the paying of it is the point.

When the light outside this window goes out, I generally know the essay for the morning is close to done, whether or not it is any good. It is not a deadline exactly. It is closer to the way the streetlight in the old house used to signal that soon my mother would be up, and the private hour, whatever I had used it for, was ending in the ordinary way mornings end — not dramatically, just one light going out after another, until the whole street runs on daylight and nobody needs the lamps anymore.
