From the street, at this hour, the house looks almost entirely dark. One window glows — small, amber, low to the ground because the lamp sits on the desk and not the ceiling — and everything else stays black. I like knowing this. I like that a stranger walking past at 04:47 would see exactly one fact about this house: someone in it is awake.

<!-- more -->

There is a specific kind of privacy that comes from being the only lit room in a sleeping building. It is not the privacy of a locked door, which keeps people out. It is the privacy of scale — the rest of the house is so much darker than my desk that the desk stops feeling like part of the house at all. It becomes its own small country, with its own weather, governed by whatever I decide to think about between now and the point the coffee runs out.

I used to work with every light on, back when I believed brightness was the same thing as clarity. It isn't. A fully lit room asks to be tidied, justified, presented — it is a room for other people, even when no one else is in it. A single lamp asks nothing. It draws a circle just wide enough for a keyboard and a cup and lets the rest of the room disappear into a kind of soft, forgiving dark. I write braver sentences in that circle than I do anywhere else in the house.

Down the hall, two doors are closed on two people who trust that I will be quiet, that the light under my door means work and not worry. That trust is its own kind of gift, one I try not to spend carelessly. I keep the lamp low. I keep the keyboard quiet. And when the essay is finished, I turn the one light off and let the house go back to being entirely, evenly dark — no evidence left that anyone was ever up, except the paragraph waiting to be read later, in daylight, by someone who will never know exactly how small the room felt while it was being written.
