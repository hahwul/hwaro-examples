There is a reason bread has been called the staff of life, and it is not only nutritional. The act of making bread -- real bread, with nothing more than flour, water, salt, and time -- is one of the most grounding practices available to anyone with a kitchen and a few hours.

## The Ingredients of Waiting

A simple country loaf requires perhaps ten minutes of active work. The rest is waiting. Waiting for the dough to rise. Waiting for the oven to heat. Waiting for the crust to darken and the interior to set. In a culture that has optimised nearly every form of waiting out of existence, bread insists that you stop.

You cannot rush fermentation. You can warm the environment slightly, you can add a pinch more yeast, but the dough will rise when it is ready and not a moment before. This is not an inconvenience. It is the lesson.

## A Basic Country Loaf

This is the recipe I return to each week. It forgives inattention and rewards consistency.

- 500g strong white flour
- 350ml water, lukewarm
- 10g salt
- 7g dried yeast (or a generous tablespoon of active starter)

Mix the flour and water. Let it rest for thirty minutes -- this is the autolyse, and it does much of the work for you. Add the salt and yeast. Fold the dough over itself a few times, then cover and leave for two hours, folding once at the halfway mark.

Shape the dough gently on a floured surface. Place it in a proofing basket or a bowl lined with a floured cloth. Refrigerate overnight, or leave at room temperature for another hour.

Bake in a preheated oven at 230 degrees, with a lid or a tray of water for steam, for thirty minutes. Remove the lid and bake for another fifteen until the crust is deep brown and the loaf sounds hollow when tapped.

## What Bread Teaches

Every loaf is slightly different. The weather affects the rise. The flour behaves differently from bag to bag. Your hands learn, over weeks and months, what the dough needs -- more water, less handling, a longer rest. This knowledge is not intellectual. It lives in your fingertips.

The bread you make at home will not look like bakery bread. It will be rougher, more irregular, perhaps slightly lopsided. It will also be the best bread you have ever eaten, because you were there for every moment of its becoming.
