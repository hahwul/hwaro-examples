For years I kept my gardening knowledge in my head, and for years I made the same mistakes. Planting too early. Forgetting which variety performed well in dry summers. Losing track of what went where. The solution was not a sophisticated app or a spreadsheet. It was a notebook.

## What to Record

A seed journal does not need to be elaborate. Mine is a plain ruled notebook, and each entry follows a loose structure:

**Date and weather.** Not a detailed forecast, but the general character of the week. Was it mild? Did we have a late frost? How much rain fell?

**What was planted.** Variety name, source, and location in the garden. A rough sketch helps more than a written description.

**What happened.** Germination dates, first true leaves, transplant dates, any problems observed. This is where the real value accumulates.

**Harvest notes.** When did it start? How long did it last? Was the flavour good? Would you grow it again?

## The Accumulation of Years

A single season's journal is useful. Five years of journals is transformative. Patterns emerge that no single year can reveal. You begin to see that certain varieties always struggle in your soil. That your microclimate runs two weeks behind the regional averages. That the bed by the south wall is reliably warmer and drier than everywhere else.

This is local knowledge in the truest sense -- knowledge that belongs to your specific patch of ground and cannot be found in any book.

## Beyond the Garden

The habit of recording and reflecting has a value that extends past the garden gate. It cultivates a particular kind of attention -- the slow, patient observation that notices not dramatic events but gradual change. The same quality of attention that makes a good gardener also makes a good neighbour, a good cook, a good companion.

The seed journal is not really about seeds. It is about learning to pay attention to the place where you live.
