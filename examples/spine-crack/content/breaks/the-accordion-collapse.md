A mass-market paperback, perfect-bound with flexible glue, subjected to a reader who sets the book face-down &mdash; open, pages splayed, spine ridged against the table &mdash; every night for six months, will develop a series of parallel cracks along the spine. Not one crack but many. The book no longer has a single spine; it has an accordion of short spine-segments, each cracked and hinged independently.

## The Mechanics

A paperback spine is a thin strip of glued paper, 3 to 5mm thick, with sufficient flexibility to accept an opening of perhaps 45 degrees without damage. At 90 degrees, the glue begins to stress. At 120 degrees &mdash; the angle of a face-down paperback &mdash; the glue is past its limit, and a crack forms.

A single crack is not the accordion collapse. The accordion collapse is the result of *multiple* face-down placements, each at a slightly different position in the book. Tonight the reader stops at page 88 and sets the book face-down; the spine cracks at pages 87-89. Tomorrow the reader stops at page 134 and sets the book face-down; the spine cracks at pages 133-135. By the end of the month, the spine has cracked at a dozen separate points, and the book no longer has any single resting position. It collapses into itself.

## What The Accordion Tells Us

An accordion-collapsed paperback is a book that has been read in unfinished chunks. The reader did not sit down with two hours free and read from start to finish. The reader read for twenty minutes, was interrupted, set the book face-down, came back an hour later, read for another twenty minutes, was interrupted again. The book is a record of a life with other claims on it.

This is, we maintain, the honest way to read a paperback. The book is a portable object, cheap enough to abuse, dense enough to carry in a bag. It is designed for the interrupted reading, not the curated reading. The accordion spine is the paperback's natural state &mdash; the state to which all paperbacks will eventually converge if they are read at all.

> A paperback without an accordion spine has been collected, not read.

## The Second-Life Value

Curiously, an accordion-collapsed paperback has positive second-life value in certain circles. A heavily accordion-collapsed copy of a novel is evidence that the book has been read, and re-read, in fragments. It signals a kind of intimate engagement that a pristine copy cannot signal. Some second-hand bookshops charge slightly more for a broken-in copy than for a new-condition copy, on the theory that the break-in labour has already been done.

This is probably true. It is certainly true that a newly purchased paperback is not pleasant to read for the first fifty pages, because the spine resists. A broken-in paperback is immediately readable. The accordion spine is, in this sense, the book's matured form. It is the form the reader has been labouring toward.
