A sewn binding on raised bands &mdash; the traditional method, executed by hand, using waxed linen thread drawn through three or five stations along each signature &mdash; does not crack the way a glue binding cracks. The failure modes are different, and they are slower, gentler, and &mdash; to the sympathetic reader &mdash; more dignified.

## The Failure Modes Of Sewn Bindings

A sewn binding can fail in three principal ways:

1. **Thread breakage at a single station.** The linen thread, after many decades, loses its tensile strength. At a point of high stress &mdash; usually the kettle stitch at the head or tail &mdash; the thread breaks. One signature loosens from its neighbour. The book still functions, but the loosened signature has additional play.
2. **Paper tear at a sewing station.** The thread remains intact, but the paper at the sewing station tears. Typically this happens at the outermost signature, which bears the most stress from opening and closing. The signature can no longer be held by its sewing, and must be re-bound.
3. **Band detachment.** The raised bands &mdash; the hemp cords around which the signatures were originally sewn &mdash; lose their attachment to the boards of the cover. The textblock remains a coherent object, but it is no longer secured to the binding.

None of these failures produces a *crack* in the sense that a glue binding produces a crack. The sewn book does not snap; it loosens. The gap widens gradually over years. The book announces its age slowly and politely.

## The Advantage Of Slow Failure

A sewn binding that has loosened but not failed is still readable. The reader can open it, hold it, carry it, leaf through it. The loosening may be cosmetic at first, and structural only much later. A reader who inherits a sewn book with some loosening at the spine has inherited a book that will outlast them &mdash; and will require only occasional bindery attention, never full reconstruction.

A perfect-bound book with a cracked spine is, by contrast, a book on its way to structural failure. Once the glue has released at a single point, the release propagates. Pages begin to come loose. The cover detaches. Within a few more years, the book is a stack of loose signatures held together by habit.

> The sewn binding fails at human tempo. The glued binding fails on a schedule.

## Why We Have Both

Hand-sewn binding is expensive. A sewn hardcover, produced in a small bindery, costs several times what a perfect-bound paperback costs at mass production. For most publishing &mdash; the mass-market paperback, the popular hardcover, the trade paperback &mdash; the economics do not support sewn binding. The perfect binding is what we have, and what most readers read on.

For certain books, however, the argument for sewn binding is overwhelming: reference works the reader will return to for decades, devotional texts the reader will read daily for years, poetry that deserves an object-life longer than the reader's. For these, sewn binding is the only honest choice. A book intended to be read three thousand times should be built to be opened three thousand times.
