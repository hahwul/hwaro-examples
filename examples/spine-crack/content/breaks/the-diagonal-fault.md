A bound codex, dropped from standing height onto a hard surface, is a near-certainty to land on one corner. The corner absorbs the full momentum of the fall. The textblock, which is the heaviest component of the book, continues moving for a fraction of a second after the cover has stopped; the spine tries to hold the textblock in place; the glue or thread at the spine fails along a diagonal line, running from the point of impact diagonally across the gutter.

The result is the **diagonal fault**: a single crack running from near the head of the spine at one side to near the toe at the other. The book, set upright on its spine, leans in the direction of the fault.

## Structural Consequences

A diagonal fault is a structural failure, not a surface blemish. The spine can no longer carry the textblock uniformly; one side of the textblock is now unsupported, and the signatures on that side will gradually shift toward the fore-edge. Over months, the book will develop an asymmetrical profile: thicker on one edge, thinner on the other, with the pages no longer aligning at the fore-edge.

This is cosmetic but not catastrophic. The book still reads. The signatures remain gathered. The cover still closes. The only difference is that the book is no longer symmetrical, and it no longer sits flat.

## What The Diagonal Fault Tells Us

A diagonal fault is the signature of a single incident. It did not accumulate over time; it happened on a particular day, at a particular moment, as a consequence of a specific movement the reader made. The book remembers the drop.

Unlike the clean crack and the accordion collapse, which are records of reading, the diagonal fault is a record of *handling*. It tells us that the book has been carried &mdash; in a bag, in a briefcase, in a pocket &mdash; rather than kept on a shelf. It is the mark of a reader who treats books as portable objects rather than precious objects.

> A book that has never been dropped has never been carried.

## On Repair

A diagonal fault can be partially repaired. A bindery will re-attach the spine, reinforce the gutter with cambric, and straighten the textblock. The repair is never invisible; the diagonal line remains, usually as a slightly thickened ridge along the spine.

Some readers choose to leave the diagonal fault alone. The reasoning is the same as with the clean crack: the damage is part of the book's history, and repair erases the history. A book with a visible diagonal ridge is a book whose previous reader dropped it. The next reader inherits this fact, and &mdash; perhaps &mdash; is less likely to drop it themselves.

This is a weak educational argument, but it is a real one. The damaged book teaches the next reader to be careful. The repaired book lies about its condition.
