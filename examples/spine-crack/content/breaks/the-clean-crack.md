A book's cleanest spine crack is a single crisp line of separation along the gutter, at the page the reader has returned to most often. The book opens at that page without being asked. Set the book on its spine on a flat surface, and it will settle with that page on top, face-up, pages evenly splayed on either side.

## How It Happens

The clean crack is not the result of a single violent opening. It is the result of hundreds of openings to the same page. Each opening places a small amount of stress on the glue or thread at one particular point on the spine; over time, the glue fatigues at that point; eventually, the adhesive releases along a clean line, and the book can open flat at that page without further stress.

This is a best-case scenario for spine damage. The book is not falling apart. The pages remain bound. The textblock is intact. The only change is that the book now *remembers* where the reader most often opened it, and it favours that page.

## What The Clean Crack Tells Us

A book with a clean crack tells us three things:

1. **The reader returned repeatedly to a specific passage.** The book has a central argument, or at least a central resonance, and the reader found it.
2. **The reader did not underline the passage.** If they had, they would not need to return to the book to find it again; the underline would let them scan for it. The clean crack is a sign of a reader who reads rather than annotates.
3. **The book has been with this reader for a long time.** A single-reading book does not develop a clean crack. The damage accumulates over months or years.

The clean crack is, in this sense, the highest form of compliment a reader can pay a book. It is the physical evidence of a passage so compelling that the reader returned to it not once but dozens of times, and not by reference but by instinct.

> A passage worth the clean crack is a passage worth passing on.

## Against Repair

Clean cracks can be repaired. A bindery will re-glue the split, reinforce the gutter with a strip of cambric, and return the book in a condition approaching new. This is the wrong approach.

The clean crack is a feature of the book, not a defect. A repaired book has lost the memory of its reading. The next reader will not know which passage mattered; they will have to read the book from the beginning, as if it had never been read before. This is, arguably, how reading should be done. But it is also, we maintain, a kind of vandalism: the erasure of the previous reader's engagement.

Leave the spine cracked. The book knows something the new reader does not.
