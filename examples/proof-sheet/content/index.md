## What This Document Is

The following pages are the first proof of the forthcoming book, typeset in full and sent to the author for correction. They are not yet the book. The author is asked to read them carefully, to mark any errors in red, to suggest revisions in blue, and to return the proof to the compositor by Monday noon of the following week.

Each section carries one of three stamps. **APPROVED** means the section has been read and requires no change; the compositor may proceed to setting the second proof. **REVISE** means the section contains one or more errors the author has marked; the compositor will reset the affected lines before the second proof. **STET**, meaning "let it stand," is used where the editor has proposed a change the author rejects, restoring the original setting.

The proof is the first setting of the book, made before the plates are locked, the ink is mixed, or the pressman is called. It is a sheet made once, by hand, to show to the author what the book will look like when it is finished. The proof is not the book. It is, in the compositor's traditional phrase, a promise.

<span class="proof-spaced">The word proof, from the Latin probare, means to test. The proof is the test of the setting. It tests whether the letters are where they should be, whether the words have not been reversed, whether the hyphens fall on the proper lines, whether the widows and orphans have been shepherded, and whether the running head is, in fact, running.</span>

A proof is always wrong. This is part of its nature. A first proof is expected to have between ten and twenty errors per page. A second proof, corrected from the first, is expected to have between two and five. A third proof, corrected from the second, is expected to have zero. A book is considered ready for press when the third proof contains no errors and the author has signed the final page.

<span class="proof-break">Errors in the proof are of five kinds: errors of the compositor (wrong letter, wrong spacing, wrong line), errors of the editor (missed revision, unapproved change, imposed house style), errors of the author (second thoughts, late addenda, regretted cuts), errors of the printer (broken type, worn plate, inked roller), and errors of nobody, which are the errors that nobody can find and nobody remembers making.</span>

</div>

The compositor is the person who sets the type. In a letterpress shop, the compositor stands at a pair of sloped cases, called the upper and the lower, each divided into compartments, each compartment holding one kind of sort. The compositor selects the sorts one at a time, arranges them in a composing stick, and builds the line from right to left, reading backward. A finished stick holds about ten lines.

<span class="proof-spaced">When the stick is full, the compositor transfers the type to a galley, a long wooden tray. When the galley is full, the compositor takes a first impression of the galley by inking it and pressing a sheet of paper over it. This impression is called a <span class="proof-insert">galley</span> proof, and is the ancestor of the document you now hold.</span>

The rate of a skilled compositor was, in the nineteenth century, about 1500 letters per hour, or roughly a column of tightly set prose. A modern digital compositor sets the same column in, perhaps, two minutes, but the proof is no faster for being digital. The reading of the proof, the marking of the errors, the waiting for the author's return &mdash; these are the same now as they were then.

<span class="proof-break">A compositor who works on a proof submits, with the proof, a note called a compositor's query, which lists the places the compositor is uncertain of and asks the editor or the author for guidance. A good query is brief and specific. A bad query is either too broad (is this right?) or too defensive (I have done what the copy says, and I am not responsible for the copy). Our queries are, we hope, of the good kind.</span>

The editor reads the proof against the manuscript, the manuscript against the proof, and both against a third book called the style sheet, in which the house conventions are recorded. <span class="proof-delete">The editor then decides, alone and by fiat, which of the two to follow</span><span class="proof-stet">STET</span>. In practice the editor proposes, the author disposes, and the proof is the record of that negotiation.

<span class="proof-spaced">The editor's principal tools are the pencil, the eraser, the ruler, and the query slip. The pencil is for marking corrections; the eraser is for taking them back. The ruler is for measuring line length and for drawing margins around particularly bad passages. The query slip is a small sheet of coloured paper, usually blue, attached to the proof at the place of the query, on which the editor writes a short note to the author. The note should fit on the slip. If it does not, the editor has forgotten her craft.</span>

A good editor is invisible. The reader of a finished book does not know which words the editor suggested, which the author resisted, which prevailed. A bad editor is obvious; her voice crowds out the author's, and the book reads like two people arguing over a typewriter.
