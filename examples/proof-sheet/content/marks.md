These are the marks used throughout the proofs returned by this compositor. They are drawn from the British Standard for proof correction, with a few local habits layered in. Marks are always made in red ink in the text and repeated in the margin for emphasis.

## The Six Essentials

<div class="mark-grid">

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">&times;</p>
<h4>Delete</h4>
<p>A single stroke through the letter or word to be removed. The mark is repeated in the margin with the deletion loop (the small pigtail), so the compositor knows the mark is an instruction and not a stain.</p>
<p class="mark-example">The quick <span class="proof-delete">brown</span> fox</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">&#94;</p>
<h4>Insert</h4>
<p>A caret placed between the two characters where the new material should fall. The material itself is written in the margin, followed by a slash to show the end of the insertion.</p>
<p class="mark-example">The quick <span class="proof-insert">brown</span> fox</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">STET</p>
<h4>Let It Stand</h4>
<p>A row of dots placed beneath a deletion, with the word STET written in the margin, to cancel the deletion. The original text is to stand as it was.</p>
<p class="mark-example">The quick <span class="proof-delete">brown</span><span class="proof-stet">STET</span> fox</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">tr</p>
<h4>Transpose</h4>
<p>A curled line drawn around two adjacent characters or words, with the letters "tr" written in the margin. The compositor swaps the two items.</p>
<p class="mark-example">The fox quick brown <em>&rarr;</em> The quick brown fox</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">&para;</p>
<h4>New Paragraph</h4>
<p>A sharp right-angle drawn at the point where a new paragraph should begin, with the pilcrow repeated in the margin. Used when the author wants to break a long paragraph.</p>
<p class="mark-example">...and went home. &para; The next morning...</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">#</p>
<h4>Insert Space</h4>
<p>A hash mark placed where a space should be inserted, repeated in the margin. A forward slash through the existing space means "close up," the opposite of insert space.</p>
<p class="mark-example">the quick# brown fox</p>
</div>

</div>

## The Less Common Ones

<div class="mark-grid">

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">lc</p>
<h4>Lower Case</h4>
<p>A slash through a capital letter and the abbreviation "lc" in the margin. Used when the author or editor has changed their mind about a proper noun or a sentence beginning.</p>
<p class="mark-example">The QUICK fox &rarr; The quick fox</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">uc</p>
<h4>Upper Case</h4>
<p>Three short lines drawn beneath the letter to be capitalised, with "uc" in the margin. The three lines are sometimes called the flag.</p>
<p class="mark-example">the fox &rarr; The fox</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">ital</p>
<h4>Italic</h4>
<p>A single line drawn beneath the word, with "ital" in the margin. For a word already italic that should become roman, the mark is "rom" with a ring around the word.</p>
<p class="mark-example">The word fox <em>&rarr;</em> The word <em>fox</em></p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">bf</p>
<h4>Bold Face</h4>
<p>A wavy line drawn beneath the word, with "bf" in the margin. The wavy line is the compositor's signal to reach for the heavier weight of the same face.</p>
<p class="mark-example">The word fox &rarr; The word <strong>fox</strong></p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">wf</p>
<h4>Wrong Font</h4>
<p>A ring around the errant letter or word, with "wf" in the margin. The compositor has set the wrong face, weight, or size. A bad habit of nineteenth-century shops; rare now.</p>
<p class="mark-example">A circled letter in the midst of a clean line</p>
</div>

<div class="mark-card">
<p class="mark-symbol">eq#</p>
<h4>Equal Spaces</h4>
<p>A small division sign between two sets of inconsistent spaces, with "eq#" in the margin. The compositor is to make the spaces equal, usually in a run of short words.</p>
<p class="mark-example">the&nbsp;&nbsp;quick brown&nbsp;&nbsp;fox</p>
</div>

</div>

## How A Query Is Raised

When the compositor is not certain whether an error is an error, a query slip is attached to the proof. The slip is a small sheet of blue paper, about two inches by four, on which the compositor writes one of four standard forms:

<div class="proof-page">

<div class="proof-page-head">
<span class="proof-no">QUERY SLIP 007</span>
<span>SAMPLE FORMS</span>
<span>W &amp; CO.</span>
<span>15 V 26</span>
</div>

**Query one, on the matter of consistency.** "This word is set as *grey* on p. 12 and as *gray* on p. 34. Please advise which spelling to retain; the remainder of the manuscript uses both about equally." A good query. The compositor has looked; the author has not.

**Query two, on the matter of a missing reference.** "The footnote on p. 22 calls for a reference to Blackmore 1894, but the bibliography does not list Blackmore. Is the date correct; is the author correct; is the reference intended?" A good query, not a correction. The compositor asks; the author decides.

**Query three, on the matter of a suspected error.** "The sentence on p. 48, line 12, reads 'he *dose* not mean to say.' This is suspected to be *does* rather than *dose*. Please confirm." A good query, polite in tone. Compositors should not assume error; they should ask.

**Query four, the compositor's confession.** "We have set the chapter head on p. 56 in Baskerville rather than the specified Caslon, because our stock of Caslon ten-point was out. The setting will be revised for the second proof when the Caslon is restocked, on or before Thursday." The compositor apologises in advance.

</div>

## Margin Calls

<div class="proof-margin">
This is a specimen of a margin call. It reads to the right of the column and is addressed to the author or the editor.
</div>

Margin calls are brief. They should fit in the available margin, and they should refer to the line in question by its number. A margin call that runs over into a second line, or that wanders down the page, has failed. If there is more to say, it belongs on a query slip.

The margin is where the proof speaks back to itself. It is also where the reader speaks to the proof. Both voices are written in red. Neither voice is the final word. The final word is set in black on clean paper, two or three proofs later, when everyone has agreed to stop.

## A Note on Colours

Red is for corrections. Blue is for queries. Pencil is for marginalia and second thoughts; pencil is the safest mark because it can be erased. Black is for the editor's approvals, and black is also for the author's final signature at the end of the last proof, which is the signature that tells the compositor the book may now be cast in plates.

Green and purple are never used. Yellow is used only for highlighting passages that need a third reading; it is not a correction and it is not a query. A proof returned with yellow highlighting and no other marks is said to be "sunned," and the compositor treats it as approved.
