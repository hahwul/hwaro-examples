During an interception mission over the metropolitan airspace, two interceptor jet pilots reported a total loss of electronic control and communications system when coming within five nautical miles of a glowing spherical contact. The object was described as emitting brilliant, changing colors that fluctuated in intensity.

<!-- more -->

Radar operators on the ground tracked the object executing sharp ninety-degree turns that are structurally impossible for conventional aerodynamic frames.

As the lead interceptor pilot attempted to lock onto the spherical contact with short-range guided missiles, the cockpit avionics completely deactivated, forcing the pilot to abort the intercept. The second wingman reported that their communications suite suffered severe static interference, with radio logs showing broadband electromagnetic noise spikes across all communication frequencies.

Immediately following the aircraft's turn away from the contact, all cockpit systems returned to normal operational status. A comprehensive post-flight inspection of the aircraft's electrical wiring and avionics systems revealed no blown fuses or hardware degradation, suggesting that the electromagnetic interference was transient and externally generated. Similar electronics shutdowns have been logged by regional radar stations during related anomalous transits.
