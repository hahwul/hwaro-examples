During a routine training exercise off the eastern seaboard, the radar operators of the carrier strike group detected a highly anomalous radar return at an altitude of approximately eighty thousand feet. The contact was tracked descending to sea level in less than three seconds, representing a velocity that exceeds the structural limits of all known atmospheric aircraft.

<!-- more -->

A tactical flight group was dispatched to intercept the contact. Upon closing to visual range, observers reported a wingless, cylindrical craft approximately forty feet in length with no visible propulsion systems or thermal exhaust signatures.

The craft exhibited rapid, erratic lateral shifts that disrupted the weapon targeting systems of the intercepting aircraft. Infrared camera tracks show the object matching the movements of the intercepting fighters before accelerating out of sensor range.

Subsequent metallurgical and atmospheric checks in the wake of the contact revealed no fuel residue, ionized air clusters, or sonic disruption patterns that typically accompany supersonic flight. Review of carrier battle group sensor suites confirmed that the radar logs were consistent across three independent platforms, ruling out instrumentation calibration errors or atmospheric ducting anomalies.
