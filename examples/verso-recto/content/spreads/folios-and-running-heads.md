### Mirror Symmetry

The running head on the verso reads **left-to-right** with the folio at the right edge and the title at the left. ### Why

When a reader opens a spread, the outer edges are the thumbs' edges &mdash; the places most easily visible and most naturally grazed. The folio belongs there, on the outer edge of each page, because the folio is what the reader reaches for when navigating.

The section title belongs at the inner edge, near the gutter, because the title is what the reader consults once they have already opened to the correct page. The two roles are different, and the mirror layout gives each its correct place.

### The Discipline

- **Folio on the outer edge.** Always, on both verso and recto.
- **Title on the inner edge.** Always, in a smaller weight.

The **folio** is the page number. The **running head** is the small piece of text, usually at the top of every page, that identifies the publication, the section, or the article. Together, they are the navigation apparatus of the codex &mdash; the small text that tells the reader where they are, what they are reading, and how far they have to go.

## What The Folio Says

The folio says, simply, which page you are on. In a classical book, folio numbers are **Roman** in the front matter (i, ii, iii), **Arabic** in the body (1, 2, 3), and occasionally **Greek or lettered** in the back matter (A, B, C). This signalling is not merely decorative; it tells the reader the structural position of the page within the book.

Our publication uses Arabic numerals in the body and Roman in the front and back matter. The distinction is a signal: *you are in the core of the publication, or you are not.*

## What The Running Head Does

The running head does double duty. In classical typography:

- The **verso running head** carries the publication title
- The **recto running head** carries the article title, section, or chapter

This arrangement means that, at a glance, the reader can identify both the publication they are holding (on the verso) and the specific piece they are reading (on the recto). It is a compressed orientation, offered to the reader at the top of every spread.

> The running head is a small courtesy. It says: *I know you may have opened this book to any page. Here is where you are.*

## The Reverse Symmetry

On the verso, our running head places the folio at the outer (left) edge and the title at the inner edge, near the gutter. On the recto, the arrangement mirrors: the folio is at the outer (right) edge, and the title is at the inner edge. The result is a spread in which the folios bracket the text on both outer margins, and the titles converge on the gutter. This is the classical arrangement, and it is the correct one.
