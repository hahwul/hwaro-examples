### The Gutter Is Not A Wall

Most contemporary publication design treats the gutter as a *barrier* &mdash; a dead zone where no element may tread, lest it be lost to the perfect binding or mis-aligned at the sewing station. This caution produces spreads in which the two pages are effectively independent, joined only at the spine, each an island.

### We Propose

The gutter is *infrastructure*. It is the central axis that holds the spread together. Elements should **cross the gutter when the argument demands it** and respect it when it does not. The gutter is a spine, not a fence.

### In Practice

- Headlines that span both pages are set **across** the gutter, with the word break chosen for balance
- Rules and underlines that span both pages continue through the gutter, broken only by a 4mm allowance for binding
- Images that span both pages are composed so that the axis of the image falls **on** the gutter, not across it

The reader's eye will compensate for a 4mm binding gap. The reader's eye will not compensate for a spread that refuses to be a spread.

The gutter is the central fold of a bound codex &mdash; the place where the two halves of a spread meet, where the binding holds them, and where the casual reader rarely looks. In a perfect-bound book, the gutter narrows as it approaches the spine; pages stop opening flat; the text near the gutter becomes harder to read. This is a physical fact, not a design failure.

## The Convention

For these reasons, designers are taught from the first week of their training: keep your content out of the gutter. Allow a generous inner margin. Never set a photograph across the gutter unless you are willing to accept that its subject will be distorted at the spine. Never set a headline across the gutter unless the word break falls on a hyphenable syllable.

The effect of this training, over decades, is a publication design in which the spread is not a composed object but a pair of separately composed pages that happen to be bound together. The two pages face one another out of binding necessity, not editorial intent.

> A spread that could be ripped apart without loss is not a spread.

## The Exception That Teaches

We have found, in seven issues of this magazine, exactly one rule: **the gutter is infrastructure**. It is the axis of the spread, the seam that holds the pages together, the place where the composition is strongest. Respect it; do not avoid it.

The consequence is that our spreads are composed *with the gutter in mind*. Every spread is designed so that the eye, travelling across the gutter, encounters continuity rather than rupture. This requires more work of the designer and more care of the binder. It is, we maintain, the correct response to the physical fact of the codex.
