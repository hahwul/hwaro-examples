### The Recto Speaks In Sans

### Why Sans

Sans-serifs, since the nineteenth century, have been the voice of assertion. They are the voice of the signboard, the headline, the declaration. They do not qualify; they state. When a recto sits opposite a verso of classical italic, the sans speaks as the modern interlocutor: informed by the verso, responsive to it, but unwilling to leave the argument purely in the verso's tempo.

### The Conversation

- Verso proposes; recto responds
- Verso quotes; recto analyses
- Verso describes; recto argues
- Verso asks; recto answers &mdash; or asks its own question back

The magazine sets every spread in two display faces: **Cormorant Garamond Italic** on the verso, **Inter Bold** on the recto. The body text on both pages is **Source Serif 4**, which provides a shared foundation. The two display faces speak in different tempers; the body text unifies them.

## The Verso Speaks In Italic Serif

A classical italic serif is a particular voice. It is the voice of the scribal hand, the cursive italica that Aldus Manutius adapted for type in the 1490s. It is a voice that proposes, suggests, and implies. It is slower than an upright Roman; it asks the reader to slow down and listen.

We use Cormorant Garamond Italic, Christian Thalmann's revival of Garamond's 1540s Roman, reset in italic with exaggerated entry and exit strokes. The face has the formal authority of an old-style serif with the conversational warmth of italic cursive.

## The Argument For The Duet

A spread set entirely in one face is a monophony; both pages speak with the same voice, and the reader experiences the two pages as one continuous argument. This is fine for most of publishing, but it is not what we are trying to do.

A spread set in two faces &mdash; one on each page &mdash; is a duet. The verso and the recto are distinct voices, each with its own temper. The reader hears them as two speakers in a conversation, not one speaker in monologue. This is the editorial stance of the magazine, and every face on every page is chosen to sustain it.

> A monologue persuades by accumulation. A duet persuades by answer.

## The Body Voice

The shared body face is **Source Serif 4**, a text face designed for long-form reading. It is neither the classical italic of the verso nor the contemporary bold of the recto; it is a neutral voice that both can share. The running body text of each article is set in this face on both pages, even while the display type differs. This prevents the duet from becoming a quarrel.
