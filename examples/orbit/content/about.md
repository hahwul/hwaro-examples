Orbit started in a shared document called "sites we keep sending each other." Six of us — friends scattered across three time zones, all still running our own personal sites — had spent years pasting links into a group chat because none of us trusted a search engine to surface the small, strange, hand-built web we actually wanted to read. Eventually someone asked why the document couldn't just be the search engine. That document is now the index desk.

## What we refuse to do

We do not run a crawler. We do not train or query a language model anywhere in the indexing pipeline. We do not accept a submission that a curator has not personally opened in a browser. This is slower than the alternative on purpose — a crawler can index a million pages a night; our desk indexes somewhere between forty and ninety, because forty to ninety is what a person can actually read and judge in a day's work.

## Who is on the desk

Forty-two curators work Orbit part-time, each assigned a handful of categories they already know well. Priya reviews wikis and notebooks because she has run one since 2011. Tobias handles webrings because he moderates two of the oldest surviving ones. Marguerite reads nothing but personal sites and journals, on the theory that you can tell within two paragraphs whether a person or a template is writing. Dae-jung covers tiny tools and utilities and rejects anything that needs an account before it does the one thing it promised. Fen curates guestbooks and forums, chases down zines and web ephemera, and keeps a running list of curiosities and oddities too strange to categorize any other way. Whoever is newest to the desk usually starts on learning and how-to guides, because judging whether an explanation actually works is good training for judging everything else. Nobody reviews a category they don't already read for pleasure — we found early on that indifferent curation looks a lot like automated curation, just slower.

## What "indexed" means here

A listing on Orbit means a specific person read the page, confirmed a human wrote it, checked that it still resolves, and filed it under a category by hand. We recheck every listing at least once a year and remove anything that has gone dark, been sold to a content farm, or started reading like it was written by something other than its owner.

If that sounds like a strange way to build a search engine in this decade, read [How We Index](/how-we-index/) — the process is stranger, and slower, than it sounds.
