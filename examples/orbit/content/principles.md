These are load-bearing. We revisit the wording sometimes; we have never revisited the substance.

## 1. A person reads every page

No exceptions for volume, no "spot-checking" a batch. If nobody on the desk has personally opened a page, it does not go in the index — no matter who submitted it or how it was found.

## 2. No crawler, ever

Orbit has never run automated fetching against the open web and never will. Curators browse the way anyone browses: clicking links, following webrings, reading a site's own "sites I like" page.

## 3. No model in the pipeline

Nothing in submission, review, description-writing, or ranking touches a language model. Descriptions are short because a curator wrote them in a spare ten minutes, not because a model was told to be concise.

## 4. One category, chosen by a human

We resist the instinct to tag a page into six categories to raise its odds of being found. A page belongs somewhere specific, and browsing should mean something.

## 5. Removal is free and fast

Any site owner can ask to be delisted, no reason required, and it happens within a week. Owning your page's absence is as easy as owning its presence.

## 6. Small is a feature

We have talked ourselves out of "scaling the desk" with automation every time it's come up. Sixty listings a week, by hand, is the pace that keeps the index honest — a faster index would be a different, worse product.

## 7. The index is never for sale

No listing is paid placement, no category is sponsored, and no curator ranks a page higher because of who submitted it. Funding comes from readers and site owners who chip in — see [Donate](/donate/) — not from anyone buying position.

Read these again if Orbit ever starts to feel like it's cutting corners. If it has, tell us — hello@orbit.example reaches the whole desk, not a support queue.
