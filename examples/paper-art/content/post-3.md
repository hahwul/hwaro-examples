A torn edge is a record of force. Where a blade leaves a clean, anonymous boundary, a tear preserves the direction of the pull, the resistance of the fibers, and the moment the sheet gave way. Each tear is unrepeatable, which is why paper artists treat it as a structural decision rather than a finishing touch.

The mechanics are straightforward. Long-fibered papers, such as kozo or cotton-based stocks, separate gradually and produce a soft, feathered ridge. Short-fibered papers like newsprint break abruptly and leave a sharper, more brittle line. Wetting the paper along the intended tear line softens the bond between fibers and lets the artist guide the rupture with greater control. Dry tearing against a metal ruler produces a tighter edge, useful when the torn line needs to read as a boundary rather than a gesture.

In a layered composition, torn edges interact with light differently than cut edges. The exposed fiber catches incidental illumination and casts a diffuse, low-contrast shadow that softens the transition between planes. This is particularly effective at the bottom of a stack, where a clean edge would read as an artificial seam. By contrast, the top layer often benefits from a deliberate cut, providing a crisp anchor against which the lower torn edges feel organic.

The choice of which edges to tear and which to cut becomes a structural language. A piece composed entirely of torn edges can feel weathered or improvisational. A piece composed entirely of cut edges can feel diagrammatic. Mixing them allows the artist to direct attention, separating the considered passages from the moments where the material itself is allowed to speak.
