There is a question that sounds naive until you sit with it: *why red, blue, and yellow?* Of all the colours available to a painter — the bruised violets, the sea-greens, the thousand greys of a Dutch sky — why did the artists of De Stijl strip their palette down to three, and always the same three?

The honest answer is that the choice was never really about colour. It was about **politics**, in the largest sense of the word. The primaries were meant to carry a claim about how everyone, everywhere, should be able to see.

## The Argument from Irreducibility

A primary colour, in the simplest pigment theory the De Stijl painters worked from, is one that cannot be mixed from others. Red, blue, and yellow are the floor of the colour world. Everything else — orange, green, purple, the entire muddy middle — is a *combination*, a compromise, a derivative.

For Mondrian and Theo van Doesburg, that irreducibility was the entire point. A secondary colour is contingent: it depends on a recipe, a proportion, a taste. A primary colour just *is*. It is the same red for a Dutch theorist and a peasant in Java; it does not require education to perceive or culture to decode.

> "The new spirit must be manifested in all the arts without exception. The primary colours are the means by which painting becomes the property of all, and not the privilege of a refined few."

This is the utopian claim hiding inside the squares. By reducing the palette to the irreducible, De Stijl believed it was building a **universal language** — a visual Esperanto, owned by no nation and no class, legible to anyone with eyes.

## Against Taste

To understand why this felt urgent, you have to remember what the primaries were defined *against*.

Nineteenth-century bourgeois interiors were a riot of refinement: dusty rose, eau de nil, ochre, mauve, gold leaf. Colour was a marker of money and education. Knowing that this particular green was *fashionable* this season and that one was vulgar — that was a form of social capital. Taste was a wall.

De Stijl wanted to knock the wall down. Red, blue, and yellow are not *tasteful*. They are loud, flat, and frank. You cannot be more sophisticated than someone else about pure red; there is nothing to be sophisticated *about*. By choosing the primaries, the movement was refusing the entire economy of taste. It was a deliberately classless palette — colour that could not be used to humiliate.

There was an industrial logic here too. Consider how the choice scales:

1. **Primaries are reproducible.** A factory can mix the same red a million times.
2. **Primaries are nameable.** "Red," "blue," "yellow" need no catalogue of fashionable shades.
3. **Primaries are honest about ink.** A printing press lays down flat, separable colours — exactly what De Stijl already wanted to paint.

The same palette that promised a universal art also happened to suit the machine age that the movement embraced. A poster, a building, a chair, and a canvas could all speak the same three-colour language.

## The Black That Holds It Together

It is worth noticing what the primaries *cannot* do alone. Three loud colours, placed side by side, simply fight. What makes a Mondrian sing rather than scream is the **black line** — the heavy rule that separates one plane from another, gives each colour an edge, and assigns it a place.

In the political reading, the black line is the law: the structure within which the free colours coexist without conflict. Remove it and you get chaos; keep it rigid and you get equilibrium. The composition becomes a tiny model of a well-ordered society — every element bounded, balanced, and at peace with its neighbours.

That is a lot to ask of three pigments and a few black bands. And we should be candid: the dream did not arrive. The universal classless visual language did not dissolve hierarchy; it became, in time, its own kind of refined taste — the very thing it set out to abolish. Today a primary-coloured grid reads as *sophisticated*, which is exactly the opposite of what it was for.

## What Survives the Argument

So the politics failed, in the way most artistic politics fail — by being absorbed into the culture it meant to overturn. But strip away the manifesto and something stubborn remains.

Stand in front of those three colours, bounded by black, and you still feel the original wager: that the simplest, least negotiable elements might be the most democratic; that an art owned by everyone would have to be made of things no one can claim to understand better than anyone else. Red. Blue. Yellow. No recipe, no secret, no taste required.

It was, in the end, a generous idea. It just turned out to be a painting.
