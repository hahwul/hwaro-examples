Piet Mondrian did not begin as the painter of grids. He began with trees. In the early 1910s he painted apple trees over and over, and you can watch, canvas by canvas, as the branches lose their bark and their leaves and finally their curvature. The tree dissolves into a lattice of short, nervous lines. By the end there is no tree at all — only a structure that the tree had been *hiding*.

That dissolution is the whole of Neoplasticism in miniature. Mondrian was not trying to draw the world. He was trying to subtract the world until only its underlying order remained.

## The Theory of Pure Plastic Art

Mondrian called his method **Nieuwe Beelding** — "New Plasticity," usually rendered in English as *Neoplasticism*. The word "plastic" here has nothing to do with the material; it comes from the Greek *plastikos*, "to shape." Plastic art is art that *forms*, that gives shape to relationship rather than to appearance.

His claim was radical and almost theological. He believed that nature shows us the universal dressed up in particulars — this tree, that sky, this fading light — and that the particulars are a kind of noise. Behind them lies a pure structure of opposing forces: vertical against horizontal, expansion against limitation, matter against spirit. The job of the modern artist was to paint *that*, and nothing else.

To do it, he allowed himself a strict vocabulary:

- The **straight line**, only horizontal and vertical
- The **right angle**, where those two directions meet
- The three **primary colours** — red, blue, yellow
- The three **non-colours** — white, black, grey

That was the entire palette of the universe, as far as Mondrian was concerned. Everything else was a compromise.

## Why the Right Angle

The right angle was not an aesthetic preference. It was a metaphysical commitment.

Mondrian saw the horizontal and the vertical as the two fundamental opposites of existence — and the act of holding them in tension, at exactly ninety degrees, as the visual form of *equilibrium*. A curve resolves itself; it flows and relaxes. A diagonal is restless, dynamic, always falling toward one side. But the right angle holds. Two forces meet and neither wins. The result is balance without stillness, repose that is alive.

> "I construct lines and colour combinations on a flat surface, in order to express general beauty with the utmost awareness. Nature (or, that which I see) inspires me... but I want to come as close as possible to the truth."

This is why his mature paintings are so hard to paraphrase. They are not *of* anything. A canvas like *Composition with Red, Blue and Yellow* (1930) is a field of white crossed by a few black lines, with one red rectangle weighted against a small blue square and a sliver of yellow. Move any line a centimetre and the whole thing collapses. The painting is a held breath.

## The Discipline of Subtraction

What makes Neoplasticism difficult — for the painter, and for us — is that it is an art of taking away. Most art adds: more detail, more story, more feeling. Mondrian's adds nothing. He spent weeks adjusting the position of a single black band, papering over and repainting, hunting for the one arrangement that felt *resolved*.

You can think of the method almost like writing code that refuses every convenience:

```
canvas      = white
allowed     = [horizontal, vertical]
colours     = [red, blue, yellow, black, white, grey]
forbidden   = [curve, diagonal, shading, depth, symbolism]

while not balanced(canvas):
    adjust(line_position)
    adjust(plane_weight)
# stop only when no element can move without loss
```

There is no shortcut in that loop. *Balance* is not a formula; it is a judgement, made by eye, again and again, until the composition can no longer be improved.

## The Promise Behind the Squares

It is tempting to see Mondrian as a formalist — a man obsessed with arrangement for its own sake. He was not. He believed, with real conviction, that a society which learned to see pure relationship in art would eventually learn to *live* in equilibrium too. The grid was a rehearsal for a better world.

That utopian hope did not come true, and we are right to be sceptical of it. But the paintings outlived the theory. We no longer need to accept Mondrian's metaphysics to feel what his canvases do: the click of a composition falling into place, the strange authority of a single red plane against a white field, the sense that *something has been told the truth*.

He reduced the world to right angles. And against all odds, the world looks back from them.
