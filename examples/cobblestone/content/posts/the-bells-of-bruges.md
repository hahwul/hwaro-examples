The Belfry of Bruges rises eighty-three meters above the Markt square, a tower of dark brick that has watched over the city since the thirteenth century. Inside its upper chambers hangs a carillon of forty-seven bells, ranging from a small treble weighing a few kilograms to a great bourdon of nearly five tonnes. Together, these bells form one of the finest musical instruments in the Low Countries, and their sound has been woven into the daily life of Bruges for more than five hundred years.

A carillon is not merely a set of bells that ring on the hour. It is a keyboard instrument, played by a carillonneur who strikes wooden batons with loosely clenched fists and presses pedals with the feet, translating physical effort into cascading melody high above the rooftops. The tradition of carillon playing in Flanders dates to the early sixteenth century, and Bruges has maintained a city carillonneur without interruption since that era. The current musician gives public concerts on Wednesday and Saturday evenings during summer, and visitors who climb the three hundred sixty-six steps of the tower can watch the performance from a small gallery beside the playing cabin.

The repertoire of the Bruges carillon spans everything from Renaissance dance tunes and Baroque fugues to arrangements of folk songs and contemporary compositions written specifically for the instrument. The sound that reaches the streets below is shaped by the architecture of the city itself: the narrow canal-side houses, the slate roofs, and the open squares all reflect and diffuse the bell tones in ways that no concert hall could replicate. On a still evening, the music can be heard clearly from the Begijnhof, a full kilometer to the south.

Beyond their musical role, the bells of Bruges have always served a civic function. They announced market openings, curfews, fires, and the arrival of dignitaries. The largest bell, called Mary, still tolls for solemn occasions. In a city where tourism often dominates the narrative, the carillon remains a living institution that belongs to the residents first and foremost, a voice that has spoken to this community across generations and that continues to mark the rhythm of life in one of Europe's most beautifully preserved medieval towns.
