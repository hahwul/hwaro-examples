The Bauhaus did not invent the grid, but it was the first school to teach the grid as the *organizing principle* of design rather than a layout convenience. A first-year student at Dessau spent more time understanding the grid than they did rendering type.

## Three Lessons That Translated

1. **The grid precedes the content.** Composition begins with structure, not with subject. A poster is solved on the underlying lattice before any imagery enters.
2. **Asymmetry within order.** A grid does not require centered, mirrored layouts. The Bauhaus celebrated dynamic compositions that respected the grid while breaking the user's expectation of balance.
3. **The grid is invisible.** A finished work hides its grid. Students who left the construction lines visible were correcting in the wrong direction — the grid is scaffolding, not ornament.

## Why It Stuck

Modern design tools — Figma, CSS, Tailwind — all rest on the same assumption: design starts with a grid, content lives in cells, and decisions about hierarchy are decisions about grid spans. Walter Gropius's curriculum is, structurally, what most modern interfaces are still built on.
