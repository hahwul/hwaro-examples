When Herbert Bayer drafted the Universal type in 1925, he removed every serif and every uppercase letter. The motivation was clarity — a single-case, geometric letterform was, the school argued, easier to read at speed than the inherited mix of capital and lowercase forms.

## What Was Gained

- **Visual neutrality.** Sans-serif type sits more quietly on the page; the eye reads structure rather than ornament.
- **Scalability.** Geometric letters retain their identity at very small and very large sizes. Serifs distort on both ends.
- **Modernity by association.** The new type was inseparable from the new architecture, the new furniture, the new grid. It signaled membership in a movement.

## What Was Lost

Serifs do work. They guide the eye horizontally along a line, which is part of why long-form reading still happens in serif faces. The Bauhaus traded long-form readability for short-form clarity — a fair trade for posters and signage, less obvious for a 400-page book.

The lesson is not that sans-serif is better. The lesson is that typography is a tool with a use case, and the Bauhaus made the use case explicit.
