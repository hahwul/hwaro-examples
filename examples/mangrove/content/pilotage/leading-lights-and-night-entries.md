Night pilotage uses the same skills as daylight pilotage — bearings, transits, tidal allowance — worked against light characteristics instead of landmarks. The method is the same shape; the margin for error is smaller, because a light misidentified in the dark rarely announces its mistake the way a daylight landmark usually does.

<!-- more -->

## Reading a light's characteristic

The chart abbreviation for a light describes exactly how it flashes, and the almanac lists the same information with the period spelled out. `Fl(2) 10s` means two flashes in a repeating ten-second cycle; `Oc 4s` means occulting — on longer than off — on a four-second cycle; `Iso 2s` means equal periods of light and dark. Time a light's cycle with a watch before deciding what it is; a light two cables further along the coast with a similar-sounding characteristic has put more than one boat in the wrong channel.

| Light | Characteristic | Sector | Range |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sable Head | Fl(2) 10s | white 004°–187° | 15 nm |
| Halyard Sound, front leading light | Oc 4s | white, narrow | 8 nm |
| Halyard Sound, rear leading light | Iso 2s | white, narrow | 8 nm |
| Egret Bar buoy | Q Fl | — | 4 nm |

## Sector lights

A sector light shows different colours, usually white, red, and green, across different arcs — white for the safe approach, red and green marking the edges of a hazard on either side. Hold the white sector and you are in the safe water it was built to mark; drift into red or green and you have your answer immediately, without needing to identify anything else. Confirm the sector boundaries against the chart before the approach, since the colour alone tells you which side you have drifted to, not by how much.

## What to check before dark

Do the identification work in daylight if the approach allows it: run the same track by day, note where the leading-light structures actually stand against the daylight landmarks, and time how long the approach takes at your boat's speed. A night entry is a poor place to discover that a chart symbol you read confidently at the table looks nothing like what is actually visible from the cockpit once the sun is down and every other light on the shore is competing for attention.

The daylight dry run doubles as practice for [taking a three-point fix](/pilotage/taking-a-three-point-fix/) against the same structures you will be reading as lights after dark.
