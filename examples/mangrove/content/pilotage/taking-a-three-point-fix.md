A three-point fix takes bearings on three charted objects, plots the reciprocal of each as a line on the chart, and calls the position where the three lines meet — or nearly meet — your fix. It is slower than a transit and needs a working hand-bearing compass, but it works anywhere you can identify three objects, not only where a lucky transit exists.

<!-- more -->

## Choosing the three objects

Spread them around the compass rose. Three objects bunched within thirty degrees of each other give lines that cross at a shallow, poorly defined angle, and a small bearing error swings the fix a long way. Objects spread closer to sixty degrees apart, ideally with one roughly abeam, give lines that cross close to square and shrink the effect of any one bearing being slightly off.

## Taking and plotting

Take all three bearings as close together in time as the boat's motion allows — a fix is a snapshot, and a slow boat covers real distance between the first and third bearing on a rough day.

```text
1140  St Osric spire        047°T
1140  Tern Ledge beacon      132°T
1141  Sable Head light       241°T
      cocked hat: 0.4 nautical mile at chart scale — accepted, fix plotted 1140
```

Convert each bearing to its reciprocal (add or subtract 180°), lay a parallel rule from each object along its reciprocal bearing, and draw the line across the chart. Three bearings from three real objects almost never meet at one exact point — the small triangle where the lines cross is the cocked hat, and its size is an honest picture of how much error is in your fix.

## Reading the cocked hat

A cocked hat you could cover with a fingertip at chart scale means your bearings were good; plot the fix at its centre and move on. A cocked hat sprawling across a quarter mile of chart means something was wrong with one bearing — a misidentified object, a compass read in a rolling sea, a beacon confused with another beacon further along the coast. Retake the bearings rather than trust a fix with a cocked hat that size, particularly anywhere near a hazard the fix is meant to be clearing.

Plot the fix at the corner of the cocked hat nearest the hazard, never at its centre, when you cannot retake the bearings and the situation does not allow the luxury of doubt.

Where a suitable transit exists, prefer [clearing lines and transits](/pilotage/clearing-lines-and-transits/) for the final approach — a three-point fix confirms where you were a minute ago; a transit tells you where you are right now.
