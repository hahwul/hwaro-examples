A transit is two charted objects that happen to line up when you view them from a particular spot — a chimney behind a church tower, a beacon behind a headland. The moment they separate, you know you have moved off that exact spot, and which way. No compass, no plotting, no arithmetic: just two marks, kept in line, telling you the truth faster than any instrument on the boat.

## Clearing lines

A clearing line is a transit chosen deliberately to mark the edge of safe water rather than a single position. Pick two charted objects that, kept in line, pass just clear of a hazard — a bar, a reef, a patch of drying rock. As long as the nearer object stays on the safe side of the far one, you are on the safe side of the hazard. Cross to the wrong side and the marks swap order. It is binary in a way a depth sounder in murky water is not.

The best clearing lines are permanent and unambiguous: a lighthouse and a hill, not a moored yacht and a buoy that might not be there next season. Admiralty charts often print recommended clearing marks for exactly this reason — use them before inventing your own.

## Using a transit to check deviation

Any transit you can identify precisely doubles as a deviation check. Steer through it, note the compass course at the instant the marks align, and compare that to the transit's charted true bearing corrected for variation. The difference is your deviation on that heading — no swinging the compass required, just five seconds of attention at the right moment.

```text
0912  Sable Head monument behind chimney   — steady, no correction
0916  chimney opens right of monument ~1°  — set to port, correct 5° to starboard
0921  marks recombine                      — back on the transit, hold this heading
```

## Where it stops working

Transits fail the moment either object is obscured — fog, a passing vessel, low sun straight into the object. They also fail if you misidentify the object; a similar-looking headland a mile further up the coast will give you a confident, wrong answer. Confirm identification against the chart before trusting a transit you have not used before, and never let a transit substitute for a fix when visibility is already marginal — by the time you need the transit most is exactly when it is least likely to be visible.

When no transit is available, or the light has gone, fall back to [taking a three-point fix](/pilotage/taking-a-three-point-fix/) instead.
