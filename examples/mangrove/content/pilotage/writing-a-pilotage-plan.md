A pilotage plan is written before you leave, not worked out live at the helm with a hazard closing on the bow. It converts everything the previous chapters cover — bearings, transits, tidal streams — into a single page you can glance at while actually running the boat, rather than a chart you would need both hands and a calm sea to read properly.

<!-- more -->

## What belongs on the page

Keep it to what you would actually reference under way: waypoints with a course and distance between them, at least one clearing bearing or transit per hazard, any area that is a hard no-go regardless of state of tide, and a bail-out track for the specific failure you are most likely to have — engine, wind direction, or visibility.

```text
PILOTAGE PLAN — Halyard Sound entrance, ebb tide
Waypoint 1   51°21.4'N 004°26.8'W   fairway buoy, course 034°T to WP2
Waypoint 2   51°22.6'N 004°25.1'W   turn onto Tern Ledge transit
Clearing bearing   Sable Head light must bear less than 210°T
No-go              Egret Bar, drying 0.4 m — stay clear below half-tide
Bail-out            if visibility drops, return 214°T to the fairway buoy
```

## Working backwards from the hazard

Start with the hazard, not the harbour entrance. Identify what could go wrong on this specific approach — a bar that dries, a headland with a foul tide, a channel that narrows against a lee shore — and build the plan's clearing bearings and no-go areas around that hazard first. The waypoints and course lines come after, fitted around the safety marks rather than the other way round.

## The bail-out is not optional

Every plan needs an answer to "what do we do if this goes wrong right now," worked out calmly beforehand rather than improvised with a hazard already close. For a narrow entrance that answer is usually a course back out the way you came; for an open approach it might be standing off and waiting for better light or a better state of tide. Write it down. A bail-out decided under way is nearly always worse than one decided at the chart table the evening before, however sound your judgement under pressure.

## Keep it legible in the cockpit

A pilotage plan that needs two hands and good light to read is a plan you will not use exactly when you need it. Write large, keep it to the one page, and laminate or bag it — spray on the page is not a hypothetical for this kind of approach.

Draw the clearing bearings straight from [clearing lines and transits](/pilotage/clearing-lines-and-transits/), and check the plan's timing against [reading a tidal stream atlas](/pilotage/reading-a-tidal-stream-atlas/) before you commit to a waypoint's course and distance.
