Mangrove is organized the way a skipper actually learns pilotage: not chart to chart, but skill to skill. The sidebar groups every chapter under the technique it teaches, so a chapter on transits sits beside the other bearing-and-fix methods rather than wherever it happened to fall in a table of contents.

Four skills carry the whole handbook. **Bearings and Fixes** covers the eye-and-compass work that needs no instrument beyond a hand-bearing compass and a pencil. **Tides and Streams** covers reading a stream atlas and carrying a standard port's tide table to a secondary one. **Passage Planning** covers turning all of that into a single page you can read at the helm in a rising sea. **Night Pilotage** covers the same ground after the light characteristics take over from the landmarks.

Every chapter follows the same shape: a method, a worked example set out the way you would actually write it in a logbook, and the situation where the method quietly stops being safe to use. That last part is not an afterthought — knowing where a technique's assumptions break is most of what separates a plan from a habit.

This edition folds in three seasons of corrections from skippers working the Vane Estuary and the water east of Sable Head. Where a method has a well-known failure mode, it says so in the text rather than leaving it for a note in the margin.
