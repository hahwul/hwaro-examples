Blue ice forms when air bubbles trapped in firn are progressively expelled under sustained pressure. The result is a dense, transparent solid with very few internal scattering centers. When sunlight enters, the long path length absorbs longer wavelengths first — red, then orange, then yellow — leaving the transmitted light skewed toward the blue end of the visible spectrum.

## Density and Bubble Loss

Newly fallen snow has a density near 100 kilograms per cubic meter. Firn at depth reaches 830, the pore close-off threshold. Glacial ice settles around 917, only a few percent below the maximum density of pure ice. In that final compression stage, residual bubbles either dissolve into clathrate hydrates or are flattened into thin lenses aligned with the flow direction. Both transformations reduce light scattering and intensify the blue cast.

## Where It Surfaces

The most striking exposures occur in ablation zones near the glacier terminus, where ice that originated hundreds of meters below the surface is exhumed by sublimation and wind erosion. The Antarctic blue ice areas around the Allan Hills and the Patriot Hills are the best-known examples; they also concentrate meteorites lifted to the surface by the same upward flow.

## Practical Notes

Photographers seeking the color find it easiest in overcast conditions. Direct sunlight introduces specular reflection from the surface, which dilutes the saturated transmitted color. The deepest blues appear in shaded crevasse walls, where the only light reaching the camera has traveled meters through the ice itself.
