A crevasse is a brittle response to stress in a material that otherwise deforms by viscous creep. Glacial ice behaves plastically below roughly the upper 30 meters, where overburden pressure exceeds the tensile strength. Above that depth — the so-called brittle layer — accumulated strain releases as fracture rather than flow.

## Stress Fields

Three stress regimes generate distinct crevasse patterns. Transverse crevasses open perpendicular to the flow direction where the bed steepens and the ice accelerates, putting the surface under longitudinal tension. Longitudinal crevasses appear where the channel widens, allowing lateral spreading. Splaying crevasses fan out near the terminus, combining both modes as the glacier loses confinement.

## Depth Limit

The Nye formula gives a first-order estimate of crevasse depth based on tensile strain rate and ice density. Field measurements in temperate glaciers typically find depths of 20 to 30 meters, capped by the transition into the ductile regime. Cold polar glaciers can fracture deeper because the brittle layer extends further down. Surface meltwater complicates the picture: a water-filled crevasse can propagate to the bed by hydrofracture, regardless of the dry-ice depth limit.

## Mapping Practice

Surveyors classify crevasses by orientation, width, and snow bridge condition. Ground-penetrating radar resolves hidden cracks beneath wind-deposited snow that would otherwise look continuous. Aerial photography taken at low sun angle reveals the lattice of open and bridged fractures across an entire flow unit, providing the route data that field parties rely on for safe traverses.
