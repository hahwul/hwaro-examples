## Overview

The peacock mantis shrimp is not a shrimp at all but a stomatopod, a distinct order of crustaceans that diverged from other malacostracan lineages over 400 million years ago. It is among the most visually striking inhabitants of Indo-Pacific coral reefs, with a body patterned in vivid green, blue, and red.

But the peacock mantis shrimp's most remarkable features are not cosmetic. It possesses the fastest predatory strike ever recorded in the animal kingdom and a visual system of unparalleled complexity.

## The Strike

The raptorial appendages of *Odontodactylus scyllarus* are spring-loaded clubs that accelerate faster than a .22 caliber bullet, reaching speeds of 23 meters per second. The strike generates localized cavitation bubbles that collapse with enough force to produce light, heat exceeding the surface temperature of the sun for a fraction of a nanosecond, and a secondary shockwave that can stun or kill prey even if the initial blow misses.

This mechanism allows the mantis shrimp to crack the shells of gastropods, bivalves, and crabs that would be impervious to most predators. In captivity, large individuals have shattered aquarium glass, earning them a cautious respect among marine biologists and aquarists.

The clubs themselves are composed of a hydroxyapatite composite arranged in a helicoidal structure that resists catastrophic fracture. Materials scientists have studied this architecture as a model for developing impact-resistant materials.

## Vision

The compound eyes of the mantis shrimp contain sixteen types of photoreceptor, compared to three in humans. Twelve of these are dedicated to color perception, spanning a range from deep ultraviolet to far red. The remaining four are specialized for detecting different planes and orientations of polarized light.

Each eye can move independently on a mobile stalk, and each is capable of trinocular vision, meaning a single eye can gauge depth without input from the other. The result is a visual system that perceives the world in dimensions inaccessible to nearly all other animals.

Paradoxically, behavioral experiments suggest that mantis shrimp are poor at discriminating between similar wavelengths. Their visual system may function less like a high-resolution camera and more like a rapid classification system, recognizing broad categories of color without fine-grained comparison.

## Behavior

Peacock mantis shrimp are solitary and territorial. They excavate burrows in coral rubble and defend them aggressively against intruders, including conspecifics. Ritualized threat displays, involving the spread of colorful maxillipeds, often precede physical combat.

When disputes escalate, combatants strike each other's telson, a reinforced tail plate that functions as a shield. These contests are rarely fatal. The loser retreats, and the winner retains the burrow.

Mantis shrimp can remember and recognize individual neighbors, maintaining stable territorial boundaries over weeks or months. They adjust their aggression based on the outcome of previous encounters, a form of social memory that implies a cognitive sophistication unusual for crustaceans.
