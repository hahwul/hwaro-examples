A lava flow is a moving solution of silicate melt, dissolved volatiles, and suspended crystals. Its behavior on the surface depends almost entirely on viscosity, and viscosity depends on temperature and silica content. Hotter, lower-silica basalt flows like the ones at Kilauea move freely. Cooler, higher-silica andesite flows pile up into thick, blocky lobes that barely move at all.

When a basaltic flow descends a steep slope, it forms pahoehoe: a smooth, ropy surface caused by a thin elastic crust folding over the moving interior. As the flow loses heat and gas, the crust thickens and tears. The same magma transitions to aa, a rough, clinkery surface that grinds forward like a slow tractor tread.

Topography then takes over. Channels form when crust solidifies along the margins of a flow but the center keeps moving. If the channel roof closes, a lava tube develops. Tubes are efficient insulators. Magma can travel kilometers underground at near-eruption temperature, then emerge through a skylight and continue downhill as if it had just left the vent.

Field observation focuses on three measurements: surface temperature using infrared cameras, advance rate at the flow front, and SO2 flux at the source. Together these constrain effusion rate, which is the volume of magma leaving the vent per second. Effusion rate predicts how far a flow will travel before it stalls.

The terrain itself records previous flows. Older lobes act as levees, channeling new material along familiar paths. Mapping these layers gives a record of decades or centuries of activity at a single vent.
