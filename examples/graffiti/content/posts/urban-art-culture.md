# The Unwritten Rules of Urban Art Culture

Street art operates outside the law, but it is not without rules. The graffiti community has developed its own codes of conduct over decades -- unwritten laws that govern everything from where you paint to how you earn respect.

## Respect the Hierarchy

In most graffiti communities, respect is earned through consistent work over time. A new writer does not walk up to a prime wall and throw up a piece. You start small -- tagging, then throw-ups, then simple pieces. You prove your dedication before claiming prominent spots.

Going over (painting over) another writer's work is one of the most serious offenses. The general rule: you only go over someone if your work is clearly better, and even then, it is considered disrespectful to go over a fresh piece. Old, faded work is more acceptable to cover.

## Crews and Alliances

A crew is a collective of writers who paint together, share supplies, and look out for each other. Crew names are abbreviated -- three or four letters -- and appear alongside individual tags. Being accepted into a respected crew is a significant milestone.

Crew dynamics mirror those of any tight-knit community. There are rivalries, alliances, internal politics, and occasionally, splits. But the crew system also provides mentorship, with experienced writers teaching newer members technique, style, and the culture's codes.

## The Role of the Blackbook

A blackbook (sketch notebook) is a writer's most personal possession. It contains drafts of pieces, experimental lettering, and often, guest sketches from other respected writers. Asking someone to sketch in your blackbook is a sign of respect. Being asked to sketch in someone else's is an honor.

Many writers maintain blackbooks for decades, creating an evolving archive of their stylistic development.

## Permission Walls and Legal Spaces

The rise of legal walls and permission spaces has created a parallel track within graffiti culture. Some purists dismiss legal work as inauthentic. Others see it as an opportunity to develop skills and create more elaborate pieces without the time pressure and legal risk of illegal painting.

Most active writers operate in both worlds -- legal walls for practice and portfolio building, illegal spots for the cultural credibility that still forms the backbone of graffiti respect.

## The Code of Silence

Despite the rise of social media, a strong code of discretion persists. You do not reveal another writer's real identity. You do not share information about spots, techniques, or security patterns with outsiders. Trust is the currency of the community, and breaking it has real consequences.

## Evolution, Not Revolution

Street art culture has changed enormously since its origins. Instagram has made it possible to build a following without ever touching a wall. Commissioned murals blur the line between street art and commercial work. The NFT boom briefly pulled some artists into the digital space.

Through all these changes, the core values persist: originality, dedication, respect for the craft, and the belief that the best gallery in the world is the one with no walls at all.
