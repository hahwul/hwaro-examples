Flat design taught us clarity. But depth — real, considered depth — teaches us hierarchy. The best interfaces guide you through layers of information as naturally as walking through rooms of a gallery.

## The Return of Dimension

After years of aggressively flat aesthetics, designers are rediscovering the power of subtle depth. Not the heavy skeuomorphism of the past, but a refined layering that uses shadow, transparency, and gradient to create spatial relationships on screen.

## Tools of Depth

**Gradients** — A well-crafted gradient can suggest light source, create warmth, and add richness that flat color alone cannot achieve. The transition from deep garnet to soft rose evokes the glow of a gemstone catching light.

**Shadows** — Elevation through shadow tells users what is interactive, what is content, and what is background. Soft, warm shadows feel more natural than harsh black ones.

**Glass effects** — A frosted glass layer over a rich background creates a sense of looking through, of depth beyond the surface. It invites exploration.

## Restraint in Richness

The danger with depth is excess. Every shadow must earn its place. Every gradient must serve the content, not distract from it. The goal is an experience that feels luxurious because every detail has been considered, not because every effect has been deployed.
