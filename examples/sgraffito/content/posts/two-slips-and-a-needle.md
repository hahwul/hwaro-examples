title = "Two Slips and a Needle"
date = 2024-09-22

The technique reduces to three materials: a base body, a contrasting slip, and a tool sharp enough to cut through one without disturbing the other. Everything else is timing.

A potter throws or presses the form, then waits. The clay must reach leather-hard—firm enough to hold its shape under a tool, soft enough to be cut without crumbling. Too wet and the slip smears. Too dry and the surface flakes away in chips rather than shaving in clean ribbons.

The slip itself is a thinned suspension of clay tinted with metal oxides or stains. Iron yields red and brown. Cobalt produces the deep blues found in Hispano-Moresque ware. Manganese gives a near-black. The slip is brushed or poured over the leather-hard form in a layer thick enough to fully cover the body but thin enough to dry in step with the piece beneath it. Mismatched shrinkage is the enemy; a slip that pulls tighter than its substrate will craze and lift.

Once the slip has stiffened to the same leather-hard state, the artisan begins to cut. A loop tool removes broad fields. A needle or wooden point handles outlines and fine hatching. The cut exposes the body underneath, producing a two-tone image whose contrast depends entirely on the difference between body and slip.

Italian Renaissance facades used the same logic at architectural scale: a coat of dark plaster covered with a coat of lime, then scraped to reveal figures, swags, and grotteschi. The wall ages, the lime weathers, and the cut lines remain legible centuries later because the contrast was built into the substrate rather than painted on its surface.
