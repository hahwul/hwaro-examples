## SECURITY ASSESSMENT: TYPEFACE SELECTION FOR SENSITIVE MATERIALS

File Reference: RD-2024-0007. This document contains the results of a formal evaluation of `Courier New` for continued use in official records. It was produced in response to an internal query asking whether the agency should migrate to sans-serif typefaces for clarity.

The recommendation is: **No. Courier New stays.**

---

## THREAT MODEL

Before evaluating the typeface, the threat model must be established. The question is not which typeface looks better. The question is which typeface *fails gracefully*.

Documents in this system are:

1. Photocopied repeatedly, often at degraded quality
2. Scanned and re-scanned across different hardware generations
3. Printed on equipment that has not been serviced since the previous administration
4. Read under fluorescent lighting by personnel who are tired and running on poor cafeteria coffee

Under these conditions, proportional typefaces with subtle stroke contrast perform poorly. The thin strokes of `Georgia` disappear. The tight spacing of `Helvetica` collapses. The humanist curves of any number of expensive contemporary faces become indistinct smears.

> "A typeface that requires ideal conditions to be readable is a typeface that will eventually fail at a critical moment. Government documents are rarely read under ideal conditions."

---

## FINDINGS

**Stroke uniformity.** Courier New uses a near-monolinear stroke. There are no thin hairlines to vanish in reproduction. Under fifth-generation photocopy conditions, the text remains legible. This is a security feature.

**Fixed-width grid.** The monospace grid means that character alignment is predictable. When documents are compared across versions, column positions are consistent. Alterations are more visible. A changed digit in a proportional face can hide in adjusted spacing. In Courier New, the grid does not negotiate.

**Visual weight.** The apparent heaviness of the face on page creates psychological presence. A document in Courier New does not look casual. It looks processed. It looks official. This is not an accident.

**Historical continuity.** The typeface was designed in 1955 for IBM typewriters. It carries seven decades of institutional use. It is, functionally, the visual vernacular of the mid-century state. When people see Courier New, they read *document*. You do not have to earn that association. It is already there.

---

### COUNTER-ARGUMENTS CONSIDERED

The proposal to migrate to `Arial` or a similar sans-serif was evaluated on the following grounds:

- **Modernity.** Sans-serif typefaces are perceived as contemporary. This assessment finds that contemporary is not a value in this context.
- **On-screen readability.** Valid concern. Rejected on grounds that primary use case remains print.
- **Accessibility.** Dyslexia considerations were noted. The office has no current mandate on this topic. Recommendation deferred to a future assessment.

---

## CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION

`Courier New` is approved for continued use. It is recommended for all documents requiring the appearance and functional reality of official processing. Its limitations are known and acceptable. Its advantages under reproduction stress are significant.

Agencies seeking to appear modern are advised to consult a different office. This office is concerned with documents that work.

**ASSESSMENT STATUS: APPROVED**
