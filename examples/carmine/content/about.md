Eleanor Voss found Hazel in 2013, listed in three lines of a farm equipment classified as "old iron press, parts only." The press had spent the previous decade under a collapsed section of barn roof outside Corrin's Crossing, missing her flywheel, her ink disk cracked clean through. Eleanor paid for a truck rental and a case of beer for the two neighbors who helped load a nine-hundred-pound platen press onto a trailer, and did not entirely know what she had done until it was already done.

Rebuilding Hazel took two winters. A machinist in the next county recast the flywheel from a pattern Eleanor carved by hand after measuring the broken half a dozen times. The ink disk was replaced with a stand-in taken from a defunct hobby press. By the third spring, Hazel could pull an even impression again, and Carmine Press opened in the back of a converted feed depot with one press, four cases of metal type, and no orders.

The shop now prints two or three broadside editions a year, always poetry, always hand-set, always run in two pulls of ink — carmine, then black. Eleanor mixes the carmine herself from a base she has adjusted for a decade, and has never written the recipe down. She reads submissions by mail and by hand, sets them by hand, and answers correspondence at the same rate a person answers correspondence when there is a nine-hundred-pound press waiting in the next room. The most recent proof of that is [Instructions for Leaving a Light On](@/broadsides/instructions-for-leaving-a-light-on.md), which came off Hazel in May.

## Visiting

Carmine Press is open to visitors on the first Saturday of the month, by the simple mechanism of the front door being unlocked and Eleanor being somewhere inside it. There is no shop cat, despite frequent requests. There is a great deal of paper dust, and a kettle that is almost always warm.

## Commissioning a broadside

Poets are welcome to send work for consideration. Carmine Press prints slowly — typically one to two broadsides are accepted per reading period — and favors poems short enough to sit comfortably on a single hand-set forme, generally under thirty lines. There is no submission fee. There is also no guarantee of a reply faster than the mail itself allows. The [full log of broadsides](@/broadsides/_index.md) gives a fair sense of the kind of work Eleanor tends to say yes to.
