Carmine Press has run out of the same converted feed depot in Alder Hollow since 2014 — one printer, one platen press, and a chase full of metal type that outweighs the printer by a factor of about nine. Hazel, the press, was cast in 1893 and spent sixty years setting handbills for a hardware wholesaler before she was pulled out of a collapsing barn, missing her flywheel, and rebuilt over two winters on a kitchen table.

Every broadside that leaves the shop is set by hand, letter by letter, from a case of metal type kept in the same order it was kept in 1893, because that order is the only one that makes sense once your fingers learn it. Poems arrive from poets Eleanor has read for years and a few she has only ever met through the mail. She reads each one aloud in the shop before she sets a single line, listening for where the breath wants to fall, because that is where the line break goes. Read more about the shop, the press, and [how to send a poem](@/about.md).

The press runs two pulls on most jobs — carmine first, soot black second — and Eleanor does not fuss over hairline registration between the two. A little doubled red ghost sitting just behind the black is how you know a sheet came off Hazel and not a copier. It shows up in the type on this page too, if you look for it.
