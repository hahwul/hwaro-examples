Wren Okafor sent this poem in the last week of January, with a note that said only "the trees did this to me, not the other way around." It is about an orchard that blooms a week too early and pays for it.

<!-- more -->

> The peach trees bloomed a week too soon,
> and frost came back to prove a point —
> white on white, an argument
> the orchard always somehow wins.
>
> I stood at the fence line counting losses
> the way my grandmother counted stitches,
> under her breath, not stopping
> even when the number frightened her.

## Notes from the chase

This edition was set in 14pt Voss Text, the workhorse book face in the shop's case, with the title pulled in 24pt Hollow Antique for the top line. Hazel ran the sheets in two passes — carmine first for the title and the orchard rule beneath it, soot black second for the body — on Bellhaven Mill's soft white cotton stock. Sixty copies were pulled in early February; each is numbered and signed by the poet on the verso.

The run sold slowly through late winter and picked up once the actual thaw arrived, which felt, Eleanor said, like the appropriate order for things to happen. For more on how the shop mixes its carmine, see [the shop history](@/about.md).
