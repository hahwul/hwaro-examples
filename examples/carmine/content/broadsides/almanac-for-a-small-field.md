Susanna Pryce has kept the same quarter-acre field behind her house for eleven years and wrote this poem, she says, mostly to have something to hand people who ask what she does out there.

<!-- more -->

> January: nothing, and a plan for nothing.
> March: the first green lie of the season.
> May: everything at once, which is its own kind of drought.
> July: more than I can give away.
> September: the field asking to be let go.
> November: bare dirt, honestly relieved.
>
> Twelve months to grow six months of food
> and I would not trade the other six for anything.

## Notes from the chase

This is the largest edition Carmine Press has printed — eighty copies, set in 14pt Voss Text with a small hand-carved wheat ornament from the shop's junk drawer of cuts standing in for a period after the title. The size of the run was Eleanor's decision, not the poet's; she suspected, correctly, that an almanac would find a wide readership among people who also keep a field, however small. Printed on Bellhaven Mill stock in carmine and black, two pulls, dried flat for three days before trimming.
