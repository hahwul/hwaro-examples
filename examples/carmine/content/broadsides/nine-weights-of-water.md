Idris Calloway has been sending Eleanor poems since 2016, usually one a year, always in an envelope addressed by hand. This one arrived with no letter at all, just the poem and a return address.

<!-- more -->

> A cup weighs nothing until it is full.
> A bathtub weighs nothing until you sit in it
> and remember someone who used to.
> A lake has no weight I can name,
> only a shoreline, which is a kind of ledger,
> and a depth, which is a kind of debt.
>
> Grief, it turns out, floats.
> It is the carrying of it that has weight,
> and I have been carrying it
> up nine flights of stairs
> since March.

## Notes from the chase

The title line was pulled in 18pt Hollow Antique, a bolder wood-cut face reserved for poems that ask for it, with the body set in 12pt Voss Text. Forty-five copies were printed on Corvid Paper Co.'s laid stock, whose faint ribbing catches the carmine pass in a way Eleanor particularly likes on poems about water. The edition sold out within six weeks, mostly to readers who had lost someone within the year and said so in their order notes, unprompted.
