Samuel Cody's box kite is the workhorse of the cellular family: two open box
cells braced by winglike side panels that add lift and lateral stability. It is
a proper engineering project — square, true, and tensioned — and it hauls like a
draft horse once it is up.

<!-- more -->

## Cell geometry

A box kite lives and dies by being square. Every strut is the same length, every
diagonal tensioned equally, or it flies like a wounded pigeon. I build the cells
around 6 mm carbon struts and connect them with 3D-printed corner cubes.

```text
Two-cell Cody box
  cell cross-section ...... 30 × 30 cm square
  cell length ............. 35 cm each
  gap between cells ....... 40 cm
  side wing chord ......... 20 cm
  longerons ............... 6 mm carbon, 4 × 150 cm
  struts .................. 6 mm carbon, 8 × 30 cm
  covering ................ 40 g ripstop, sewn sleeves
```

## Tensioning

The trick is the diagonal bracing thread inside each cell. Sew a Dacron loop at
each corner, run a continuous line corner-to-corner, and pull it taut with a
small tensioner before you tie off. A square that flexes is a square that folds.

```text
Bridle (two-leg, front cell)
  upper leg ..... 55 cm to top-front longeron
  lower leg ..... 70 cm to bottom-front longeron
  tow point ..... 30% of total length back from nose
```

## Flight report

This needs wind and rewards it. In a soft breeze it just sat on the grass,
smug. When the afternoon picked up to a steady 25 km/h it rose off the launcher
and climbed to the end of 60 m of 50 kg Dacron with line I could barely hold. I
clipped on a lightweight line-cam rig; it lifted the extra 90 g without a
complaint. Landing a box kite is the hard part — walk it down slowly, never let
it stall, or it tumbles and you spend the evening re-truing the cells.
