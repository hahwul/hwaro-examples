For two years my sails puckered. Little accordion ripples along every seam that
spilled air and made even a good sail plan fly soft. The fix was not one trick;
it was five small habits. Here they are, so you can skip the two years.

<!-- more -->

## 1. Hot-cut every edge

Ripstop is woven nylon, and a scissor cut frays into a fuzzy mess that will not
hem straight. Cut with a soldering iron fitted with a knife tip, run along a
steel rule. The heat seals the weave into a clean, thin bead.

## 2. Baste with tape, not pins

Pins pucker light nylon and leave holes. Use 6 mm double-sided basting tape to
hold the hem fold, then sew through it. The tape washes out and the fabric stays
flat under the foot.

```text
Seam recipe (leading edge)
  fold ............ 12 mm, double-turned
  baste ........... 6 mm basting tape inside the fold
  needle .......... 70/10 microtex (sharp, thin)
  thread .......... bonded polyester, Tex 40
  stitch length ... 2.5 mm
  tension ......... lower than cotton; test on scrap first
```

## 3. Let the feed dogs pull

Do not push or hold back the fabric. Guide it and let the machine's feed set the
pace. Any hand tension fights the feed and that mismatch is exactly what a
pucker is.

## 4. Sew spar sleeves on the flat

Attach pockets and sleeves before you close the sail into its final shape, while
everything still lies flat on the table. Sewing a tube onto tensioned fabric is
how you build ripples in on purpose.

## 5. Press, do not iron

A quick, cool press with a pressing cloth sets a seam. A hot iron shrinks nylon
in seconds and turns a flat panel into a potato chip. Ask me how I know.

Follow these and the sail comes off the machine flat enough to see your
reflection in a raking light. A tight sail is free performance — no new spars,
no new plan, just a seam that behaves.
