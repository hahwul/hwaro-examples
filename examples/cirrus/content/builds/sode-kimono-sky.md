The sode is the most quietly beautiful kite I know. Its silhouette is the sleeve
of a kimono — narrow at the shoulder, wide and square at the cuff — and in the
air it hangs with a slow, dignified sway. This one is my eighteenth kite and the
first I have been sorry to see land.

<!-- more -->

## Sail plan

The sleeve shape is two mirrored panels. The proportions matter: the cuff is
wider than the shoulder, and the whole thing is taller than it is wide, or it
loses its grace. I dyed the ripstop a soft indigo before cutting.

```text
Sode half-panel (cut 2, mirrored)
  shoulder width ......... 22 cm
  cuff width ............. 34 cm
  height ................. 110 cm
  spine .................. 6 mm bamboo
  three horizontal spars . 5 mm bamboo (top, middle, cuff)
  hem .................... 10 mm, single-turned, hot-cut
```

## Bridle and tail

A sode wants a four-leg bridle to hold its face flat to the wind, and a long
tail to write with. The tail is not decoration — it is the stabiliser that turns
a nervous sway into a slow, deliberate calligraphic line.

```text
Bridle (four-leg)
  upper legs ..... 40 cm to shoulder spar ends
  lower legs ..... 60 cm to cuff spar ends
  tow point ...... 35% down from the shoulder
  tail ........... 6 m ribbon, 4 cm wide, tapered
```

## Flight report

Launched into a gentle 14 km/h and it rose slowly, almost reluctantly, then
settled at fifty degrees and began its sway — left, pause, right, pause — the
tail drawing long looping strokes beneath it. It is not a performer. It does not
climb high or pull hard. It simply hangs there being lovely, and every person
who walked past the field stopped to watch it. That is a kind of performance too.
