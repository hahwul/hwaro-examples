The field behind the allotments is a gust machine. The hedge on the west side
throws the wind into little rolling boils, so anything twitchy just tips over
and dives. A delta is the cure: the loose sail spills the gust instead of
fighting it, and the deep keel keeps the nose pointed downwind on its own.

<!-- more -->

## Sail plan

I cut this from 40 g ripstop nylon, hot-knifed so the edges seal as they part.
The half-sail is mirrored on the fold, so you only draw and cut once.

```text
Delta half-sail (cut 1, mirror on fold)
  nose to wingtip .......... 84 cm
  nose to tail (spine) ..... 60 cm
  trailing edge (tip→tail) . 66 cm
  keel depth ............... 22 cm
  hem allowance ............ 12 mm, double-turned
  spar pocket ............ Dacron tape, 20 mm wide
```

## Spars and bridle

The spreader is a 6 mm fibreglass rod; the spine and leading edges are 5 mm.
Everything pushes into sewn pockets, no ferrules, so a hard landing pops a rod
out instead of snapping it. The bridle is a single leg off the keel — a delta
self-adjusts, so one tow point is honestly all it wants.

```text
Bridle
  single leg, keel apex to tow ring
  leg length ............ 34 cm
  tow point ............. 40% back from nose
  knot .................. larks head on a #4 split ring
```

## Flight report

First launch, dead into a 15 km/h push: it climbed to sixty degrees and sat
there like it was nailed up. The gusts I was worried about just made the sail
breathe. I moved the tow ring one hole forward to trade a little altitude for a
steadier nose, and that was the whole tuning session. This is the kite I now
hand to anyone who says they are "not good with their hands."
