This is the entry I nearly did not write. The diamond is the oldest, simplest
kite there is, and I have made a dozen. This one flew for nine glorious minutes
and then came down in two pieces. A build log that only shows the wins is a
catalogue, not a workshop.

<!-- more -->

## What it was

A classic Eddy: a bowed cross-spar that gives the sail dihedral so it needs no
tail. 90 cm tall, 76 cm across the bow, 40 g ripstop, 5 mm fibreglass spine and
a 4 mm spreader. The bow was set with a leech line pulled to a 12 cm sag.

## What broke

The spreader snapped clean at the centre cross. Not the sail, not the bridle —
the spar, at its most loaded point, in a gust that jumped well past 30 km/h.

{{ alert(type="warning", body="A 4 mm spreader on a 76 cm bow is under-specced for anything above a steady breeze. The centre of a bowed spar is where all the bending load concentrates. Do not save four grams here.") }}

## Why

I had used a 4 mm rod to shave weight for light-wind days, then flew it on a day
that was anything but light. The bow multiplies the bending moment at the
centre; the rod reached its limit and let go. The bridle and sail were fine —
the sail actually cushioned the fall, which is the only reason the spine
survived.

## The fix

```text
Revision B
  spreader ....... 5 mm fibreglass (was 4 mm)
  centre cross ... bound with waxed thread + a dab of CA glue
  bow sag ........ reduced to 9 cm (less centre load)
  wind ceiling ... retire the light rod above 20 km/h
```

Rebuilt, re-flown, still airborne. The lesson costs four grams and one honest
paragraph.
