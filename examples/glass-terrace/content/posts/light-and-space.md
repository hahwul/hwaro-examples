title = "Balancing Light and Space"
date = "2025-05-12"
+++
A key component of this design system is the interplay between light and negative space. Generous padding and bright highlights are essential. Unlike darker themes, the Glass Terrace aesthetic relies on a light, often pastel or vibrant, background to make the glass elements pop. The challenge here is ensuring that the glass panels remain distinguishable from the bright background.

To achieve this, we use a combination of a very subtle inner white border—often referred to as an inner glow—and a soft, colored drop shadow. The inner border provides a crisp edge, catching the simulated light, while the shadow grounds the element. The shadow color shouldn't be pure black or gray; instead, it should be a darker, more saturated version of the background color. This technique, sometimes called a "tinted shadow," makes the elevation feel much more natural and integrated into the overall color scheme.

Negative space, or "white space," is equally important. Because glass elements visually interact with the space around them through the blur effect, clustering them too tightly can create a messy, muddy look. Giving each panel ample room to breathe allows the frosted glass effect to be fully appreciated and maintains the airy, open feel that defines the Glass Terrace style.
