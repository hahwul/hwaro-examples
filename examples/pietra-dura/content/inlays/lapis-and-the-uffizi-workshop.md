## The Material

Lapis lazuli is a metamorphic rock composed primarily of lazurite, with secondary inclusions of calcite, pyrite, and sodalite. Its working color is an intense blue that does not fade in light and is chemically stable in the presence of mineral binders. Until the synthesis of ultramarine pigment in 1828, lapis was the only source of a saturated, lightfast blue available to European craft. The Florentine workshops paid for it by the ounce, and the material's price determined how it was cut.

The raw stone arrived in Florence by sea from the Sar-i Sang mines in what is now the Badakhshan province of Afghanistan, transported overland to the Persian Gulf and then westward through the Mediterranean trade routes. By the time it reached the Uffizi workshop, a single block weighing 200 grams might have changed hands four or five times. The waste rate during cutting was high — a poorly placed calcite vein could halve the usable material in a single cleaving operation. Cutters were among the highest-paid craftsmen in the workshop and were typically the only workers permitted to handle the raw stone.

## The Cut

A piece of lapis destined for inlay is first oriented with respect to the depth of color. The most uniformly blue regions of the stone are reserved for large flat fields — the sky in a landscape panel, or the body of a robe in a figural composition. Regions with visible pyrite are cut for accent placements where the pyrite reads as a small bright fleck against the surrounding blue. Regions with calcite veins are typically cut as small filler pieces for backgrounds, where the irregular coloration is less visually disruptive.

The cutting itself was done with a wire saw drawn against a slurry of corundum or emery in oil. The wire was iron, drawn back and forth by hand, while the slurry did the actual abrading. A typical cut through a centimeter of lapis took twenty to forty minutes. The cut surfaces were then ground flat against a cast-iron lap charged with progressively finer abrasive grits, and finally polished against a tin lap with rouge.

## The Setting

The lapis pieces, once cut and polished, were set into a recessed bed in a marble or slate substrate. The recess was undercut slightly so that the stone could be locked in place with a mineral-based cement — typically a mixture of beeswax, resin, and powdered limestone. The cement was applied warm and the stone pressed in while it was still pliable. The joints between adjacent pieces were closed to better than 0.1 millimeter; on a finished panel, the joints disappear under direct light.

The technique requires that the substrate, the cement, and the stone all expand and contract together. A panel that is exposed to large temperature swings — a fireplace surround, for example — will eventually fail at the joints regardless of the quality of the original work. The surviving Florentine panels are almost without exception interior pieces, made for cabinets and tabletops in climate-controlled rooms.
