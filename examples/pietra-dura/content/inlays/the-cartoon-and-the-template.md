## The Cartoon

A pietra dura panel begins as a full-scale painted cartoon, executed by a workshop painter on heavy paper. The cartoon establishes the composition, the color scheme, and the precise outline of every individual stone element. It is the negotiation point between the painter who originated the design and the stoneworkers who will execute it. A cartoon for a 60-by-90 centimeter panel might take a single painter two weeks to complete, with daily review by the master of the workshop.

The cartoon is not a sketch. Every line in the drawing corresponds to a future joint in the stone. A line that is ambiguous in the cartoon will produce a joint that is ambiguous in execution, and a joint that is ambiguous in execution will be visible in the finished panel. The cartoon is therefore drawn with the same precision that the final inlay will require — typically with a fine sable brush and an iron-gall ink that holds a clean edge under magnification.

## The Template

Once the cartoon is approved, it is dissected. A copy is made on a second sheet, and the second sheet is cut along the outline of every individual stone element. Each cut piece becomes a paper template for one stone in the final inlay. The templates are numbered, and a master copy of the original cartoon is kept intact as the reference for assembly.

A panel of moderate complexity may have between 80 and 200 individual stone elements. Each template carries its own number and a notation indicating the intended material — "lapis" for the sky, "alabastro fiorito" for the architectural detail, "corniola" for the warm accents. The templates are stored in a numbered tray that follows the panel through every subsequent stage of work.

## Cutting to the Template

The stoneworker selects a piece of raw material — a thin slab approximately three to five millimeters thick — and lays the paper template on it, with consideration for the orientation of the natural color and figure of the stone. The template is held in place with a thin layer of pine pitch, applied warm. The stoneworker then cuts the stone to the template using a wire saw, working in a small bath of water and abrasive.

The accuracy of the cut depends entirely on the eye and hand of the worker. There is no jig, no clamp, and no machine assistance. A skilled worker produces a finished cut that follows the template to within 0.05 millimeter; an apprentice produces cuts that visibly deviate. The difference between a master-quality panel and a workshop-quality panel is, in large part, the difference between these two tolerances.

## Assembly

The cut pieces are assembled face-down on a flat slate, with the cartoon used as a reference for placement. Each piece is set into its position, the joints checked against the master cartoon, and adjacent pieces fitted to each other by light grinding of the edges. When the assembly is complete, the entire face is bonded to its substrate in a single operation, and the slate is then removed by sliding a thin blade between the slate and the finished face. The panel is then ready for final polishing.
