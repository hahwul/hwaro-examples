## The Coarse Lap

After assembly, the working face of the panel is uneven. Adjacent stones, even when cut and set with great care, sit at slightly different heights — typically within 0.1 to 0.3 millimeter of each other. The first polishing operation, the coarse lap, brings the entire face to a single common plane. The lap is a flat cast-iron disk, charged with silicon carbide grit at approximately 220 mesh, mounted on a foot-driven or hand-driven turntable.

The panel is held face-down against the rotating lap, with light pressure applied evenly across the back. The coarse lap removes material at a rate of perhaps 0.05 millimeter per minute, depending on the hardness of the stones in the panel. Lapis lazuli abrades faster than agate; agate abrades faster than rock crystal. Within a single panel containing all three materials, the lap will tend to leave the lapis slightly recessed and the rock crystal slightly proud. The skill of the polisher is in compensating for these differential rates by varying the pressure across the panel.

## The Fine Lap

When the panel is uniformly flat, it is moved to a fine lap. The fine lap is a second cast-iron disk charged with finer grit — typically 600 mesh, sometimes finer. The purpose of the fine lap is not to remove material but to remove the deep scratches left by the coarse lap and replace them with a uniform pattern of much finer scratches. A panel that has been properly worked through the fine lap shows no visible scratch at arm's length, but at close inspection still has a matte surface texture.

The fine lap is run for approximately twice as long as the coarse lap. Working too briefly leaves residual coarse-lap scratches that will reappear after the final polish. Working too long is harmless to the surface but wastes time on a stage that produces no visible improvement after a certain point.

## The Tin Lap and Rouge

The final polish is done on a tin lap charged with iron oxide rouge in water. Tin is soft enough to deform under the pressure of the panel, allowing the rouge particles to be held against the stone surface in a way that a harder lap cannot achieve. The rouge particles are small enough — well below the wavelength of visible light — that they leave no measurable scratch. What they produce instead is a true optical polish.

The tin-and-rouge stage takes between forty minutes and two hours, depending on the size and complexity of the panel. The polisher works slowly, frequently re-charging the lap with rouge and water, and rotating the panel to ensure that no region receives less attention than any other. When the polish is complete, the panel reflects without visible distortion, and the joints between adjacent stones — the joints that have been the entire concern of the panel from the cartoon onward — have disappeared from view.
