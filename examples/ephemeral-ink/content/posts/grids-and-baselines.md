A grid is a contract between the designer and the page.

The baseline grid sets the vertical rhythm. Every line of body text sits on a regular interval, derived from the leading of the primary text style. Headings, captions, and figures align to multiples of that interval. When the rhythm is consistent, the reader does not notice it. When it is broken, the reader notices the break before identifying its source. Something feels wrong, and the page feels amateur.

The column grid divides the page horizontally into measured units. Two columns produce one kind of reading; six columns produce another. A twelve-column grid is flexible, but flexibility costs discipline. The grid is only useful when most of the content actually sits on it, and the exceptions are deliberate.

A grid does not eliminate decisions. It moves them. Instead of choosing every position, the designer chooses how the grid is built and where to break it. The breaks then carry weight, because they are visibly chosen rather than accidental. A pulled quote that violates the column reads as emphatic. A stray paragraph that violates the column reads as a mistake.

The instruction is unromantic. Decide the grid early. Set the baseline and the columns before the first paragraph is placed. Resist adjusting the grid to accommodate awkward content. Adjust the content instead, or accept that this particular content does not belong on this page.

The page reads better when the substrate is settled.
