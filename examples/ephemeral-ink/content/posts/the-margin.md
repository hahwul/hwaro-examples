The margin is not waste. It is structure.

A page set edge to edge in type cannot be read for long. The eye loses its place between lines, the column becomes a wall, and the reader stops reading. The standard book margin, with its asymmetry between gutter and outer edge, evolved over centuries of practical bookbinding and quiet observation of how readers actually behave. The proportions vary between traditions, but every tradition reserves a frame.

The margin holds the thumb. It admits annotation. It absorbs the small skew of an old press, and it forgives the deckle of handmade paper. None of these functions are visible when reading goes well. They become visible only when the margin is taken away.

The same logic governs interfaces, conversations, and rooms. Empty space is not the absence of content. It is the precondition for the content to be legible. A schedule with no gaps is a schedule that breaks at the first delay. A wall with no breath cannot hold a single object well.

The discipline is to leave the margin alone. The temptation, always, is to fill it. There is more to say, more to show, more to add. The page complies until it does not.

Set the margin first. Compose inside it. Let the unprinted region do its work.
