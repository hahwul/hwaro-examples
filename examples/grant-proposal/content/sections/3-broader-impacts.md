## Societal Impact

The research addresses a problem of direct relevance to millions of Americans. The three partner municipalities -- Boston, Houston, and Phoenix -- represent distinct climate vulnerability profiles (coastal flooding, hurricane-driven precipitation extremes, and arid heat stress respectively) and serve a combined population of 5.2 million. The computational tools and decision frameworks developed by this project will be directly applicable to water utilities nationwide facing similar climate adaptation challenges.

## Partner Municipality Engagement

Each partner municipality has committed a utility liaison to participate in the project throughout its duration:

- **Boston Water and Sewer Commission** (Letter of Support attached): Primary concern is combined sewer overflow management under intensifying precipitation. The BWSC has committed staff time for quarterly data exchanges and participation in all stakeholder workshops.

- **Houston Water** (Letter of Support attached): Primary concern is infrastructure resilience following extreme precipitation events (Hurricane Harvey, 2017; Tropical Storm Imelda, 2019). Houston Water has committed access to real-time SCADA data and participation in scenario development workshops.

- **Phoenix Water Services Department** (Letter of Support attached): Primary concern is long-term water supply reliability under projected aridification. Phoenix WSD has committed to pilot testing the decision support tool in their 2028 infrastructure master plan update.

## Education and Training

The project incorporates a comprehensive education and training plan:

**Graduate Training.** Four PhD students and six MSc students will receive interdisciplinary training spanning hydraulic engineering, climate science, optimization, and stakeholder engagement. All students will participate in cross-institutional research exchanges (minimum 2 weeks at a partner institution) to build collaborative skills.

**Undergraduate Research.** Each institution will host two undergraduate researchers per summer through existing REU programmes. We will recruit specifically from minority-serving institutions through partnerships with the National GEM Consortium and the American Indian Science and Engineering Society (AISES).

**Curriculum Development.** Project findings will be integrated into new graduate courses at each partner institution:
- MIT: "Climate-Resilient Infrastructure Systems" (new course, Year 2)
- Stanford: Module in existing "Water Resources Engineering" course
- UT Austin: Module in existing "Statistical Climatology" course
- Georgia Tech: "Optimization Under Deep Uncertainty" (new course, Year 3)
- U. Michigan: Module in existing "Participatory Design Methods" course

## Open-Source Deliverables

All software, data, and educational materials produced by the project will be released under open-source licences (MIT for code, CC BY 4.0 for documentation and data). The decision support tool will be deployed as a freely accessible web application hosted by MIT's infrastructure computing group with a 10-year sustainability commitment.
