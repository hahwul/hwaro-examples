Woke to a sound the cabin only makes in a foehn: a deep, even push against
the west wall, no gusts to speak of, like a hand laid flat and leaning. The
06:00 line read 250 degrees, mean 47, gust only 71 — remarkably smooth for
that speed — and the thermometer had climbed nine degrees overnight while
the humidity fell off a cliff, 91 percent down to 34.

<!-- more -->

The picture from the door was the classic one. To the west, a solid grey
wall of cloud pressed against the crest, boiling in place and never
crossing. To the east, directly downwind of the summit line, a slot of
utterly clean blue — the foehn gap — where the descending air, warmed and
dried by its fall, eats every droplet it touches. The valley farmers call
this a snow-eater wind, and by noon the drift behind the woodshed, which
had survived six weeks of April sun, was simply gone. Not melted so much as
*removed*.

Foehn days are false-spring days and I log them with suspicion. The warmth
is borrowed from compression, not from the season, and the dryness makes
the larch slopes below the station crackle underfoot. If the bearing backs
south of 230 tonight, the wall wins, the gap closes, and tomorrow's entry
will be written in rain. The evening reading will tell.
