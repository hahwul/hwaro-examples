The ridge has been inside the cloud since yesterday afternoon, and the
northwest wind is doing what northwest wind does here in deep winter:
building rime. Every windward edge — the mast guys, the door hinges, the
handle of the snow shovel I foolishly left outside — now carries a white
feather of ice growing *into* the wind, each feather a frozen record of the
flow that made it.

<!-- more -->

The 06:00 reading put the mean at 38 km/h with a gust to 62, bearing steady
on 315. That steadiness is the story. Valley wind wanders; ridge wind in a
winter outbreak locks onto a bearing and holds it the way a train holds a
track. The cups sang all night at one unchanging pitch, which is how I knew
without looking that the direction had not moved five degrees since supper.

By afternoon the rime feathers on the north guy wire were long enough to
touch each other, so I went up with the wooden batten and knocked them off
before the extra sail area could work the anchors loose. The feathers came
away in curved plates, all pointing northwest, like the fossil of a gale.
Thirty hours in cloud so far. The aneroid has flattened out at 1009.8 and
stopped falling, which up here usually means the worst of it has already
happened; the wind just hasn't been told yet.
