Seventh consecutive morning of the cloud sea. The monsoon front is stalled
somewhere south of the range, and the warm southerly feed rides up the
foothills, condenses at about 1,100 metres, and stops there — a lid of
cloud filling every valley to the horizon while the summit line stands in
clear, wet-smelling air. At 06:00 the sea was so flat that the radio mast
on Kalmoe summit, eleven kilometres away, appeared to be moored in it like
a buoy.

The wind itself is modest — mean 19, gust 33, bearing wobbling either side
of due south — but it is doing enormous quiet work. Every hour it delivers
another valley's worth of saturated air to the slope below the station, and
the cloud sea rises and falls with the strength of the feed like something
breathing. When the mean picks up past 25 the surface climbs to within a
hundred metres of the cabin and the first rags of scud come over the east
railing; when it slackens the sea settles back down and the ridge is an
island again.

Pressure is low and easing lower, 1002.1 tonight. When the front finally
unsticks and comes north, this journal will get loud: last July the ridge
took 240 millimetres in two days. For now the log records patience —
a soft wind, a full sea, and laundry that has not been dry since June.
