The cold surge the valley office had been advertising for three days
arrived at 21:40 last night, and it arrived the way continental air always
does — not as a storm but as a decision. The bearing snapped from 260 to
330 in under twenty minutes, the temperature fell eleven degrees in two
hours, and the mast began the flat roar it reserves for means above 50.

<!-- more -->

The 06:00 line is the hardest I have logged this winter: mean 54, gust 89,
air at −17.9, pressure climbing violently through 1031. High pressure this
brutal confuses people who learned their weather from textbooks — the
anticyclone is supposed to be the fair-weather symbol — but this is the
Siberian kind, a dome of air so cold and dense it flattens the pressure
field around it and pours off the continent like slag.

At 03:00 the cup anemometer stopped reporting: spindrift had packed the
rotor and frozen — the same failure the mast survived in
[last February's rime gale](@/observations/rime-and-a-northwest-gale.md).
This is why the sonic sits on the east railing. I
switched the logger to the backup by headlamp, in a windchill I decline to
compute, and the record is unbroken — the station's small vanity, five
winters without a gap. The cups thawed in the sun by noon and came back
reading within 2 km/h of the sonic. Good instruments, like good neighbours,
forgive.

The snow smoke off the crest at sunrise stood a hundred metres downwind,
pure white against a sky the colour of gun steel. Nobody sees this and
stays indoors about it. Second reading tonight may set the winter's record.
