The anticyclone arrived on Thursday and parked. Pressure 1024.7 and barely
moving, sky scrubbed clean, and the cups turning so slowly at midday that I
tapped the mast to make sure they hadn't seized. A mean of 6 km/h is the
lowest figure in the log since I started it. On a ridge named for wind,
a calm is the exotic event, and it gets its own entry.

<!-- more -->

What little wind there is comes at night, and it is my favourite kind to
log: katabatic drainage. An hour after sunset the ground up here has given
its heat to a black sky, the air touching it chills, grows dense, and
begins to slide downslope into the valleys — a wind made purely of
falling. It sets in from the northeast, off the summit dome above the
cabin, never more than a whisper at 40 degrees on the vane, and it flows
like water: I can stand at the east railing with a bare hand up and feel
it pour over the rock lip in a layer no thicker than a blanket.

By 02:00 the drained air has ponded in the valleys and made fog of itself,
and at the 06:00 reading the lowlands are white lakes joined by white
rivers. The vane swings idle by mid-morning as the sun stirs it all back
up. Winter is close; stillness like this is how the mountain inhales
before it.
