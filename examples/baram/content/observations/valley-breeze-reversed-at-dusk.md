A day with no weather system within five hundred kilometres, which is when
the mountain runs its own private circulation and I get to log the
clockwork. By 09:30 the sun had heated the east-facing slopes enough to
start the valley breeze: warm air peeling upslope, arriving at the station
from 120 degrees, mean 14 by mid-afternoon. Every summit on the range grew
its one obedient cumulus by 14:00, each cloud pinned to its peak like a
flag marking the top of a thermal.

<!-- more -->

The part worth writing down is the reversal. At 19:10, with the slopes
falling into shadow, the upslope breeze faltered; by 19:25 the vane hung
slack and the evening went so quiet I could hear the stream at Kalmoe,
nine hundred metres below. Twenty minutes of true calm — the hinge of the
day — and then, at 19:45, the first cool thread of the mountain breeze came
*down* off the summit dome from the northwest, and the circulation was
running in reverse, air that had spent the day climbing now sliding home
downhill. It is the same physics that made
[the October calm](@/observations/october-calm-katabatic-nights.md) worth an
entry, run on a daily clock instead of a seasonal one.

The log calls it 120 degrees at the 18:00 reading and will call it 320 by
midnight, and neither line is wrong; they are the same engine seen at
different hours. Cumulus dissolved on schedule at dusk. Pressure steady at
1017.9. Some days the ridge gets gales worth a page each; today it got the
quiet diagram every textbook draws, performed without a flaw.
