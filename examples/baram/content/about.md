Baram is the Korean word for wind, and this journal is a record of little
else. It is written from a one-room cabin bolted to the granite of Saebyeok
Ridge in the fictional Taeryong Range, at an elevation of 1,463 metres —
high enough that the weather arrives here first, several hours before it
reaches the valley towns.

The station is deliberately simple. A cup anemometer and a wind vane sit on
a ten-metre mast anchored twenty paces west of the cabin, clear of the roof
eddy. A sonic anemometer on the east railing serves as a check instrument
when the cups ice over, which on this ridge is not a rare event. Temperature
and humidity are read from a screened sensor on the north wall; pressure
from an aneroid drum inside, corrected to station level, never to sea level.
The sea is far away and does not care.

## The observing routine

Readings are taken twice daily, at 06:00 and 18:00 KST, and more often when
the ridge has something to say. Each entry in [the log](@/observations/_index.md)
fixes the wind first —
the direction it arrives from, the ten-minute mean, and the strongest gust
of the watch — because on a ridge the wind is the weather; everything else
is commentary. A raw line in the station notebook looks like this:

```text
2026-06-30 06:00 KST  dir=245  mean=22.4  gust=31.0  t=+14.2  rh=87  p=1011.6
```

The arrows you see beside every entry are drawn from that `dir` field: each
one is rotated to the recorded bearing, flying downwind, the way a streamer
tied to the mast would point. A bearing of 315 means the air came out of the
northwest; its arrow leans away toward the southeast.

## The observer

The log is kept by Mun Dahee, a retired route-forecaster who came up the
ridge for one season and has stayed for six. Corrections, disagreements
about cloud names, and reports of what the same wind did in your valley are
welcome by post — the box at the Kalmoe trailhead is checked every second
Thursday, weather permitting.
