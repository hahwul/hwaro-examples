Cloth was the study that convinced me the rule was worth keeping. A fold is
already a continuous thing — fabric does not stop and restart, it flows over a
ridge and drops into a valley and rises again — so the single line and the
subject want the same thing. The pen follows the top of one fold, dives into
the shadow between it and the next, and climbs back up the far side without
ever needing to lift.

<!-- more -->

This is the longest path in the collection at eight hundred and sixty-six units,
and the length is honest: drapery is mostly hidden distance. What you read as a
few soft folds is a line that has travelled twice as far as it appears to,
because every valley is a there-and-back the eye compresses into a single dark
crease.

I hung a linen dishcloth over the back of a chair, weighted one corner with a
spoon, and drew it before the folds could settle. The little hooked return near
the base — where the line dips, catches, and reverses — is the spoon's corner
pulling the weave off true. It is my favourite mistake in the set: entirely
unplanned, and impossible to have drawn if I had been allowed to lift the pen
and reconsider.
