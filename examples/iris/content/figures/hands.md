Hands are where the single-line rule earns its keep. Draw them the ordinary way
and you produce ten fingers, each its own closed shape, each demanding the pen
be lifted and set down again. Under the rule there is only one path, so the two
hands must be *woven* — the line for the left thumb becomes the knuckle of the
right, the loop that describes one finger doubles as the gap beside the next.

<!-- more -->

I traced this from my own hands held in front of a lamp, folded the way you
fold them when you are waiting for news. The tension in a real clasp is all in
the small reversals — a finger tucked, a thumb laid over — and the line catches
that tension precisely because it, too, is under strain, forced to reverse on
itself to reach the next contour without a break.

The three small cusps in the middle of the drawing are where the path pinches
to nothing and turns back: those are the interlaced fingers. They were the
hardest passage to keep legible, because a continuous line wants to smooth
them into a single bulge. Six hundred and eighty-two units, and roughly a fifth
of that length is spent inside the knot of the fingers alone.
