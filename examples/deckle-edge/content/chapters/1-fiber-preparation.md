## Choosing the fiber

<p class="lede">The papermaker's first decision is the choice of fiber. This choice determines nearly everything about the finished sheet -- its color, texture, strength, opacity, and the way it accepts ink, paint, or type. There is no single best fiber; each is suited to particular purposes, and the skilled papermaker maintains a stock of several fiber types to meet different needs.</p>

**Cotton** is the most forgiving fiber for the beginning papermaker. Cotton linters -- the short fibers that cling to the seed after ginning -- are available in prepared sheets that require only soaking and moderate beating. The resulting paper is soft, opaque, and slightly warm in tone. Cotton rag, made from textile scraps, produces a superior sheet but requires more extensive preparation.

**Linen** makes the finest European-style papers. The fibers are longer and stronger than cotton, producing sheets of exceptional durability. The paper used by Rembrandt for his etchings, by the great printing houses of Venice and Lyon, and by modern fine-press printers is overwhelmingly linen rag. Its preparation is more demanding: the fibers must be retted, cleaned, and beaten for longer periods.

**Kozo**, the inner bark of the paper mulberry, is the principal fiber of Japanese papermaking. Its extraordinary length creates an interlocking web that gives washi its legendary strength. A sheet of kozo paper of ordinary weight can be folded and unfolded thousands of times without tearing -- a property that made it indispensable for screens, lanterns, and even clothing in traditional Japan.

## Beating

<div class="process-step">
<p class="step-number">Process Note</p>
<p>Beating is the most critical operation in papermaking. The purpose is not to cut the fibers short but to fibrillate them -- to fray and soften the outer walls of each fiber so that they bond more readily with their neighbors during sheet formation. Under-beaten fibers produce a weak, porous sheet; over-beaten fibers produce a dense, translucent sheet that may be difficult to dry without cockling. The papermaker learns to judge the degree of beating by touch, by the feel of the slurry between thumb and finger, and by the way a small sample drains on the mould.</p>
</div>

The Hollander beater, invented in the Netherlands around 1680, replaced the stamp mill as the primary means of preparing fiber for Western papermaking. Its rotating cylinder of metal bars works against a fixed bedplate, fibrillating the fibers in a continuous circulation of water and pulp. The gap between the cylinder and the bedplate controls the degree of fibrillation: a wide gap produces a gentle, slow beat; a narrow gap produces a fast, aggressive cut.

Japanese papermaking traditionally uses a different approach. The kozo fibers are beaten by hand with wooden mallets on a stone surface -- a technique called naginata-beater beating. This method fibrillates the fibers without cutting them, preserving their full length and producing the characteristically strong, translucent sheets of washi.
