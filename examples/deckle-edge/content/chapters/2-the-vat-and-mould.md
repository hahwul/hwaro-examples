## The papermaker's vat

<p class="lede">The vat is the heart of the paper workshop. It is a large, shallow vessel -- traditionally made of wood, now often of fiberglass or stainless steel -- filled with water in which a small quantity of beaten fiber has been suspended. The concentration of fiber in the water, called the consistency, is remarkably low: typically between 0.5 and 2 percent by weight. The vatman works in a medium that is almost entirely water, dipping and lifting to capture a thin, even layer of fiber on the surface of the mould.</p>

The size of the vat determines the maximum size of sheet that can be made. A vat for letter-sized sheets may be little larger than a kitchen sink; a vat for imperial sheets (22 by 30 inches) requires a vessel of substantial volume, and the preparation of enough pulp to fill it represents a significant investment of labor.

The mould is a rectangular frame strung with a fine wire or bamboo screen that serves as the surface on which the sheet is formed. In Western papermaking, the traditional mould uses brass laid lines -- closely spaced horizontal wires -- supported by heavier chain lines at wider intervals. The pattern of these wires is visible in the finished paper when held to the light, and the laid lines and chain lines are among the primary evidence used by paper historians to identify the origin and date of historical papers.

The deckle is a removable frame that sits on top of the mould during sheet formation. It serves as a fence, containing the liquid pulp on the mould surface while the water drains through the screen. Because the deckle does not form a watertight seal with the mould, a small amount of pulp seeps under its edges, creating the irregular, feathered margin that gives handmade paper its name: the deckle edge.

## The deckle edge

The deckle edge is the most visible sign that a sheet of paper was made by hand. Machine-made paper is trimmed to precise dimensions with sharp blades; the deckle edge is irregular, slightly thinner than the body of the sheet, and varies from sheet to sheet. In fine book production, the deckle edge is sometimes preserved as a mark of quality and craftsmanship, though printers and binders may trim one or more edges for practical reasons.

## Watermarks

Watermarks are designs formed by wire shapes sewn to the mould surface. Where the wire sits higher than the surrounding screen, the paper is thinner and more translucent, creating a visible image when the sheet is held to the light. Watermarks have been used since the thirteenth century to identify the papermaker, the mill, and the quality of the paper. Today, hand papermakers continue to create watermarks as marks of identity and as decorative elements.
