Tramontana is kept by Nel Aldeguer, a sketcher who never quite learned to draw indoors. The name is borrowed from the cold north wind that scours the Mediterranean sky clean — the wind that, once a year, hands you a morning so sharp you can count the tiles on a roof a kilometre off. Those are the mornings worth catching.

The rule of the journal is simple: everything is drawn from life, in one sitting, standing up or perched on whatever the plaza offers. No photographs, no finishing the roofline at home from memory. If a bus parks in front of the fountain halfway through, the bus goes in. The point was never a perfect building; it was the half-hour of looking hard at one.

## Why plazas and rooftops

A plaza is a town explaining itself. Where the awnings go, where the old men sit, which corner keeps its shade until two — you learn a place faster with a pen than with a guidebook. Rooftops are the opposite lesson: from above, a city drops its manners and shows you its patchwork, the illegal terraces and the drying laundry and the satellite dishes bolted to the seventeenth century.

## What rides in the bag

Kept deliberately small, so there is no excuse not to start:

```text
1× A5 sketchbook, 200gsm hot-press, elastic band gone slack
1× fountain pen, EF nib, De Atramentis document black
1× waterbrush, medium, filled at the nearest fountain
6× half-pans: raw sienna, burnt sienna, ultramarine,
   a warm red, a payne's grey, an unnameable green
1× folding stool, refuses to fold
```

If you want to trade sitting spots or argue about greens, start with the growing [wall of sittings](@/sketches/_index.md) — newest first — or subscribe by the feed linked in the footer of every page.
