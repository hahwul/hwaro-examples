The tramontana had come through in the night, and by seven the Spianata Castelletto handed me the whole city sharpened to the edges — the kind of morning where you can see the difference between one grey roof and the next.

<!-- more -->

Genoa from above is nothing but roofs, and every one of them is grey, which sounds like a problem until you sit with it. There is the blue-grey of new slate, the warm mouse-grey of old tile gone soft, the near-black of the wet north slopes that never see the sun. I drew the pen lines first — just the ridgelines and the chimneys, no windows — and let the shapes stack the way they actually do, each terrace shouldering the one below it down the hill toward the port cranes.

Then one wash. I mixed a single grey from ultramarine and burnt sienna and leaned it warm or cool by how much water the brush still held, working fast and wet so the roofs bled into each other the way they do in real haze. The trick this morning was leaving the paper alone: the white of the page is the glare coming off the harbour, and the moment you touch it you lose the light. I stopped when the funicular came up behind me and the bench filled with commuters reading their phones over my shoulder.
