Everyone draws Notre-Dame de la Garde up on its hill. I turned my back on it and drew down into the Vieux-Port instead, where the pleasure boats pack in so tight the masts make a second, thinner city standing on the water.

<!-- more -->

Rigging is a trap. Draw every stay and shroud and you get a diagram; draw none and you get lollipops on sticks. The way through, I have decided, is to draw the masts as confident vertical strokes — full pressure, one pull each — and then let the rigging be four or five lines that only *imply* the rest. The eye is generous. It will build the whole tangle from a good suggestion and resent you for the real thing.

I kept the whole sketch in graphite, a soft 4B, working the hulls as flat dark shapes and leaving the masts as bare paper so they would read as pale against the harbour. The mistral was up and everything was pinging and clanging, the halyards slapping their masts like a hundred impatient fingers. At the very end I broke my own rule and touched three points of white gouache onto the water where the sun hit the chop — the only bright thing on the page, and the reason it works. The smell of frying panisse from the quay is not on the paper, but it is in my memory of it, which is the same thing.
