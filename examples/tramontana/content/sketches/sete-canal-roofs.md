Sète calls itself the Venice of Languedoc, which is the sort of thing towns say, but the canals are real enough and so are the roofs that lean over them — ochre, rose, a faded pistachio — all softened this February morning by a low, kind winter sun.

<!-- more -->

Winter light is a watercolourist's friend: it comes in sideways, rakes across the roof tiles, and separates every plane into a warm lit face and a cool shadowed one. I could paint almost the whole street as pairs of shapes — a slab of raw sienna beside a slab of the same grey I mix everywhere, ultramarine and burnt sienna, tilted cool. No line work at first, just the roofs as flat coloured patches fitting together like a dropped deck of cards.

Then the awnings. The fishmarket along the quai flies these bold red-and-white striped awnings, and they are the whole reason to draw this view — one shot of pure warm red in a street of muted earth, the note that makes all the quiet notes sing. I saved them for last and painted them at full strength, wet and confident, letting one stripe bleed slightly into the next because a bleed reads as sunlight and a hard edge reads as a diagram. A gull landed on the page. I let it stay a moment, then turned the block over before it could contribute.
