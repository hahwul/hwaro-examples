I only had the twenty-five minutes before the market swallowed me, so I drew fast and drew once: no pencil under-drawing, no second chances, straight in with the brush pen while a churro seller narrated my mistakes.

<!-- more -->

There is a discipline I love and fear in equal measure — the continuous line, where the pen does not leave the paper from the first stroke to the last. You cannot fix anything. A wrong proportion just becomes part of the character of the thing. El Micalet, the great octagonal bell tower, came out a touch too narrow, which makes it look even taller and more improbable than it is, leaning over the Modernista ironwork of the Mercat Central like a schoolteacher over a desk.

The market domes were the gift. Their curved zinc caught the flat morning light and I could describe each one with a single arced pull of the brush, thick on the shaded underside where I pressed, thin on the lit crown where I lifted — the whole tonal range living in how hard I leaned. That is the argument for a brush pen in twenty-five minutes: line and weight in the same tool, no waiting for anything to dry. I signed it standing up, already smelling the roasted almonds, and went in to be robbed happily by a woman selling saffron.
