The Quattro Canti is not really a plaza — it is four concave palace faces meeting at a crossroads, a room with the ceiling taken off. I set up on the least suicidal corner and waited for the light I wanted.

<!-- more -->

Blue hour in a baroque square lasts about ten minutes and then it is gone, so the drawing has to be built before it arrives. I spent the first half hour just with the dip pen, laying in the tiers of statues, the columns, the round-topped niches, working corner to corner so the architecture would hold together even if the colour failed. Baroque wants to overwhelm you; the defence is to draw the big rhythms first and let the details be suggestions.

When the sky finally dropped to that bruised ultramarine and the sodium lamps warmed up underneath it, I went in with two washes only. Cool blue across everything above the cornice line, then — while it was still damp — the warm lamp glow pooled into the lower niches, wet-in-wet, so the two temperatures met without a hard edge. The recipe I keep going back to for this hour:

```text
sky wash:   ultramarine + a whisper of payne's grey, 70% water
lamp glow:  raw sienna + warm red, 40% water, dropped in wet
shadow:     let the two mix on the paper — never in the pan
```

A carabiniere asked to see the page, decided it was a monument and not a threat, and waved me on before the colour was even dry.
