The series closes by handing the last decision to physics. A large panel is levelled with a spirit level and its edges dammed with low wax walls. Diluted ink is flooded across the whole surface in one pour, then the room is left still for two days while it dries. No brush touches it after the pour. Where the ink sits deepest and dries slowest, it gathers into a soft dark centre; toward the edges it thins and silvers, leaving a faint tideline where the last moisture retreated.

<!-- more -->

This is the most patient plate and the least controlled. Every earlier study was an accumulation of deliberate marks; this one is a single gesture followed by a long surrender. The pool finds its own centre, slightly off from the geometric middle because the panel is never perfectly level and the air never perfectly still. That drift is the plate's signature — proof that the world, not the hand, finished the work.

Drying ink behaves like weather. Convection currents in the film carry pigment toward warmer regions, and the final map of tone is really a record of two days of quiet air moving across a room. Set at the end of the series, after ground, ink, clay, and graphite, it offers a last version of the movement's founding idea: do a little, wait a long time, and let the surface become itself.

The series began with a nearly empty plate; see [Ground, Unpainted](/studies/ground-unpainted/), or read the [colophon](/colophon/) for how each surface was made.
