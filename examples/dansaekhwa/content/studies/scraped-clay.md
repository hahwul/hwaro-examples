This plate turns from ink to earth. A fine grey clay, ground by hand and slaked overnight, is bound with warm hide glue into a thick slip and troweled across the panel in five horizontal bands. While each band is still wet, a steel blade is drawn along it once, edge held low, scraping the clay back to varying depths. Where the blade bites, the primed ground shows through as a pale scar; where it skips, the clay stands proud and dries with a matte, mineral tooth.

<!-- more -->

The scraping is the whole event. It cannot be corrected — a second pass tears the surface — so the gesture has to be committed in a single breath, the way a calligrapher commits a stroke. The bands are not level. They sag and rise with the pressure of the arm, and those small deviations from the horizon are what keep the plate from reading as a diagram.

Clay behaves nothing like ink. It shrinks as it dries, opening hairline fissures along the thickest ridges, and it holds the light instead of absorbing it. Set beside the ink study, the two plates argue quietly about what a monochrome surface can be: one dyed into the paper, the other built up and cut away. Both refuse colour. Both insist that the mark and the material are the same thing.
