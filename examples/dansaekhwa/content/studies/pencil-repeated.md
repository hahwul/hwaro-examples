The quietest labour in the series. A single hard pencil, sharpened often, draws short strokes across a linen panel at one fixed angle — roughly thirty degrees from vertical — over and over, for days. There is no drawing in the usual sense, no contour, no subject. There is only the accumulation of identical marks until the hatching thickens from a whisper at the edges into a dense, breathing grey through the centre.

<!-- more -->

Graphite has a low sheen that shifts as you move past it, so the plate is never quite the same tone twice. Walk left and the centre darkens; walk right and it silvers. This is deliberate. A truly flat field would be dead; the faint sheen keeps the surface alive to the viewer's own motion, the way still water registers a passing cloud.

Counting is part of the method. I work in sessions of a fixed length rather than to a fixed appearance, trusting the hours to decide when the field is full:

```
session   strokes (approx)   region
  1           ~900            lower third
  2           ~1100           lower + centre
  3           ~1400           full field, first coat
  4           ~1000           centre, second coat
```

By the fourth session the pencil is only reinforcing what is already there, closing the last gaps of bare linen. The edges are left thin on purpose. They record the beginning, when the hand was fresh and the marks few, before repetition took over and became the picture.
