The series begins with restraint. Before a single stroke is laid down, the ground itself is asked to perform. Two sheets of hanji are wet-mounted, dried under weight for a week, then primed with a thin rabbit-skin gesso that sinks into the fibre rather than sitting on top of it. What remains is a warm ivory field with almost nothing on it: one faint vertical seam where the sheets meet, and the paper grain the light finds at a low angle.

<!-- more -->

This is the wager of dansaekhwa, the Korean monochrome movement of the 1970s: that the surface, worked patiently, is the subject. The painters of that generation spoke of *bang-beop* — method — over image. Repetition was a discipline, not a decoration.

Left almost bare, the plate becomes a measure for everything that follows. The five studies after it each add one gesture — ink, clay, graphite — and each is read against the memory of this empty ground. When the eye tires of the later plates, it returns here to rest. Nothing is depicted. The paper is simply allowed to be paper, and that turns out to be enough to hold the attention for a long, quiet while.
