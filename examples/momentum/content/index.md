Most notes apps ask you to make a decision before you've finished the thought: which notebook, which project, which of the six tags you set up in January. Momentum skips the decision. Open it, type, and the note exists — filed nowhere, because there is nowhere to file it to.

What replaces the folder is the feed. Every note carries a quiet timestamp for the last moment you actually engaged with it — wrote in it, reread it, linked something new to it — and the feed simply sorts by that. Reopen a call recap three days later to add a follow-up, and it jumps back to the top on its own. A grocery list from October sinks below everything until the next time you need one, at which point it resurfaces the instant you touch it. Nothing is ever deleted or archived; it just settles toward the bottom of a pile that behaves the way your attention actually moves.

The three motions below are the whole interface. There is no fourth screen where organizing happens, because organizing was never separate from thinking in the first place.
