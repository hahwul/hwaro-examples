Every notes app since the 1980s has borrowed the same object: the filing cabinet. Folders inside folders, a taxonomy you're supposed to design before you have anything worth organizing. It made sense when the metaphor was literal — an office worker moving paper into drawers someone had already labeled. It stopped making sense the moment note-taking became something you do mid-thought, standing up, between meetings, half-listening to a call.

## The filing decision is the wrong first question

Ask someone to write down an idea and they'll do it in seconds. Ask them to also decide which of nine folders it belongs in, and the seconds turn into a stall — a small tax charged at the exact moment they're least equipped to pay it, because they're mid-thought, not mid-filing. Most captured ideas that die, die right there, in the folder picker, not from lack of importance but from lack of patience for a decision that didn't need to happen yet.

## Location was always a proxy for something else

What a folder really encodes is "this matters to a project I care about right now." But *right now* is exactly the variable a static folder can't represent — a note about a house renovation matters intensely for three weeks and then not at all for a year, and no folder updates itself to reflect that. Momentum, the property, replaces location: instead of asking where a note belongs, we ask when you last found it worth your attention, and let that answer reorder itself continuously.

## Capture first, organize never

This is the part people find hardest to trust: Momentum has no inbox-zero ritual, no weekly folder cleanup, no tag hygiene. The claim is that none of it is necessary, because the feed already does the work that manual organizing used to do, and does it every time you touch a note instead of once a week on a schedule you'll eventually skip. A pile that reorders itself by real use turns out to need remarkably little tending.

## What we're not saying

We're not saying structure is bad — links between notes, the margin rail of connections, even an occasional pinned note are all structure, and Momentum keeps all of them. What we're against is *pre-structure*: the requirement to decide a note's permanent address before you've written the sentence that would tell you what it's about. Structure that emerges from use beats structure you had to guess at in advance, and a stack of note cards on a real desk has always known this — it's why the important ones end up on top.

If this argument is more convincing on a screen than in principle, the fastest way to test it is to [try the features](../features/) yourself for a week and see whether you miss the folder picker at all.
