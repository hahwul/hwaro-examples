Light does not travel in straight lines through curved spacetime. When a massive object sits between an observer and a more distant source, the gravitational field of the foreground object bends the light path. This is gravitational lensing, predicted by general relativity in 1915 and first confirmed during the 1919 solar eclipse.

## The Geometry of the Effect

The deflection angle scales with the mass of the lens and the impact parameter of the light ray. For a galaxy cluster with a mass of roughly 10^15 solar masses, deflection angles of tens of arcseconds are routine. The result is a distorted, magnified, and sometimes multiplied image of the background source.

Lensing produces several distinct configurations:

- Einstein rings appear when the source, lens, and observer align almost perfectly
- Arcs and arclets form when the alignment is partial
- Multiple images emerge when the lens potential has a sufficiently irregular shape
- Microlensing events reveal compact objects through transient brightening

## Observational Use

Hubble and JWST have used massive clusters such as Abell 2744 and SMACS 0723 as natural telescopes. Magnification factors of 10 to 50 are common, with some configurations exceeding 100. This allows imaging of galaxies at redshifts beyond what either instrument could resolve directly.

The technique trades field size for depth. The lensed region is small, the geometry is complex, and the background source must be reconstructed by inverting the lens model. The reward is access to objects from the first 500 million years after the Big Bang.

## Mass Mapping

Lensing is also a probe of the lens itself. By measuring the distortion of many background galaxies behind a cluster, astronomers reconstruct the projected mass distribution. These maps include dark matter, which is otherwise invisible. The agreement between weak lensing maps and X-ray gas distributions has been one of the strongest pieces of evidence for non-baryonic dark matter.

## Limits

The technique is limited by the small number of suitable lenses, the difficulty of modeling cluster mass, and the chromatic effects of intracluster gas. Surveys like Euclid and the Vera Rubin Observatory will identify thousands of new lensing systems, expanding the catalog faster than detailed follow-up can keep up.
