Saturn's rings extend from 7,000 km to 80,000 km above the planet's equator, yet their average thickness is only about 10 meters. If scaled to the diameter of a football field, the rings would be thinner than a razor blade.

## Ring Structure

The rings are divided into named sections, lettered in order of discovery:

| Ring | Distance (km) | Width (km) | Notes |
|------|--------------|-----------|-------|
| D | 66,900 - 74,510 | 7,500 | Faintest, innermost |
| C | 74,658 - 92,000 | 17,500 | Semi-transparent |
| B | 92,000 - 117,580 | 25,500 | Brightest, most massive |
| A | 122,170 - 136,775 | 14,600 | Contains Encke Gap |
| F | 140,180 | 30-500 | Shepherded by moons |

## Composition

Ring particles are 95% water ice with trace silicates and organic compounds. They range in size from micrometers to several meters in diameter, with the bulk of mass concentrated in particles between 1 cm and 5 m.

The ice composition was confirmed by spectroscopic analysis during Cassini's 13-year orbital mission. Near-infrared observations revealed crystalline ice signatures consistent with relatively recent formation or continuous resurfacing.

## Shepherd Moons

Several small moons maintain sharp ring edges through gravitational interaction:

- **Prometheus and Pandora** shepherd the F ring
- **Pan** clears the Encke Gap in the A ring
- **Daphnis** maintains the Keeler Gap

These moons create wave patterns in nearby ring material, producing structures visible in high-resolution imaging.

## Origin and Lifespan

Current models suggest Saturn's rings may be only 100-200 million years old -- far younger than the planet itself. They may have formed from the tidal disruption of a comet or moon that ventured too close.

The rings are slowly losing mass through micrometeorite bombardment and plasma drag, falling into Saturn as "ring rain." At current erosion rates, the rings may disappear entirely within 300 million years. We are fortunate to exist during the window when they are visible.
