Joule began, as these things do, with an oven that was too big for a house
and too small for a business. In 2019 we bought a decommissioned wood-fired
bread oven from a farm sale, hauled it into a former bicycle workshop on
Ferment Lane, and spent a winter learning to read its moods. It holds heat
like a stone cathedral and forgives almost nothing. We named the bakery
Joule, for the unit of heat, because in the end that is all bread is: flour,
water, time, and a carefully spent quantity of fire.

## Two bakers

There are two of us on the bench. One came from a decade of restaurant
pastry and cannot leave a canelé alone until its shell rings when tapped. The
other fell into bread through a sourdough starter that refused to die through
three house moves — it is still our mother culture, fed twice a day, older
now than the bakery itself.

## Why the mornings are short

People ask why we do not simply bake more. The honest answer is that our
oven and our arms have a limit, and the less honest but truer answer is that
we like the discipline of scarcity. Baking exactly one morning's worth means
every loaf leaves warm, nothing is discounted at dusk, and we are home by
early afternoon to build the next day's levain. It is not a growth strategy.
It is a way of living that happens to produce very good bread.

## What we believe

- Long, cold ferments make bread that is easier to digest and better to eat.
- A wood fire gives a crust that a steel deck simply cannot.
- Bread is cheap to make and priceless to share; we try to price it so that
  it stays a daily thing, not a luxury.

We are a fictional bakery, dreamed up for the Hwaro examples gallery — but if
Ferment Lane were real, this is exactly how we would run it.
