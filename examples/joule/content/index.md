There is a particular hour, somewhere around 5 a.m., when the bakehouse
smells like the inside of a warm loaf and the only sound is the tick of the
cooling oak. We have been up since midnight by then. The levain went into
the mix at one, the loaves were shaped by three, and the canelé batter has
been resting in the cold since yesterday afternoon, learning patience.

We bake in a single wood-fired hearth, which means we bake in sequence and
we bake by feel. The oven runs hottest right after the fire is raked, so the
boules go first and take the char. As it falls through the morning we slide
in the enriched doughs, then the rye, then finally the canelés in their
copper moulds when the deck has mellowed to a steady, forgiving heat.

Nothing here is made to keep. We mix what we can sell in a morning and we do
not make more. If you arrive at eleven and the shelves are bare, take it as
a compliment to the person ahead of you, and come a little earlier tomorrow.
The bread keeps baker's hours, and so, gladly, do we.
