Every year, roughly 1.5 million wildebeest set out on a circular journey across the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem, covering nearly 1,800 kilometers in search of rain-ripened grasses. This movement, often called the Great Migration, is the largest terrestrial migration on the planet. It is not a single event but a continuous cycle, driven by seasonal rains and the ancient instinct to follow water and forage.

The migration begins on the southern Serengeti plains, where calving season takes place between January and March. Hundreds of thousands of calves are born within a narrow window of weeks, a survival strategy that overwhelms predators through sheer numbers. The plains here are rich in phosphorus and calcium, nutrients essential for lactating mothers and growing young. Within minutes of birth, a wildebeest calf can stand; within days, it can keep pace with the herd.

As the dry season advances, the herds turn northwest toward the woodlands and eventually north toward the Maasai Mara in Kenya. The most perilous chapter of the journey unfolds at river crossings, where thousands of animals plunge into crocodile-inhabited waters. The Grumeti and Mara rivers claim hundreds of wildebeest each year, yet the columns keep pressing forward, driven by an imperative older than memory. Stragglers and the weak fall to lions, hyenas, and wild dogs that shadow the herds along the route.

By October and November, the short rains begin to fall on the southern Serengeti once more, and the herds wheel back toward the calving grounds to begin the cycle again. The Great Migration is not merely a spectacle of animal movement. It is a keystone ecological process, fertilizing grasslands, feeding predators and scavengers, and shaping the very landscape through the trampling and grazing of millions of hooves. To witness even a fragment of it is to observe one of nature's most profound rhythms.
