# Copper Patina: The Green Surface

While iron rusts orange, copper oxidizes green. The distinctive turquoise patina—copper carbonate formed by weathering—creates some of the most striking colors in industrial decay photography.

## Chemistry of Color

Copper patina forms through a complex process:

1. **Cuprite**: Initial brown/red copper(I) oxide
2. **Tenorite**: Black copper(II) oxide layer
3. **Brochantite**: Green copper sulfate from atmospheric pollution
4. **Malachite**: Bright green copper carbonate
5. **Azurite**: Blue copper carbonate (rare, but spectacular)

The result: surfaces that range from blue-green to turquoise to deep emerald.

## Where to Find It

We documented copper patina at:

- Abandoned electrical substations (copper bus bars and transformers)
- Old brewery equipment (copper brewing vessels)
- Roofing and architectural elements (if aged for 20+ years)
- Marine environments (accelerated copper corrosion)

> "Copper patina is self-limiting—it forms a protective layer that slows further corrosion. These surfaces can remain stable for decades."

## Visual Notes

The contrast is what makes patina photography work: green oxidation against dark metal, against rust-orange steel, against weathered wood. The patina itself has depth—crystalline in some areas, smooth in others, sometimes incorporating other minerals that create unexpected colors.

### Recommended Approach

Photograph in diffused light to avoid blown highlights on the bright green surfaces. The patina is often highly reflective. Look for areas where multiple oxidation colors appear together—green patina, orange rust, and bare metal creating natural color studies.
