The question we get asked most often, after the moonphase mechanism itself, is why three hundred specifically — not a rounder five hundred, not the more collectible hundred. The honest answer is capacity, not marketing. Casing, regulating, and inspecting a Perigee end to end takes one trained person roughly a day per watch once the parts exist, and we run two people on final assembly. Working a normal calendar with holidays and the inevitable week lost to a broken tool or a bad batch of crystals, that's somewhere between three hundred and three hundred and forty watches a year from two benches. We chose the lower, more repeatable number.

<!-- more -->

The alternative — the one every consultant we've talked to eventually suggests — is to add a third bench and a third trained caser, and push toward five hundred a year. We've turned that down twice. Training someone to case a Perigee to our tolerance takes about four months of supervised work before they're regulating unsupervised, and for most of that four months they're slower than the tolerance requires, which means a stretch where every watch from a new bench needs a second inspection from someone who's already fast. Scaling the workshop isn't free; it's borrowed time from the two people already here, spent making a third person as careful as they are. We're not against it forever. We're against doing it because a spreadsheet says three hundred should really be five hundred.

There's a second reason, smaller but real: closing a waitlist means something. When Run One's three hundred spots filled, we stopped taking names rather than keep a growing list for "maybe Run Two, maybe Run Three." Anyone who orders knows their watch is one of an actual, finished set — not an open edition that happens to pause between batches. That only works if three hundred is a hard number we've committed to, not a soft target we'll quietly stretch to three-fifty if demand runs hot. More on the workshop itself, and why we've kept it to five people, is on the [about](/about/) page.
