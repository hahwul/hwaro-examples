Run Two started this week with the least photogenic step in the whole process: bridge blanks. Before there's a movement to admire, there's a slab of German nickel silver clamped to the mill, and a program that has to cut three hundred sets of top plate, barrel bridge, and balance bridge without a single tool change drifting the tolerance on the jewel seats. We cut two sets at a time, which means a hundred and fifty cycles before the run's raw material even exists as recognizable watch parts.

<!-- more -->

We kept the exact program from Run One rather than revise it, on purpose. It would have been easy to shave the balance bridge's foot slightly thinner — we know now it could go 0.15mm slimmer without weakening it — but a change like that is exactly the kind of quiet drift we don't want between runs. If the balance bridge geometry changes, it changes with a name attached to the decision and a reason written down, not as an unannounced tweak buried in "Run Two, improved." For this run, improved meant faster turnaround on the waitlist and a slightly better silver source for the case stock, not a different movement architecture.

The bridges go from the mill to a deburring pass, then sit for two weeks before the first one gets jeweled — long enough for us to run a full inspection pass across the batch and pull anything with a visible tool mark before it's worth anyone's time to jewel it. Out of the first fifty blanks this week, three got pulled. That's a normal rate for us, and part of why three hundred pieces a run, rather than a thousand, stays the ceiling: inspecting fifty blanks by hand is a Tuesday morning; inspecting two hundred is a different job that wants a different kind of workshop.
