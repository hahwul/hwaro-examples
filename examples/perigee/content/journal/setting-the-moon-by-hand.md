The last thing that happens to a Perigee before it goes in its box is also the smallest: someone steps outside, looks up, and comes back in to turn a corrector pin until the aperture at six o'clock matches what's actually overhead that night. We could set every watch to a calculated phase from a table and let the 122-year drift handle accuracy from there — the math would hold up fine. We do it by eye instead, because a watch that leaves the building already agreeing with the real sky feels different to hand over than one that's merely calculated to be correct.

<!-- more -->

The corrector sits recessed at 4 o'clock, reachable with the same stylus we use for the date correction on other calibers, though the P-38 has no date to fight with — one less thing to keep synchronized, one less reason to ever pull the crown out to the wrong position. A full press-and-release advances the moon disc by one day; getting from a fresh movement's default position to tonight's actual phase can take anywhere from a single press to nearly a full lunar month of them, depending on when in the cycle a given watch happens to be finished. We keep a small paper calendar taped above the final-assembly bench for the current year rather than trust a phone, on the theory that a workshop tool shouldn't depend on a battery any more than the watch does.

On overcast nights, we use the almanac instead and note it in that watch's build record — not because the two methods give different answers, but because we'd rather be able to say honestly, watch by watch, whether the moon was actually visible the night its disc was set. It rarely matters to an owner years later. It matters to us the day the watch leaves. The mechanism behind why that setting holds for decades rather than months is covered in [Regulating the Moonphase Disc](/journal/regulating-the-moon/).
