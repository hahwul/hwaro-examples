Most people assume a mechanical moonphase is either right or decorative, with nothing in between. It's neither. The standard mechanism found in the majority of moonphase watches — ours included, until the P-38 — uses a 59-tooth wheel that advances one tooth every 24 hours, on the assumption that a lunar month is 29.5 days. The real synodic month, moon to moon, is 29.53059 days. That 0.03-day gap sounds trivial until you let it compound: a 59-tooth mechanism drifts a full day out of sync with the actual sky roughly every two and a half years. Most owners never notice, because most owners never check the moon against the watch on a clear night. Some do.

<!-- more -->

The P-38's moon-works use a 135-tooth wheel instead, advanced by a finger cam rather than a simple pawl, timed off the same 24-hour drive as the hour wheel. A 135-tooth wheel can represent the lunar month to a finer resolution than a 59-tooth one — the mechanical equivalent of a longer decimal expansion — and the drift falls to roughly one day per 122 years. That number gets repeated often enough in watch marketing that it's worth being specific about what it actually buys you: not perfection, just a mechanism accurate enough that a Perigee sold today will still show the correct phase to whoever inherits it decades from now, without a single correction.

Every moon disc gets checked twice before a movement is cased. First on the bench, against a printed lunar calendar for the current year, turned by hand to the correct phase and left running for four days to confirm the finger cam is indexing cleanly with no hesitation or double-advance. Second at final assembly, set again against that day's actual moon, because a movement can sit in a drawer between the first check and casing for anywhere from a week to two months. The second check is the one that actually matters — it's the phase the watch leaves the building with.
