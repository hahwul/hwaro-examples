Perigee is a small watch by current standards, and that is the point. Thirty-eight millimeters across, ten and a half thick, on a 44 millimeter lug-to-lug that sits close on most wrists rather than overhanging them. The case is cut from bar stock in fine silver, then oxidized by hand in small batches — a controlled tarnish, not a plating — so the case reads a near-black warm grey rather than the bright white of polished silver. No two batches oxidize quite the same, which is disclosed rather than hidden: your case will darken further over years of handling, the way a well-used silver spoon does, and we consider that part of owning one rather than a defect to prevent.

## Dial and hands

The dial is a single oxidized-silver disc, sunk slightly at the center and turned on the same small lathe we use for the case backs, so the concentric tool marks catch light at an angle rather than lying flat and dead. Twelve applied indices in polished silver mark the hours; the hour and minute hands are gilt brass, warmed to a champagne gold that reads as the one bright note on an otherwise quiet face. At six o'clock, a small arched aperture opens onto the moonphase disc — a hand-painted star field with the moon's current phase showing through a window cut to the exact width of a full moon, so a true full moon fills the aperture edge to edge rather than floating inside it with visible margin.

## Moonphase accuracy

Most mechanical moonphase watches use a 59-tooth wheel advanced one tooth a day, which tracks a 29.5-day lunar month against the real 29.53059-day synodic month and drifts a full day roughly every two and a half years — close enough for a dress complication, wrong often enough that a careful owner notices. Our moon-works use a 135-tooth wheel instead, advanced by a finger cam timed to the same 24-hour drive that turns the hour wheel. The extra teeth buy a finer approximation of the true lunar month: the mechanism drifts one full day only after roughly one hundred and twenty-two years, which in practice means a Perigee sold today will still show the correct moon phase to anyone who inherits it. The full reasoning behind the 135-tooth wheel, and how we check it before a movement is cased, is in [Regulating the Moonphase Disc](/journal/regulating-the-moon/).

## Case back and finishing

Every case back is a sapphire display window, engraved around its edge with the caliber name and the watch's individual number and total — <em>No. 138 of 300</em>, in this run — so the number is part of the object rather than a card that gets lost in a drawer. Water resistance is rated to 30 meters: enough for rain and hand-washing, not intended for swimming or diving. The strap is hand-stitched calf leather on quick-release spring bars, so it comes off without tools if you want to put the watch on a different strap for an evening. The complete measurements for all of the above are laid out on the [specifications](/specifications/) page.
