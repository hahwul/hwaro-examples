Murmur is produced aboard whichever research vessel will have us. Most dispatches come from the RV *Kelvin Rift*, a converted ice-class trawler retrofitted for the Hadal Research Consortium's trench survey program, but we ride along with partner ships when their itinerary crosses ours. What stays constant is the format: one expedition, one dive, one conversation recorded somewhere between the moon pool and the mess hall.

## Who's aboard

Chief scientist **Dr. Imogen Vasterling** anchors most episodes — a hadal ecologist who has spent the better part of two decades arguing that the trenches are not empty, just quiet in a register we haven't learned to listen in. Field producer **Teo Rin** runs the hydrophones, the satellite uplink, and the tape recorder that has outlived three laptops. Guest scientists join for the dives in their specialty; you'll meet a few of them in the dispatch notes.

## What the depth gauge means

Every dispatch on this site carries a vertical gauge alongside its title — a ruled instrument face marking exactly how far down the reported dive went, set against the full water column from the sunlit surface to full ocean depth at 10,935 meters, the bottom of Challenger Deep. The band lit up in every one of our gauges is the hadal zone: below 6,000 meters, where pressure runs past 1,000 atmospheres and the only light left is what the animals make themselves. We built the gauge because a number like "8,376 meters" doesn't mean much until you see how little of the column is left beneath it.

## How we record down there

Nothing that goes over 6,000 meters can carry a live microphone — pressure housings for that depth are heavy, expensive, and mostly reserved for cameras and CTD sensors. Instead we mount hydrophones and data loggers on the landers and, for the deepest dives, on the submersible's external frame, then recover the hardware, pull the files, and build each episode around what came back: ambient trench acoustics, telemetry chatter, and whatever narration we managed to record on the transit down or the transit back up, when everyone still has the dive fresh enough to talk about it honestly.

## Getting in touch

Corrections, pitches for a trench we haven't covered yet, or questions about a specific dive: write to the production line and someone will read it between expeditions, which is to say not quickly, but for real. In the meantime, the full [dispatch archive](/episodes/) and the [tag index](/tags/) are the fastest way to find a specific trench, species, or vessel.
