Sirena Deep sits about ninety kilometers northeast of Challenger Deep, in the same trench, at nearly the same depth, and Dr. Vasterling has been coming back to this specific patch of ooze floor for three field seasons because of what lives on it: xenophyophores, single-celled organisms that build shells up to ten centimeters across out of sediment, mineral grains, and their own waste.

<!-- more -->

## One cell, ten centimeters

A xenophyophore is, structurally, a single giant cell with thousands of nuclei distributed through a branching internal network, encased in an agglutinated shell called a test. At Sirena Deep, Vasterling's team has photographed tests the size of a clenched fist — which, for an organism with no organs, no nervous system, and no known predator-avoidance behavior, is a genuinely strange amount of structure to build.

## Why the ooze floor matters

Sediment at 10,600 meters accumulates at a rate of roughly a centimeter per thousand years, which means the mineral grains a xenophyophore incorporates into its test can be extraordinarily old — some tests examined from comparable depths elsewhere have tested positive for radioactive isotopes consistent with mid-twentieth-century nuclear testing, embedded in layers that are otherwise geologically ancient. Vasterling calls xenophyophores "unintentional archivists," recording surface-world history in trench mud that nothing else disturbs.

## The ecosystem engineer argument

Wherever xenophyophore tests cluster densely, Vasterling's cameras also find elevated numbers of small crustaceans, nematodes, and juvenile invertebrates using the structures as shelter and feeding ground — enough of a pattern that she now argues xenophyophores function as ecosystem engineers for the hadal ooze floor, the trench equivalent of a coral head, built by an organism most people have never heard of and would walk past if it were sitting on a shelf.

## What's still unexplained

Nobody has a confirmed answer for why the tests take the specific branching, fractal-like shapes they do, or whether the shape serves a function beyond structural support — filter feeding efficiency, current resistance, and simple biological accident have all been proposed. Vasterling's working hypothesis, which she is careful to flag as unconfirmed, is that test architecture optimizes the internal cytoplasmic network for nutrient transport across a cell with no circulatory system to move anything for it.
