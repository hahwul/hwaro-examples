Murmur's first season opens the way most hadal seasons do: not with a dive, but with forty straight hours of the ship running transect lines while an EM124 multibeam array pings the seafloor eight kilometers down and waits, sometimes eleven seconds at a time, for the echo to come back.

<!-- more -->

## The argument before the transect

Dr. Vasterling wanted to run the lines east to west, following the trench axis; the ship's survey lead wanted north to south, citing better swath overlap given the current heading. Both were right in a narrow, expensive way, and the compromise — a staggered diagonal grid — added six hours to the survey but produced the cleanest bathymetric model the Consortium has of this stretch of the Atacama Trench to date. The recording picks up mid-argument, in the wet lab, over the sound of the printer that nobody could get to stop feeding out contour lines.

## What eight kilometers of water does to sound

The multibeam pulse leaves the hull at roughly 1,500 meters per second and slows as it drops through colder, denser water. By the time it reaches bottom and returns, the ship has drifted enough that the sonar has to correct for its own motion, continuously, on every ping. Vasterling narrates the process in real time for about six minutes of this episode, which is, honestly, the most patient anyone on this podcast has ever been about explaining an acronym.

## Depth of record

Deepest sounding on this transect: 8,065 meters, in a narrow axial channel the crew nicknamed "the letterbox" for how it shows up on the display. That number becomes the baseline every later Atacama dive on this show gets measured against.

## What's next

The bathymetric model from this survey is what the season's lander deployments were plotted against — you'll hear those results in later dispatches, once the gear that actually touches bottom starts coming back up with data on it.
