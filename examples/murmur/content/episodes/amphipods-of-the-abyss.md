This dispatch comes from a partner ship: the RV *Auroral Drift*, on loan from a collaborating institute for a three-week Tonga Trench campaign led by crustacean biologist Dr. Mireille Kwan. Murmur rides along for one leg, at Horizon Deep, the second-deepest point on Earth after Challenger Deep itself.

<!-- more -->

## The bait experiment

Kwan's landers carry mackerel bait and a time-lapse camera, and the strategy is deliberately crude: drop, wait twelve hours, recover, count. At 10,882 meters, this deployment attracted an estimated four hundred to six hundred amphipods within the camera's field of view inside the first three hours — a swarming response so fast and so dense that Kwan, on tape, briefly wonders aloud whether the camera has malfunctioned.

## Supergiants

Most hadal amphipods run one to three centimeters, but a subset recovered in the lander's collection trap on this dive measured over twenty centimeters — a phenomenon researchers call hadal gigantism, in which certain crustacean lineages grow far larger at extreme depth and pressure than their shallow-water relatives, for reasons still actively debated. Kwan's leading hypothesis ties it to the combination of near-freezing temperatures, elevated dissolved oxygen at this depth, and a near-total absence of the size-limiting predators that keep shallow-water amphipods small.

## Not "opportunistic" — obligate

A recurring point Kwan makes on this dispatch: the hadal amphipods swarming her bait aren't simply generalists that happen to live deep. Genetic work across multiple trenches suggests many hadal amphipod lineages are trench-specific, isolated from their neighbors in adjacent trenches by the abyssal plains between them, which are too shallow in pressure terms — counterintuitively — for a hadal-adapted animal to cross.

## The number that stuck with the crew

Horizon Deep tops out at 10,882 meters, just fifty-three meters short of Challenger Deep's floor. Kwan puts it plainly near the end of the recording: "We keep calling this the second-deepest place on the planet, like fifty-three meters is a consolation prize. Down here, it isn't. Down here, it's the whole world."
