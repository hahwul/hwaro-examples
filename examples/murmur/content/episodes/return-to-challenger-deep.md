Eighteen months and six dispatches after [Murmur's first transect line went into the Atacama Trench](/episodes/mapping-the-atacama-trench/), this one goes all the way to the bottom of the water column itself. Dr. Vasterling and submersible pilot Otieno Bramble spend just over three hours on the floor of Challenger Deep, in the three-person submersible *Limen*, with a recorder running for almost all of it.

<!-- more -->

## Full ocean depth

10,935 meters is, by current consensus measurement, as deep as the ocean gets anywhere on the planet — a figure this show has used as the ceiling on every depth gauge since episode one, without ever actually reaching it until now. Bramble narrates the descent in flat, procedural calm: ballast checks at 1,000 meters, thermocline at 1,200, last trace of daylight gone by 1,000 exactly, on schedule, same as always.

## Three hours on the bottom

*Limen* touches down in a wide, pale sediment plain inside Challenger Deep's western pool at just under four hours into the dive. What Vasterling narrates from there is closer to a field notebook read aloud than a rehearsed segment: a holothurian crossing the external light's beam almost immediately, a cluster of what she tentatively calls xenophyophore tests smaller than the ones documented at [Sirena Deep two trenches and one season ago](/episodes/xenophyophores-and-the-ooze-floor/), and, nine minutes before ascent, an amphipod swarm on the bait canister that Bramble describes, on tape, as "the exact same commotion as every other trench, just further down than anyone's filmed it before."

## The gauge reads full

For the first time on this show, the depth gauge on a dispatch reads the full scale — no headroom left beneath the marker, no water column left to compare against. Vasterling addresses this directly near the end of the recording: "Every gauge on every episode we've made has had room underneath it. This one doesn't. I'm not sure what to do with that, honestly, except tell you it happened."

## What comes after full depth

Murmur isn't ending at the bottom of the ocean — there are trenches this show hasn't reported from yet, and depth was never really the point of the gauge to begin with, just the clearest number we had for showing how far a dispatch had to travel to reach you. The next expedition is still being scheduled. When it's confirmed, you'll hear about it here first, same as always.
