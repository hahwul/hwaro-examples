Sastrugi are the wind-carved ridges that form on old snow: hard, asymmetric, and pointing the same direction as the prevailing wind, which makes them a useful compass if you forget to bring one. The council that split from the parent station's admin block asked for a flag that read as a landmark rather than a badge, something a field party could use to check their bearing at a glance.

<!-- more -->

The field does the reading: charcoal above, glacier white below, split low so the pale band reads as exposed snow catching the light. The ridge line is the only charge, and it is the only place on the plate a straightedge never touches — I traced it from a photograph of the actual formation the station is named for, then simplified until it held at flagpole distance. Ember orange was chosen for one reason: it is the same pigment used on the station's crevasse-marker flags, so a Sastrugi flag and a hazard flag read as kin from across the plateau, which the council decided was a feature rather than a conflict.

Construction is deliberately plain. Two flat fields, one stroke, a 2:3 ratio that matches the spare flagpoles already racked at the station stores — no new hardware, no new supply request. The provisional council ratified it on the second reading, the fastest approval in the studio's file.
