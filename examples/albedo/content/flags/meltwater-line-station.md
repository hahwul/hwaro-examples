The station sits at a glacier terminus the survey team has been core-sampling for three consecutive seasons, and the flag they asked for is, structurally, a core sample turned on its side.

<!-- more -->

Five bands, each the same height, graduate from pale grey-blue at the top to near-black at the bottom — the exact sequence a compression core pulls up from recent snow through firn into old, dense ice. I resisted making the gradation continuous, which would have read as decorative shading rather than data; five discrete steps keep the flag legible as a chart, not a sunset. The palette was pulled directly from the team's own stratigraphy photographs rather than from the studio's usual polar set, which is why this is the one plate in the catalog that shares no exact hex value with any other.

The small teal pennant pinned near the hoist is the one charge, and it is doing double duty: on the flag it marks the survey line the way the team's actual field pennants mark a bore hole, and in the construction sheet it is the only shape drafted off-grid, angled four degrees from true to match the lean of the real pennants in wind. Adopted on the shortest day of the season the team was on site, which the station log records but the flag itself does not.
