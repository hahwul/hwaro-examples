The authority's entire founding document is a single fixed point: the exact meridian the original survey team pinned before instruments drifted and every later measurement had to be corrected against it. The flag is that document redrawn as geometry.

<!-- more -->

Two lines, one vertical and one horizontal, cross at the fix point and run almost to the edges of the field — almost, not quite, because a line that touches the border reads as a border and a line that stops short reads as a mark. The gap is four percent of the hoist on every side, tested at three sizes before the council signed off. The diamond at the intersection is the only filled shape on the plate, and it is teal rather than the charcoal of the lines themselves, so the eye finds the fix point before it finds the grid that locates it.

I resisted the urge to add a frost pattern radiating from the center, which was the first-round brief. Restraint won the argument: a lattice would have made a precise survey mark look decorative, and decorative is the one thing a meridian authority cannot afford to be. What shipped is the plainest flag in the catalog, and the authority's council told me afterward that plainness was the entire point — a flag that argues with itself is a flag nobody trusts to mark true north.
