Solve enough puzzles and you start to notice the regulars — the small words that show up far more often in crosswords than in life. A river in Italy you have never visited. A three-letter Egyptian sun god. A type of antelope spelled with suspiciously convenient letters. This is **crosswordese**: the working vocabulary of the grid, beloved by constructors and resented by solvers in roughly equal measure.

It is easy to sneer at crosswordese. It is harder to build a grid without it.

---

## Why the Grid Demands It

The problem is structural. English is lopsided. Common letters like S, T, R, and N pile up; vowels are precious; words that *end* in a vowel and *start* with a consonant are gold. When a constructor paints themselves into a corner, the only escape is often a short word with a forgiving letter pattern.

Consider a corner that needs a three-letter word matching `_R_`, crossing two other answers. Your realistic options:

- ARE
- ERA
- ORE
- ERE
- ARM

Notice how many are vowel-consonant-vowel or built from the most common letters. That is not an accident. **Crosswordese survives because its spelling is useful, not because anyone uses the words.**

---

## The Hall of Fame

A partial roster of the entries every regular solver knows on sight:

| Entry | Clue you will see |
| --- | --- |
| `ESNE` | Anglo-Saxon laborer |
| `ETUI` | Small case for needles |
| `OLEO` | Margarine, quaintly |
| `ANOA` | Wild ox of Celebes |
| `EWER` | Pitcher with a wide spout |
| ` ALEE ` | Away from the wind |
| `ORLE` | Heraldic border |

Read that list aloud and you will hear the pattern: alternating vowels, soft consonants, nothing that clusters. ETUI is four letters with three vowels. ANOA is four letters with three vowels *and* a symmetric shape. These words are not chosen for meaning. They are chosen for **letter chemistry**.

> Crosswordese is the duct tape of construction. Nobody admires it. Everybody owns a roll. The skill is using as little as the job requires.

---

## The Ethics of the Easy Out

Here is the working constructor's dilemma. You have a beautiful theme, a clean grid, and one stubborn corner. You can either:

1. Rip out four crossing words, rebuild the corner from scratch, and lose an hour — or
2. Drop in `ESNE` and move on.

The honest answer is *it depends on the day*. A Monday puzzle aimed at new solvers should never make someone guess an Anglo-Saxon laborer. A Friday themeless, built for veterans who *enjoy* the deep cuts, can afford one. Knowing your solver is half the craft.

Good editors enforce this. The modern trend, led by a generation of constructors who grew up resenting OLEO, is to minimize crosswordese aggressively — to treat every `ETUI` as a small failure rather than a clever save. The grids are fresher for it. They are also much harder to build.

---

## A Glossary in Miniature

If you are new, memorize these and you will gain a full day of solving speed:

- **ERA, ERE, ORE** — the vowel-rich three-letter backbone
- **ALEE, ANEW, ALOE** — the four-letter vowel sandwiches
- **NENE** — the Hawaiian goose, a constructor's best friend
- **ASEA, ELHI, SMEE** — the ones that feel made up but are not

You will not love them. But the next time a corner resolves to `OREO`'s less delicious cousin `OLEO`, you will at least understand *why the grid asked*. The constructor was not being lazy. The constructor was being cornered — and these little words were the only door out.
