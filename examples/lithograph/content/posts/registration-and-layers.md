Color lithography demands a separate stone for every color in the final image. The challenge is registration: ensuring that each stone, inked and pulled in sequence, lands on the paper in exactly the right place. A drift of half a millimeter is enough to soften an edge or push a highlight out of a face.

<!-- more -->

### The Pin System

Most workshops use a two-pin registration. Two needles are pushed through the paper at fixed points along one edge, and matching holes are drilled into a backing board on the press bed. Every sheet, on every pull, sits on the same pins. The stones themselves do not move; the paper does.

When a print requires four or five colors, the order matters. Lighter, transparent inks are usually pulled first, with denser opaques layered on top. A yellow that runs second over a pale gray will read very differently than the same yellow pulled last over a finished black line.

### Proofing the Stack

Before committing to an edition, printers pull a sequence of proofs. Each new stone is registered and inked, and the result is compared against the previous proof. Adjustments to the drawing are made directly on the stone, with a needle or a sharp blade for subtraction, and a greasy crayon for additions.

The proofing stack is itself a record. It tells you, in order, how the image was built: a flat ground, a midtone, a structural line, a dark accent. Reading the stack backwards is one of the few ways to reverse-engineer another printer's working method.

A clean registration is invisible. The viewer sees a single image, not the four or five stones that made it.
