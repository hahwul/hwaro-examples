A painted shadow has to obey the same geometry as a real one or the deception fails. The light source is fixed at the moment the panel is composed, and every object on the surface inherits that direction. Inconsistency is the most common reason a still life reads as paint rather than as object.

<!-- more -->

Begin by sketching the imaginary light cone on a separate sheet. Mark the angle in degrees from vertical and from the picture plane. Every cast shadow on the panel will be drawn by projecting from each object's silhouette along that fixed vector until the shadow meets the next surface. Where the shadow crosses an edge, it bends sharply. Where it falls on a curved form, it stretches.

### Edge Hardness

Shadow edges are not uniform. A sharp edge sits directly against the object that casts it. A few centimeters away, the edge softens because the light source has measurable width. By the far end of a long shadow, the boundary is almost a gradient, though it remains darker at the core.

| Distance from object | Edge | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Contact | Crisp | Darkest |
| Mid | Slightly diffuse | Mid |
| Far end | Soft | Lightest |

Painters who skip the gradation produce shadows that read as cutouts. The viewer's eye registers the error before it can name it, and the panel loses its claim to being a window.
