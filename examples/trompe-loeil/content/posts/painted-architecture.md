Trompe l'oeil ceiling work depends on a single fixed viewpoint. Once the painter commits to a vantage, every cornice, balustrade, and figure must be foreshortened around that exact line of sight. Andrea Pozzo formalized this in his treatise on perspective for painters and architects, working out how to extend a real entablature into an apparent dome that never existed.

<!-- more -->

The technique relies on three constraints. First, the architecture in the room must continue uninterrupted into the painted zone, so a real pilaster reads as the support for a fictive arch. Second, light direction has to match the actual windows. A painted column lit from the wrong side reveals itself the moment a viewer steps under it. Third, atmospheric perspective compresses the highest tiers, since detail the eye cannot resolve at distance must not be supplied at close range either.

### Working from a Plumb Line

Most surviving studies show a grid dropped from the chosen viewpoint to the ceiling plane. The painter marks intersections on the plaster, then transfers cartoons square by square. Errors of a few centimeters at the apex translate to meters of distortion when read from the floor.

```text
viewpoint -> plumb -> grid -> cartoon -> wet plaster
```

The reward for this discipline is brief. Move five steps off the marked spot and the whole construction collapses into a flat, awkward map. That collapse is part of the work. The painting only exists where the viewer is told to stand.
