Stepped outside before dawn this morning and the grass crunched underfoot. The first hard frost of the season had arrived overnight, painting every surface in a thin white veil. The water trough near the barn had a sheet of ice across its surface, and the fence posts were rimmed with crystals that caught the early light.

We had been watching the forecasts for days, knowing it was only a matter of time. The root vegetables still in the ground -- carrots, parsnips, a few stubborn turnips -- should be fine beneath their mulch blankets. The real concern was the last of the late greens in the cold frame. I checked them at first light and found them wilted but alive, the row cover having done its work through the night.

By midmorning the frost had burned off under a pale winter sun and the fields returned to their usual muddy brown. The cattle seemed unbothered, standing in their usual spots along the south-facing hillside. There is something clarifying about the first frost. It draws a firm line under autumn and signals that the quiet, inward season has truly begun. The land rests now, and we rest with it, turning our attention to mending fences, sharpening tools, and planning for spring.
