The soil temperature finally crossed the threshold this week, and we wasted no time getting seeds into the ground. Peas went in first along the eastern fence line, followed by rows of spinach and radishes in the kitchen garden. The earth was dark and crumbly in our hands, still cool but workable -- exactly the condition you hope for after a long winter.

The old almanac on the shelf above the woodstove suggested planting by the waxing moon, and whether by coincidence or tradition, the timing felt right. Robins were working the freshly turned rows almost before we had finished, pulling earthworms from the loose soil. The air smelled of damp earth and cut grass, and there was a warmth to the afternoon sun that had been absent for months.

We marked each row with hand-lettered stakes cut from scrap cedar. The potatoes will go in next week once the ground warms another few degrees, and the tomato seedlings on the windowsill are nearly ready for hardening off. There is a particular satisfaction in these early days of planting -- the year stretches out ahead, full of possibility, and every seed dropped into its furrow is an act of faith in the season to come.
