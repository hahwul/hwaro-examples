The barometer had been falling since yesterday afternoon, and by early evening the western horizon was a wall of bruised gray. The wind shifted from south to west and picked up steadily, bending the tall grass in the hay meadow flat against the ground. We secured the barn doors, brought the chickens in early, and checked the tie-downs on the equipment shed roof.

The storm broke just after eight o'clock. Lightning split the sky over the ridge and the thunder that followed rattled the windows in their frames. Rain came down in sheets, hammering the tin roof of the barn so loud you had to shout to be heard inside. The creek at the bottom of the south pasture rose fast, turning from a clear trickle to a brown torrent within the hour. We watched from the porch as the gutters overflowed and the yard became a shallow lake.

By midnight the worst had passed and the rain settled into a steady, gentle fall. Morning revealed the damage: a large branch down across the lane, the corn in the west field leaning but not flattened, and the rain gauge showing just over three inches. The air was clean and cool, washed of the oppressive humidity that had built all week. The garden drank deep, and the pastures, parched from two weeks of dry weather, were already greening up by afternoon. Hard as they are, these summer storms bring what the land needs most.
