For most of human history, the corona was visible only during total solar eclipses. That thin ring of pearly light surrounding the black disk of the moon was a mystery -- sometimes feared, sometimes worshipped, always awe-inspiring. Today we understand it as the sun's outer atmosphere, but understanding has not diminished its beauty.

## A Million Degrees

The corona is paradoxically far hotter than the sun's visible surface. While the photosphere sits at roughly 5,500 degrees Celsius, the corona reaches temperatures of one to three million degrees. This remains one of the great unsolved problems in solar physics, known as the coronal heating problem.

Several mechanisms have been proposed:

- **Magnetic reconnection** -- field lines snap and release enormous energy
- **Alfven waves** -- magnetic oscillations carry energy outward
- **Nanoflares** -- countless tiny explosions too small to resolve individually

No single explanation has proven sufficient. The corona keeps its secrets.

## Structure and Shape

The corona's appearance changes dramatically over the eleven-year solar cycle:

During **solar minimum**, the corona is elongated along the equator, with delicate streamers extending several solar radii into space. Polar regions show short, fine brush-like structures called polar plumes.

During **solar maximum**, the corona is roughly symmetrical, with streamers and loops extending in all directions. It appears fuller, more chaotic, and more energetic.

```
Solar Cycle Phase     Corona Shape
--------------------  ---------------------------
Minimum               Equatorial streamers, polar plumes
Rising                Asymmetric, growing complexity
Maximum               Full, symmetrical, active
Declining             Returning asymmetry
```

## Observing the Corona

During totality, the corona is roughly as bright as the full moon. It is safe to observe with the naked eye -- in fact, binoculars reveal remarkable detail in the streamer structure. Look for:

- **Helmet streamers**: broad, pointed structures that taper outward
- **Polar rays**: fine lines radiating from the solar poles
- **Prominences**: red-pink loops of plasma at the lunar limb
- **Coronal holes**: darker regions where the magnetic field opens to space

## The Coronagraph

Outside of eclipses, the corona can be observed using a coronagraph -- an instrument that creates an artificial eclipse by blocking the solar disk with an opaque disk called an occulting disk. Space-based coronagraphs aboard missions like SOHO and STEREO have provided continuous monitoring since the 1990s.

However, no coronagraph perfectly replicates the view during totality. The inner corona, closest to the sun's limb, is best observed during a real eclipse. This is why solar physicists still mount expeditions to eclipse paths, just as they did a century ago.

## Writing in Light

The word "corona" comes from the Latin for crown, and indeed the sight resembles a crown of light placed upon the dark circle of the moon. It is, in a sense, the sun's signature -- written not in ink but in superheated plasma, visible only when everything else is hidden.
