This glossary compiles essential terminology used throughout this monograph, designed to aid the heritage orchardist in navigating classic botanical and agricultural texts.

### Botanical & Husbandry Terms

- **Scion**: A young shoot or bud from a parent tree that is grafted onto a rootstock. The scion determines the variety of the fruit (e.g., Cox's Orange Pippin or Golden Russet) that the tree will bear.
- **Rootstock**: The root system and lower stem of a tree onto which a scion is grafted. Rootstocks are selected for their vigor, size-controlling characteristics (dwarfing vs. seedling), and resistance to soil-borne pathogens.
- **Russeting**: A rough, brownish, or golden skin texture found on certain heirloom varieties, caused by corky tissue development. Often prized for adding deep nutty and pear-like nuances to dessert fruits.
- **Pomology**: The branch of botany and horticulture that studies the cultivation, physiology, and classification of pome fruits, particularly apples and pears.
- **Terroir**: The complete natural environment in which a particular orchard grows, including factors such as soil composition, climate, exposure, and water retention, all of which influence the unique flavor profile of the fruit.

### Cider & Chemistry Terms

- **Tannins**: Polyphenolic compounds derived from the skin and seeds of the fruit. Tannins provide astringency and structure, essential for giving traditional cider body and aging potential.
- **Pome**: A type of fruit produced by flowering plants in the subtribe Malinae of the family Rosaceae, characterized by a central core containing seeds surrounded by a fleshy receptacle.
