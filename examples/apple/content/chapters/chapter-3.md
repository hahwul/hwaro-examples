A heritage orchard is defined by its varieties. Unlike modern commercial plantings that rely on a handful of sweet, thin-skinned cultivars, the heirloom orchard celebrates a vast diversity of flavors, textures, and utilities.
<!-- more -->

Dessert apples, intended to be eaten fresh, represent some of the highest achievements in pomology. The Cox's Orange Pippin, introduced in England in 1825, remains the benchmark for aromatic complexity, offering a unique blend of sweet, pear-like flavors and sharp acidity. For winter storage, the Golden Russet is unmatched; its thick, sandpaper-like skin protects the dense, sugary flesh from shriveling, keeping it sweet well into spring.

We can represent the profiles of these varieties using a structured configuration block, which can be parsed by orchard catalog systems:

```yaml
varieties:
  - name: "Cox's Orange Pippin"
    type: "Dessert"
    origin: "Buckinghamshire, England"
    acid: "High"
    sugar: "Medium-High"
  - name: "Golden Russet"
    type: "Dessert / Cider"
    origin: "New York, USA"
    acid: "Medium"
    sugar: "Very High"
```

For culinary purposes, the Bramley's Seedling reigns supreme. Its high malic acid content causes it to cook down into a fluffy, translucent puree. In the cider house, traditional bittersweet varieties like Yarlington Mill and Kingston Black provide the necessary tannin and acid profile to create balanced, complex fermentations.
