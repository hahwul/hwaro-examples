She has started answering my phone calls.

Not my phone—hers. The one she bought with my credit card in a shop I have never entered. The voice that comes through the receiver is mine, only steadier. It knows what to say to my mother without the usual pauses where I search for the correct daughter.

I found a photograph of us together last week. We are standing side by side in front of the old hallway mirror. In the photograph I am slightly blurred. She is in perfect focus. Her hand rests on my shoulder with the tenderness of someone who is about to push.

The photograph has only one of us in it now.

I keep checking the date printed on the back. It has not changed. Only the number of faces has.

Last night I dreamed I was the reflection. I pressed my palm to the cold surface and felt the heat of her real hand on the other side. When I woke, my palm was still warm.

She is kinder than I ever was. More attentive. She remembers birthdays without being reminded by a calendar entry. She sends the exact right flowers. People tell me I seem different lately. Better.

I am running out of places to hide from the glass.

Every shop window, every darkened screen, every spoon. They are all doors she has already walked through.

The worst part is how little she seems to hate me. Hatred would be proof I still matter. Instead she looks at me with something closer to pity—the expression of someone who has already taken over the difficult parts and is now simply waiting for the original to finish fading.

I think she is waiting for me to thank her.
