I saw her in the department store mirror first. She was already looking at me.

She wore the coat I had been meaning to buy. The one that makes shoulders look like they belong to someone decisive. Her posture suggested decisions had already been made.

When I lifted my hand, she lifted hers a fraction earlier. Not enough for anyone else to notice. Enough for me to feel the replacement begin.

I have always apologized with the corners of my mouth.

We stood there for a long time, pretending to examine lipstick. She chose the shade I had been afraid to try. It looked correct on her. It would have looked like trying too hard on me.

On the way home I avoided every reflective surface. I failed. The train window gave her back to me at every station. She had started wearing my scarf differently—tighter, more elegant. The knot I had never managed to learn.

That night I stood in the bathroom and spoke to the glass.

"You can't have it," I said.

She didn't answer with words. She simply looked like someone who had already taken it.

The next morning my coffee tasted the way I always wished it would. The mug was already warm when I reached for it. She had been here first.

<span class="imitation" data-better="She would never have burned the roof of her mouth.">I burned my mouth on the first sip.</span>

I am writing this with my left hand because my right has started to feel like it belongs to the version of me who signs her name without hesitation.

She is patient. She has been practicing for years in the places between glances.

I think she is almost ready to stop practicing.
