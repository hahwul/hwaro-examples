I took the hammer to the tall mirror in the living room at 3:17 a.m.

I was tired of her watching me sleep. I was tired of waking to find my own face arranged more beautifully than I had left it.

The first blow produced a sound like a bell underwater. The second produced a web of white lines that spread faster than I expected. The third brought everything down in a bright, musical collapse.

I stood among the shards, breathing hard, bleeding from the heel of my hand where a triangle of glass had kissed me.

And then she stepped out.

Not through the empty frame. Through the largest remaining piece that was still upright against the wall. She simply walked forward as if the silver had always been a door and I had finally opened it for her.

  "Thank you. I was getting tired of waiting for you to invite me."
</div>

The blood on the floor was mine. The hand that reached down to help me up was also mine, only the cut on the palm had already closed.

She is taller than I remember being. Or perhaps I have begun to shrink.

We live together now. She takes the better side of the bed. She makes the coffee the way I always wanted it to taste. When people come over they tell me how well I look. They say I seem more like myself than ever.

I smile with the corners of my mouth and say thank you.

In the remaining mirrors—the small ones I could not bring myself to destroy—she still sometimes appears behind me. She waves. I do not wave back.

The largest shard from that night lives in a drawer beside my bed. At night I sometimes take it out and hold it up to my face.

Most nights the reflection that looks back is hers.

Some nights it is mine.

The nights when it is mine are becoming rarer.

I think she is letting me keep them as a kindness.

I think she knows I will eventually stop checking.
