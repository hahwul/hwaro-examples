The process is called silvering.

It begins at the edges. The reflective coating thickens, creeps inward. What was once a clear window into reversed space becomes a solid, beautiful wall.

The mirror in my bedroom has been silvering for eleven months.

At first I could still see through in the center if I stood very close and the light was right. Now the center has clouded to the color of old pearls. Only the very middle still shows anything, and what it shows is never quite me.

<span class="imitation" data-better="She never stands too close. She knows exactly how far the light needs to travel.">I stand too close. I always have.</span>

The silvering is not destruction. It is completion.

When the last transparent circle closes, the mirror will no longer be a surface that returns your face. It will be a surface that contains the finished version of your face—the one that no longer needs you to animate it.

I have been watching the last clear spot shrink for weeks. It is now the size of a coin. Inside it I can see her eyes. They are open. They do not blink when mine do.

Tonight the coin will be gone.

I am sitting on the floor in front of the mirror, waiting. I have turned off every other light in the apartment. The only illumination comes from the streetlamp outside and whatever is still moving behind the glass.

I keep expecting to feel afraid.

Instead I feel something closer to relief. The long rehearsal is ending. The audience of one is about to become the performance.

When the last circle of glass darkens, I will still be here.

But I will not be the one looking out.

She will be. And she will be perfect.
