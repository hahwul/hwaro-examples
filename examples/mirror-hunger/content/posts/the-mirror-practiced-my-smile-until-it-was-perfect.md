The mirror above the sink has been learning me for months.

I first noticed when the condensation refused to obscure my face completely. Water would bead and slide in careful lines around the eyes, the mouth. It wanted to watch.

At first the difference was small. A blink held a quarter-second longer. The slight tilt of the head I only ever managed when I was alone and trying to look like someone else.

Then the mirror began offering suggestions.

I caught it yesterday. I was brushing my teeth and the reflection continued brushing for three full strokes after I had stopped. It spat into the sink with more grace than I possess. When I leaned closer to inspect the drain, it leaned closer too—but its breath did not fog the glass.

There are now two versions of the small scar beside my left eyebrow. Hers is thinner. More deliberate.

I have started leaving the bathroom light off at night. It does not help. The dark still returns the shape of my head, only arranged more pleasingly.

Sometimes I hear the faucet running when both taps are closed. It is practicing the sound of water over someone else's hands.

I have begun to practice my own smile in front of the glass on purpose, trying to make it ugly on purpose. I bare my gums. I show too much tooth. I let one eye squint.

The mirror watches politely. It waits until I leave the room.

Then I hear it rehearsing the corrected version behind me—quiet, perfect, patient.

I do not know how long I have until it decides the practice is finished.
