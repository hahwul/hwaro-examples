Silver responds to the hammer with less forgiveness than copper. The metal work-hardens quickly, and a panel that began the morning soft and pliable will grow brittle by midday. The artisan keeps a small annealing torch within reach, raising the sheet to a dull cherry red whenever the surface refuses further movement.

Bossing, the technique of pushing volume from the reverse, requires steady pitch beneath the work. Pitch holds the metal firm without cracking it, absorbing the strike while permitting controlled deformation. The recipe varies by workshop: rosin, plaster of Paris, beeswax, sometimes a measure of tallow to keep the mixture pliable in winter.

A snarling iron extends the reach of the hammer into deep recesses where a punch cannot follow. The iron is clamped in a vise, the workpiece guided over its tip, and the free end struck from above. The vibration travels down the shaft and rises into the silver, lifting a boss from below as if the metal itself had inhaled.

When the relief is complete, the front receives chasing tools that sharpen edges and define detail. This is where the design earns its character. Lines that were merely suggested by the bossing now become declarations: the curl of a leaf, the fold of a sleeve, the rim of an eye. Every chase mark is final, every error a permanent record.

The finished panel is pickled in dilute sulfuric acid to remove firescale, then burnished with agate to recover the white luster of the silver beneath.
