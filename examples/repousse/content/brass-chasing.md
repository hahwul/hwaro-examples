Chasing is the quieter half of the repoussé craft. The loud, percussive bossing has already done its work, and the panel sits on the bench with its rough volumes pressed outward. Now the artisan turns the sheet over and addresses the visible face, refining what bossing only sketched.

The chasing tools are short blunt punches, each ground to a different profile: trace, liner, planisher, matter. None of them cut the metal. Each merely pushes it. A trace tool scribed along a shoulder line tightens the silhouette of a raised form. A liner driven across a flat field leaves a chain of small marks that read as texture rather than as individual blows.

Brass is well suited to this work. It holds the chase mark cleanly without smearing, and its yellow tone registers tool impressions with sharper contrast than copper or silver. A planished surface adjacent to a matted ground will catch light differently, separating figure from field without recourse to color or applied finish.

The artisan rests the sheet on a leather sandbag during chasing, allowing the surface to deform slightly under each strike rather than ringing back. Pitch is unnecessary at this stage; the relief is already established, and the chasing tools merely refine geometry that the bossing has fixed.

A panel may receive several thousand discrete tool strikes before it leaves the bench. Each one is placed deliberately, and each one is permanent.
