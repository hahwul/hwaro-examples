Typography earns its weight slowly. The first paragraph sets a contract with the reader, and every line break, leading value, and measure either honours that contract or breaks it.

<!-- more -->

A long-form layout starts with measure. Forty-five to seventy-five characters per line is the practical range; anything wider and the eye loses the next line, anything narrower and the rhythm of clauses fractures. Setting body copy in a humanist serif at sixteen pixels with a leading of 1.6 produces a reading texture that sustains attention across thousands of words.

Hierarchy is what allows a reader to skim, anchor, and return. Three levels are usually enough: a primary heading sized at twice the body, a secondary heading at one and a half times, and a tertiary heading set in small caps or italics at the body size. Beyond that, additional levels add noise without adding meaning.

Pull quotes, captions, and metadata sit outside the main column. Treat them as marginalia: lighter weight, smaller size, slightly muted colour. They guide without interrupting. The same restraint applies to inline links — an underline at fifty percent opacity is enough to signal interactivity without pulling the eye off the line.

Vertical rhythm is the quiet backbone. Pick a base unit, usually the body line height, and align every margin, padding value, and spacing token to multiples of it. The page does not need to feel like graph paper, but the spacing decisions should never feel arbitrary. When a designer abandons the grid for a moment — a wide image, a centred epigraph — the return to rhythm afterwards is what makes the disruption feel intentional rather than careless.

Restraint is the through-line. Reading is a long activity, and the type system that supports it should disappear.
