Whitespace is not the absence of content. It is the framing that tells a reader where one idea ends and another begins, and the cadence at which those ideas should be absorbed.

<!-- more -->

In a dense interface, the temptation is to pack every column with information, on the assumption that more density signals more value. The opposite tends to be true. When a layout reaches a certain density threshold, the reader stops parsing individual elements and begins skimming for keywords, which means most of the carefully placed content is never properly read.

Whitespace operates at four scales. Macro whitespace separates sections of a page, often ranging from sixty-four to one hundred and twenty-eight pixels of vertical room. Mid whitespace separates groups within a section, usually thirty-two to forty-eight pixels. Micro whitespace separates lines and inline elements, set by line height and letter spacing. Outer whitespace is the gutter between the layout and the viewport edge — the part that lets the page breathe inside the browser frame.

The discipline is in the consistency. A layout that uses six different vertical gaps without rationale will feel uneven even if every individual choice was tasteful. Once a spacing scale is fixed, the page begins to compose itself: new elements slot into existing rhythms instead of demanding bespoke decisions.

Whitespace also encodes priority. A headline with one hundred pixels of space above it reads as more important than the same headline with twenty pixels of space, even at the same type size. Designers sometimes try to communicate hierarchy with weight or colour first, then reach for spacing as a last resort. The order should be reversed. Spacing is the most powerful and least costly hierarchical tool a layout has.
