Isobar publishes one endgame study at a time — a single diagram, the technique behind it, and enough marginalia to make the idea stick past the browser tab. There is no engine evaluation bar here and no move-by-move game recap. The premise is closer to an old tournament book than a modern chess site: a position worth memorizing, printed large, with notes in the margin.

The name is a small joke about pressure. On a weather map, an isobar connects points of equal pressure; in a rook ending, the losing side is often just one tempo from the same relief, if they can find the line that connects them. Endgame technique rewards that kind of thinking — not raw calculation so much as recognizing a pattern you have filed away from a study exactly like this one.

Most entries live in the rook-and-pawn family, because that is where technique does the most visible work: Lucena, Philidor, Vančura, the short-side defense, the Tarrasch rule. Occasionally a purely composed study earns a place too, when the geometry is elegant enough to be worth the detour from practical technique into pure art — Réti's 1921 study among them.

New studies are published irregularly, whenever one is worth the writing. Older entries do not get revised once published; if a later study contradicts or refines an earlier one, that refinement gets its own entry rather than a silent edit, in keeping with the idea that a study, once printed, stays printed.

Tags collect studies by technique rather than by opening or era — start from [rook endings](/tags/rook-endings/) if you are working through the fundamentals in order.
