Ochre works out of a former signal-print works on the Leith waterfront — a building that spent sixty years printing harbor flags and safety placards before it printed anything that moved. The pigment for which the studio is named lived in that trade too: ochre yellow, the color of warning signs and quarantine flags, chosen because it was the one dye that held its warmth under gaslight, gantry light, and now, under an LC panel at three in the morning during a color pass.

We are six people. Two title designers, two animators, a compositor, and a producer who also answers the phone. We make main title sequences for feature films, opening and end credits, and the short kinetic idents that broadcasters and streaming platforms run between programs — the five-second stings that tell you which channel you are still watching.

## The curve library

Every studio has a type system. Ours is a curve system. Since 2016 we have kept a single running library of named cubic-bezier easing curves — currently a little over three hundred — each one built for a specific job on a specific project and then kept, because a curve that solves a problem once will usually solve it again. **Undertow Pull** overshoots and settles, good for anything that needs to feel like it is fighting its way to the surface. **Coldwater Drift** decelerates slowly and never overshoots, good for anything that should feel like it is running out of energy on purpose. When a new brief comes in, the first design meeting is not about typefaces. It is about which curves in the library already carry the right feeling, and which ones we need to cut from scratch.

<!-- more -->

Cutting a new curve is unglamorous work. Someone opens the internal curve board, drags two control handles against a piece of reference footage — a door closing, a diver breaking the surface, a subway train losing momentum into a platform — and nudges the handles until the timing of the animation matches the timing of the reference in a way that stops feeling like software and starts feeling like the thing it is imitating. A curve usually takes longer to get right than the shot it ends up driving.

```text
name:   undertow-pull
bezier: cubic-bezier(0.65, -0.55, 0.28, 1.55)
notes:  dips before commit, overshoots on arrival — built for
        Undertow's logotype, reused since on three stings
        that needed the same held-breath quality.
```

## How a project runs

A title sequence starts the same way a film score does: with a spotting session. We watch a cut of the film or the channel's on-air reel with the director or the network's creative lead and mark the beats — the places where a cut, a needle-drop, or a line of dialogue asks for a specific kind of motion. From there we storyboard the type kinetics beat by beat, choose or build the curve for each beat, and animate against a scratch mix so the timing survives contact with real sound design. Nothing goes final until picture and sound are locked, because a curve tuned to a temp track almost never survives the final mix unchanged.

## Work with us

Ochre takes on four to six projects a year, mostly features, limited series, and channel rebrands. We do not do explainer videos, motion logos for startups, or anything that needs to be turned around in under two weeks — the curve work does not compress. If that sounds like your project, get in touch through your producer or agent; we do not run open submissions.

The studio, its curve library, and everyone named on this site are fictional, built for the Hwaro examples gallery.
