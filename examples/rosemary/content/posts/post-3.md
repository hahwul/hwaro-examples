## No Cooking Required

Pesto is the purest expression of basil -- raw, bright, and intensely aromatic. This is not a recipe that benefits from improvisation or substitution. Use the best basil you can find (or better, grow your own), real Parmigiano-Reggiano, and a good extra-virgin olive oil. The result speaks for itself.

## Ingredients

- 80 g (about 4 packed cups) fresh basil leaves
- 50 g (1/3 cup) pine nuts, lightly toasted
- 2 cloves garlic, peeled
- 60 g (3/4 cup) finely grated Parmigiano-Reggiano
- 120 ml (1/2 cup) extra-virgin olive oil
- Fine sea salt to taste
- Freshly ground black pepper

## Method

1. Toast the pine nuts in a dry skillet over medium-low heat, stirring frequently, until they are golden and fragrant. This takes only 3 to 4 minutes. Watch them carefully -- they burn quickly. Transfer to a plate to cool.

2. Place the basil, toasted pine nuts, and garlic in a food processor. Pulse several times until coarsely chopped.

3. Add the Parmigiano-Reggiano and pulse a few more times to combine.

4. With the processor running, pour the olive oil in a slow, steady stream through the feed tube. Process until the pesto reaches your preferred consistency. Some prefer it quite smooth; others like a rougher texture with visible bits of leaf and nut.

5. Season with salt and pepper. Taste and adjust. The cheese is salty, so add salt cautiously.

## Serving

Toss with freshly cooked pasta (reserve a splash of pasta water to help the pesto coat the noodles), spread on crusty bread, or stir into a bowl of minestrone.

Pesto also freezes beautifully. Pour it into ice cube trays for single-serving portions. Lay a thin film of olive oil over the top before freezing to prevent browning.

## Herb Pairing Notes

Genovese basil is the traditional choice for pesto, but experiment with other varieties. Thai basil adds an anise note, and lemon basil brings a citrus brightness that works beautifully with fish and seafood.

Basil thrives in warm weather and full sun. Pinch off the flower buds as they appear to keep the plant producing leaves. Harvest from the top of the plant, cutting just above a pair of leaves, and the plant will branch and grow bushier.
