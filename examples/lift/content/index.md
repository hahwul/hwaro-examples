The No. 9 tram stopped running in 1962 and the depot sat closed for the better part of sixty years — four brick bays, a trussed roof built to shelter rolling stock, and a turntable pit nobody had the budget to fill in. Mara Voss and Denny Okafor leased it in 2018 with a single argument for the landlord: a room built to turn a twelve-tonne tram around could hold a climbing wall without breaking a sweat structurally, and the light from three rows of sawtooth skylights was better than anything they could afford to build from scratch.

We kept what the depot gave us. The rails are still there, polished into the new concrete floor as a bronze line that runs the length of the building and loops once around the old turntable circle, which is now where the Saturday [classes](@/classes.md) warm up. The bay numbers stayed too — they just describe wall angles now instead of platforms.

Eight years on, [the wall](@/the-wall.md) is a full bay of routes for every kind of climbing day: slab for footwork, vertical for technique, overhang for power, and a roofed fourth bay we still call the Cave. Read more about [the building and the crew](@/about.md), or just [come by](@/visit.md) and see it.
