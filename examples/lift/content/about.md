Lift opened in 2019, but the argument for it started a few years earlier over a beer after a Tuesday night session at a much smaller gym across town. Mara Voss had spent nine years on the competition circuit before her knees told her to find a slower way to love the sport; Denny Okafor was a structural engineer who kept getting dragged along and kept asking better questions about load paths than anyone wanted at last call. When the No. 9 depot came up for lease — empty since the tram line closed, too big for most tenants, too odd-shaped for a warehouse — the two of them walked the trusses with a flashlight and decided the roof was the whole business case.

## What we kept

Converting a tram depot into a bouldering gym meant restraint as much as ambition. We didn't drop a ceiling, box in the skylights, or carpet over the rail line — we built around them. The old turntable pit became a sunken stretching circle at the center of the floor. The loading doors at the north end became the front desk and a wall of cubbies. Everything structural that the trams needed, the climbers get for free: clear span, generous height, and light that changes all day instead of buzzing at one temperature.

## What we believe

A gym is a room, but a climbing community is a habit, and habits need a place that doesn't feel like a chore to return to. We set routes for the climber who shows up once a week as carefully as we set them for the one who's there every day the doors are open. We close on Mondays so the setters can work without an audience — every wall you climb on Tuesday was touched by hand the day before.

## Who's on the crew

Mara leads route-setting and the [youth squad](@/classes.md). Denny handles the building, the budget, and — reluctantly — the group emails. Around them is a setting crew of six and a front-desk team who can rent you shoes, tape a torn tip, and tell you which [bay resets this week](@/the-wall.md) without checking a clipboard.
