A blade-arm prosthetic is, in mechanical terms, a stored-energy delivery system. The forearm housing carries a folded edge, a tensioned spring, and a release mechanism wired to the operator's neural cuff. The full deployment cycle from intent to locked extension typically runs between ninety and one hundred and forty milliseconds. Anything slower is unsuitable for close quarters work, where the engagement window rarely exceeds two seconds.

Edge composition is where most operators make the wrong tradeoff. Monofilament blades, drawn to a single-molecule width along the cutting line, will pass through soft armour without resistance. They will also chip on contact with structural steel, and a chipped monofilament leaves debris in the housing that fouls the next deployment. Operators who work in mixed environments, where contact with rebar or vehicle frames is likely, often switch to a layered ceramic edge. The cut is rougher, but the blade survives the strike and remains seated for the second extension.

Spring tension matters more than most catalogues suggest. A factory-tuned blade-arm ships with conservative tension to extend service intervals. Field operators routinely retune the spring to draw faster, accepting a shorter maintenance window in exchange for a deployment latency closer to seventy milliseconds. The retune is not subtle. The release makes a different sound, sharper and shorter, and that sound is one of the cues a trained opponent will recognise.

Neural cuff calibration is the final variable. The cuff reads intent, not motion, which means a poorly calibrated cuff will fire on adrenaline spikes alone. Operators who have lost a partner to a misfire tend to dial the cuff threshold high enough that conscious intent is required, and they live with the slower trigger. The trade is not glamorous, but it is the one experienced runners make.
