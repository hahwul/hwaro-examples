Every beginner cuts charoite the way you split firewood: along the grain, because that is where the stone wants to open. The archive cuts against it, and this entry is the standing explanation for why.

<!-- more -->

Chatoyancy is an angle-of-view effect. The silk shines because thousands of parallel fibers catch the lamp together, and a face cut parallel to the fiber shows the fibers end-on, as dots. All the light dies. The luminous band that makes this stone worth keeping only appears when the polished face crosses the fiber at a working angle, somewhere between forty and ninety degrees, and the exact angle decides whether the shine travels as a broad soft wash or a hard narrow bar.

So the saw room keeps a simple protocol. Chip a corner, wet it, and turn it under a fixed lamp until the flash is strongest; scribe that plane; cut a test face a few millimeters proud of the scribe line and read it on the lap before committing the full block. Slower by a day, cheaper by a stone. CH-011, the Silk Parallel slab, is the protocol's best argument: its first face was cut by eye and wasted four millimeters, and its second face was cut by the scribe line and shines edge to edge.

The saw is a reading instrument. The blade just happens to be how it turns the page.
