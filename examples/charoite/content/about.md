The archive is kept by Marfa Kychkina, a lapidary who grew up in Olyokminsk hearing the stone called sirenevyi kamen, the lilac stone, before she ever heard the word charoite. She trained as a cutter in Irkutsk, spent nine seasons buying rough at the Murun workings, and now keeps a small saw room with a twelve-inch blade, two flat laps, and a drafting table that gets more use than either.

Six specimens is a deliberate ceiling. A collection you can hold in one drawer is a collection you can actually know: every slab here has been cut by hand, polished through five grits, weighed, measured, and drawn in ink before it entered the catalog. When a better stone arrives, a lesser one leaves. The archive grows in depth, not in number.

The drawings are the heart of it. A photograph flattens chatoyancy into a lucky angle; a drawing has to decide what the stone is doing and say so in six inks. The plates on this site use exactly those six, from pearl lilac to violet black, plus white for quartz and a green-black for aegirine.

The archive is fictional, built as a demonstration site for the Hwaro static site generator. The geology is real, and the Chara valley really is the only place on Earth this stone occurs.
