Where the Kaskad slab pours, CH-011 is combed. The fiber runs in tight parallel bands from edge to edge, ten to the centimeter in the finest passages, with barely a degree of drift across the whole face. It is the closest this deposit comes to woven cloth, and the least typical stone in the drawer.

<!-- more -->

Parallel fiber is a cutting problem before it is anything else. Aligned this precisely, the face is effectively a diffraction comb: polish it a few degrees off-axis and the chatoyancy dies to a dull sheen. The keeper cut this slab twice. The first face wasted four millimeters proving the alignment was wrong; the second sits within a degree of the fiber axis, and the shine now travels edge to edge as a single unbroken bar.

On the drafting table this stone nearly broke the six-ink rule; the bands beg for gradients. The plate refused, and is better for it: alternating flat lines, pearl against grape, exactly as the stone argues.
