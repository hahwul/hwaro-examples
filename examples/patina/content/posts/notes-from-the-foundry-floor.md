I spent a week at a small bronze foundry outside of Sheffield. The building itself is unremarkable from the street --- a low brick structure with soot-darkened windows. Inside, the air shimmers with heat.

## The Process

Bronze casting has not changed in its fundamentals for thousands of years. A pattern is made. A mold is built around it. Molten metal is poured in. The mold is broken away to reveal the casting.

The specifics, of course, have evolved. Modern foundries use ceramic shell molds and electric furnaces. But the rhythm of the work --- the patient buildup of layers, the violent pour, the slow reveal --- remains essentially the same.

<!-- more -->

## What the Metal Teaches

Bronze is an alloy of copper and tin, and its behavior during casting is instructive. It flows differently depending on temperature, mold geometry, and even humidity. Experienced casters read the metal's surface like a language.

> "You pour a hundred times and every pour is different. The metal remembers things you forgot."

The castings I watched being made were architectural fittings: door handles, letter plates, hinges. Small objects, but each one required hours of preparation. The mold-making alone took three days per piece.

### What Struck Me

Several things stood out during the visit:

- **Sound matters.** The ring of a good casting is distinct from a flawed one. Workers tap each piece and listen before inspecting it visually.
- **Finishing is half the work.** The raw casting is rough and marked with sprue lines. Hours of grinding, filing, and polishing follow.
- **Nothing is wasted.** Failed castings and metal scraps go back into the furnace. The material cycles continuously.

The foundry produces objects that will outlast the people who make them. There is something centering about witnessing that kind of timescale in a world that measures progress in software release cycles.
