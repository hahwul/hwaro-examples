The instinct in design is often to add. Another feature, another color, another layer of visual interest. But the work that ages best tends to be the work that knew when to stop.

## The Temptation of More

Consider a well-made wooden table. Its beauty comes from proportion, from the grain of the wood, from the joinery at the corners. Add a carved flourish and you have shifted attention from the material to the decoration. Add several and the material disappears entirely beneath ornament.

This is not an argument against decoration. It is an argument for knowing what carries the design and protecting it.

## Restraint as Confidence

When a designer leaves space --- real, generous, unoccupied space --- they are making a statement about confidence. They trust the remaining elements to do the work. They trust the viewer to meet them halfway.

The opposite of restraint is not generosity. It is anxiety. Over-designed work often reveals a fear that the core idea is not strong enough to stand alone.

### Principles

Some guidelines that have proven reliable over centuries of craft:

1. **Remove until it breaks.** If something can be taken away without loss, it was not needed.
2. **Let materials speak.** The texture of stone, the warmth of wood, the weight of metal --- these have their own voice.
3. **Proportion over decoration.** Get the relationships right first. Ornament is the last layer, not the first.
4. **Serve the reader.** Every design decision should make the content clearer, not compete with it.

The best work often looks inevitable in retrospect. As if nothing else could have been done. That appearance of inevitability is the reward for restraint applied with care.
