---
title: "The Case for Restraint"
date: "2023-12-03"
---

Restraint is the most undervalued skill in editorial design. It does not announce itself. It produces work that looks effortless, which is often confused with work that required no effort. The pages that read most smoothly are the ones where the most decisions were made and then withdrawn.

Every element on a page asks for the reader's attention. A second weight of type, a rule, a colored accent, a sidebar -- each costs something. The cost is rarely catastrophic, but it accumulates. By the third or fourth indulgence, the reader is no longer reading; they are negotiating with the layout.

A useful exercise is to remove one thing from every page and observe what is lost. In most cases, very little. The rule above the byline. The decorative cap on the opening paragraph. The pull quote that repeats text already in the column. These elements were added for reasons that seemed sound at the time. None of them, in isolation, would cause the reader to put the page down. Together, they crowd the room.

Restraint is not the same as austerity. An austere page refuses ornament on principle. A restrained page accepts ornament when it serves the reader and declines it when it does not. The difference is judgment, not ideology.

The hardest part of restraint is that it requires the designer to be willing to make a page that looks unfinished, until they recognize that finish, in the typographic sense, is not the same as ornament. A finished page is one that does its job without strain. Most ornament strains.
