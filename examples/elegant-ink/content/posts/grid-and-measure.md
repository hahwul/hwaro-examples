---
title: "Grid and Measure"
date: "2023-11-12"
---

A grid is not decoration. It is a contract between writer and reader, an agreement about where the eye will land and where it will rest. Without a grid, every page is improvised. With one, the page has somewhere to stand.

The measure -- the length of a line of body text -- is the first decision in any reading interface. Too long, and the eye loses its place at the line's end. Too short, and the reader is forced to break and reset more often than thought allows. Sixty to seventy-five characters per line, including spaces, has held up across centuries of book design. There is no reason to discard the figure now.

A baseline grid carries less weight in screen typography than it does in print, where ink registration matters across many pages. On screen, vertical rhythm is more forgiving, and rigid baseline alignment often costs more than it returns. What remains useful is consistent leading: a line height proportional to the type size, never less than 1.4 for body text set at sixteen pixels.

Columns serve hierarchy. A single column communicates one voice. Two columns suggest comparison or commentary. More than three, and the reader is no longer reading but scanning. Each column added removes a degree of intimacy from the page.

Grids are most effective when they are felt rather than seen. The reader should not notice the underlying structure; they should only notice that the page is calm, that the text is unhurried, that the spaces between things feel correct. The work of the grid is to disappear once it has done its job.
