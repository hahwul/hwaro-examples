Most planting failures on cold ground trace back to two mistakes: setting the graft union too deep, and skipping winter protection the first year when the root system is still small. Everything below is written for a plant that just arrived from Newton, whether it came bare-root in April or potted in September.

## Bare-root trees

Soak roots in a bucket of water for two to four hours before planting; longer than a day can drown them. Dig a hole wide enough that roots lie flat without curling, and only as deep as the root flare. On a grafted apple, pear, plum, or cherry, keep the graft union &mdash; the slight knuckle where scion meets rootstock &mdash; two to three inches above the final soil line. Bury it and the scion will root on its own, and you lose the size control and hardiness the rootstock was chosen for.

Backfill with the native soil you dug out, not a bagged amendment; a rich pocket surrounded by ordinary ground encourages roots to circle instead of spreading out. Water in heavily to settle the soil and close air pockets, then water again the next day.

## Potted stock

Potted shrubs and trees can go in any time the ground is workable, spring through early fall. Score the root ball vertically in three or four places before setting it in the hole to keep it from continuing to grow in a circle. Plant at the same depth it sat in the pot &mdash; no deeper.

## Staking and mulch

Stake standard trees only if the site is windy or the tree is taller than four feet; over-staking produces a weak trunk that cannot flex on its own. Use a soft tie and remove it after one full growing season. Mulch every new planting three to four inches deep in wood chips, pulled back a hand's width from the trunk or crown so bark stays dry.

## First winter

Young trees are the most vulnerable to vole and rabbit damage in their first two winters, when bark is thin and snow cover hides the ground. Wrap the lower eighteen inches of trunk in a plastic or wire guard before the ground freezes, and leave it on until spring growth starts. Berry shrubs generally need no wrap, but a wire cage helps where rabbit pressure is heavy.
