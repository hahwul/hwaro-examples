Newton Nursery started in 2011 on five acres that had failed as an orchard twice before under previous owners, both times planted with cultivars bred for warmer ground two states south. The trees looked fine in the catalog photos and died back to the snow line by their third winter. That is the whole reason this nursery exists: to stop selling northern gardeners plants that were never tested on northern ground.

## How we test

Every cultivar spends a minimum of three winters in our trial rows before it is offered for sale, and it has to come through an open winter &mdash; one without unusually deep or early snow cover protecting the lower trunk &mdash; before we trust the zone rating enough to stamp it. Cultivars that lose more than minor tip dieback get pulled from the trial and are never listed, no matter how good the fruit is. We keep records on every row, and the zone printed on a catalog card traces back to those records, not to a supplier's marketing sheet.

## How we propagate

Trees are whip-and-tongue or bud-grafted onto rootstock we grow from seed or stool bed ourselves, selected over the years for vigor on our particular clay-over-till soil rather than for a specific dwarfing ratio. Berry shrubs are grown own-root from cuttings taken off our oldest, most productive plants each dormant season, so a bush you buy from us is a direct clone of a bush that has already proven itself, not an unknown seedling.

## Sourcing and practices

We do not buy in unrooted liners from wholesale brokers to relabel and sell as our own; everything either started as seed or cutting on this property or was grafted here from scionwood we collected ourselves. Pest management on the display rows and trial beds is cultural first &mdash; spacing, pruning for airflow, resistant rootstock &mdash; and we do not use neonicotinoid treatments on anything that flowers, since the bees that work our saskatoon windbreak every May are doing us a favor we would rather not repay with a systemic insecticide.

The nursery is run by a small crew year-round, larger during spring digging and grafting season, and everyone on staff can tell you which row a given cultivar came out of and how it did the winter it mattered most.
