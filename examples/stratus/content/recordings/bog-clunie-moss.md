Forty minutes at ankle height in a blanket bog, staying as still as the mic stand, because every step onto sphagnum makes a squelch loud enough to ruin the take for a full minute afterward.

<!-- more -->

Clunie Moss does not have a single dominant sound the way a station or a substation does — it has a low, wide bed of noise built from a hundred small unsynchronised things, which is exactly why an MS pair earns its keep here. The side channel carries the width of the wind moving through cotton grass and heather at slightly different heights and speeds, while the mid channel holds what actually has a location: a broken chorus of craneflies and heather beetles close to the mic, a single curlew call that arrives at eleven minutes and does not repeat, water moving somewhere under the peat that you only notice once someone tells you it is there.

The bog was 14°C at ground level, cooler than the 19°C air a metre up, and the temperature gradient does something audible to the wind noise — it arrives in soft rolling gusts rather than a steady hiss, the sound of air dragging across a surface that is not uniform in temperature or texture. Windshielding still could not stop every gust from thumping the diaphragm; I have left two of those thumps in rather than gate them out, because a bog is a windy place and pretending otherwise felt dishonest.

No hum here. No reverb. Just distance doing all the work.
