Fog thick enough to lose the harbour light after thirty metres, a fourteen-knot onshore wind, and a foghorn sounding its pattern from a buoy I could hear but never see. Thirty-seven minutes on the north breakwater with a shotgun mic rigged under a golf umbrella against the drizzle.

<!-- more -->

The foghorn is the spine of this recording — a low two-second blast every thirty seconds, close enough to feel in the chest wall of the breakwater, its pitch bending slightly each time depending on which way the wind happens to be leaning at that moment. Underneath it, the swell against the stone does something a calm-day recording never catches: a long rolling build, a slap, and then a drawn-out hiss as the water runs back off the granite blocks, the whole cycle taking eight or nine seconds and never quite repeating identically twice.

Wind management was the real work of the night. A shotgun mic under an umbrella rig cuts direct wind noise well but does nothing for the low rumble the wind puts into the microphone body itself when it gusts past about 20kt, so I have left three or four soft rumbles in the recording rather than filter them into an artificial calm the night did not actually have. The one long swelling rise around the two-minute mark is not the wind at all — it is the foghorn's pattern briefly synchronising with an unusually large set of waves, the two low sounds stacking into one for about four seconds before drifting apart again.

Nobody else was on the breakwater. Fog does that to a harbour.
