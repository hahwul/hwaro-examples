Minus six, no wind, frost thick enough on the access ladder that the contact mic's clamp needed both hands to seat. Eighteen minutes at the base of a 400kV tower, listening to a hum that most people walk under without registering.

<!-- more -->

The pickup coil and the contact mic disagree slightly, which is the point of running both. The coil hears the field directly — a clean 100Hz fundamental with harmonics stacked at 200, 300, and 400Hz, the signature of a magnetostriction hum rather than anything mechanical. The contact mic, bolted to the galvanised steel, adds the tower's own resonance underneath it: a soft structural creak as the cold contracts the steel unevenly, slower than the hum by a factor of thousands.

People call this a B-flat hum out of habit, copying a fact about American 60Hz mains that does not transfer. At 100Hz it sits closer to G2, and even that is generous — the pitch drifts a few cents with load, rising almost imperceptibly as the morning commute into the market town starts pulling more current through the line around 07:15. You can hear the shift if you know to listen for it, a hum very slowly tightening its throat.

No birds this early. Just the tower, doing arithmetic on itself in the cold, and a slow brightening in the east that the microphones could not record.
