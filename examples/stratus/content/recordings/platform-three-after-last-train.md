The last service of the night clears Marsh Junction at 23:47 and does not come back. I set the boundary mic on the platform edge at 23:40 and let the recorder run into the emptiness that follows.

<!-- more -->

Most of this recording is close to silence, which is the point — the boundary mic laid flat against the platform slab hears the room itself rather than anything happening in it, and an unstaffed station at midnight is mostly room. The canopy roof, a shallow curved iron structure original to the 1887 station, gives everything a specific short slap-back: footsteps, a dropped ticket machine panel two platforms over, the PA system's standby hum switching off automatically at midnight with an audible relay click and then nothing.

Two events break the quiet enough to show on the waveform. At around three minutes, a freight service passes through on the through line without stopping, close enough that the platform edge itself resonates faintly under the boundary mic — you can feel it more than hear it on playback through headphones. Later, past twenty minutes, someone crosses the footbridge, unseen, footsteps arriving with the particular hollow ring of painted iron stairs before fading toward the car park. I never saw who it was. The recorder does not need to.

By 00:15 nothing further happens. I let it run anyway, because the specific grain of that nothing — hiss, distant road noise, one dripping gutter — is itself the entry.
