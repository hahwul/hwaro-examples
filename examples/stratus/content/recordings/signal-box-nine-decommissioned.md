Box 9 has not swung a signal since 2014, but the interlocking frame is still exercised once a month by a preservation volunteer, purely to keep the mechanism from seizing. I was allowed in for one of those passes.

<!-- more -->

Twenty-two levers, painted in the old colour code that nobody currently working the line would recognise, connected by rodding under the floor to signals and points that were cut up for scrap years ago. Pulling one now moves nothing but its own linkage — a long rattling clack that runs the length of the frame and dies against the end stop, then a much longer decay ringing off the timber lining and the glazing, most of it original, none of it draught-proof.

What the recording captures is really a room learning to be empty. Each lever pull is a burst of very specific, very located sound — you can hear exactly which one is being worked from the pitch of its clack — followed by four or five seconds of reverberant tail that has nothing else competing with it, because there is no traffic outside to mask it and no staff talking over it. Between pulls, a transformer in the locking room underneath keeps up a small steady hum, the box's only remaining job now being to stay just barely alive.

The volunteer worked the frame end to end and back, the sequence engineers used to call "swinging the road," out of habit more than necessity.
