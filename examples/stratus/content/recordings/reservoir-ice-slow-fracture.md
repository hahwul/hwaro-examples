Eight centimetres of ice, minus four overnight, and a service reservoir that keeps making sounds like a much larger animal shifting its weight. Half an hour with a hydrophone through an auger hole, waiting for the next one.

<!-- more -->

Reservoir ice is not lake ice — it forms fast against concrete-lined banks with almost no current to relieve it, so it locks itself into a shape and then spends every cold night fighting that shape as the temperature keeps dropping. What you hear underwater is the fighting: long silences broken by a rising tonal sweep, almost a whistle, as a crack races along the sheet faster than sound travels through open air. Above the surface, the same event arrives half a second later as a flat crack, sometimes a boom if the fracture runs under the thicker ice near the outflow tower.

Between events the hydrophone picks up almost nothing — no fish in a compensation reservoir, no current, just the occasional tick of thermal contraction settling into the ice grain by grain. I counted five clean fractures in thirty-three minutes, each one preceded by a few seconds of near total silence that made the following crack land harder than its actual volume, a trick of expectation the waveform cannot show but the ear remembers.

Left before the ice sheet nearest the tower let go for good, some hours after I'd packed up, according to the reservoir keeper the next morning.
