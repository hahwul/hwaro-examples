In the deep, narrow valley of Olden, the sheer rock walls of the fjord rise nearly vertically from the dark water, creating a natural cathedral with unique, long-lasting acoustic properties.
<!-- more -->
We set up our microphones along the shore to capture the complex reflections of sound waves bouncing between the cliffs, recording the deep rumble of falling water from glaciers above and the gentle, rhythmic lapping of the tide.

We speak with Emil, a local geomorphologist who has studied the valley's geological structure. He shares his observations on how the acoustics of the fjord change with the seasons. During the autumn, when the cold, dense air settles in the valley bottom, it creates a temperature inversion that traps sound near the water's surface, causing echoes to travel further and sound clearer. Emil discusses how the soundscape is affected by the seasonal retreat of the glacier, which alters the volume and pitch of the water flowing into the fjord. The recordings capture the delicate connection between geology, atmosphere, and sound.
