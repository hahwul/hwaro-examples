At the southern tip of the Lofoten archipelago, the islands of Røst host some of the largest nesting colonies of seabirds in Europe. We set up our microphones near the steep cliffs of the outer islands to capture the immense acoustic energy of these colonies during the peak of the breeding season.
<!-- more -->
The air is filled with the deafening chorus of thousands of puffins, kittiwakes, and guillemots calling to their mates, a complex soundscape layered over the deep, rhythmic crash of Atlantic waves breaking against the rocky shore below.

We speak with Karen, an ornithologist who has monitored the bird populations on Røst for over a decade. She describes how the soundscape of the cliffs acts as a direct indicator of the colony's health. The intensity and density of the bird calls have noticeably decreased in recent years, reflecting the declining numbers of nesting pairs due to changes in food availability in the surrounding ocean. Karen discusses the acoustic behavior of the birds, explaining how parents and chicks locate each other using unique vocal signatures amidst the noise of the colony. The recordings capture the vitality and vulnerability of these remote island ecosystems.
