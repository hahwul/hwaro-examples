We drop our hydrophones through a two-meter hole drilled in the solid fjord ice of Kulusuk. What sounds like a frozen silence from above is revealed as a bustling acoustic playground beneath the surface.
<!-- more -->
Underneath the heavy winter cover, the ocean carries the high-pitched clicks of ringed seals, the distant groan of drifting icebergs grinding against the seabed, and the sharp, metallic snaps of thermal cracking.

In this dispatch, we also speak with Jonas, a local fisherman who has navigated these waters for over forty years. He explains how the acoustic properties of the ice have changed during his lifetime. The ice used to sing with a deep, resonant pitch when it was thick and stable; now, he says, it sounds brittle and fast, clicking like dry wood. The sonic change reflects a physical one, as the ice becomes thinner and less predictable. Jonas guides us through the traditional vocabulary used by his community to describe the different textures of ice, words that carry a deep understanding of the subarctic maritime environment.
