Nora began in the winter of 2024 as a collaborative acoustic mapping project between audio documentarians, geographers, and climate researchers. Our goal is simple yet urgent: to preserve the delicate auditory heritage of subarctic communities before they are irrevocably altered by shifting climates, industrialization, and rural depopulation. Soundscapes are highly sensitive indicators of ecological and social health, and the voices of the north have stories to tell that are often missed by visual mediums.

To capture these environments with maximum fidelity, our recordists travel to some of the most remote settlements in Greenland, Svalbard, northern Norway, and Iceland. We utilize high-resolution omnidirectional hydrophones to record beneath the fjord ice, customized binaural head rigs to document village soundscapes from a human perspective, and ultra-low-noise preamps that can withstand temperatures as low as minus forty degrees Celsius. 

We focus on two distinct sonic threads. First are the oral histories: unfiltered conversations with villagers, weather observers, lighthouse keepers, and scientists about their daily lives, memories, and observations of the changing landscape. Second are the environmental recordings: the groaning of ancient glaciers, the high-frequency whistling of wind through radar domes, the splash of fishing nets, and the silence of the tundra. Each dispatch represents a single location, preserved in time and coordinate.
