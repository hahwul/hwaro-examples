I don't write about the easy days often enough, so let this one stand for all the ones I didn't log. High pressure sitting comfortably at 1022 and building, a clean westerly holding at fourteen knots without a single gust worth naming, and eleven miles of open water between Corvid Harbor and the Ansel group laid out on a beam reach that asked nothing of the boat but to be sailed.

<!-- more -->

Petrel likes a beam reach more than any other point of sail, and I like it too, for the same reason: nothing is fighting anything else. The sheets were set once at the harbor mouth and never touched again until I rounded up into the anchorage four hours later. I spent most of the crossing not really steering so much as suggesting, a light hand on the tiller correcting a degree here and there while the self-steering line did the actual work.

An hour out from the islands, three dorsal fins broke the surface off the port bow, close enough that I cut speed without quite deciding to. A resident pod, six or seven animals by the time they'd all surfaced, working the tide line where the current piles bait fish against the drop-off north of the islands. They stayed with the boat for eleven minutes by the cockpit clock, closing to boat-length distance twice, before peeling off toward open water and whatever they'd actually come for.

Anchored in the lee of the outer island by early afternoon, barometer still climbing, not a cloud worth mentioning anywhere on the horizon. I'm aware this log runs heavier on gales than on afternoons like this one. It shouldn't. This is most of why I do it.
