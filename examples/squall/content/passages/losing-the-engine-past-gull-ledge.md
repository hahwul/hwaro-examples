The engine had been running rough for two days, which I'd noted in this log and then done exactly nothing about, the way you do when the weather's been fair and the water's been flat and there's been no reason yet to make the fuel filter your problem. Gull Ledge gave me the reason. It cut out abeam of the rocks with a falling barometer and twenty-two knots building from the southeast.

<!-- more -->

There's a version of this story where I panic, and there's the version that actually happened, which is that I put the tiller over to bear away from the ledge, got the genoa fully out to build speed while I still had sea room, and only then went looking for what had killed the engine. Water in the fuel, near enough certainly — the filter bowl told the story once I was calm enough to check it, three miles later, hove to under staysail alone.

Six miles of confused, wind-against-current sea between Gull Ledge and the harbor entrance, sailed on genoa and a reefed main with no engine to fall back on if a tack went wrong. It concentrates the mind wonderfully. I gave the ledge and its outlying rocks a full half mile more clearance than I normally would, tacked twice more than the course strictly demanded rather than cut it fine, and came into Corvid Harbor on a close reach under sail alone for the first time in three years of owning this boat.

The filter's changed now, the fuel's been polished, and I've added a line to the pre-departure checklist that simply says *when did you last actually look at the primary filter bowl*. Some lessons are worth writing down twice.
