I woke at ten past two to a motion that was wrong before I'd worked out why. Not the roll I'd fallen asleep to, but a slow, deliberate yaw, the bow swinging further each time instead of settling back. Anchor alarm hadn't gone off yet. It went off eleven seconds after I sat up, which tells you something about margins.

<!-- more -->

Foss Cove looks sheltered on the chart and mostly is, right up until a front swings the wind ninety degrees in under an hour, which is exactly what had happened while I slept through the first of it. The 25lb Rocna I trust in mud had found a patch of the cove's one rocky shelf, and thirty knots of gusts funneling off the ridge behind the anchorage was enough to break it out.

I brought the engine up before I brought the anchor up, motored forward to take the strain off the chain, and re-set fifty yards further into the cove where the chart showed nothing but the soft holding I'd wanted the first time. This time I paid out closer to seven-to-one scope instead of the five I'd been lazy enough to settle for at sunset, backed down hard on it in reverse, and watched the chain go taut and stay taut for a full minute before I believed it.

By the time the front had properly cleared through, the barometer was already climbing back past 1019 and the wind had eased to something a boat could sleep through undisturbed. I stayed up anyway, wrapped in the cockpit with tea, watching the anchor bearing on two shore transits until the sky started to grey. Some nights the log entry is the passage. This one was the anchor.
