The forecast had promised twenty knots by early afternoon, which is the kind of promise a barometer at 1005 and dropping does not keep. I was three miles into the crossing, main full, when the water ahead went from chop to a hard, dark line moving fast on a course to meet me exactly amidships.

<!-- more -->

There is a rule I try to keep and mostly do: reef before you think you need to, not when you know you do. So the second reef went in with the squall still a mile off, sheets eased, and the tiller pilot switched out for my own hand on the helm. By the time it hit, Petrel was already carrying the sail she needed instead of the sail I'd wanted an hour earlier.

It came in as thirty-four gusting through the gap between Corbie Rock and the mainland shore, the strait doing what straits do to wind — funneling it, quickening it, giving it nowhere to go but forward and down onto the water in long grey combs. Visibility dropped to under a mile in the driving spray. I held a course fifteen degrees up from rhumb line to claw back the leeway I knew was coming, watched the depth sounder more than the compass, and let the boat do the work she's built for.

Forty minutes later it passed the way squalls do, all at once, leaving a washed-out sky and a strait gone glassy at the edges where the wind had backed off first. Callow Bay by four, sail plan back to full main and genoa for the last two miles, kettle on before I'd finished securing the halyards. The barometer was still falling when I logged this, which means there is more coming through tonight. Ground tackle is out, snubber on, and I intend to sleep through whatever it decides to do.
