Sentry Head had been visible at eight, a grey wedge four miles off the bow under a sky that wasn't threatening anything in particular. By nine it was gone, along with everything else past the pulpit rail, swallowed by the kind of fog that doesn't roll in so much as simply arrive, all at once, like a decision someone else made for you.

<!-- more -->

The radar earns its keep on days like this and I'll never again cruise this coast without one, but a screen full of returns off the headland's rock shelf is only half the picture when there's inshore traffic you can't always separate from clutter. So it was radar, dead reckoning worked in five-minute increments, and the foghorn cord within reach the whole way — one long blast every two minutes, listening after each one for anything answering back that I hadn't put there myself.

The current sets hard past Sentry Head, harder than the pilot book quite conveys, and I'd rather trust a stopwatch and a known boat speed than assume the chartplotter's dead-reckoning line is accounting for it properly. Every five minutes: log the position, check it against the radar picture, adjust the course a degree or two if the drift says to. It is slow, unglamorous work, and it is the only kind of navigation I fully trust in conditions like these.

The fog didn't lift until I was inside Callow Bay's outer marks, at which point Sentry Head reappeared behind me exactly where the dead reckoning had said it would be, which is either good navigation or good luck, and after eleven years of this I've stopped needing to know which.
