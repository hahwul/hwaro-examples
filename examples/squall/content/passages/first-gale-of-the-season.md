The refit came out of the yard in April and I'd told myself the first real blow of the season would be the honest test of it, the rigging and the reefing lines and my own rustiness after a winter spent mostly reading about sailing instead of doing it. I did not expect the test to arrive quite this early, or with a gale warning attached.

<!-- more -->

The forecast had upgraded twice in six hours, from a fresh southerly to a gale warning with gusts to forty, and by the time I'd accepted I wasn't making the harbor entrance before it arrived, the sensible choice was to stop trying. Eight miles offshore, storm jib and triple-reefed main, I hove to and let Petrel find her own equilibrium — bow forty-five degrees off the wind, drifting slowly to leeward, riding each set of waves without needing much from me at all.

Heaving to is one of those things you read about for years before you actually trust it, and there is nothing quite like watching a barometer fall through 993 while the boat sits there, quiet as a parked car, to convert you completely. The motion belowdecks was almost gentle. Spray came over the bow in sheets and the wind in the rigging climbed to a genuine howl for three hours running, and none of it much mattered because the boat wasn't fighting any of it.

By first light the front had passed, the barometer had bottomed out and begun its slow climb back, and I sailed the last eight miles into Corvid Harbor under working jib alone, tired in the particular way that comes from a night of trusting gear rather than working it. The refit held. So did I.
