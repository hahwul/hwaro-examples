I sail alone, mostly along one stretch of coast, in a boat that is older than I am and better built than most things made today. This log is the record I keep of it — not a cruising guide, not a how-to, just the passages as they actually happened, with the barometer and the wind noted the way I'd note them in the paper logbook if I still trusted myself to keep one up to date.

I started keeping it properly after a passage two winters ago that I got wrong in three small ways that would have compounded into a serious problem if the weather had been five knots worse than forecast. Writing it down, honestly, turned out to matter more than I expected. The barometer reading beside each entry isn't decoration — it's the number I was actually watching when I made the calls that mattered. The whole run of them lives in [the log](/passages/), filed by the [conditions](/tags/) that shaped each one.

## How I log a passage

Every entry here is written within a day of making harbor, before memory has a chance to improve on what happened. The barometer reading is the one I read at the helm, not one corrected afterward against a forecast that turned out to be right. Wind notes are estimated, not measured — I don't carry an anemometer I trust enough to quote as fact, so what you'll read is a sailor's honest guess, informed by a lot of years of guessing.

If you're planning your own passages through weather like this, treat everything here as one boat's experience on one afternoon, not instruction. The strait doesn't care what worked for me last time.
