## Weight of Record

A physical document carries weight that its digital counterpart cannot. Not merely the weight of paper — though that matters — but the weight of presence. A document occupies space. It can be held, folded, torn, burned. It degrades. It yellows. It tells time.

The photocopied document occupies a peculiar position in this hierarchy. It is neither original nor purely derivative. It is a physical object that references another physical object, carrying forward some properties while introducing new ones.

## Chain of Custody

Every copy in a chain tells two stories simultaneously: the content of the original and the history of its own reproduction. The fingerprint smudge on page three. The coffee ring on the cover sheet. The handwritten annotation in the margin that was itself copied forward into subsequent generations.

These are the metadata of the physical world, embedded in the document without intention or indexing. They cannot be searched, but they can be read by anyone who knows how to look.

## Fidelity and Betrayal

No copy is perfectly faithful. The xerographic process introduces distortion at every step: the slight misalignment of the original on the glass, the uneven distribution of charge on the drum, the variable fusion temperature across the page.

These infidelities accumulate. By the fifth generation, a document has drifted measurably from its origin. Yet it remains recognizable. The essential content survives even as the form mutates. This is perhaps the most optimistic property of information: it is remarkably resilient, even when its vessel is not.
