There is a persistent myth in design that more options lead to better outcomes. A broader palette, a wider selection of typefaces, an expanded toolkit -- surely these produce richer work. But the opposite is often true.

When you remove colour from the equation, every other decision becomes more consequential. Spacing must carry the rhythm that hue once provided. Typography must establish the hierarchy that contrast once delivered. White space stops being empty and starts being structural.

## The Sculptor's Advantage

Grisaille painters understood this intuitively. By limiting themselves to shades of grey, they forced the viewer to attend to form. A grisaille painting of a draped figure cannot rely on the richness of velvet red or the coolness of sky blue to convey texture. It must achieve everything through tonal variation alone.

This is not deprivation. It is precision.

## Applying the Principle

Modern interface design often suffers from an excess of colour. Alerts in red, success states in green, links in blue, warnings in amber -- the result is a visual environment that communicates through convention rather than composition.

Consider instead what happens when you design a dashboard in greyscale first:

- You discover which elements truly need emphasis
- You learn whether your hierarchy works without colour crutches
- You build a foundation that remains accessible under any palette

> "The constraint is not the enemy of expression. It is the condition that makes expression necessary."

The discipline of grey is not about austerity for its own sake. It is about building systems that work at their most fundamental level, before any decoration is applied.
