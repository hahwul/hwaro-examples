<p class="lead">It was at a country-house sale in the autumn of last year that I first came upon the almanac which has occasioned these reflections.</p>

The house itself was of no great consequence &mdash; a modest brick villa, built in the later part of the eighteenth century, whose contents were being dispersed upon the death of the last of its family. The library, so called, was a small room at the rear, fitted with glazed cases of the cheaper sort, and containing perhaps four hundred volumes, of which the almanac was one.

I had not intended to purchase anything. I had gone to the sale merely to pass an idle Saturday, and I stood for some time in the library looking along the shelves, as is my habit, reading the titles upon the spines. Most of the books were of the common sort &mdash; bound sets of the novelists, a run of the *Gentleman's Magazine*, a quantity of theology &mdash; and I had nearly finished my inspection when I noticed, upon the bottom shelf, a small duodecimo in worn calf, without lettering upon the spine.

## The discovery

<p>I drew it out. It was *Rider's British Merlin* for the year 1742, a small, densely printed almanac of the sort that every country gentleman once kept upon his desk. It had evidently been used as a memorandum book; the margins of many pages were written upon in a careful, clerkly hand, with notes of the weather, of livestock prices, of the arrival of visitors, and occasionally of a death or a birth.</p>

<p>But what most struck me about the book was the foxing. The pages were so thickly spotted that the print was in places nearly lost, and the marginal notes, being in a fainter ink than the print, were partly obliterated. The book had evidently been stored, for a considerable period, in a damp place; the endpapers were beginning to separate from the boards, and the title page bore a pale water ring, perfectly circular, about the size of a small tumbler.</p>

<blockquote>One might have said the book was ruined; I should prefer to say it was fully witnessed.</blockquote>

## The bidding

<p>I bid for it, and secured it for a trifle. The auctioneer, holding it aloft, had remarked that the book was "in very poor condition" &mdash; a judgement with which I could not wholly agree. I have since spent several evenings with it, reading not only the printed matter but the careful annotations of its former owner, whose identity I have been at some pains, and with some partial success, to establish.</p>

## On the annotations

<p>The writer of the annotations was, I am nearly certain, one Ezra Pentrith, farmer of the parish of &mdash;, who died in 1759 at the age of seventy-two. The almanac covers the years 1742 to 1752, after which the notes cease. The last entry, written in a shakier hand than the earlier ones, reads:</p>

<blockquote>July xviii. &mdash; Much thunder this month. Wm. Pentrith, my son, ordained at Wells. A good hay.</blockquote>

<p>There is, I confess, something profoundly moving in the juxtaposition of these three facts, recorded in the same breath. A storm, an ordination, a good hay. It is, one feels, a life.</p>
