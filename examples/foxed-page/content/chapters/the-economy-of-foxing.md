<p class="lead">It will not, I trust, be thought a digression if I set down here some particulars of the chemistry of foxing, in so far as that obscure process can be said to admit of explanation.</p>

The term itself is of uncertain provenance. Some have derived it from the rust-coloured cast of the spots, supposed to resemble the coat of a fox; others, less plausibly, from the verb "to fox," meaning to confuse or to render obscure. The earliest printed use I have been able to trace occurs in a bookseller's catalogue of 1848, where a copy of Holinshed is described as "much foxed but otherwise sound." The condition was no doubt observed long before it was named.

## On the agents

The literature is divided upon the question of cause. The older authorities held that foxing was the work of an iron impurity in the paper, oxidising in the presence of damp and producing the characteristic stain. The more recent view assigns a principal role to certain fungi of the genus *Aspergillus* and *Eurotium*, whose hyphae penetrate the fibres of the sheet and leave behind a deposit of organic acids and ferric salts. It is probable that both agents are at work; the metal provides the colour, the fungus the pattern.

The conditions favourable to the process are well established. Relative humidity above sixty-five per cent, temperatures between fifteen and twenty-five degrees, and an ambient atmosphere rich in pollutants will between them ensure the swift decline of any volume so exposed. The matter of light is more equivocal: ultraviolet radiation accelerates the oxidation, but its absence does not arrest the process, only retards it.

## On the distribution

<p>The spots are not random. They cluster, characteristically, along the gutter and the fore-edge, where the leaf has been most often touched, and along the upper margin, where airborne spores settle most readily. A volume opened often shows a heavier infestation than one left undisturbed for a generation; a volume bound in calf shows differently from one bound in cloth. The endpapers, being usually of a coarser stock, are almost always the worst affected.</p>

<blockquote>One reads the foxing as one reads a hand &mdash; for what it discloses of the volume's history.</blockquote>

## On the remedy

<p>Of the various procedures that have been recommended for the removal of foxing &mdash; the application of hydrogen peroxide, of sodium borohydride, of chloramine-T &mdash; I shall say only that they are best left to the professional conservator, and that even in skilled hands they are not without hazard to the paper. The amateur is advised to attempt nothing, and to content himself with the stabilisation of the volume's environment: a steady temperature, a moderate humidity, and an absence of dust. The spots, once formed, are unlikely to disappear; but they need not advance.</p>

<p>I have come, in the course of these studies, to regard foxing less as a defect than as a record. It is the visible memory of every place the book has been, every season it has weathered, every hand that has held it. To remove it would be, in some measure, to falsify the volume.</p>
