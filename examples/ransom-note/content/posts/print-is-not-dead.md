Every eighteen months, like clockwork, a digital publisher runs a piece announcing that print is dead. Every eighteen months, the *New York Times* prints several million physical copies. We note this irony from our undisclosed location with considerable satisfaction.

Print is not dead. Print is *watching you.*

## THE PRINT PARADOX

Here is what the obituary writers consistently miss: the death of *mass-market print* is not the same as the death of *print as a medium.* The former is an economic story. The latter would be a civilizational one.

What has actually happened is more interesting and considerably more inconvenient for the "print is dead" thesis:

- **Print has become selective.** The publications that survive are the ones people *choose* to pay for, hold, fold, and return to.
- **Print has become dense.** The casual content has evacuated to the feed. What remains on paper tends to be longer, more considered, and more demanding of the reader.
- **Print has become physical evidence.** In a world of infinitely editable digital text, a printed and dated page is a *document.* It cannot be quietly revised at 2 a.m.

> "The scroll is still the scroll. The codex is still the codex. The broadsheet is still the broadsheet. Only the people who never read them think they have disappeared."

---

## A BRIEF HISTORY OF PRINT DEATH ANNOUNCEMENTS

The telegram was going to kill the newspaper. The telephone was going to kill the telegram. Radio was going to kill the newspaper. Television was going to kill radio. The internet was going to kill television. The smartphone was going to kill the desktop internet.

*Each of these predictions was partially correct.* Mass market shares shifted. Revenue models collapsed and rebuilt. But the fundamental human appetite for **a bounded, finished artifact** — something that says *this is what we knew on this day* — has not gone anywhere.

### What Print Now Demands From You

This is the part that the industry finds inconvenient. Print, in its current form, demands more from the reader, not less:

1. You must *sit still long enough to read it*
2. You must *tolerate the absence of hyperlinks*
3. You must *accept that the author has finished speaking* — no comment section, no quote-tweet
4. You must *carry it with you* if you want to continue reading

These are not weaknesses. These are **terms.** Print has become a subscription to slowness, and the people accepting those terms are doing so deliberately.

---

## THE DEMAND

The correspondent hereby requires that publications, designers, and media theorists:

- Stop writing print obituaries until print has actually stopped printing
- Acknowledge that **constraint is a feature**, not a bug, of physical media
- Recognize that `ink on paper` carries a different epistemological weight than `text in a database`

The RANSOM NOTE is, itself, a print artifact that happens to exist on a screen. We consider this appropriate. The terms remain non-negotiable.
