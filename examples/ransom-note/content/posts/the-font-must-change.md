We have been watching. We have been *patient.* We are no longer patient.

For the fourth consecutive quarter, the global design community has defaulted to Helvetica Neue as if it were oxygen — invisible, assumed, non-negotiable. This dispatch serves as formal notice: **the font must change.**

## THE EVIDENCE

Consider the following crimes, committed in broad daylight:

- A fintech startup that launched with a logo set in **Helvetica Light**, kerning optional
- A museum exhibition on *the history of letterpress printing* whose signage used — we are not inventing this — Arial
- A newspaper redesign that replaced its century-old gothic masthead with something described in the brief as "clean and modern"

*Clean.* They said *clean.* As though the accumulated character of a century of printing was merely a sanitation problem.

> "To choose a typeface thoughtlessly is to speak without inflection. Every word delivered at the same pitch. Every sentence a monotone demand."

---

## THE DEMAND

The correspondent hereby requires, under pain of continued strongly-worded dispatches, that all parties involved in the commission of typeface selection:

1. Open a type specimen book before specifying anything
2. Consider what the *letterform itself* communicates, independent of the words
3. Ask whether the typeface has *personality* or merely *legibility*
4. Understand that legibility without character is a ransom note with no ransom

### What "Clean" Actually Means

There is a persistent confusion in the design industry between **clarity** and **blandness.** Clarity is a virtue. A well-set page of Garamond is supremely clear. It is also *alive.* You can feel the hand that cut the punch, the press that pulled the proof.

A page set in Helvetica Light at 12/16 is equally legible. But it says nothing about itself. It has laundered its personality through fourteen layers of "modernity" until nothing human remains.

This is not design. This is **camouflage.**

### The Alternatives Exist

`Freight Text.` `Caslon.` `Antique Olive.` `FF Scala.` `Plantin.` The shelves are full. The reading public deserves better than the designer's failure of imagination dressed up as restraint.

*Do not disappoint us.*
