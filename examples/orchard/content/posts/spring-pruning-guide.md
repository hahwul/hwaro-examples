Pruning is one of the most important tasks in the orchard calendar, and early spring -- while the trees are still dormant but the worst of winter is past -- is the ideal time for most fruit trees. A well-pruned tree produces better fruit, resists disease more effectively, and lives longer.

## Why Prune in Spring

The goal is to prune after the danger of severe cold has passed but before the buds begin to swell. In most temperate climates, this window falls between late February and mid-March. Pruning during dormancy means the tree is not actively growing, which reduces stress and minimizes the risk of disease entering fresh cuts.

## The Four Principles

Every pruning decision comes down to four objectives:

**Remove the dead.** Start by cutting out any branches that are clearly dead, damaged, or diseased. This is straightforward and always correct.

**Open the center.** Most fruit trees benefit from an open center or modified central leader shape. Remove branches that cross through the interior of the canopy. The goal is to allow sunlight and air to reach every part of the tree.

**Reduce crowding.** Where two branches compete for the same space, keep the one with the better angle -- ideally around 45 to 60 degrees from the trunk. Branches that grow straight up (water sprouts) or straight down should generally be removed.

**Shape for the future.** Think about where you want fruit next year and the year after. Pruning is not just corrective; it is directive. Each cut encourages the tree to grow in a particular way.

## Tools

Keep your tools sharp and clean. A pair of bypass pruners handles branches up to three-quarters of an inch. Loppers extend your reach and cutting capacity to about two inches. A pruning saw manages anything larger. Wipe blades with rubbing alcohol between trees to avoid spreading disease.

## Common Mistakes

The most frequent error is timidity. Many gardeners prune too little rather than too much. A healthy fruit tree can tolerate the removal of up to a quarter of its canopy in a single season without difficulty. Under-pruning leads to dense, shaded interiors that harbor fungal problems and produce small, poorly colored fruit.

The second most common mistake is topping -- cutting back the main leader or all branches to the same height. This produces a flush of weak, upright growth that is structurally unsound and unproductive.

## After Pruning

Clean up all cuttings from the ground beneath the tree. Diseased wood should be burned or disposed of, not composted. Healthy prunings can be chipped for mulch. Do not paint or seal the cuts -- research consistently shows that trees heal better when wounds are left open to air.
