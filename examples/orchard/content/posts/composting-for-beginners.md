Composting is the single most impactful thing a home gardener can do. It transforms kitchen scraps and garden waste into rich, dark humus that feeds the soil, improves its structure, and supports the invisible ecosystem of organisms that make plants thrive. It is not complicated, but understanding a few principles makes the difference between a productive compost pile and a smelly, stagnant heap.

## The Basics

Composting is controlled decomposition. Microorganisms break down organic material in the presence of oxygen and moisture, generating heat as a byproduct. Your job is to provide the right balance of materials and conditions.

## Greens and Browns

All compostable materials fall into two broad categories:

**Greens** are nitrogen-rich materials: vegetable scraps, fruit peels, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings, and garden trimmings. They provide the protein that fuels microbial activity.

**Browns** are carbon-rich materials: dried leaves, straw, cardboard, newspaper, wood chips, and dry plant stalks. They provide energy and structure, keeping the pile aerated.

The ideal ratio is roughly three parts brown to one part green by volume. Too many greens and the pile becomes wet, compacted, and odorous. Too many browns and decomposition stalls for lack of nitrogen.

## Choosing a Method

**Open pile.** The simplest approach. Heap materials in a corner of the garden, at least three feet in each dimension. Turn with a fork every week or two. Free, effective, and low-maintenance.

**Enclosed bin.** A bin contains the pile neatly, retains heat better, and discourages animals. Commercial tumbler bins make turning easier. Good for smaller spaces.

**Trench composting.** Dig a trench twelve inches deep, fill with kitchen scraps, and cover with soil. The material decomposes in place. No turning, no bin, no maintenance. Particularly useful for overwintering.

## What to Compost

Most plant-based kitchen waste is fair game: vegetable and fruit scraps, eggshells, coffee grounds and filters, tea bags (without staples), and stale bread. From the garden: leaves, spent plants, weeds (without seeds), and small prunings.

## What to Avoid

Do not compost meat, fish, dairy, or oily foods in a standard home pile -- they attract pests and create odor problems. Avoid diseased plant material, weed seeds, pet waste, and anything treated with persistent herbicides.

## Troubleshooting

**Bad smell.** The pile is too wet or has too many greens. Add browns, turn to aerate, and mix thoroughly.

**Not heating up.** The pile is too dry, too small, or lacks nitrogen. Add greens, moisten if needed, and ensure the pile is at least three feet across.

**Attracting pests.** Bury food scraps in the center of the pile, cover with a thick layer of browns, and avoid adding meat or dairy.

## Using Finished Compost

Finished compost is dark, crumbly, and smells like clean earth. Work it into garden beds before planting, use it as mulch around established plants, or brew it into compost tea for a liquid feed. There is no such thing as too much good compost.
