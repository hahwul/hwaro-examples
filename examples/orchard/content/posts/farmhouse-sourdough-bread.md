Sourdough is the oldest form of leavened bread, and in many ways it remains the best. This recipe produces a rustic, well-risen loaf with a crackling crust and a moist, slightly tangy interior. The method is forgiving and does not require precise timing -- the dough waits for you, not the other way around.

## Before You Begin

You will need an active sourdough starter. If you do not have one, it takes about a week to create from scratch using nothing more than flour and water. Feed your starter the night before baking so it is bubbly and active when you begin mixing the dough in the morning.

## Ingredients

- 100 grams active sourdough starter
- 375 grams lukewarm water
- 500 grams bread flour (or a mix of 400 grams bread flour and 100 grams whole wheat)
- 10 grams fine sea salt

## The Levain

The night before you plan to bake, feed your starter with equal parts flour and water. By morning it should have roughly doubled in volume and be full of bubbles. The float test works well here: drop a small spoonful into water. If it floats, the starter is ready.

## Method

1. **Mix.** Combine the starter and water in a large bowl, stirring until the starter dissolves. Add the flour and mix with your hands or a wooden spoon until no dry patches remain. The dough will be shaggy and rough. Let it rest, covered, for thirty minutes.

2. **Salt.** Sprinkle the salt over the dough and work it in by folding and squeezing. The dough will feel tighter and slightly slippery from the salt.

3. **Bulk fermentation.** Over the next four to five hours, perform a series of stretch-and-folds: wet your hand, reach under the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over itself. Rotate the bowl ninety degrees and repeat. Do this every thirty minutes for the first two hours, then leave the dough undisturbed for the remaining time. It should increase in volume by roughly fifty percent and feel airy when you tilt the bowl.

4. **Shape.** Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Shape it into a round by pulling the edges toward the center, then flipping it seam-side down and using the friction of the counter to create surface tension. Let it rest ten minutes, then shape again more tightly.

5. **Cold proof.** Place the shaped dough seam-side up in a floured banneton or a bowl lined with a floured tea towel. Cover and refrigerate overnight, or for at least twelve hours.

6. **Bake.** Preheat your oven to 500 degrees Fahrenheit with a Dutch oven inside for at least forty-five minutes. Turn the cold dough out onto parchment paper, score the top with a sharp blade, and lower it into the Dutch oven. Cover and bake twenty minutes, then remove the lid, reduce to 450 degrees, and bake another twenty to twenty-five minutes until deeply browned.

7. **Cool.** This is the hardest part. Let the bread cool on a wire rack for at least one hour before cutting. The interior continues cooking as it cools, and cutting too early results in a gummy crumb.

## Notes

The beauty of this bread lies in its adaptability. A warmer kitchen accelerates fermentation; a cooler one slows it. The cold overnight proof develops flavor and makes the dough easier to handle. Adjust timing to fit your life -- sourdough is patient by nature.
