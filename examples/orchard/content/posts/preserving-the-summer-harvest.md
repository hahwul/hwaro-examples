The peak of summer brings an abundance that no family can eat fresh. Tomatoes ripen faster than you can slice them. Cucumbers multiply overnight. Stone fruit drops from the branches. Preservation is the art of capturing this fleeting abundance and extending it through the lean months.

## Water Bath Canning

Water bath canning is the most traditional method and works well for high-acid foods: tomatoes (with added acid), fruits, pickles, jams, and fruit butters.

The principle is straightforward. Food is packed into sterilized jars, sealed with two-piece lids, and submerged in boiling water for a specified time. The heat destroys spoilage organisms, and as the jars cool, a vacuum seal forms.

**Essential equipment:** A large stockpot with a rack (a purpose-built canner is nice but not necessary), jar lifter, canning funnel, and new lids for each batch.

**Key rules:** Follow tested recipes from reliable sources. Do not reduce acid or alter proportions. Process for the full recommended time adjusted for your altitude.

## Freezing

Freezing is the simplest preservation method and retains flavor and nutrition well. It works for nearly everything: berries, sliced stone fruit, blanched vegetables, herbs in olive oil, and prepared sauces.

**Blanching** is essential for most vegetables. A brief dip in boiling water followed by an ice bath stops enzyme activity that causes flavor and texture degradation over time. Green beans, corn, peas, and broccoli all benefit from blanching before freezing.

Spread items in a single layer on a sheet pan to freeze individually before transferring to bags. This prevents the dreaded frozen block and allows you to use just what you need.

## Drying

Dehydration concentrates flavors and creates shelf-stable ingredients that take up very little space. A food dehydrator gives the most consistent results, but a low oven works adequately.

**Tomatoes** are magnificent dried. Halve paste tomatoes, sprinkle lightly with salt, and dry at 135 degrees until leathery but still pliable. Store in olive oil for a pantry staple of remarkable versatility.

**Herbs** dry beautifully tied in bundles and hung in a warm, airy space. Oregano, thyme, rosemary, and sage retain their potency well. More delicate herbs like basil and cilantro are better frozen.

**Fruit leather** is made by pureeing fruit, spreading it thin on lined dehydrator trays, and drying until pliable. It is an honest, wholesome alternative to commercial candy.

## Fermentation

Fermentation is the oldest preservation method and produces foods with complex flavors and genuine health benefits. Lactic acid fermentation requires nothing more than vegetables, salt, and time.

**Sauerkraut** is the gateway ferment. Shred cabbage, toss with salt (about two percent by weight), pack into a jar, and keep submerged under brine for one to four weeks. The transformation from raw cabbage to tangy, complex sauerkraut is remarkable.

**Fermented hot sauce, pickles, and kimchi** all follow the same fundamental principle: salt creates an environment that favors beneficial lactobacillus bacteria while suppressing harmful organisms.

## Planning Ahead

The key to successful preservation is planning before the harvest arrives. Stock up on jars, lids, and salt. Clear freezer space. Test your dehydrator. When the tomatoes start ripening all at once, you want to be ready -- the window of peak quality is brief, and the best preserves begin with the best raw ingredients.
