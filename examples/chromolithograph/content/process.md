## Upon the Stone

The stone employed is a fine-grained limestone quarried in the neighbourhood of Solnhofen in Bavaria, where its extraction has been the business of the same families for five generations. A block is sawn, ground perfectly flat upon a bed of sand and water, and polished with pumice until it will receive the lightest touch of the greasy chalk without blurring. A single volume such as this one may require the dressing of twenty or thirty stones, one for each colour of each plate.

The draughtsman lays the design upon the first stone in a greasy crayon, reserving for subsequent stones the parts of the image which are to receive a different colour. The separation of the design into its colour layers is the particular genius of the chromolithographer - an art which cannot be taught by book, only by long apprenticeship.

## Registration

Each stone is printed in its turn upon the same sheet of paper, which must be returned to the same position with the greatest precision. This is accomplished by small needle holes in two corners of the sheet, which are dropped over pins upon the press bed. The eye may detect a misregistration of a twentieth of an inch; the pressman's hand must be within a hundredth.

## The Tissue Guard

When the five impressions have been struck and the sheet lifted from the press, the colours are laid down in a state of some moisture. They cannot be bound against a facing page until they are quite dry, lest the pigment should offset. A leaf of fine translucent tissue - commonly of Japanese manufacture - is interposed between each plate and the facing page, both for its decorative appearance and for the protection of the colours during the century of use which the volume is expected to enjoy.

The reader is requested to lift the tissue guard with some reverence. A torn tissue cannot easily be replaced.

## Binding

The sheets are gathered in signatures of sixteen pages, sewn upon cords, and bound in half-morocco over cloth. The spine is stamped with gold upon six raised bands; the boards are blocked in black with the arms of the press and the title of the volume. Edges are gilded all round upon the top, and left uncut upon the fore-edge and tail, that the reader may appreciate the full measure of the paper.
