## Description

The specimen figured is of the common Whitby ammonite, taken from the Alum Shales of the Lias, where it occurs in company with gryphaea and belemnite. The Rev. Whitlock, whose private cabinet at Grosmont has furnished much of the material for this volume, prepared the section by grinding upon a lapidary's wheel, thereby revealing the chambered interior which is the most instructive feature of the shell.

The plate shows the specimen somewhat reduced, a scale in inches being given beneath. The chambers, or camerae, communicate through a siphuncle which passed along the ventral margin of the whorl - the pressman has coloured these in contrasting fields of red, green, yellow and blue, that the student may readily distinguish one chamber from its neighbour.

## Upon the Animal

Of the creature which inhabited this shell we have little direct knowledge, the soft parts being soldom preserved. By analogy with the living Nautilus, we suppose a cephalopod of the squid's general disposition, tentacled and carnivorous, occupying the terminal chamber and jet-propelled upon the ejection of water. The septa closed off the older chambers, which served as buoyancy tanks.

## Stratigraphical Note

The Whitby Alum Shales belong to the Upper Pliensbachian and Lower Toarcian, and have yielded the most complete succession of British ammonites of the period. Collectors should consult the monograph of Buckman for the detailed zonation, and may purchase further specimens of the jet-workers at Whitby during the summer.
