## Description

This most splendid of the Japanese buprestids was known to the Japanese craftsmen for many centuries before it came to the attention of Western science. The wing cases, preserved after death, retain their brilliant colour for decades, and were formerly employed by the lacquer-workers of Kyoto in the decoration of the Tamamushi shrine and similar objects of veneration.

The specimen figured was secured by Mr A. R. Wallace during his second journey through the archipelago. It is shown life size, and the pressman has been at some pains to convey the metallic brilliance by the juxtaposition of flat fields: green upon the outer elytra, carmine upon a central stripe, and gold upon the innermost division. The true iridescence, depending as it does upon the microscopic structure of the cuticle, is beyond the reach of the stone.

## Upon the Cuticle

The metallic appearance arises not from any pigment within the wing case but from the interference of light between superposed lamellae of chitin. This has been elegantly demonstrated by Lord Rayleigh in a recent communication to the Royal Society, though the means of the effect was known, in a practical way, to the Japanese craftsmen long before the discovery of the principle.

## Habits

The imago emerges in midsummer from host trees of the Zelkova and Celtis, feeding upon the foliage and depositing its eggs in the bark of the same. The larva is a xylophagous grub of some years' development before pupation.
