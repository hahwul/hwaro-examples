Every mechanical connection in an electrical panel has a manufacturer-specified torque value. The value is usually printed on a label inside the panel door, stamped on the lug itself, or referenced in the installation sheet packed with the breaker. Until the 2017 code cycle, calibrated torque tools were rarely used in residential work. They are now required by Article 110.14(D).

<!-- more -->

### Why Torque Matters

A lug holds a conductor against a current-carrying bus through compression. If the connection is too loose, the contact area is small and the resistance at the joint is high. Resistance dissipates heat, which oxidizes the metals and increases resistance further. The joint enters thermal runaway and eventually fails, usually in a way that scorches the panel and trips upstream protection only after damage has occurred.

Overtightening is also harmful. Aluminum conductors deform plastically when compressed past their yield point. The lug body itself can crack. A connection that has been overtightened may pass an initial inspection and fail months later when the conductor cold-flows out from under the lug.

### Typical Values

Residential breaker lugs cluster around fifteen to thirty inch-pounds for fourteen and twelve gauge wire. Service entrance lugs on a two hundred ampere panel run between two hundred and seventy-five and three hundred seventy-five inch-pounds. The exact number varies by manufacturer, conductor material, and lug geometry. The label is authoritative; published averages are not.

### Verification

A calibrated click-type torque screwdriver is the most common tool for the smaller values. Larger service connections use a beam or dial torque wrench with appropriate sockets. Verification is performed once at installation and again during any maintenance that disturbs the connection. Re-torquing on a fixed schedule is not required and may cause harm by repeatedly working the conductor.
