Visitors sometimes ask why a registry — a thing of columns and figures — should be dressed in fans, chevrons, and stepped frames. The answer is that ornament is a form of accountability. A hall that takes its appearance seriously is announcing that it takes its records seriously.

## What The Ornament Signals

The Deco grammar of the Exchange is not decoration for its own sake. Each motif carries a meaning the clerks recognise:

- The **sunburst fan** marks the opening of the floor, radiating from the dais.
- The **stepped frame** denotes a sealed holding — built up, deliberate, immovable.
- The **diamond** is the clerk's mark, set between sections as a sign of reconciliation.
- The **chevron rule** divides one day's ledger from the next.

A clerk once described the house style to a new member in a single line:

```
ornament := signal( record_is_witnessed )
```

> The gold is not there to flatter the figures. It is there to remind us that the figures were earned.

We keep to the grammar because it keeps us honest. When every seal must be drawn with the same care as every sum, neither is allowed to slip. The hall and the ledger hold each other to the same standard.
