This log was kept the way most ocean logs are kept: in pencil, at noon, in a notebook that got progressively less waterproof the longer the passage went on. What follows here is a cleaned-up version of six of those twelve entries — the days that mattered, in the order they happened, from the last sight of the Lizard to the wall at Horta.

I have changed almost nothing beyond spelling. The positions are the positions the sextant and the plotter agreed on; the winds are the winds as logged, not as later exaggerated; the distances are the distances the log actually shows, including the thirty-eight miles that make day seven the least impressive entry in the book and, I think now, one of the better days of the whole crossing.

Read the six entries in order — each one links to the next through **Day** and each carries the noon position it was written under, the way a real logbook does. Or start wherever the passage log below draws your eye. The boat does not mind which day you begin on. She only minds that you finish somewhere near Horta.
