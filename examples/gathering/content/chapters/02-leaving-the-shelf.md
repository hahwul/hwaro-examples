The echo sounder gave up at 1420 on the second full day, the display flicking from a tidy ninety-one metres to a dash where a number should be, and that dash is as good a border as any I have crossed. Behind it lay the continental shelf: fishing grounds, shipping lanes, a seabed you could point to on a chart with some confidence. Ahead lay four thousand metres of water and, underneath that, whatever an ocean actually rests on, which I have never needed to picture until now.

## The swell changes its mind

Shelf swell and ocean swell are not the same animal, and *Selkie* told me so before I worked it out myself. Through the Chops of the Channel the sea had been short and confused, chopped up by shoaling water and the wakes of everything with an engine. By evening on day three it had lengthened into something with real intention behind it — long low-frequency rollers out of the southwest, a twelve-second period, the boat lifting and setting down again with a patience the inshore sea never has. I stopped bracing for impacts that weren't coming and started, without deciding to, moving with a bigger rhythm.

I crossed the Ushant traffic separation scheme in the last of the daylight, three ships hull-down to the south all holding their lanes with the calm indifference of buses on a timetable, and once through it the VHF went quiet in a way it hadn't been since Falmouth. No more coastal chatter, no more forecast area names I knew by heart. Wind held southwest four to five, the self-steering vane finally earning its keep on a fetch it liked, and I logged noon at 47°52′N 007°41′W with a hundred and thirty-two miles on the clock since the day before — the first full day's run that felt like the passage proper had begun, rather than merely the leaving of somewhere.
