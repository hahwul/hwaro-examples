The GRIB file had shown nothing worse than a ridge of scattered cells, the kind of forecast noise you learn to read past on a long passage. What it could not show me was the one cell that mattered, a hard black line that came up from the northwest a little after two in the morning and announced itself with a wind shift so abrupt that the boat rounded up before I had properly finished swearing about the noise on the coachroof.

## Reefing by feel

I have practised reefing in daylight, in a marina, with the sail bagged and forgiving and no consequence to getting it wrong. Reefing at 0210 in thirty-five knots of gusting wind with rain coming sideways is a different discipline entirely, done almost entirely by feel: the halyard by the third turn on the winch drum, the tack hook by the shape of the grommet under a wet glove, the clew line hauled hand over hand while bracing a knee against the boom gallows and trying not to think too hard about the harness tether being the only thing keeping the next lurch from being a swim.

It took perhaps four minutes, though the log entry I made afterwards, hands still shaking slightly, guessed twenty. Under the second reef and a scrap of staysail *Selkie* settled immediately, heeling hard but tracking true, and the same wind that had frightened me at full sail became, reduced, almost exhilarating — a boat doing exactly what she was built for, with a skipper finally out of her way.

By dawn the squall line had passed through and left a hard, clean northwesterly behind it, six to seven, the best sailing breeze of the whole crossing. Noon position 41°05′N 020°05′W, a hundred and fifty-one miles run, the biggest day's distance of the passage — logged, this time, with the specific, slightly incredulous pride of someone who did not embarrass themselves when it mattered.
