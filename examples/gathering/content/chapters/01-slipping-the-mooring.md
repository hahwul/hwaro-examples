The forecast had not changed overnight, which was the only permission I was waiting for. A slow-moving high sat over Biscay, a weak front trailing off Ireland that would clear the Lizard by noon, and nothing named in the shipping forecast between here and the horizon. I signed the customs form at the marina office at six, drank the last coffee I would make against a wall that wasn't moving, and rowed out to *Selkie* in the flat grey light before the town was properly awake.

## Clearing the bar

There is a specific, almost administrative feeling to the last hour before departure that nobody mentions in the books. I checked the stove gimbals, the liferaft hydrostatic release, the second reef line I had re-whipped the week before, and found myself checking them again for no reason except that my hands wanted something to do. At 0740 I called Falmouth Coastguard on channel sixteen, gave them my passage plan — Falmouth to Horta, single-handed, expected twelve to fifteen days, next contact by satellite tracker — and heard my own voice sound steadier than I felt.

Motoring out past Black Rock the water was olive-green and busy with the ordinary traffic of a Tuesday: a pilot boat, two crabbers, a training yacht full of shouting teenagers who did not look up. None of them knew, or had reason to know, that I was leaving for the Azores. That anonymity felt right. A big departure wants an audience; this one wanted quiet.

I raised the main off St Anthony, killed the engine at 0905, and for a moment there was only the creak of the boom and water going past the hull. Behind me the Lizard was still a solid green line. By the time I next looked up it was a smudge, and by early afternoon, logged at 1258 against a position of 49°58′N 005°12′W, it was gone. Eighteen miles run since the harbour mouth. Twelve hundred and two to go, by the great-circle measure I am not foolish enough to expect the wind to honour.
