By the fifth day the passage had stopped being an event and become a job, which is the best thing that can happen to a passage. The wind settled into the northwest, five to six, and stayed there long enough that I trimmed the poled-out genoa once at dawn and did not touch a sheet again until dusk. *Selkie* held her line under vane steering with a steadiness I have never quite managed by hand, and for the first time since Falmouth I slept in proper blocks instead of the anxious twenty-minute doses of the shelf and its shipping.

## Ninety minutes at a time

The routine that emerged, unplanned, was ninety minutes on watch and ninety off, day and night, adjusted only for cooking, sail changes, and the noon sight. Ninety minutes is long enough to actually fall asleep and short enough that nothing gets far out of hand before I am back on deck. I cooked on the gimballed stove properly for the first time — a lentil stew that took forty careful minutes and rewarded every one of them — and ate it in the cockpit watching the wake go pale and phosphorescent as the light dropped.

A pod of common dolphins found the boat mid-morning and stayed the better part of an hour, running the bow wave in the effortless, slightly showing-off way they have, peeling off only when a fulmar started working the same patch of water and seemed to bore them. I have read every account of ocean sailing that mentions dolphins and always suspected the writers of sentimentality. I take it back. There is something in being noticed, out here, by anything at all, that undoes a person a little.

Noon position 45°30′N 011°15′W, a hundred and forty-five miles logged since yesterday — the best day's run so far, and one I recorded in the log with more satisfaction than the number strictly warrants. Wind still northwest, backing slowly. I do not entirely trust a passage that is going this well, and I have started saying so out loud to the boat, which either means the solitude is working exactly as advertised or that it has already won.
