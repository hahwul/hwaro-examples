The barometer had been climbing since the westerlies backed, and I knew what it meant before the sails confirmed it at first light: the genoa hanging slack, the main slatting with each roll, the boom preventer earning wages it hadn't in five days. I had sailed into the eastern shoulder of the Azores High, the same slow-turning system that gives the islands their gentle summers, and it had no intention of giving me any of its wind.

## The diesel arithmetic

I carry sixty litres, enough for roughly forty hours of motoring at an economical revs, and I had already promised myself those hours were for the doldrums-adjacent nothing near the islands themselves, not for a mid-ocean high I might sail out of the northern edge of if I was patient. So I did the arithmetic twice, decided patience was cheaper than diesel, and let the boat drift-sail at a knot and a half on whatever cat's-paws crossed the swell, adjusting sheets for eddies of wind I could see arriving on the water two hundred metres out.

Becalmed, mid-Atlantic, four thousand metres of water underneath and not another vessel on the AIS in any direction, I did the thing every solo sailor's account of a high threatens to do and I finally understood why: I stopped the boat, checked twice for anything with fins, and went over the side on a long line clipped to the stern cleat. The water was startlingly clear and startlingly cold for June, and *Selkie*'s hull, seen whole for the first time since the yard, looked smaller and more capable than she does from on deck.

Thirty-eight miles logged by noon, position 43°10′N 015°40′W, most of it made good before the wind died entirely around ten. Not a distinguished day's run. I would not trade it.
