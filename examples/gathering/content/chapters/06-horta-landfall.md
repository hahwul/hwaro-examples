Pico announced itself before Faial did, the way a mountain that tall always beats the island underneath it to the horizon: a grey cone above a shelf of cloud, disbelieved for a full hour before I let myself log it, then confirmed against the chart with something close to relief. I had crossed twelve hundred miles of ocean navigating mostly by sun sights and dead reckoning, backed up by a satellite plotter I trusted and checked constantly, and the mountain was still the moment the whole passage became real rather than merely correct.

## The wall

The last ninety-six miles took the better part of a day and a night, the wind falling light and fluky in Faial's lee, and I was glad of the slowness in the end — it let Horta arrive properly instead of all at once. I motored the final approach past the breakwater a little after nine in the morning, tied up in the visitors' marina to a chorus of shouted advice from three boats who had clearly been waiting all season for someone new to arrive, and stood on a dock that did not move for the first time in twelve days, swaying anyway.

Horta keeps a tradition every ocean sailor learns about eventually and only half believes until they see it: you paint your boat's name on the harbour wall, or the passage is said to bring you bad luck on the next one. I bought paint from a chandlery two streets back, borrowed a brush I never returned, and spent an afternoon adding *Selkie*, Falmouth to Horta, twelve days, single-handed, June 2025 to a wall already covered in decades of other people's crossings, some faded to ghosts, some fresh enough to still smell of turpentine.

That evening, at the harbourside bar everyone eventually washes up at, I ordered the first drink I hadn't made myself in nearly two weeks and opened the logbook to the last blank line. Noon position, the final entry: 38°32′N 028°37′W. Distance run since Falmouth: one thousand, two hundred and eleven nautical miles. Nothing left to log but the date, and the fact that I would, without much doubt, do it again.
