Berry Gordy did not want to release this album. The head of Motown thought it was uncommercial, too political, too far from the hit-making formula that had built his empire. Marvin Gaye refused to record anything else until Gordy relented. Gordy relented. The album sold over two million copies in its first year.

## The Context

By 1970, Gaye's brother Frankie had returned from Vietnam. The stories he brought back -- combined with the social upheaval Gaye witnessed from his window in Detroit -- shattered the singer's ability to keep making party music. He needed to say something real.

The title track began as a song written by Renaldo Benson of the Four Tops after witnessing police brutality at a protest in Berkeley. Gaye took the idea and transformed it into something universal -- a plea for empathy wrapped in one of the most beautiful vocal performances ever captured on tape.

## The Innovation

*What's Going On* was revolutionary in its construction. Gaye insisted on recording the entire album as a continuous suite, with songs flowing into each other through shared musical motifs and ambient interludes. The party chatter that opens the record -- real conversation from a gathering of friends -- establishes an intimacy that never breaks.

The arrangements, by David Van DePitte under Gaye's direction, layer strings, congas, saxophone, and multiple vocal tracks into something dense but never cluttered. Gaye overdubbed his own voice several times, creating harmonies with himself that sound like a congregation of one.

## The Message

"Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)" addressed environmental destruction half a century before it became a mainstream concern. "Inner City Blues (Make Me Wanna Holler)" documented the frustration of Black America with a directness that Motown had never allowed. "Save the Children" asked the question that no political system has adequately answered.

Yet the album never preaches. Gaye's delivery is conversational, tender, sometimes barely above a whisper. He sounds like a man talking to a friend, not shouting from a podium.

## The Verdict

*What's Going On* proved that popular music could be both commercially successful and artistically uncompromising. It is an album about war, poverty, and environmental collapse that somehow makes you feel held rather than lectured. Gaye's voice -- that impossible instrument -- carries the weight of the world and makes it sound like grace. This is what a perfect record sounds like.
