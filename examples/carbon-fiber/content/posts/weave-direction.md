A single layer of carbon fiber cloth is strong along the direction of the fibers and weak across them. A finished part has to behave well in every direction it might be loaded, which is why every carbon component is built from multiple layers laminated at different angles.

## Common Layups

- **0° / 90° / 0°** — the simplest balanced layup. Stiff in two directions, weaker in the diagonals. Used for plates and simple panels.
- **0° / +45° / -45° / 90°** — the standard quasi-isotropic layup. Behaves nearly the same in every direction. Used for tubes, frames, and structural shells.
- **Unidirectional with selective biaxial reinforcement** — the racing approach. Most fibers run in the loaded direction; biaxial layers are added only where the part is locally torsioned.

## What the Surface Tells You

The visible weave on a finished part is almost always a cosmetic top layer. The real structural fiber is underneath, oriented for load — not for show. A part that looks plain often has more thoughtful internal layup than a part that shows off its 2x2 twill.
