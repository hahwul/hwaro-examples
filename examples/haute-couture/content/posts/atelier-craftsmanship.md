A working couture atelier is divided by tradition into two principal workshops. The flou handles soft, draped construction in silk, chiffon, and lighter wools. The tailleur handles structured construction in heavier wools, gabardines, and outerwear fabrics. Each workshop is led by a premiere who oversees a team of seamstresses and apprentices, and each garment passes through a defined sequence of stages before it leaves the building.

## The Toile

Every couture piece begins as a toile, a working pattern executed in plain cotton calico to test proportion, drape, and fit. The client is fitted in the toile two or three times before the actual fabric is cut, with adjustments marked in tailor's chalk and transferred back to the master pattern. This stage absorbs the bulk of the design decisions and may take several weeks for a complex evening look.

## Cutting and Assembly

Final fabric is cut on a long table with a single layer to preserve the grain and pattern register. Pieces are basted together by hand for the first fitting, then taken apart and reassembled with permanent stitching once the fit is confirmed. Linings, interfacings, and structural elements such as horsehair canvas in jacket fronts are inserted in parallel with the outer construction.

## Hand Finishing

The final stage is reserved for hand finishing: pickstitched edges, hand-rolled hems, covered buttons, bound buttonholes, and any embroidery or beadwork from the specialist embroidery houses such as Lesage or Lemarié. A simple day dress may carry forty hours of hand finishing. An embroidered evening gown can require several hundred hours across multiple workshops before it is ready for the final fitting.
