A circular bacterial chromosome of four million base pairs contains roughly four hundred thousand turns of the double helix. Every time the replication machinery advances one turn along the template, an equivalent positive supercoil accumulates ahead of the fork. Without a mechanism to relieve the strain, the fork stalls within seconds. Topoisomerases solve the problem by introducing transient breaks in the DNA backbone, allowing controlled rotation, and resealing the backbone when the topology has been adjusted.

## Type I Enzymes

Type I topoisomerases break a single strand of the duplex, pass the intact strand through the gap, and rejoin the cut strand. The enzyme covalently attaches to one end of the broken strand through a phosphotyrosine intermediate, holding the energy of the broken phosphodiester bond and obviating the need for ATP. Each catalytic cycle changes the linking number by one. The reaction is processive in some bacterial enzymes and distributive in others.

## Type II Enzymes

Type II topoisomerases break both strands of one duplex and pass a second duplex through the resulting gap. The reaction requires ATP and changes the linking number by two per cycle. DNA gyrase, the bacterial type II enzyme, is unusual in its ability to introduce negative supercoils into relaxed DNA, a function essential for chromosome compaction and the regulation of transcription.

## Therapeutic Targeting

The catalytic cycle of type II topoisomerases, in particular the transient double-strand break, is a target for several classes of antibacterial and anticancer drugs. Quinolone antibiotics trap the bacterial gyrase in the cleaved state, converting the enzyme into a source of lethal double-strand breaks. Etoposide and doxorubicin act analogously on human topoisomerase II in tumour cells. The therapeutic window depends on differences in expression level and substrate specificity between the bacterial and human enzymes.
