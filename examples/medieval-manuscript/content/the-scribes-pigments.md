Brother Cuthbert, who keeps the cabinet of pigments, has set down this account at the request of the abbot. The cabinet holds nine principal colors, each prepared in the manner taught to him by the elder scribes who came before.

For black ink, oak galls are crushed and steeped in rainwater for a fortnight, then bound with iron sulfate and a measure of gum arabic. The ink flows well from a quill cut from the swan, less well from the goose. It darkens over years, which is why the older codices in the lower library appear nearly opaque while a fresh page is the color of strong tea.

### The Reds

Vermilion, made from cinnabar, must be ground in small batches. The dust is harmful when inhaled, and the brothers who prepare it are kept to a strict rotation. A second red is taken from the kermes insect, dried and crushed; this yields a softer hue suited to the bodies of saints rather than the mantles of kings.

### The Blues

Azurite, ground from the blue stone, gives a serviceable blue at modest cost. Lapis lazuli, brought from beyond the eastern sea, gives a far purer blue, but its cost is such that a single ounce represents the labor of a season. The abbot has decreed that lapis is reserved for the robes of the Virgin and for the script of the Gospels themselves.

### Failings of the Damp Season

In the wet months the gum binder grows soft, and the pigments lift from the page when handled. Brother Cuthbert advises that finished folios be kept pressed under weighted boards until the air returns to its proper dryness. Without this, the labor of weeks may be lost to a single careless touch.
