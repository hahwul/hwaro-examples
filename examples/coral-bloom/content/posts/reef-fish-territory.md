A single head of branching coral can host an economy. Damselfish, especially the genus Stegastes, defend small patches of dead skeleton against any other herbivore that approaches. Inside the patch, they cultivate a lawn of filamentous algae by weeding out species they do not eat and aggressively chasing parrotfish, surgeonfish, and even divers who drift too close. The territory is small, often less than a square meter, but it is held against the entire reef.

## Farmer Fish

The lawn is not incidental. Damselfish bite back competing macroalgae, drag debris out of the patch, and repeatedly graze the preferred turf species to keep it short and productive. The result is a denser, more nitrogen-rich algal community than the open reef supports. From a food-web perspective, the territory is a managed pasture defended by a single shepherd.

## Costs to the Coral

The relationship is not neutral for the coral underneath. Damselfish prefer territories on living coral because the structure provides shelter and the dead skeleton at the base supports the lawn. To enlarge a territory, they sometimes bite at the coral tissue itself, killing polyps and exposing fresh substrate for algae. On reefs already weakened by bleaching or storms, dense damselfish populations can slow recovery noticeably.

## A Useful Indicator

Because territory size, density, and aggressiveness all respond to fishing pressure and habitat condition, damselfish are useful monitors. Where larger predators have been removed, damselfish populations rise and the algal lawns expand. Where coral cover collapses, territories contract because the suitable substrate disappears. Counting and mapping these patches gives surveyors a quick read on how the reef is holding together at a small scale, which is often where reef-wide changes first show up.
