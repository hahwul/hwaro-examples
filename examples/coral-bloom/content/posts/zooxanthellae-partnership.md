Reef-building corals in tropical shallows live in close partnership with single-celled algae called zooxanthellae. The algae settle inside the gastrodermal cells of the coral polyp and continue to photosynthesize there. The polyp supplies a stable home, carbon dioxide, and waste nitrogen. The algae supply oxygen and a steady stream of fixed carbon, mostly as glucose and glycerol. In productive conditions, this transfer can cover the majority of the coral's daily energy budget.

## Why It Works in Clear Water

The arrangement depends on light. Zooxanthellae need it to photosynthesize, so the host coral has to live where light penetrates: clear water, shallow depth, low sediment load. Reefs cluster on the sunny shoulders of continental shelves and around oceanic islands for exactly this reason. Turbid estuaries and deep slopes are largely off-limits to symbiotic, reef-building species.

## When the Partnership Breaks

Heat stress disrupts the chemistry of photosynthesis inside the algae. The byproducts become toxic to the host, and the coral expels its symbionts. Without them, the tissue turns transparent and the white skeleton shows through, an event known as bleaching. A bleached coral is not yet dead. If conditions return to normal within a few weeks, surviving polyps can take on new symbionts and recover. If the stress persists, the coral starves.

The partnership is therefore both the engine of reef growth and its main vulnerability. Reefs are productive because the symbiosis is efficient, and they are fragile because the symbiosis is narrow. A degree or two of sustained warming is enough to push large areas past the threshold, and the consequences propagate through every species that depends on the structure the corals build.
