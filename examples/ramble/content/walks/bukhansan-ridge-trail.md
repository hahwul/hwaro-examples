The morning air was sharp and dry when I left the Bukhansanseong trailhead. The first kilometer is deceptive in its gentleness, a wide gravel path winding through bare deciduous trees that have not yet decided it is spring.

## The Ascent

Past the old fortress wall, the trail narrows and steepens. Granite steps, worn smooth by decades of boots, lead upward in relentless switchbacks. The trees thin out. The wind picks up. By the time I reached the first exposed section, I could see the city spreading out below like a grey quilt stitched with roads and rooftops.

The rock here is pale and rough-grained, warm to the touch even in March. Steel cables bolted into the cliff face mark the route along the ridge. Hands are as important as feet on this stretch.

## The Ridge

The ridgeline itself is narrow and exposed. To the left, a steep drop into a forested valley. To the right, another drop toward the city. The path runs along the top like a seam between two worlds.

There is no shade here. The sun was directly overhead by the time I traversed the main ridge section. Other hikers moved in single file, stepping carefully where the rock was polished. Nobody spoke much. The wind carried sound away before it could settle.

## Descent

The descent on the eastern side follows a different character entirely. Packed earth and pine needles underfoot, the trail drops through dense forest. The temperature fell noticeably in the shade. A small stream appeared beside the path, barely a trickle at this time of year.

I reached the bus stop at Ui-dong by mid-afternoon. The legs were heavy but the mind was clear, the way it only gets after sustained effort at altitude.

## Trail Notes

- Bring at least 1.5 liters of water; there is no resupply on the ridge
- Gloves are useful for the cable sections
- The Bukhansanseong entrance can get crowded on weekends; arrive before 8 AM
- The eastern descent is significantly easier on the knees
