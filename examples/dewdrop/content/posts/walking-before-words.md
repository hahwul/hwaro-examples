For the past year, I have made a single non-negotiable commitment: I walk before I write. Before I open a notebook, before I sit at a desk, before I read anyone else's words -- I walk.

## The Route Does Not Matter

I do not have a scenic path. My morning walk takes me through ordinary streets, past parked cars and quiet houses. The beauty is not in the scenery. It is in the rhythm of movement, the way your thoughts begin to untangle when your body is in motion.

## What Happens When You Walk

Researchers at Stanford found that walking increases creative output by an average of sixty percent. But the mechanism is not purely physical. Walking occupies just enough of your conscious mind -- navigating steps, avoiding puddles, noticing the weather -- that your unconscious mind is freed to work on deeper problems.

This is why so many writers and thinkers have been devoted walkers. Kierkegaard walked the streets of Copenhagen for hours. Wordsworth is estimated to have walked 175,000 miles in his lifetime. Beethoven carried manuscript paper on his daily walks through Vienna.

## The Practice

The ideal morning walk is:

- **Without headphones**: Let your thoughts be the soundtrack
- **Without destination**: Wander rather than commute
- **Without urgency**: Twenty minutes is enough, but do not watch the clock
- **Without judgment**: Some walks produce ideas, some produce nothing, both are valuable

## Returning Home

The transition from walk to work is its own small ritual. I remove my shoes. I make tea. I sit down. And the words come more easily than they would have without those twenty minutes of quiet movement. Not every time. But often enough that I no longer question the practice.

The morning walk is not preparation for the real work. It is the real work. Everything that follows simply benefits from it.
