## About This Board

Stand in any great railway hall built before 1990 and you will hear it before you read it: a dry, rolling clatter that ripples down the concourse like distant rain on a tin roof. That sound is a **split-flap display** rewriting itself — hundreds of small hinged tiles flipping in sequence to spell out where you are going and when. This blog is built in its image.

**Split-Flap** is a publication about the machines that tell people where to go, and the design discipline of saying a great deal in a single glance. We care about the engineering of the Solari board, the choreography of public sound, and the ruthless economy of information design for people who are, almost by definition, in a hurry.

---

## What We Cover

- **Mechanism** — gears, drums, and the alphabet rendered as a stack of physical leaves
- **Sound** — why a station full of flapping tiles sounds like an audience, and what that does to a crowd
- **Glanceable design** — typography, contrast, and hierarchy for the half-second read
- **Analog craft** — the case for displays you can hear, repair, and outlive

> "The board does not ask for your attention. It rewards the half-second you can spare."

## About the Author

The dispatcher writes here. By trade, a maintainer of mechanical displays; by temperament, someone who believes a sign that *moves* earns trust a screen never will. Years spent on ladders behind departure boards — replacing flap belts, re-seating drum motors, listening for the one tile out of time — left a conviction that the best public information is felt as much as read.
