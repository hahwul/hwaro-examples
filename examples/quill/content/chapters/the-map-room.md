The basement of the Royal Archive smelled of dust and forgotten ambition. Seren descended the stone steps carefully, one hand trailing along the wall where condensation gathered in thin rivulets. Somewhere above her, the reading rooms held their orderly silence, scholars bent over manuscripts that had been catalogued and cross-referenced and rendered harmless by familiarity.

Down here, nothing was familiar.

She had been given the key by the Archivist herself, a woman whose trust was harder to earn than a royal commission. "Third corridor, fourth door on the left," the Archivist had said, pressing the iron key into Seren's palm with a grip that suggested she might change her mind. "You will find what the Survey Office sent us last autumn. Nobody has looked at it yet."

Nobody had looked at it. In an archive staffed by people whose entire purpose was to look at things, that sentence carried the weight of a warning.

---

The door opened onto a room smaller than Seren had expected. A single table occupied its center, and on the table lay a map.

She knew, before she touched it, that something was wrong. The paper was too smooth, the edges too clean. Maps from the Survey Office arrived creased and stained, bearing the evidence of field work: mud, rain, the occasional drop of blood from a cartographer who had pricked a finger on a compass point. This map looked as though it had been drawn in a sealed room by someone who had never seen the territory it described.

And yet.

Seren leaned closer, adjusting the oil lamp she had brought. The coastline was unmistakable. She had spent three years mapping the Northern Reach herself, and she recognized every inlet, every headland, every treacherous sandbar that the tides rearranged each season. Whoever had drawn this map knew the coast as well as she did.

Better, perhaps. Because the map showed features she had never seen.

---

There were buildings marked in the interior, in places where Seren knew there were only pines and silence. Not just buildings, but a settlement of considerable size: streets laid out in a careful grid, a central square, what appeared to be defensive walls. The cartographic symbols were standard Survey Office notation. Population density was indicated by the spacing of the hatch marks. If the map was accurate, more than two thousand people lived in a city that did not exist.

She pulled her notebook from her satchel and began to copy what she saw. Her hand moved quickly, the charcoal stick scratching against the page in the quiet room. She noted the coordinates, the elevation markers, the careful annotations in a hand she did not recognize.

At the bottom of the map, in script so small she had to bring the lamp within inches of the paper, someone had written a single line:

*Do not look for this place. It will find you when it is ready.*

Seren sat back in her chair. The flame of the oil lamp trembled, though there was no draft in the sealed room. She read the line again, then a third time, as though repetition might strip it of its strangeness.

She had been a cartographer for twelve years. She had learned, in that time, that maps were instruments of power. A map could claim territory, erase borders, make a wilderness into a province with a stroke of ink. But she had never encountered a map that issued instructions.

---

She worked through the night. By the time morning light crept through the narrow basement windows, she had filled twenty pages of her notebook with measurements, observations, and questions she could not yet answer. The map refused to yield its secrets easily. The more she studied it, the more details she noticed: faint lines connecting the phantom city to other points along the coast, symbols she had never seen in any cartographic reference, and a series of numbers along the margin that might have been dates or might have been something else entirely.

When she finally climbed the stairs back to the reading rooms, the Archivist was waiting.

"Well?" the old woman asked.

Seren chose her words with the same precision she applied to her measurements. "I need to go north," she said. "I need to see if that place is real."

The Archivist nodded, as though she had expected nothing less. "I will arrange provisions," she said. "But Seren, take someone with you. The last cartographer we sent to verify an impossible map did not come back."

"What happened to them?"

The Archivist's expression did not change. "That," she said, "is precisely the question."
