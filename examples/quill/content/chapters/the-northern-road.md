The capital thinned gradually, its stone buildings giving way to timber, then to farmsteads, then to nothing at all. Seren had made this journey before, but always in summer, when the road was firm and the days stretched long enough to make real progress. In late autumn, the Northern Road was a different creature entirely.

Her companion was a man named Edric, whom the Archivist had produced from somewhere within the labyrinth of the Archive's administrative offices. He was tall and quiet, with the look of someone who spent more time outdoors than his official title of "research assistant" suggested. He carried his pack with the ease of long practice and asked no unnecessary questions, which Seren appreciated more than she could say.

---

They walked for three days before Seren told him about the map.

She had intended to wait longer, to observe him and decide whether he could be trusted with information that she herself did not fully understand. But on the third evening, sitting by a fire in a coaching inn that served watery stew and excellent bread, she found herself spreading her notebook on the table between them.

"These are the coordinates," she said, pointing to the figures she had copied. "They correspond to a location roughly forty leagues north-northeast of Port Callum. I surveyed that area myself, six years ago. There is nothing there."

Edric studied the notebook. His eyes moved slowly across her sketches, pausing at details she had marked with small crosses to indicate uncertainty. "Nothing?" he said.

"Pine forest. A river. Some evidence of old settlement, but nothing from the current era. Certainly nothing like this." She tapped the sketch of the phantom city's grid layout.

"And yet someone at the Survey Office drew it."

"Someone drew it," Seren agreed. "Whether they were at the Survey Office when they did so is another question."

---

The road narrowed as they moved north. The coaching inns became less frequent, then vanished entirely. They slept in their bedrolls under the pines, where the canopy was thick enough to break the worst of the rain. Edric proved to be competent at the small tasks of travel: building fires, finding water, reading the weather. He deferred to Seren on matters of navigation without making a performance of it.

On the fifth day, they reached Port Callum.

It was a town that existed primarily because the coast demanded one. Ships needed a harbor, timber needed a dock, and the crown needed a customs office. Port Callum fulfilled these requirements with a minimum of enthusiasm. Its buildings hunched against the sea wind, and its people had the weathered patience of those who lived at the edge of the mapped world.

Seren had contacts here. A woman named Tova, who ran a chandlery near the docks, had supplied Seren's previous expeditions. Tova asked no questions about destinations, charged fair prices, and had once, during a storm that kept them sheltered together for two days, told Seren things about the northern interior that no official survey had ever recorded.

"Heading out again?" Tova said, when Seren appeared at her counter.

"Northeast. Forty leagues, maybe more."

Tova's expression did not change, but her hands paused in their work of coiling rope. "Past the Vane River?"

"Well past it."

The chandler was quiet for a moment. Then she reached beneath the counter and produced a compass unlike any Seren had seen. Its case was dark wood, polished smooth by handling, and the needle was not iron but something pale and translucent, like bone.

"Take this," Tova said. "When your instruments disagree with your eyes, trust your eyes."

Seren took the compass. It was warm, as though it had been sitting near a fire, though the shop was cold. "What is it?"

"Something my grandmother left me. She was a surveyor too, in her way." Tova resumed coiling rope. "Come back and tell me what you find. Or don't come back, and I'll know you found it."

---

They left Port Callum before dawn. The road, such as it was, followed the coast for a few leagues before turning inland. By midday they were among the pines, walking on a carpet of needles so thick that their footsteps made no sound. The silence was not empty. It was the silence of a place that was listening.

Seren checked her compass every quarter hour. The standard-issue Survey Office instrument pointed north with its usual reliability. Tova's compass, in her other hand, pointed north as well.

For now.

Edric walked beside her, matching her pace. He had not asked about the bone compass, and Seren had not volunteered an explanation. There were things that could be discussed on the road and things that required stillness and firelight and the particular honesty that comes from exhaustion.

They made camp that evening by the Vane River, which was running high with autumn rain. The water was dark and cold, carrying leaves and small branches from somewhere upstream. Seren sat by the fire and opened her notebook to the page where she had copied the map's final annotation.

*Do not look for this place. It will find you when it is ready.*

Tomorrow they would cross the river and enter the territory marked on the impossible map. Tomorrow they would begin to learn whether the phantom city was a cartographer's delusion or something far more unsettling: a place that was real, and that had chosen to reveal itself.

Seren closed the notebook and watched the fire. Across the flames, Edric was already asleep, breathing with the steady rhythm of someone who had learned to rest whenever the opportunity arose.

She envied him that. Sleep, for Seren, had always been a negotiation.
