The Art Deco era treated material as a primary design element. A chrome accent on a toaster was not a coincidence; it signaled that this object belonged to the same world as the express train and the ocean liner.

## The Material Palette

- **Polished brass** — for hardware, edges, and brackets where the eye wanted catch points.
- **Black lacquer** — over wood, for surfaces that needed to disappear under reflection.
- **Bakelite** — the era's plastic, used for radio shells, clock cases, and jewelry. It allowed Deco geometry to scale into mass production.
- **Inlay wood** — burl walnut, ebony, satinwood, used in concentric or herringbone patterns to draw the eye toward symmetry.

## Restraint Inside Excess

The Deco vocabulary of expensive materials was always paired with restraint. A piece of furniture might be lacquered black on five surfaces and use polished brass only on the drawer pulls. The luxe was concentrated where the hand actually touched the object. Everywhere else, the form did the work.
