Art Deco came out of a generation that had grown impatient with curves. The Art Nouveau forms that defined the previous decades — flowing botanical motifs, organic asymmetry — were exhausted. What replaced them was a vocabulary built from straight lines and tight angles.

## Three Recurring Forms

- **The chevron.** A repeating V, often arranged in vertical stacks. It reads as motion frozen mid-pulse.
- **The sunburst.** Radiating lines from a fixed center. Found on theatre marquees, watch dials, and elevator doors.
- **The stepped pyramid.** A profile borrowed from Mesoamerican architecture and immediately turned into a façade language for skyscrapers and radio cabinets.

## Why It Lasted

The forms scaled. The same chevron worked on a hairpin, a powder compact, a railway poster, and the spire of the Chrysler Building. Few design languages survive intact from gold-leafed manuscripts to skyscrapers; Art Deco did, because its vocabulary was abstract enough to translate.
