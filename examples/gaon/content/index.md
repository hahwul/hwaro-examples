Read the rule before the examples, not after. Every entry opens with how the correct form is chosen — usually a one-line mechanical test, like which sound the previous syllable ends in — because guessing the pattern from examples alone is slower and less reliable than learning the switch first and watching it fire.

Then read the examples out loud. Korean particles carry almost no weight when your eyes skim past them silently; saying 학교에 가요 instead of just seeing it is what makes 에 register as a sound worth noticing next time you hear it in the wild.

Finally, read the contrast section even when you already feel confident. Nearly every entry in Gaon exists because two markers get confused with each other — 은/는 with 이/가, 에 with 에서 — and the fastest way to stop mixing them up is to hold both in view at once, not to drill one in isolation and hope the other stays out of the way.
