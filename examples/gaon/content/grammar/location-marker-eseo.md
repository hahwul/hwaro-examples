에서 has no allomorphs — it attaches the same way after every noun regardless of the final sound. What it needs instead is a clear boundary against its close relative 에, because both translate loosely to English "at," and mixing them up is one of the most common early plateaus for self-study learners.

<!-- more -->

## The core distinction

에 marks a destination or the location where something simply exists or is located. 에서 marks the location where an action takes place — somewhere something is *done*, not just where something *is*.

{{ example(hangul="도서관에서 공부해요.", roman="doseogwan-eseo gongbuhaeyo.", gloss="(I) study at the library.", particle="에서", particle_roman="eseo") }}

{{ example(hangul="학교에 가요.", roman="hakgyo-e gayo.", gloss="(I) go to school.", particle="에", particle_roman="e") }}

A useful test: if the verb describes an action happening at the location (studying, working, eating, meeting), reach for 에서. If the verb is 있다/없다 describing plain existence, or a motion verb like 가다/오다 naming a destination, reach for 에.

{{ example(hangul="집에 사람이 있어요.", roman="jib-e saram-i isseoyo.", gloss="There is a person at the house.", particle="에", particle_roman="e") }}

{{ example(hangul="집에서 저녁을 먹어요.", roman="jib-eseo jeonyeog-eul meogeoyo.", gloss="(I) eat dinner at home.", particle="에서", particle_roman="eseo") }}

## The second sense: "from"

에서 also marks a starting point, most often paired with 오다 ("to come") or in phrases naming where something originates:

{{ example(hangul="부산에서 왔어요.", roman="busan-eseo wasseoyo.", gloss="(I) came from Busan.", particle="에서", particle_roman="eseo") }}

{{ example(hangul="이 편지는 서울에서 왔어요.", roman="i pyeonjineun seoul-eseo wasseoyo.", gloss="This letter came from Seoul.", particle="에서", particle_roman="eseo") }}

Both senses — "site of an action" and "starting point" — trace back to the same idea: 에서 marks the location a thing moves out from or acts within, while 에 marks a fixed point a thing moves toward or simply occupies.

## Common mistakes

- Using 에 with action verbs: 도서관에 공부해요 sounds like the library itself is the target of "studying," which is not a coherent action-verb relationship in Korean.
- Using 에서 with 있다 when the sentence means simple existence rather than an activity: 집에서 있어요 implies "doing something (unspecified) at home," while 집에 있어요 simply means "(I) am at home."
- Forgetting the "from" sense exists and reaching for a separate word for "from" that Korean does not need here.
