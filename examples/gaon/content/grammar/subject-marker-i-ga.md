이/가 marks the grammatical subject of a clause. In practice it does more than that: it tends to fall on whatever is new, unexpected, or the specific answer to a question — the opposite instinct from the topic marker 은/는, which prefers established, already-framed information.

<!-- more -->

## Choosing the form

Same mechanism as the topic marker: the choice is decided by the sound at the end of the preceding noun, not by meaning.

| Preceding sound | Form | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Consonant (받침) | 이 | 선생님 + 이 → 선생님이 |
| Vowel | 가 | 학교 + 가 → 학교가 |

{{ example(hangul="고양이가 소파 위에 있어요.", roman="goyangi-ga sopa wie isseoyo.", gloss="A cat is on the sofa.", particle="가", particle_roman="ga") }}

{{ example(hangul="선생님이 오셨어요.", roman="seonsaengnim-i osyeosseoyo.", gloss="The teacher has arrived.", particle="이", particle_roman="i") }}

## Three contractions worth memorizing

A handful of very common pronouns fuse with 가 instead of simply appending it. These are irregular in form only — the meaning and function are ordinary subject-marking.

| Base | + 가 | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 나 | 내가 | I (plain speech) |
| 저 | 제가 | I (polite speech) |
| 누구 | 누가 | who |

{{ example(hangul="누가 왔어요?", roman="nuga wasseoyo?", gloss="Who came?", particle="가", particle_roman="ga") }}

{{ example(hangul="제가 할게요.", roman="jega halgeyo.", gloss="I'll do it.", particle="가", particle_roman="ga") }}

## When new information wins over topic

이/가 shows up reliably after question words, in "it is X that ..." focus structures, and with existential verbs 있다/없다 stating what exists rather than commenting on something already known:

{{ example(hangul="냉장고에 우유가 없어요.", roman="naengjanggo-e uyu-ga eopseoyo.", gloss="There is no milk in the fridge.", particle="가", particle_roman="ga") }}

Compare that with a topic-marked version of a similar idea — 우유는 냉장고에 없어요 ("as for milk, it's not in the fridge") — which presumes the listener was already thinking about milk. The 이/가 version simply reports what is, and is not, in the fridge.

## Common mistakes

- Reaching for 이/가 on every subject out of habit, including ones that were already established as the topic a sentence or two earlier. If in doubt, ask whether the noun is being newly asserted (이/가) or commented on (은/는).
- Forgetting the three contractions and producing 나가 or 저가 — grammatical in the sense that a listener will understand, but a clear sign of a beginner still translating from the base pronoun.
- Assuming 이/가 always corresponds to the English subject of the sentence. In many casual utterances the subject particle is dropped entirely once context makes it obvious who or what is meant.
