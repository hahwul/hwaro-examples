을/를 marks the direct object of a transitive verb — the thing being read, eaten, liked, or watched. It is the most mechanically simple particle in this wiki: the allomorph choice follows the same consonant/vowel rule as every other marker in this family, and the meaning rarely strays from "this noun is what the verb is acting on."

## Choosing the form

| Preceding sound | Form | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Consonant (받침) | 을 | 책 + 을 → 책을 |
| Vowel | 를 | 커피 + 를 → 커피를 |

## Verbs that surprise English speakers

A handful of common Korean verbs take a direct object with 을/를 where the English translation uses a preposition. Memorizing these as fixed pairs early avoids a persistent source of small errors:

- 좋아하다 ("to like") — 을/를, not 에. 커피가 좋아요 (adjective "coffee is likable") is also correct but grammatically different from 커피를 좋아해요 (verb "I like coffee").
- 만나다 ("to meet") — 을/를 for the person met: 친구를 만나요, not 친구와/에.
- 타다 ("to ride/board") — 을/를 for the vehicle: 버스를 타요 ("(I) ride the bus").

{{ example(hangul="저는 매일 버스를 타요.", roman="jeoneun maeil beoseureul tayo.", gloss="I ride the bus every day.", particle="를", particle_roman="reul") }}

## Dropping the particle

In relaxed spoken Korean, 을/를 is frequently omitted once the object is obvious from context or word order — much like subject and topic particles. Written Korean, formal speech, and any sentence where omitting the particle would be ambiguous keep it in place.

{{ example(hangul="이거 먹어도 돼요?", roman="igeo meogeodo dwaeyo?", gloss="Can (I) eat this? (particle dropped)", particle="", particle_roman="") }}

## Common mistakes

- Substituting 에 for 을/를 with verbs like 좋아하다 and 만나다, carrying over an English preposition instinct.
- Dropping the particle in formal writing, where it reads as unfinished or careless rather than casual.
- Doubling up — attaching 을/를 to a noun that already has 은/는 or 이/가 attached, when only one topic/subject/object marker belongs per noun phrase.
