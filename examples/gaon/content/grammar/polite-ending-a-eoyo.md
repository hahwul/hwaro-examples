-아/어요 is the present-tense ending of 해요체, the polite-informal speech level used in the overwhelming majority of everyday spoken Korean — with coworkers, classmates, shopkeepers, and anyone who is not close family or clearly below you in age or rank. It is usually the second thing a self-study learner memorizes, right after the alphabet.

<!-- more -->

## The vowel harmony rule

The choice between -아요 and -어요 depends on the vowel in the verb stem's last syllable — not the whole word, just that one vowel.

| Stem's last vowel | Ending | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ㅏ or ㅗ | -아요 | 좋다 → 좋아요 |
| any other vowel | -어요 | 먹다 → 먹어요 |
| 하다 verbs | -해요 (irregular) | 공부하다 → 공부해요 |

{{ example(hangul="날씨가 좋아요.", roman="nalssiga joh-ayo.", gloss="The weather is nice.", particle="아요", particle_roman="ayo") }}

{{ example(hangul="저는 매일 학교에 가요.", roman="jeoneun maeil hakgyo-e gayo.", gloss="I go to school every day.", particle="가요", particle_roman="gayo") }}

{{ example(hangul="이 노래를 좋아해요.", roman="i noraereul joh-ahaeyo.", gloss="(I) like this song.", particle="해요", particle_roman="haeyo") }}

Note 가요 in the second example: the stem 가- already ends in the ㅏ vowel that -아요 would add, so the two syllables merge rather than stacking (가 + 아요 → 가요, not 가아요).

## Why 하다 gets its own row

하다 ("to do," and the suffix on hundreds of verb-forming nouns like 공부하다 "to study") does not follow the ㅏ/ㅗ rule at all — its stem vowel is ㅏ, which would predict -아요, but the actual polite form is always 해요. This is the single irregularity in an otherwise fully mechanical system, and it is worth memorizing as its own rule rather than an exception to chase down case by case.

{{ example(hangul="주말에 운동해요.", roman="jumal-e undonghaeyo.", gloss="(I) exercise on weekends.", particle="해요", particle_roman="haeyo") }}

## Question, statement, and suggestion — one ending, three jobs

Spoken Korean leans on intonation rather than separate grammar for these three sentence types at the 해요체 level. The written form -아/어요 stays identical; only the punctuation and, in speech, the rising or falling pitch change.

{{ example(hangul="같이 가요.", roman="gachi gayo.", gloss="Let's go together. (or: Go together.)", particle="가요", particle_roman="gayo") }}

## Common mistakes

- Applying the ㅏ/ㅗ rule to 하다 verbs and producing 공부하요 instead of 공부해요.
- Forgetting the vowel merge and writing 가아요 or 오오요 instead of the contracted 가요 / 와요.
- Treating -아/어요 as informal in the way English "informal" implies rudeness — it is polite and entirely appropriate for strangers and colleagues; it is only "informal" relative to the more distant 합니다체 used in news broadcasts, formal presentations, and the military.
