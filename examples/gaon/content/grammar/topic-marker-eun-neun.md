The topic particle 은/는 attaches to a noun and announces what the rest of the sentence is *about*. It is not "the subject" in the English sense — it marks the frame the listener should hold in mind while the sentence unfolds, which is a related but genuinely different job from the one 이/가 does.

<!-- more -->

## Choosing the form

The choice between 은 and 는 is mechanical, decided entirely by the sound the preceding noun ends in. There is nothing to memorize beyond the rule itself.

| Preceding sound | Form | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Consonant (받침) | 은 | 책 + 은 → 책은 |
| Vowel | 는 | 저 + 는 → 저는 |

{{ example(hangul="저는 학생이에요.", roman="jeoneun haksaeng-ieyo.", gloss="As for me, (I) am a student.", particle="는", particle_roman="neun") }}

{{ example(hangul="이 책은 재미있어요.", roman="i chaegeun jaemiisseoyo.", gloss="As for this book, (it) is interesting.", particle="은", particle_roman="eun") }}

## Topic vs. subject

은/는 tends to mark information the listener already has some access to — a person, thing, or idea being set up as the frame for a comment. 이/가 tends to mark new information, or to answer an implicit "who?" or "what?". The clearest test case is an opening line of a story: a character is introduced with 이/가 because they are new to the conversation, then referred back to with 은/는 once they are established.

{{ example(hangul="어느 날, 한 소년이 마을에 나타났어요.", roman="eoneu nal, han sonyeon-i maeul-e natanasseoyo.", gloss="One day, a boy (subject, new) appeared in the village.", particle="이", particle_roman="i") }}

{{ example(hangul="그 소년은 이름이 없었어요.", roman="geu sonyeoneun ireumi eopseosseoyo.", gloss="The boy (topic, now established) had no name.", particle="은", particle_roman="eun") }}

## Contrastive use

When two topics are set side by side, 은/는 does double duty as a contrast marker — closer to English "as for X, ... but as for Y, ...":

{{ example(hangul="저는 커피를 좋아하지만 동생은 차를 좋아해요.", roman="jeoneun keopireul joh-ahajiman dongsaeng-eun chareul joh-ahaeyo.", gloss="I like coffee, but my younger sibling likes tea.", particle="는", particle_roman="neun") }}

Notice both 저 and 동생 take 는/은 here even though only one of them is the "main" topic of the sentence — contrast licenses a second topic marker where a plain sentence would use 이/가 or drop the particle entirely.

## Common mistakes

- Matching the wrong allomorph to the sound — writing 책는 instead of 책은. This is purely a listening/spelling error, not a grammar one; say the noun aloud and the correct form is usually obvious.
- Treating 은/는 as a direct translation of "is" and reaching for it on every noun. A sentence can have several nouns and only one of them — the one being framed as the topic — takes 은/는.
- Over-marking topics in a single paragraph. Korean drops the particle once a topic is already clear from context; repeating 은/는 on every sentence reads as unnaturally emphatic.
