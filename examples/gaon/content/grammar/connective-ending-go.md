-고 attaches directly to a verb or adjective stem — no vowel harmony, no irregular forms to track — and joins it to a following clause with a meaning close to English "and." It is usually the first connective a self-study learner reaches for, and it earns that position: the rule is simple and the two senses it carries cover a lot of ground.

<!-- more -->

## Two senses, one ending

Attached to adjectives or states, -고 lists qualities side by side. Attached to action verbs in sequence, it reads as "and then," implying the first action finished before the second began.

{{ example(hangul="날씨가 춥고 바람이 불어요.", roman="nalssiga chupgo baram-i buleoyo.", gloss="The weather is cold and windy.", particle="고", particle_roman="go") }}

{{ example(hangul="아침을 먹고 학교에 가요.", roman="achim-eul meokgo hakgyo-e gayo.", gloss="(I) eat breakfast and then go to school.", particle="고", particle_roman="go") }}

Context, not the grammar itself, tells you which sense is active — two adjectives or states side by side almost always read as simple listing, while two action verbs in a row almost always imply sequence.

## Independent subjects are allowed

Unlike some connective endings that require both clauses to share a subject, -고 does not. Each half of the sentence can name its own subject freely:

{{ example(hangul="저는 요리하고 동생은 설거지해요.", roman="jeoneun yorihago dongsaeng-eun seolgeojihaeyo.", gloss="I cook and my younger sibling does the dishes.", particle="고", particle_roman="go") }}

This is one of the clearest signs -고 is doing pure listing/sequencing work rather than describing a single actor's chain of actions — the two clauses here are two different people doing two different things, stitched into one sentence.

## -고 versus a full stop

Nothing stops a learner from writing two short sentences instead of joining them with -고, and early on that is often the safer choice. But native written Korean uses -고 constantly to avoid a chain of short, choppy sentences — the same instinct that makes "and" useful in English paragraphs rather than a period after every clause.

{{ example(hangul="영화를 보고 저녁을 먹었어요.", roman="yeonghwareul bogo jeonyeog-eul meogeosseoyo.", gloss="(I) watched a movie and (then) ate dinner.", particle="고", particle_roman="go") }}

## Common mistakes

- Attaching -고 to a noun instead of a verb/adjective stem — 고 is a verb-ending connective, not a particle, so it needs a conjugatable stem in front of it (학생이고 "is a student, and..." is fine because 이다 "to be" is a verb; a bare noun is not).
- Assuming -고 always implies strict time order. In the listing sense (two adjectives), there is no sequence at all, only simultaneous description.
- Overusing -고 to chain four or five clauses into one long sentence — grammatical, but reads as run-on in both languages.
