가온 (gaon) is an old Korean word for center, or middle — the point equidistant from every edge. It survives today mostly in names: apartment blocks, ships, the occasional software product looking for something that sounds native and calm at once. It seemed like the right name for a reference site, because a good reference is exactly that: a fixed center you can return to from any direction, rather than a path you have to walk start to finish.

## Who this is for

Gaon is written for self-study learners who already have some exposure to Korean — enough to read hangul without sounding it out letter by letter — and who keep hitting the same wall textbooks tend to gloss over: *why does this sentence use 는 and not 이, and why did the last one do the opposite?* Each entry here picks exactly one particle or ending and stays with it until the rule is mechanical rather than mysterious.

## How an entry is built

Every grammar point follows the same shape: how the correct form is chosen, what the marker actually does, a contrast against whatever it is most often confused with, and a short list of mistakes that pattern shows up in practice. Example sentences render the same way everywhere in the wiki — hangul on top, romanization beneath it, a plain English gloss under that, with the particle or ending under study picked out in the accent color on all three lines. The idea is that your eye learns to find the pattern before your brain has finished parsing the sentence.

## Scope

Six entries live here today, grouped into three families: topic and subject markers, object and location markers, and the connective and sentence-final endings that hold clauses together. That is a deliberately small, deliberately finished set — enough to unblock the most common early plateau in self-study Korean, not an attempt at a full reference grammar. Search with Ctrl+K (or the search icon on narrow screens) if you know roughly what you are looking for; otherwise the sidebar groups everything by family so you can browse by shape.
