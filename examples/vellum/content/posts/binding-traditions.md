A book is an architecture. Its spine is a column, its covers are walls, and its pages are the rooms through which a reader moves. The binding is what holds this architecture together, and like all good construction, it should be both functional and beautiful.

<!-- more -->

## Coptic, Japanese, and Western

The oldest surviving binding technique is the Coptic stitch, which emerged in early Christian Egypt. Pages are sewn together with an exposed chain stitch that allows the book to open completely flat. It is a binding that hides nothing -- every structural element is visible and celebrated.

Japanese stab binding takes a different approach. Holes are punched along the spine edge, and thread is woven through them in geometric patterns. The binding becomes ornamentation. Function becomes form.

Western case binding -- the codex most of us grew up with -- conceals its engineering beneath cloth or leather. Signatures are sewn onto tapes, the spine is rounded and backed, and the whole assembly is encased in rigid boards. The result is a fortress of paper.

## The Choice of Materials

Thread, paper, adhesive, board, cloth -- each material in a binding makes an argument. Linen thread says permanence. Japanese tissue says delicacy. Binder's board says authority. The bookbinder's craft is the art of choosing materials that speak in harmony.

Every binding decision is also a conservation decision. Acid-free papers and archival adhesives ensure that a book will outlast its maker. The best binders think in centuries.

## Why Binding Matters

In a world of scrolling feeds and disposable content, the bound book is a radical object. It has a beginning, a middle, and an end. It demands that ideas be ordered, sequenced, and committed to a fixed form. The binding is what makes this commitment physical.
