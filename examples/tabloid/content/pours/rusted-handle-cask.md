The Rusted Handle does not have a tap wall. It has a wooden firkin on a stand behind the bar, a hand pump older than most of its clientele, and a bartender who will silently judge you for asking if the beer is cold. It is not cold. That is the point.

<!-- more -->

Cask ale at room temperature is a hard sell in a city that has spent a decade chasing frost-rimed glassware, and The Rusted Handle does not sell it so much as insist on it. The pour is soft, almost still, carbonation you have to go looking for. What you get instead is texture — a round, bread-crust malt body with a whisper of English hop, earthy and floral in a way that CO2 would have scrubbed out entirely.

The room helps. Low ceiling, a radiator that clanks, a dartboard nobody has taken down since the venue changed hands twice. It smells faintly of wet wool and hops, which is either the most honest smell in craft beer or the least marketable one, and we mean that as praise either way. Nobody here is filming their pour for anyone.

Order the mild if it's on. It's the platonic ideal of a beer you can have three of and still walk home in a straight line, which used to be the entire job description of a pub beer before ABVs got competitive.

**Verdict:** the beer is a supporting actor. The room is the headline. Go for the cask, stay because leaving feels rude.
