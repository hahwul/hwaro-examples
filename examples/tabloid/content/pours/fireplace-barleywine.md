Kettle & Fang rests this barleywine in bourbon barrels for fourteen months before it ever sees a bottle, and you can taste every one of those months. This is not a beer you order to cool down. This is a beer you order because it is December and you have made peace with that fact.

<!-- more -->

The pour is deep amber-mahogany, thick enough to leave slow legs down the glass, and the nose is dried fig, caramelized brown sugar, and a real wallop of oak vanilla before the bourbon heat even registers. The first sip is sweet up front — toffee, dark honey, a raisin-like richness — and then the alcohol shows up fashionably late and stays for the rest of the conversation, a slow burn that starts behind your sternum and radiates outward like you swallowed a small ember.

At 12.4% this is sipping territory only, and Kettle & Fang knows it — they serve it in four-ounce pours at the taproom, which sounds stingy until you finish one and understand exactly why. It's dessert in a glass, the kind of beer that makes an argument for skipping dessert entirely and just ordering a second pour instead.

We were told it cellars for a decade if you can resist it. We could not resist it. Nobody in this room could resist it.

**Verdict:** a fireplace you can drink, built for the one night a year you actually want to feel the burn. Share it slowly or don't share it at all.
