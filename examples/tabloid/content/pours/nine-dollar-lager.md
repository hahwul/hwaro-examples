The beer is fine. We want to say that up front, before we say anything else, because everything else we're about to say is about the room, and the room is doing its absolute best to distract you from how ordinary the beer actually is.

<!-- more -->

The Ledger Room's house pilsner is clean, dry, appropriately bitter, a genuinely well-made lager that would cost six dollars anywhere with less exposed concrete. It doesn't cost six dollars here. It costs nine, and the extra three dollars is buying you a hand-thrown ceramic tasting flight rest, a bartender who describes lager as "approachable," and a chalkboard menu that lists the water source like it's a single-origin coffee bean.

None of that is the beer's fault. Poured blind, this pilsner would win most head-to-head tastings in its category — crisp, a little bready, a clean noble-hop snap on the finish that plenty of breweries charging half as much can't manage. The Ledger Room just insists on wrapping it in enough atmosphere that you leave feeling like you paid for the room, not the glass.

If you can tune out the reclaimed-wood theater, it's a genuinely good crusher of a beer. If you can't, order it to go and drink it somewhere the walls aren't billing you by the hour.

**Verdict:** great lager, exhausting room. Nine dollars is a room tax, not a beer tax — budget accordingly. If atmosphere tax isn't your thing, go get lost in [The Tin Whistle's flight board](@/pours/tin-whistle-flight.md) instead — same neighborhood, a fraction of the pretense.
