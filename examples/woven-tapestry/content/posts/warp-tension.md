title = "Calibrating Warp Tension"
date = "2024-04-12"
description = "Notes on consistent tension across a wide loom."

Even tension across the warp is the quiet condition that lets every other decision in a tapestry hold together. Inconsistent tension shows up much later than it begins, often only when a finished panel is removed from the loom and one edge curls or one section sags. The fix at that point is rarely subtle.

A common method is to wind the warp in narrow sections rather than as one continuous sheet. Each section is dressed onto the back beam under the same weight, then advanced together. On a wide loom, two weavers working from opposite sides keep the cross even and prevent twist. The lease sticks remain near the back beam through dressing so the threading order is never ambiguous.

After threading the heddles and reed, the weaver runs a short header in plain weave. The header is the first honest report on tension. Bands of looser warp show as small pillows in the cloth; tighter sections compress into ridges. The back beam is loosened, the offending sections tugged forward, and the beam is retensioned. Two or three cycles of this adjustment are normal.

Over the course of weaving, tension drifts. Beating the weft, advancing the cloth beam, and humidity shifts all contribute. Many weavers keep a finger gauge: pressing the warp at a fixed point next to the heddles and noting how far it deflects under a known force. That tactile reference is faster than any mechanical reading and survives changes in the rest of the studio.
