title = "Keeping Dye Batch Records"
date = "2024-05-03"
description = "A practical format for tracking color across multi-year projects."
+++

Tapestries that take years to weave outlive the dye baths that color them. A weaver who returns to a piece after eight months and finds the studio out of a particular madder lot has only one defense: a record system precise enough to reproduce the color from raw materials.

The most useful records are short and structured. Each batch entry holds the date, the fiber type and weight, the dye source and weight, the mordant and its concentration, the water volume, the bath temperature curve, and the time held at each stage. A small clipped sample of the resulting yarn is taped to the page. Photographs help, but a physical clipping is the only honest reference under studio light.

For natural dyes, the source matters as much as the species. Madder grown in different soils yields different reds; indigo from one supplier may carry residues that shift the hue blue-green. Recording the supplier and the lot number on every batch turns a vague disappointment into a solvable problem.

A batch number written directly on each cone of yarn closes the loop. When weaving resumes after a gap, the weaver can pull a cone, check the number against the binder, and confirm the bath conditions before committing the thread to the work. If the cone runs short, the binder describes exactly how to replicate the bath, including the small adjustments that were made to the original.

The record system is also where mistakes earn their value. A bath that ran too hot and shifted from rose to brick is not a failure if the conditions are written down. The next weaver, possibly the same one, knows what to avoid and what to repeat.
