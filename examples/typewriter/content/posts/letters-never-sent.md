Before email, before text messages, before the instantaneous delivery of thought across the globe, there were letters. Written by hand or by typewriter, folded into envelopes, stamped, and entrusted to the postal service.

## The Space Between

A letter took days to arrive. In that space between sending and receiving, something important happened: patience. The writer could not unsend their words. The reader could not demand an immediate reply. Both parties lived, for a time, in the productive uncertainty of waiting.

This delay was not a limitation. It was a feature. It gave both writer and reader time to think, to compose not just sentences but thoughts.

## The Ritual of Correspondence

Writing a letter was a physical act. You chose your paper. You loaded the typewriter or uncapped your pen. You sat at a desk and addressed yourself to one specific person, with all the attention that implies.

The ritual mattered. It signaled to your own mind that this communication was worthy of care. Every letter was, in some small way, a gift of time.

## What We Lost

Modern communication is miraculous in its speed and reach. But speed encourages brevity, and brevity encourages shallowness. We send dozens of messages a day and say very little.

The letter writers of the past -- Virginia Woolf to Vita Sackville-West, Rilke to a young poet, Keats to Fanny Brawne -- produced correspondence that was also literature. They wrote with the knowledge that their words would be held, reread, kept in a drawer and returned to on quiet evenings.

## An Experiment

Write a letter this week. Not an email. A letter. On paper, with a pen or a typewriter if you have one. Address it to someone you care about. Say something you have been meaning to say.

You do not have to send it. Sometimes the act of writing is enough.
