The Hallworth Fragment arrived at Thornfield in a box of miscellaneous partbook leaves donated in 1961 and was cataloged, minimally, as "sacred vocal music, anon., 17th c." Six leaves, one voice part, no title page, no composer, no date beyond a librarian's best guess written in pencil on the folder: "c. 1650–1690." That forty-year window is where the fragment sat, essentially invisible to scholarship, for six decades.

## The attribution problem

Stylistically the motet sits comfortably in the mid-century English sacred repertoire — homophonic verses alternating with a fuller chorus texture, a bass line that leans on suspended fourths in a way that several composers of the period favored. That similarity is exactly the problem: three or four composers active in the 1650s–1680s wrote in a near-identical idiom, and internal musical evidence alone could not narrow the fragment's date or authorship further. What the fragment needed was a piece of evidence that had nothing to do with the notes on the page.

## Method

Paper does not lie about when it was made, even when a copyist lies about who wrote something. Using transmitted-light photography, I traced the watermark on each of the six leaves — a fairly worn but legible fleur-de-lis-and-monogram design — and compared it against paper-stock reference collections for English music paper of the period, cross-checking chain-line spacing as a secondary confirmation that all six leaves came from the same stock.

The decisive match came from an unrelated source: a diocesan register at Ashcombe, precisely dated to 1669, that used paper from the same mill run, identifiable by a small mold flaw producing an identical watermark distortion in the same position on the sheet.

## Findings

That match narrows the fragment's paper to a stock in active use circa 1668–1671, tightening the previous forty-year attribution window to roughly three years. Combined with a handwriting comparison against three known copyists working in cathedral scriptoria in that window, the fragment's hand is now provisionally attributed to a workshop associated with one regional cathedral, rather than left as fully anonymous. The catalog record has been updated accordingly, with the earlier attribution retained as a superseded note — the fragment's own small provenance trail, now legible to the next person who opens the folder.
