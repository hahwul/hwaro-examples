Thornfield acquired a single tenor partbook at a regional auction in 2021, cataloged provisionally with no known provenance beyond the seller. It carried an ownership stamp, a binder's ticket, and a pencil shelf mark in an unfamiliar hand — three separate threads, none of which pointed anywhere on their own. This case study reconstructs where the volume had been before 2021, and argues for treating incomplete provenance evidence as cumulative rather than conclusive.

<!-- more -->

## Three fragments, no chain

The ownership stamp belonged to a private collector active in the early twentieth century whose library was dispersed at auction in 1958 — a documented sale, but the surviving catalog lists only lot numbers and vague descriptions ("a quantity of manuscript partbooks, various"), not enough to confirm this specific volume was among them. The binder's ticket named a workshop that operated for roughly thirty years, narrowing rebinding to a wide window but not a date. The pencil shelf mark matched no cataloging scheme at any institution I could identify.

Individually, each thread is inconclusive. Together, they became useful.

## Following the ledger

The breakthrough came from an unlikely source: a handwritten stock ledger kept by a manuscript dealer active from the 1930s through the 1970s, later donated to a regional archive and only partially indexed. Searching the ledger's entries against the collector's known 1958 sale year turned up a matching lot description — "tenor partbook, sacred, worn boards, ticket of [workshop name]" — sold onward in 1959 to a buyer identified only by initials.

Those initials, cross-referenced against membership records of a now-defunct amateur music society, matched a member whose own estate was sold in a small, undocumented 1994 house sale — the likely source of the lot that eventually reached the 2021 auction where Thornfield bought it.

## The reconstructed chain

| Period | Holder | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Unknown – 1958 | Private collector (stamp identified) | Ownership stamp |
| 1958 – 1959 | Auction lot, dealer resale | Dealer stock ledger entry |
| 1959 – 1994 | Amateur society member (initials only) | Ledger buyer initials, society roll |
| 1994 | Undocumented house sale | Inferred, no direct record |
| 1994 – 2021 | Unknown intermediary holder(s) | No evidence recovered |
| 2021 – present | Thornfield Conservatory Library | Auction purchase record |

## What this establishes, and what it doesn't

This chain does not rise to documentary proof; no single sale record names this specific volume by an identifier that survives comparison today. What it establishes is a plausible, evidence-supported ownership sequence spanning three sales and roughly sixty years, recorded in the catalog as a provenance narrative with explicit confidence levels at each link rather than a false chain of certainty. For manuscripts that circulated through the amateur and secondhand market rather than institutional channels, a documented "probably, because" is often the most honest record obtainable — and considerably more useful to the next researcher than an empty provenance field.
