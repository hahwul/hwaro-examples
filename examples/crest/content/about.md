I came to music librarianship sideways. I trained as a musicologist, spent two years failing to finish a dissertation on English partbook copying practices, and took a reading-room job at Ashcombe Cathedral Archive to pay for the failing. The dissertation stayed unfinished. The archive did not let me leave.

What kept me was the specific, unglamorous problem at the center of the work: most manuscripts in a special collection are not lost, exactly — they are simply undescribed. A partbook sits in a box with a shelf mark and no author, no date, no indication that its missing companion volume is two counties away in another institution's uncataloged backlog. Cataloging is the act of making that connection findable by someone who is not me, is not in the room, and may not be born yet.

## What I actually do

Roughly a third of my working year is acquisitions and provenance — evaluating incoming donations and purchases, tracing where a manuscript has been before it reaches us, and deciding whether a claim of authorship or date holds up under paper and hand analysis. Another third is cataloging: writing the MARC and EAD records, RISM entries, and finding aids that make a collection usable by someone who has never set foot in the building. The rest is reading-room work, instruction sessions for graduate seminars, and the slow administrative labor of grant reports and conservation triage lists.

None of it is especially photogenic. I have colleagues in departments where digitization means beautiful high-resolution images circulating on social media within a week. My version of a finished project is usually a well-formed EAD file and a shelf list that finally matches the boxes. I have made peace with that trade.

## Outside the reading room

I still play viol, badly, in a consort that meets every other Sunday and has been rehearsing the same Byrd fantasia for longer than is defensible. I collect nineteenth-century library supply catalogs — the kind that sold card stock, rod-and-tray filing cabinets, and rubber date stamps — partly for the design history and partly because understanding how a system was built to be used tells you a great deal about how to read what it left behind.

If you are a cataloger, a dealer, or a graduate student with a manuscript you can't place, I am glad to look. That correspondence, more than anything on the CV, is the actual job.
