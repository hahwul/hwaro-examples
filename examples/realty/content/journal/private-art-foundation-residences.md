A category that occupied perhaps a dozen transactions a year a decade ago now accounts for a meaningful share of our advisory work above thirty million dollars. We refer to it internally as the private foundation residence: a primary or secondary home whose construction, climate envelope, and access protocols are designed to also house an accredited collection.

These properties differ from conventional homes with art on the walls. The collection drives the program from the earliest sketches. Wall thickness, glazing specifications, mechanical redundancy, and security zoning are coordinated with conservators and registrars before the principal architect finalizes plans. Loading docks, conservation studios, and climate-controlled vaults are integrated rather than retrofitted. In several recent projects we have seen, the residence and the foundation footprint each occupy roughly half the structure.

The legal architecture is equally specific. The collection is typically owned by a private operating foundation, which leases the relevant portion of the building from a separate ownership entity. This separation, when properly structured, allows the foundation to host scheduled scholarly visits and limited public access without compromising the residential character or the principal's privacy. It also produces a defensible valuation framework that conventional residential appraisals are not equipped to handle.

For agents working in this segment, the implication is that comparable sales analysis is largely irrelevant. Pricing is driven by replacement cost of the conservation infrastructure, the appraised value of the resident collection where it transfers with the building, and the operational track record of the foundation if one is established. Buyers are sophisticated, advised by counsel, and accustomed to long diligence cycles. Transactions in this category routinely take nine to fourteen months from offer to close.
