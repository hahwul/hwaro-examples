The California Coastal Commission's revised guidance on bluff-top setbacks, issued late in the first quarter, will affect the underwriting of nearly every beachfront property in our portfolio between Santa Barbara and Encinitas. Owners and prospective buyers should review the changes carefully before committing to renovations, expansions, or rebuilds.

The principal change is the methodology used to calculate the projected erosion line. The Commission now requires a seventy-five-year horizon, up from the previous fifty-year standard, and instructs jurisdictions to incorporate accelerated sea-level scenarios drawn from the most recent state climate assessment. In practice, this pushes the buildable envelope inland by an additional fifteen to forty feet on most parcels we have evaluated.

For existing structures within the new setback, the Commission has retained nonconforming-use protections, but the threshold for triggering full compliance has been lowered. A renovation that exceeds fifty percent of the assessed structure value, rather than the previous seventy-five percent, will now require the property to be brought into conformance with current setbacks. This compresses the scope of allowable additions and, in several cases we have reviewed, eliminates the option to enlarge the principal residence.

There is also a documentation burden that did not previously exist. New geotechnical reports must be commissioned every five years for properties seeking permits, and these reports are required to be sealed by an engineer registered in California with a coastal-hazards specialization.

Owners considering a sale within the next eighteen months should obtain a current geotechnical assessment in advance of listing. Buyers, particularly those planning substantial work, should ensure that any preliminary architectural studies are reviewed against the revised envelope before a purchase agreement is signed.
