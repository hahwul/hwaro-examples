Every working building hides a second building inside it. Risers carry water and waste. Trays carry power and data. Ducts move air. The path each one takes is rarely arbitrary, even when it looks like it. The conduit grid is engineered against gravity, against fire spread, against the awkward geometry of the floor plate, and against the thousand small clearances that keep maintenance crews from cursing the original designer.

### Service Cores

In tall buildings, vertical services collapse into a service core, usually wrapped around the elevators and stairs. The core handles the loads that benefit from a single vertical run: plumbing stacks, structured cabling, mechanical shafts. A well-placed core leaves the rest of the floor plate free for occupiable space, which is why office towers tend to look the way they do from the inside.

The trade-off is rigidity. A core fixes the location of the heaviest services for the life of the building. Renovations work around it. New tenants inherit it. The cost of moving a core is, in practice, the cost of demolition.

### Horizontal Distribution

Above the ceiling and below the floor, services fan out horizontally. Cable trays, conduit, and ductwork share the plenum, and the order in which they were installed is usually visible in the order they cross each other. Coordination drawings exist to prevent collisions, but the field always has the last word.

The result, when exposed, is a kind of accidental composition: parallel runs interrupted by tees and elbows, hangers spaced at regular intervals, labels and tags at every junction. It reads as machinery, but it follows rules. Tracing those rules is part of what makes industrial interiors legible rather than chaotic.
