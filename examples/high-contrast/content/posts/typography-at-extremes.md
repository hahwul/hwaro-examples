A typeface that performs at body size will not always perform at display size, and the reverse holds equally well. The two contexts impose different constraints on counters, joins, and stroke contrast, and a single design rarely satisfies both without compromise.

## Small Sizes

At ten or eleven points, hinting and metrics dominate the perceived quality of the text. Counters must remain open; the inner space of an `e` or an `a` collapses first when the letter is rendered at low resolution. Stroke contrast should be modest, since a thin hairline disappears entirely at small sizes on a low-density display. The cap height should be close to the x-height to maintain a steady horizontal rhythm.

## Display Sizes

Above forty-eight points, the rules invert. Wide counters look slack, modest contrast looks soft, and a generous x-height looks juvenile. Display cuts narrow the proportions, sharpen the contrast, and tighten the spacing. The same family that requires loose tracking at body sizes will require negative tracking at display sizes; the optical correction is roughly proportional to the square root of the size ratio.

## Pairing

Pairing a body cut with a matching display cut produces a stable hierarchy without introducing a second family. The reader perceives continuity of voice across heading and paragraph, and the page settles into a single rhythm. Pairing two unrelated families works only if their weights, x-heights, and underlying proportions agree, which is rare without deliberate selection.

## Weight

The weight axis is the strongest tool for establishing hierarchy. A single family with a regular, a medium, and a black weight can carry a complete document; a single weight with size variation alone cannot. The medium weight is often overlooked but bridges the gap between regular text and bold emphasis without producing the harshness of a heavy face on a quiet page.
