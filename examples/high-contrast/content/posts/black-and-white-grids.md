A grid that works in color often fails in black and white. Color carries part of the work of separation: a pale background distinguishes a sidebar from the main column, a tinted callout marks a quote, a colored border draws the eye to a form. Strip the color out and the grid alone must do that work.

## Column Discipline

Without color to differentiate regions, columns have to do the separating. Set fewer columns and wider gutters than you would in a colored layout. A six-column grid at thirty-two pixel gutters reads as a single block of gray when rendered in black on white; the same content on three columns at ninety-six pixel gutters reads as three distinct regions even before any rule is drawn.

## Rules and Borders

Hairline rules are the second tool. A one-pixel rule between sections does not introduce a tonal shift to the page but provides a clear boundary. The rule should be exactly as long as it needs to be, terminating at the baseline of the adjacent text rather than running to the column edge. A rule that exceeds its purpose becomes a decorative element rather than a structural one.

## Whitespace

Whitespace is the strongest separator in a two-color layout. Doubling the leading above a heading, tripling the margin around a pull quote, or leaving a full column empty between two regions communicates structure without adding any marks to the page. The discipline is to leave the space empty rather than fill it with secondary content.

## Image Treatment

Photographs in a two-color layout require either full bleeds or framed crops. A loose photograph floating inside a column reads as a tonal accident; the same photograph extended to the gutter or contained inside a clean rectangular frame reads as a deliberate element. Duotone treatments extend the technique without breaking the palette: a single dark tone applied to a photograph holds its place beside the body text without introducing a competing hue.
