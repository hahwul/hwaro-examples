The forecast chart showed the jet stream drawn straight across the county like a strike-through, and the stars confirmed it before the scope was even level: Regulus was not twinkling so much as thrashing. Seeing 1, the worst grade in the ledger. But the same front that wrecked the air had scrubbed it clean — transparency was the best since November. Nights like this have one correct answer, and it is galaxies at low power.

<!-- more -->

Galaxies do not care about seeing the way doubles and planets do. They are faint smudges at 50×, and a smudge boiled is still a smudge. What they demand is dark, transparent sky, and tonight had it in unusual supply for a garden three streets from the streetlights.

The Triplet sits in an easy star-hop off Theta Leonis, and all three came into the 24 mm field together — the event that makes the whole trip worthwhile on its own. M66 was the showpiece as always, obviously oval, brighter along one edge, with a stellar core that survived the turbulence. M65 sat quieter and slimmer beside it, a pale lozenge with a tight nucleus.

The real business of the night was NGC 3628, the third and hardest of the three, the one the Messier list forgot. On average nights from here it is a rumour. Tonight it was an unmistakable long sliver, needle-thin and edge-on, running fully a third of the field. With ten minutes of dark adaptation and steady averted vision, the equatorial dust lane arrived — not as a line but as an *absence*, the sense that the needle had been split lengthwise and the halves nudged apart. I held that impression on and off for half an hour and I am keeping it in the ledger with a clear conscience.

A five-point night for transparency filed under a four-point star — the glyph on the spine only counts the seeing, and the seeing was a disgrace. The ledger is honest even when the sky is generous.
