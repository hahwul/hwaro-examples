Peak Geminid night and clear, which the law of averages owed me after three clouded-out peaks in a row. The session ran on two stations: the deck chair with a blanket for meteors, and the ten-inch parked on Taurus for the stretches when the eyes needed a job. The count from the chair, in gloved five-bar gates on the clipboard: **forty-one** in a little over two hours of accumulated watching, including one fireball at 00:40 that came down the length of Orion and left a train I could hold for three full seconds.

<!-- more -->

Geminids are the courteous meteor shower — slow, bright, and yellow-white, arriving at a walking pace compared to the Perseids' sprint. Several dropped straight through the zenith like flares. Between waves I bent to the eyepiece, and the eyepiece work turned out to be the quiet triumph of the night.

M1, the Crab, has been in this log a dozen times as "oval smudge, brighter middle, see previous entry." Tonight, with the air steadying to a solid seeing 4 and the crescent moon long gone, it finally gave up something more. At 119× with the narrowband filter the oval showed a ragged, torn perimeter — bays and promontories rather than a smooth rim — and twice, with averted vision, a hint of the mottled interior lacework the photographs are made of. No individual filament held steady, but the *texture* was there, and texture in M1 from a Bortle 4½ garden is a first for this ledger.

M35 filled the gaps: a big, democratic scatter of a cluster at 50×, with the little fuzzball of NGC 2158 crouched at its southwest edge pretending to be a nebula — five times the distance in the same field, which is the kind of fact that makes you sit back in the frost and stop talking for a minute.

Packed up at half two with the sky still throwing the odd meteor, on the theory that it is better to close the ledger wanting more. The night's fireball got its own line in the margin, boxed twice.
