Some sessions are a banquet and some are a single sandwich eaten standing up. Tonight was the sandwich: one hour between the haze thickening and the moon shouldering over the neighbour's roofline, spent entirely in Gemini.

<!-- more -->

NGC 2392 — the Eskimo — is forgiving of bad transparency because it is small and dense, and it needs magnification more than it needs darkness. At 50× it masquerades as a slightly thick star sitting next to a real one, which is exactly how I confirmed the field. The party trick still works every time: stare at the star, blink to the nebula, and it pops in and out like a signal lamp as the eye's rods and cones swap shifts.

The air, sadly, had other opinions about magnification. At 198× the disc dissolved into a slow boil, firming up for a second or two at a time. In those seconds there was a bright inner knot — the central star wrapped in its collar — and a distinctly darker moat between core and outer shell. The "parka hood" fringing that photographs show never resolved; at seeing 2 it was just a soft grey halo breathing in and out. The narrowband filter added contrast but stole the central star, so it came back out.

With twenty minutes left before moonrise I hopped to Castor out of stubbornness. Splitting a 5-arcsecond pair in rolling air is a coin flip, and the coin mostly landed badly: two bloated discs smearing into a figure-eight. Twice — for perhaps a second each — the air froze and both stars snapped to points with black sky knifed between them. Two seconds of a double star is a poor haul by any accounting except the one that matters, which is that I saw it and the clouds did not stop me.

Moon up, haze in, kettle on. Sixty-five minutes, door to door.
