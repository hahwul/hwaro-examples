First hard frost of the autumn, which by the private calendar of this ledger makes it New Year's Day. The scope came out of the shed for the first time since March wearing a summer's worth of dust on the tube and a secondary mirror that had drifted comfortably out of true. Twenty minutes on the doorstep with the collimation cap and a defocused Polaris put the shadows back in the middle, and the season was open.

<!-- more -->

The evening's excuse was a new 24 mm wide-field eyepiece, bought in the summer sales and untested — 50× and a two-degree field in the ten-inch, which is about as much sky as this focal length will ever serve at once. There is exactly one correct first-light target for a field like that in November, and it was already riding high between Cassiopeia and Perseus.

The Double Cluster did not disappoint; it never has. Both clusters — NGC 869 tighter and richer, NGC 884 looser with its little chains — sat in the same field with room to spare, maybe two hundred stars resolved and the pair of ember-orange supergiants in 884 showing colour immediately. The frost helped: cold, dry, early-winter air over the fen carries a blackness the humid months never manage. I sketched the brighter chains badly, as tradition requires.

Swept the field north into Stock 2 afterwards, the big sprawling "Muscleman" cluster that only a wide field makes sense of, then finished on M34 in Andromeda's direction — coarse, bright, maybe eighty stars at 50×, a cluster that looks best when you don't try too hard at it.

By ten the fen mist was climbing the hedge and the red-dot finder had grown a fur of rime. Session closed with the traditional first-night inventory: fingers, ten; eyepieces, four; resolutions to observe every clear night this winter, one, already suspect.
