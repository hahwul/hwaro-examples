Minus twelve, the coldest entry this ledger has ever carried, and the sky priced it accordingly: bone-dry arctic air, a new moon, and stars to the rooftops in every direction. The kit list expanded to meet it — two pairs of gloves, chemical hand warmers in both boots, and the eyepiece case kept indoors until the last minute so the glass would not frost on first breath.

<!-- more -->

The Bode pair rides nearly overhead in January, which means the dobsonian's least dignified posture and the observer kneeling on a foam kip mat like a penitent. It was worth the knees. M81 and M82 sat in the same 50× field, the grand spiral a soft glowing oval with a bright core, the cigar a hard-edged splinter — the finest contrast in amateur astronomy, one calm galaxy and one catastrophe in a single view.

At 198× M82 gave the night its keeper: the dark diagonal cut across the middle third, the great dust gash, visible with direct vision and *structured* with averted — the western half of the cigar clearly brighter and lumpier than the eastern. I have glimpsed the cut before. I have never simply been able to look at it.

M81 got the patrol treatment: fifteen minutes against the photocopied chart, checking the halo star by star for an interloper. No supernova tonight — there almost never is — but the patrol is a ritual now, and the one winter I skip it will be the winter one goes off.

NGC 2403 in Camelopardalis rounded out the galaxy work, big and diffuse and honest, then Rigel as a closing indulgence: at 264× the companion stood clear of the glare, a steady blue-white spark in the first diffraction ring's shadow. Seeing 4 verging on 5 by midnight, but by then the battery in the red-dot finder had died of cold and my pencil had stopped writing, the graphite apparently on strike below minus ten.

Inside by half past midnight. The kettle took four minutes and I heard every second of it.
