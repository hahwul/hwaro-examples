In Ryoan-ji, the famous rock garden in Kyoto, fifteen stones sit on a bed of raked white gravel. No matter where you stand, you can only see fourteen at once. The fifteenth remains hidden.

This is not a flaw. It is the point.

## The Garden as Constraint

A rock garden is an exercise in radical constraint. No water, no plants, no color. Just stone and gravel. Within these limits, every decision matters immensely. The angle of a stone. The pattern of the rake marks. The distance between groups.

When everything is stripped away, what remains must be precisely right.

## Focus in Practice

We can apply this to how we work. Instead of spreading effort across many projects, choose one. Instead of building features in parallel, build one well. Instead of reading ten articles superficially, read one deeply.

The garden does not try to be a forest. It does not apologize for its bareness. It commits fully to what it is, and in that commitment, it achieves something a forest cannot: clarity.

## Arrangement Matters

The fifteen stones of Ryoan-ji are arranged in five groups: 5, 2, 3, 2, 3. This asymmetry is deliberate. Symmetry is static, complete, closed. Asymmetry is dynamic, unfinished, alive.

In writing, this means varying your sentence length. In code, it means not forcing every function into the same pattern. In design, it means breaking the grid occasionally, because a small irregularity gives the eye something to discover.

## The Hidden Stone

That fifteenth stone you can never see -- it reminds us that completeness is an illusion. There is always something beyond our view. A bug we have not found. A perspective we have not considered. A reader who will understand our words differently than we intended.

This is not a problem to solve. It is a truth to sit with.

The garden does not need you to see all fifteen stones. It only needs you to be present with the fourteen you can see.
