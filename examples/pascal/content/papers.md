Paper is not a background; it is half the print. Riso ink sinks differently into
every sheet, and letterpress wants give. Here is what we keep racked and how it
behaves under the drum.

## On the shelf

| Stock | Weight | Best for | Ink note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Newsprint improved | 55gsm | Zines, flyers | Ink glows, dries fast, shows through |
| Munken cream | 120gsm | Pamphlets, text | Warm, even lay-down, low offset |
| Cotton rag | 300gsm | Cards, covers | Takes deep letterpress bite |
| Chipboard | 400gsm | Covers, tags | Riso reads muddy; foil looks great |
| Colorplan offcuts | 135gsm | Accents | Wildcard colours, limited stock |

## Splitting artwork for riso

Each ink is printed from its own pass, so your file needs one layer per colour,
each drawn in flat 100% black. Overlaps mix optically on the sheet.

```text
poster.pdf
├─ layer: RISO-RED      (100% K)  → drum 1
└─ layer: FEDERAL-BLUE  (100% K)  → drum 2
```

Set halftones between **34 and 85 lpi**. Finer than that and newsprint fills in;
coarser and it turns into a dot pattern you did not ask for. Keep critical detail
on a single layer so registration drift never breaks it.

## Bring your own

We are happy to print on stock you supply if it feeds cleanly and is not coated.
Bring three test sheets first. Coated and plastic-feel papers reject riso ink and
will smear for days, so we will turn those away rather than waste your run.
