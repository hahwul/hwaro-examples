Typography begins with constraint. The baseline. The x-height. The cap height. The ascender line. These invisible rails keep letters in formation, marching left to right in orderly procession. Without them, text becomes illegible. With them, it becomes invisible -- so familiar that the eye passes through the letters to the meaning behind them.

## Breaking the Baseline

The history of experimental typography is the history of artists asking: what if we break the rules? Filippo Marinetti shattered the page in his 1914 Futurist manifesto Zang Tumb Tumb, scattering words across the page at every angle, in every size. The text did not merely describe an artillery bombardment -- it became one.

David Carson, designing Ray Gun magazine in the 1990s, set an entire interview in Zapf Dingbats, rendering it unreadable. The content was preserved but the communication was destroyed. Or was it? The gesture itself communicated more than the words ever could.

## The Information Cost

Every departure from typographic convention carries an information cost. Rotate a word fifteen degrees and the reader must work harder to parse it. Set body text at forty-eight points and the page holds only a few words. Use six different typefaces in one paragraph and the eye has no anchor.

This cost is not always a loss. Sometimes friction is the message. A word that resists reading draws attention to itself as an object, not merely a vehicle for meaning. The letter A is both a phoneme and a triangle. We forget the triangle when we read fluently. Disordered typography reminds us.

## Order Serves Chaos

The paradox of typographic disorder is that it requires deep knowledge of order to execute well. You must understand the grid before you can break it meaningfully. Random noise is easy. Controlled disruption -- disruption that communicates -- demands mastery of the very rules being violated.

The best disordered typography exists in tension with its own legibility. It pulls the reader in two directions: toward comprehension and toward pure visual experience. That tension is not a defect. It is the point.
