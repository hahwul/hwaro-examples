In 1948, Claude Shannon published "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" and gave us a framework for thinking about information. His key insight was that information and uncertainty are the same thing. A message that tells you something you already know carries no information. Only surprise -- deviation from expectation -- is informative.

## The Noise Floor

Every communication channel has a noise floor: a baseline of random interference below which no signal can be detected. AM radio crackles. Analog television snows. Digital images pixelate. The noise is always there, a reminder that the universe does not naturally organize itself into messages.

To communicate, we must lift our signal above this floor. We use redundancy -- saying the same thing multiple ways. We use error correction -- adding structured patterns that allow the receiver to detect and repair damage. We use compression -- stripping away everything that is not essential.

## When Noise Becomes Signal

But the boundary between signal and noise is not fixed. It depends on the listener. A doctor hears diagnostic information in a heartbeat that sounds like noise to an untrained ear. A geologist reads million-year histories in rock strata that look like random layers. A musician finds rhythm in a dripping faucet.

What we call noise is often signal we have not yet learned to decode. The cosmic microwave background radiation was dismissed as antenna interference until Penzias and Wilson realized they were hearing the echo of the Big Bang. The noise was the most important signal in the history of cosmology.

## The Art of Listening

Information theory tells us that the channel capacity -- the maximum rate at which information can be reliably transmitted -- is determined by the ratio of signal power to noise power. Increase the signal or decrease the noise, and you can communicate more.

But there is a third option that Shannon's mathematics does not capture: change the receiver. Train the ear. Sharpen the eye. Develop new instruments. The noise floor is not a property of the universe alone. It is a property of the conversation between the universe and the observer.

The most profound advances in human understanding have not come from louder signals. They have come from learning to hear what was always there.
