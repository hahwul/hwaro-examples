Fern fronds resist a clean press. The rachis carries moisture longer than the pinnae, and uneven drying produces a curled specimen that no amount of weight will correct after the fact.

## Cutting

Trim each frond at the base of the stipe. Leave a length of stem long enough to label, but short enough that it sits flat against the blotting paper. A frond longer than the standard mounting sheet should be folded in a single deliberate bend rather than cut into segments. Cuts interrupt the vascular line and disturb the visual record of the plant.

## Layering

Build the press from the bottom up: corrugate, blotter, newsprint, specimen, newsprint, blotter, corrugate. The blotters draw moisture outward; the corrugates allow air to pass between layers. A single frond requires two full blotters changed at twenty-four and seventy-two hours. After the third change the specimen will hold its shape and may be left for the remainder of the drying period.

## Pressure

Apply weight evenly. Stack a press with five to seven kilograms across the surface, distributed through plywood rather than concentrated on a single point. Excess pressure at the rachis produces a flattened spine and a brittle pinna; insufficient pressure leaves the leaflets curled and rough on the dorsal side.

## Mounting

Once the frond is dry it is brittle and translucent. Position it on archival card with the dorsal surface down so the sori face the viewer. Adhere with linen strips at three points: base, midpoint, and tip. Avoid covering the venation with adhesive. Label the sheet with the collection date, locality, and habitat. The specimen is now stable and can be filed in the cabinet alongside others of the same family.
