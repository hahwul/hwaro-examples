A mounted sheet without a label is a curiosity. A mounted sheet with a complete label is a record. The difference is a few lines of plain text, written in graphite or archival ink, fixed to the lower right of the card.

## Required Fields

The herbarium label should record, at minimum, the accepted scientific name, the collector, the collection number, the date, and the locality. The locality should include both a coordinate pair and a verbal description, since coordinate systems shift over time and a place name carries context that a number cannot.

A useful label also notes the habitat: substrate, slope, exposure, and the species growing nearby. These details allow a later researcher to reconstruct the conditions under which the specimen was found, even if the site has since been altered.

## Format

Keep the label small. A sheet of two hundred and fifty by one hundred and seventy-five millimeters is enough for a dozen entries, and the standard size lets sheets stack without overhang. Print or write in a single typeface throughout the collection. Mixed handwriting and print is harder to scan, and inconsistent abbreviations slow the catalog process.

## Ink and Paper

Use carbon-based ink or pencil. Dye-based inks fade within decades; carbon survives indefinitely on cotton card. The card itself should be lignin-free and buffered to a neutral pH. Acidic paper yellows and embrittles within a generation, taking the label and sometimes the specimen with it.

## Revisions

When a determination changes, do not erase the original label. Add a determination slip beside it, signed and dated by the reviser. The history of identification is part of the specimen's value. A sheet that has been re-determined three times by three different specialists carries more weight than a sheet with a single unchallenged name.
