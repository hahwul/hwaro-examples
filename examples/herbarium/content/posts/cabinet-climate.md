A specimen survives in storage only as long as the storage environment remains stable. Fluctuation is the enemy. Wood expands and contracts with humidity; paper absorbs and releases moisture in the same cycle; insects move toward warmth and cellulose. A herbarium cabinet that holds a constant climate will outperform a chemically treated cabinet that swings through a wide range.

## Target Range

Aim for sixteen to eighteen degrees Celsius and forty-five to fifty-five percent relative humidity. Below forty percent humidity, paper becomes brittle and adhesives crack; above sixty percent, mold spores germinate on the dried tissue. The temperature ceiling matters because most herbarium pests, including the cigarette beetle and the drugstore beetle, complete their life cycle more quickly above twenty degrees.

## Sealing

Steel cabinets with gasketed doors hold their climate longer than open shelving. The interior should be lined with archival board, not painted metal, since coatings off-gas volatile compounds that interact with plant tissue over years. Group sheets by family inside acid-free folders, then stack folders no more than fifteen centimeters deep so that air can move between them when the door is opened.

## Monitoring

Place a data logger in each cabinet and read it monthly. A logger that records both temperature and humidity at hourly intervals will catch the slow drifts that sensory inspection misses. Plot the readings; a stable cabinet shows a flat line, while a failing seal shows a daily oscillation tied to the building's HVAC cycle.

## Pests

Freezing remains the most reliable treatment. Bag any incoming specimen in polyethylene, expel the air, and hold it at minus thirty degrees Celsius for seventy-two hours. Thaw it slowly inside the bag to avoid condensation, then transfer it to the cabinet. Repeat the freeze if any sign of activity, frass, or exit holes appears during routine inspection.
