Drummond's Reach lands herring the way it has for two hundred years, except now it is five boats instead of forty, and the quota board at the co-op gets shorter every season rather than longer. This sheet was cut from the second winter of a project that started as one morning's favour to a skipper who needed a photograph for an insurance claim, and turned into two years of four o'clock starts.

<!-- more -->

What holds the harbour together now is mostly habit and mostly Rendel Voss, who has skippered the Iris May for eleven years and can't yet say what he'd do instead. Frame 21A — the wheelhouse frame, his hand on the wheel with the harbour mouth caught in the reflection of the glass — is the one that got circled at the first pass, and it's the one that ran in three different regional papers over the following year, always cropped to the hand and never to the reflection, which was the point of shooting it that way in the first place.

The ice house is the real subject underneath the boat pictures. It was built to hold five days of a full fleet's catch and now rarely holds one morning's from five boats, and the empty shelving reads, frame after frame, like the actual shape of the decline in a way the tally chalkboard only states in numbers. Both are in this sheet because neither tells the whole story without the other.

This project is still open. A third winter is planned if the fleet is still fishing by then, which is not something anyone at Drummond's Reach says out loud but everyone plans around anyway.
