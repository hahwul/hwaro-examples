Kessane Roads runs one scheduled call a week now — a feeder ship up from the deep-water terminal forty miles south, carrying whatever didn't fit the bigger vessel's own night turn. The whole operation is six hours, always starting somewhere between midnight and two, and the town it serves is almost entirely asleep for it, which is what made this the project it turned into: not a study of a port, but a study of the one shift nobody in Kessane Roads ever sees, despite it being the shift that keeps the town on the shipping map at all.

<!-- more -->

Slackwater 3200 was chosen for this sheet specifically because it can hold the sodium quay lights without blocking up the shadow under the gantry, at the cost of a grain that reads, printed large, almost like a second kind of night mist over the whole frame. That grain is doing real work in frame 6A — the circled one — where the container stack loses all its written detail and becomes pure shape and colour block, which is closer to how the stack actually reads to a lash crew working by torchlight than a sharper, better-lit version would be.

Odalys Ferris has worked the lash crew for four years and features in more of this project than anyone else, mostly because she is the one who explained, on the first night out, which parts of the operation were safe to stand near with a camera and which parts were not. Frame 5, her torch raised mid-signal, was reshot four separate nights before the beam caught the crane operator's cab window at the right angle to read as a signal rather than just a light in the dark.

A second sheet from the summer turn — longer nights, different light — is planned once the port confirms it's keeping the weekly call through the next contract renewal.
