Ambrose Teal has run the chandlery and rope loft at Ashcombe Quay for thirty-one years, and before that apprenticed under the man who ran it for the thirty years before him, which means the shop has had exactly two riggers this century. He is sixty-eight, has no apprentice, and says plainly that when he stops, the nearest place to get a proper eye splice done by hand will be a chandlery forty miles up the coast — a fact he states without much emotion, the way people state facts about tides.

<!-- more -->

The order book, photographed in frame 11, is the clearest evidence in this whole sheet of how much the trade has shrunk without shrinking to nothing: six weeks between entries now, where Teal says it used to be six entries a week, but the one entry that's there is for a trawler's new mooring warp, which means somebody with a boat still needs work only he can do, and will keep needing it as long as he's willing to do it.

Frame 10 and 10A are the same splice, thirty seconds apart — the first mid-work with three strands crossed and no tool holding anything in place, the second finished and held up to the loft's one window to check the tuck by eye. 10A got the circle because it's the frame that shows the actual test Teal applies to his own work: not a gauge, not a measurement, just held up to daylight and looked at, the same way Bethan Ogilvy at [Salt House Row](/sheets/salt-house-row/) checks a kipper's colour by eye rather than a thermometer. Neither of them learned the trade from a manual.

The chandlery sign in the last frame has had a broken bracket for at least two years, by Teal's own estimate — he knows how to fix it and has simply not gotten around to it, which says something about where his time actually goes that the rest of this sheet is trying to show.
