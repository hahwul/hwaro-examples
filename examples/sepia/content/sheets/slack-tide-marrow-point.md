Marrow Point doesn't have a proper dry dock, a travel lift, or anything else a bigger yard would call standard equipment — what it has is a hard standing that only clears the tide for about forty minutes either side of low water, twice a day, and a boatwright, Colm Praed, who has built his whole working method around that window rather than around asking the harbour authority to dredge him a real slipway. It's the same low-tech logic that keeps Ambrose Teal splicing rope by eye at the [Ashcombe Quay chandlery](/sheets/rope-loft-ashcombe-quay/): work with the tide and the tools at hand rather than wait for a fix nobody's coming to pay for.

<!-- more -->

Everything in this sheet happens inside that forty minutes, which is the whole point of it as a project: five frames because five frames is roughly what the window allows time to shoot without getting in Praed's way or, worse, still being on the hard when the water comes back. The tide clock in frame 2, nailed inside the shed door rather than kept on a phone, is the actual instrument the whole operation runs on — Praed checks it before every job and plans the scrubbing, the antifouling, and the re-launch around what it tells him, not around a calendar.

Frame 1A, the circled frame, is the scraper mid-stroke with a single ribbon of weed coming away from the hull — chosen over the wider, more legible frame 1 because it's the frame that actually shows the speed the work has to happen at. There's no time in a forty-minute window for a careful, considered stroke; the weed has to come off in long pulls or it doesn't come off in time, and 1A is the frame where that urgency reads without needing a caption to explain it.

By the time frame 5 was shot, the hull was already back on its mooring and the hard standing was underwater again, which is roughly how long the whole sequence takes from start to finish, tide clock to tide clock.
