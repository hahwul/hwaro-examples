Three boats still work the ninety fathom line out of the estuary ports, down from around twenty in the 1980s, and getting a berth on one of them to photograph a full trip took most of a year of asking. The boat that finally said yes, on the condition of not being named in captions, runs five-day trips with a crew of six and a factory deck that never really stops between the first trawl and the last.

<!-- more -->

The working rhythm on a boat like this doesn't follow daylight, which is most of what this sheet is trying to show. Frame 2, the gutting line at two in the morning lit only by headlamps, and frame 5, breath fogging over an ice hold that's been loaded since well before dawn, are both from what would be called the middle of the night anywhere on land — on the boat, they're simply mid-shift, no different in tempo from any other hour of the trip.

Frame 1A, the circled frame, was almost left off the sheet. It's a trawl door mid-swing with no crew visible, which on a first look reads like an accident of timing rather than a chosen frame. It was chosen precisely for that reason: five days on the factory deck taught that most of what actually moves the gear happens in the half-second nobody's hand is on it, between one crew member letting go and the next one taking hold, and the empty-handed frame says that more plainly than any of the frames where somebody's gripping the chain.

Mira Sten, in frame 6, has fished the outer grounds for nine years and was the one who suggested cutting the sheet down to nine frames from the roll's full thirty-six — her reasoning being that a five-day trip doesn't need thirty-six frames to be understood, it needs the nine that don't repeat themselves.
