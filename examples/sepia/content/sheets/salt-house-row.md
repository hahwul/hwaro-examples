Salt House Row is six curing sheds built shoulder to shoulder along the old herring quay, four of them empty now and two still lit most weekday mornings by the Ogilvy family, who have cured fish on this row since the sheds were built. This sheet came out of a single August spent mostly standing in a smokehouse that reaches, by mid-morning, somewhere past body temperature, waiting for smoke to do something worth photographing.

<!-- more -->

Most of what a working kiln actually produces, hour to hour, is smoke rather than finished kippers, and frame 2A tries to say that plainly: no fish in the frame, no building outline, just the smoke itself moving through the roof vents in the particular grain that oak gives it. It was circled on the first pass over Bethan Ogilvy's own objection that a sheet about a smokehouse should probably have a fish somewhere near the top — the counter-argument, which won, was that the smoke frame does more to explain what curing actually looks like, minute to minute, than any frame with a finished kipper in it could.

Ogilvy checks the fish by hand and by eye, no thermometer anywhere in the shed, a method she says came down from her grandmother unchanged except for the wood — oak now, where it used to be whatever hardwood offcuts the boatyard had spare. Frame 4, her hand testing colour on a rack of herring, is the frame most people ask about when the sheet is shown as prints, usually wanting to know how she can tell, which is a question this sheet can show but not really answer.

The shed next door has been empty since 2019 and gets one frame here mostly as scale — six sheds working this row in 1960, two now, and the two that remain are both run by the same family working the same wood.
