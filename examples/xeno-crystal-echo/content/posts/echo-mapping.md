A second and third shard were recovered from sites four and seven kilometers from the original location. Both were found at the base of similar quartzite outcrops, oriented along an axis that runs approximately north-northwest. The alignment is too consistent across three samples to assume coincidence.

When the shards are placed in the same chamber, the emission pattern of each is altered. Specifically, the inversion cycle observed in the original shard is suppressed, and a new low-frequency modulation appears across all three. The modulation is identical on each shard within the precision of our instruments. Removing any one shard from the chamber restores the independent behavior of the remaining two.

We are calling this an echo response, with no commitment to the term beyond convenience. The response does not depend on physical orientation, distance within the chamber, or whether the shards are visually shielded from one another. It does depend on the presence of all three. Two shards alone produce no measurable interaction.

The simplest explanation is a shared coupling to a field we have not yet measured. The shards may be acting as resonators in that field, with the modulation reflecting the field's own structure. If that interpretation holds, the field would have to be present at all three recovery sites, with consistent character across the kilometers separating them.

Survey teams have been redirected to extend the search along the alignment axis. We are particularly interested in whether a fourth shard would extend the modulation, suppress it further, or produce a wholly different response. The instruments have been recalibrated to capture a wider band on the assumption that we are still looking at a small portion of the actual signal.
