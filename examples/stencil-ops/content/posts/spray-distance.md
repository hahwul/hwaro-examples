Edge quality on a sprayed stencil is determined by three things: the gap between the mask and the substrate, the angle of the spray cone, and the distance from the can to the surface. Of those, distance is the variable most often handled by feel, and most often handled wrong.

A standard rattle can holds a fan that opens to about 6 centimeters at 30 centimeters distance. At that working range, the paint arrives at the substrate with enough velocity to deposit a clean line, and the overspray that leaks under the mask is minimal. Closing the distance to 15 centimeters doubles the deposition rate and forces solvent into the gap, where it lifts the mask and bleeds the line. Opening to 50 centimeters drops the velocity below the threshold for a sharp edge and produces a gritty, stippled line.

Lateral angle matters as much as distance. Holding the nozzle perpendicular to the surface gives a symmetric line. Tilting it 30 degrees produces a soft edge on the far side of the cone and a hard edge on the near side. The hard edge is sometimes desirable for shadow effects, but it accelerates buildup on the underside of the mask and shortens the working life of a vinyl stencil.

Test passes are required. Spray a 10 centimeter diagonal across the same substrate, on a scrap, and inspect the edge under raking light. Bleed shows as a halo. Stippling shows as discrete dots. A clean edge has neither. Adjust distance two centimeters at a time and reshoot until the test edge is clean. Only then proceed to the work.
