A stencil is a hole pattern, but it is also a structural object. Every closed shape in the source image needs at least one bridge connecting its interior to the exterior, or the cut piece will fall through when the blade comes around. A capital O without a bridge becomes a disc. Counters in lowercase letters are the same problem at smaller scale.

Bridges are usually placed where they read as part of the design. On lettering, that means following the natural seam of the stroke. On organic shapes, the rule is to align the bridge with the dominant axis of the form, so it appears as a contour rather than an interruption. Two thin bridges across a long opening hold the mask flatter than one thick bridge in the center.

The width depends on the medium. Vinyl tolerates 0.8 millimeter bridges. Mylar at 175 microns starts to tear at anything below 1.5 millimeters. Heavy stock board needs 2.5 millimeters to survive repeated peelings, and the bridge has to be reinforced if the stencil sees more than four uses.

Layer registration changes the calculation. A four-color stencil with a three millimeter bridge on layer one will leave a visible gap on layer four if the gap falls inside an area meant to be solid. Plan the bridges across all layers before cutting any of them. Mark the registration crosses first, then trace the bridge positions onto every sheet to confirm that overlapping layers fill the gaps left by neighboring bridges.

The cleanest stencils are not the ones with the fewest bridges. They are the ones where the bridges read as deliberate marks.
