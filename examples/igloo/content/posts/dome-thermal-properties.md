A finished snow dome resists heat loss through three mechanisms: the insulating value of the wall material, the geometry of the enclosed volume, and the stratification of air inside. Each contributes about a third of the overall thermal performance, and weakening any one of them causes a measurable drop in interior temperature.

### Wall Conductivity

Compacted snow has a thermal conductivity of roughly 0.20 to 0.30 watts per meter-kelvin, depending on density. That places it between softwood and dry sand. A wall thickness of 30 centimeters provides the same insulation as 8 centimeters of cellulose batting. The tradeoff is mass: the wall weighs over 100 kilograms per square meter, which is why the dome needs a curved compression form to remain standing.

### Air Stratification

Inside a heated igloo, warm air collects in the upper third of the dome. The sleeping platform is built at this level rather than on the floor. Below the platform, a cold well traps dense air that has lost heat to the outer wall and settled. Doorways and entry tunnels exit from the cold well, so warm air never escapes through the opening.

A common error is venting the apex. The dome must have a small ceiling vent to release moisture and combustion gases, but a vent larger than 5 centimeters across will lose more heat than the occupants generate. A finger-sized hole, drilled through the keystone block, is sufficient for two people sleeping.

### Glaze Layer

After the first night of occupation, condensation freezes on the inner wall and forms a glassy layer about 2 millimeters thick. This glaze reduces wall porosity and reflects long-wave radiation back into the interior. A second night raises interior temperatures by another two to three degrees without any change in heat input.
