Compacted snow behaves more like a soft sedimentary stone than a powder. After two or three days of settling under wind pressure, drifts develop a uniform density of roughly 350 to 400 kilograms per cubic meter. That density is the threshold where saw cuts hold a clean edge and blocks resist crumbling under their own weight.

### Sourcing the Quarry

The first task is locating a stable drift on the leeward side of a slope or ridge. Probe the surface with a thin metal rod. Resistance should feel even from top to bottom. A sudden soft layer means a wind crust trapped fresh powder, and any block cut across that boundary will fracture during stacking.

Mark out the quarry in a long trench rather than scattered pits. A continuous wall face gives the saw a vertical reference and produces blocks of consistent size with minimal waste. A standard block measures 60 by 40 by 20 centimeters and weighs around 18 kilograms.

### Cutting Sequence

Use a coarse-toothed snow saw with a stiff spine. Make the two vertical cuts first, then the horizontal back cut, and finally the bottom release. The block should lift out cleanly with two hands. If it sticks, the bottom cut is too shallow.

Stack rejected blocks downwind of the work area. They serve as a windbreak for the cutter and provide spare material for chinking gaps later in the build.

### Wall Geometry

Lay the first course on level ground, with each block tilted inward by about five degrees. Stagger the joints in the second course like brickwork. By the fourth course, the inward lean produces a self-supporting curve. The wall locks itself together before any binding agent is needed.
