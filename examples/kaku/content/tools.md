In the practice of Japanese calligraphy, the tools are referred to as the "Four Treasures of the Study" (Bunbou Shihou). These include the brush (fude), the ink stick (sumi), the paper (washi), and the inkstone (suzuri). Each tool is selected with care, as their individual qualities interact to shape the final character on the page.

<!-- more -->

1. **Fude (Brush)**: Made of natural animal hair bound together and inserted into a bamboo handle. The brush's tip (saki) must be fine, and its body springy enough to return to its shape after a sweep.
2. **Sumi (Ink stick)**: A solid block made of soot mixed with glue (nikawa) and fragrance. When ground against the inkstone with water, it produces a rich, dark liquid that smells of pine and camphor.
3. **Washi (Paper)**: Hand-crafted paper made from the fibers of the mulberry tree. Washi's absorption rate dictates the flow and speed of the brush.
4. **Suzuri (Inkstone)**: A flat stone container with a sloped well. The stone's fine abrasive surface allows the sumi stick to be ground smoothly into liquid.

To learn Shodo is to enter into a relationship with these materials. By understanding their history, care, and behavior under different conditions, the calligrapher learns to adapt their brushwork and ink thickness, turning writing into a harmonious dialogue between hand, ink, and paper.
