In Japanese calligraphy, a stroke is not drawn with the fingers; it is driven by the entire body. The alignment of your spine, the placement of your feet, and the rhythmic cycle of your breath are just as important as the grip of your hand on the brush. Without proper physical posture, the brushstrokes will lack strength, and the writer will quickly tire.

<!-- more -->

To write properly, sit with your spine straight and shoulders relaxed. Your chest should be about a fist's distance from the edge of the desk, and both feet must rest flat on the floor, providing a solid foundation. Hold the brush vertically, using the double-segment grip (Twin Finger method) where the thumb, index, and middle fingers support the handle from all sides. Your wrist should be straight, and your elbow must float above the table. This allows your entire arm to move freely, transferring the energy (ki) from your core through your shoulder, arm, and down into the brush tip.

Coordination of breath is the final element of posture. Inhale slowly as you prepare the brush, and exhale smoothly as you execute the stroke. The movement of the fude should match the rhythm of your breathing. By synchronizing body, breath, and brush, the calligrapher enters a state of deep focus, allowing the brush to flow naturally. The resulting strokes carry a sense of vitality and depth that cannot be achieved through manual manipulation alone.
