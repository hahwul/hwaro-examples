Washi paper, handmade from the inner bark of the mulberry tree (kozo), gampi, or mitsumata, is the classic surface for Shodo. Unlike standard wood-pulp paper, washi possesses long fibers that give it exceptional strength and flexibility. When sumi ink meets the paper, these fibers absorb the moisture in a unique way, allowing the ink to bleed slightly (a phenomenon known as nijimi) while retaining the crispness of the stroke's core.

For beginners, selecting the correct washi is critical. There are two primary categories: absorbent paper (nama-gami) and treated, non-absorbent paper (dosa-biki). Absorbent washi is highly sensitive to the brush's speed and ink volume. If the brush stays in one place for even a fraction of a second too long, the ink will blossom outward into a large, soft black cloud. This requires the calligrapher to write with confidence and swiftness.

Conversely, treated washi has been sized with animal glue and alum, which slows absorption. This paper allows for slower, more deliberate strokes, making it ideal for delicate kana script or highly detailed kaisho characters. Choosing the right paper is about aligning the materials with your calligraphic goals. A rougher, fibrous kozo paper can yield beautiful, dry brush effects (kare-hitsu), where the white of the paper breaks through the black stroke, showing the physical texture of the brush and the paper fibers.
