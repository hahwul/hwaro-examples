Japanese calligraphy, or Shodo, begins with the character "Yong" (meaning eternal). This single kanji contains all eight of the basic strokes used in regular script (Kaisho). By mastering the character Yong, the calligrapher trains their hand and eye to execute virtually any stroke combination found in other characters. The study of this character is a lifelong pursuit, serving as both a diagnostic tool for beginners and a meditative return to form for advanced masters.

The first stroke, the dot (Ten), represents the initial drop of rain hitting the paper. It requires a firm, downward pressure of the fude, followed by a clean lift that retains the energy of the brush. The horizontal stroke (Hyo) must carry a slight upward tilt, simulating a rising wind. The vertical stroke (Do) demands absolute verticality and stability, ending with a sharp release. The fourth stroke, the hook (Kaku), is a sudden contraction of the brush tip followed by a swift release to the left. The remaining strokes—the upward sweep, the long left-falling stroke, the short left-falling stroke, and the right-falling sweep—each require precise control over the brush's heel and tip.

Through repeated practice of Yong, calligraphers learn the vital concept of structure and form. It is not merely about drawing lines, but about balancing the negative white space of the washi paper with the solid, textured blackness of the sumi ink. Every brushstroke is an unrepeatable record of the calligrapher's state of mind at that exact second.
