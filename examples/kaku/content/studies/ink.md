Preparing ink is not a preliminary chore; it is an essential phase of Shodo practice. Traditional ink is made by mixing pine soot (derived from burning pine wood) with animal glue (nikawa) and a touch of perfume, compressed into solid sumi sticks. To transform this stick into the liquid sumi ink required for calligraphy, one must grind it on an inkstone (suzuri) with a small amount of fresh water.

The process of grinding is slow and rhythmic. You should place a few drops of water in the well of the suzuri, then slide the sumi stick in circular motions against the flat surface of the stone. Apply gentle, even pressure, keeping your shoulder and elbow relaxed. The friction between the stone's fine grain and the ink stick slowly releases the pigment, gradually turning the clear water into a deep, opaque black liquid. This process typically takes fifteen to twenty minutes of quiet concentration.

During the grinding process, the calligrapher has time to focus their mind and adjust their breathing. The aroma of the ink, often scented with camphor or clove, signals to the mind that Shodo practice has commenced. The thickness of the ink is crucial: if it is too thin, it will bleed rapidly on the paper; if it is too thick, the brush will drag, leaving broken, dry strokes. A perfect sumi ink should flow smoothly from the brush, leaving a rich, slightly glossy black mark.
