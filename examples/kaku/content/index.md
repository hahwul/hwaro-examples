The essence of brushwork in traditional Japanese calligraphy lies in the control of line weight, speed, and ink dispersion. Every stroke is a physical manifestation of energy, transferring from the practitioner's core to the tip of the fude. The brush is held vertically, allowing it to glide across the washi paper with varying degrees of pressure.

When writing, one must master the three main scripts: Kaisho (block script), Gyosho (semi-cursive), and Sosho (cursive). In Kaisho, every stroke is separate and clearly defined, requiring patient precision. Gyosho allows the strokes to flow into one another, reflecting the fluid speed of the writer. Sosho is highly abstract, where characters are simplified into single continuous lines.

Regardless of the script, the calligrapher must remain conscious of the structural balance. White space is not empty; it is a positive element that defines the black ink. The tension between ink and paper, speed and stillness, form and formlessness is what gives calligraphy its enduring power.
