## Mission Parameters

Launch time: 0015 local. No moon. Stars obscured by cloud cover. Surface conditions: sea state 2, 1.2 metre swells from the south-southeast. The Tonga Trench — the second deepest oceanic trench on Earth — runs northeast-southwest along the Tonga-Kermadec subduction zone. Maximum surveyed depth: 10,882 m. Tonight's target: 8,376 m at a documented amphipod aggregation site from the 2022 HADES survey.

Night launch is procedurally identical to day launch, except the ocean surface is indistinguishable from the sky. Both are black.

---

## Descent Profile

**0–500 m.** Even at night, the epipelagic zone retains a degree of residual luminosity — scattered starlight, bioluminescence from surface plankton. By 300 m it is gone. By 500 m the exterior lighting is the only source of photons in any direction.

Temperature drop from 27.3 C at surface to 4.8 C at 500 m. The thermocline is abrupt: within thirty metres the temperature falls twelve degrees. The vehicle shudders faintly as hull materials contract.

**500–2,000 m.** The mesopelagic and upper bathypelagic. Standard observations. At 780 m, sonar returns indicate a dense scattering layer — the deep scattering layer, composed primarily of myctophid fish and crustaceans that migrate vertically each night to feed at shallower depths. The vehicle descends through this community like a stone through a cloud of insects.

At 1,400 m: a Vampyroteuthis infernalis — the vampire squid, technically a cephalopod in its own order — holds position in the beam for 28 seconds. Its bioluminescent photophores produce a steady blue-white illumination along both arms. When the manipulator arm approaches, it inverts its mantle over its body — the characteristic defensive posture — and releases a cloud of luminescent mucus before retreating into the dark.

**2,000–5,000 m.** Sparse. The bathypelagic and upper abyssal is the most productive zone for scientific surprise and the least productive for raw biomass. At 3,300 m, a Pacific black dragonfish — Idiacanthus antrostomus — appears at the edge of the camera field. Approximately 40 cm. The photophore strip along the ventral surface is active. It tracks the vehicle for approximately two minutes, ascending alongside it, then breaks off and descends.

**5,000–8,376 m.** The trench proper begins at approximately 6,000 m. The walls become visible on sonar: steep, sediment-draped slopes leading down to the axial valley. At 7,000 m, the sediment becomes finer — a pale coccolith ooze below the carbonate compensation depth, where calcium carbonate dissolves faster than it can accumulate.

The amphipod aggregation site appears at 8,200 m on the downward sonar. A density anomaly in the acoustic data that correlates with known amphipod swarm signatures.

**8,376 m — Bottom contact: 0614 local.**

---

## Bottom Operations

The amphipods are present in numbers that exceed the 2022 baseline by an estimated 40%. Hirondellea gigas — the dominant species at hadal depth in the Pacific — swarms the bait traps within 90 seconds of deployment. Individual specimens range from 20 to 54 mm in length.

Observation: the amphipods display a consistent orientation relative to the vehicle's exterior lights, remaining within the lit zone even as the light source moves. This may indicate either phototaxis at wavelengths not predicted by existing models, or a vibration/pressure response to the vehicle's thrusters.

Water samples collected at 8,376 m, 8,100 m, and 7,800 m for microplastic analysis. Previous hadal surveys have documented microplastic contamination at depths exceeding 10,000 m. The expectation is that Tonga will be consistent with this pattern.

Sediment core: 35 cm depth. Recovered intact.

Time at bottom: 2 hours 41 minutes. Temperature: 1.8 C. Pressure: 838 atmospheres.

---

## Ascent and Recovery

Ballast released at 0855. Ascent nominally uneventful until 4,200 m, where the acoustic modem signal degraded due to an uncharted temperature inversion layer. Communication loss: 22 minutes. The vehicle's autonomous systems maintained ascent course.

Signal reacquired at 3,800 m. All systems normal.

Surface recovery in darkness at 1923 local. A crew of six worked the recovery in 1.8 metre swells. Vehicle secured aboard Falkor at 1941.

## Notes

The night descent changes the perceptual experience of the operation, but the physical parameters are identical. The darkness that greets you at 500 m is the same darkness regardless of the surface conditions above.

What changes is the return. Breaking the surface at 1923, the exterior cameras show nothing — no horizon, no light, no reference. Then the recovery spotlights hit the vehicle and the ocean resolves again into something navigable.

The amphipods will be processed at the onboard biology lab over the next 48 hours. Preliminary visual inspection suggests at least two individuals with morphological characteristics inconsistent with H. gigas. Genetic sequencing will determine whether these represent variation within the species or an undescribed taxon.

Fourteen days until the next descent. The Kermadec Trench, 400 km south.
