A lens brings parallel light to a point. A tumbler does not — its curved wall is not ground to any single focal length, so the rays that pass through its rim cross the rays that pass through its centre at a different depth, and the result is a net of light rather than a point, the caustic every kitchen has seen thrown across a countertop at breakfast.
<!-- more -->

## What a caustic actually is

Each curve on this plate is one ray's path from the flat top of the field to wherever it lands after the tumbler's curved wall bends it. Because the wall's curvature changes continuously from centre to rim, no two rays converge at exactly the same depth — they braid instead, densest where the most rays happen to cross, and that dense braid is the bright rope of light you see wobbling on a table under a water glass.

## Measuring a moving target

The waist — the depth where the braid is tightest — was logged at thirty-four millimetres below the tumbler's rim for this sample, but it drifts by several millimetres with the water level inside, which is why the bench keeps this plate dry: glass alone, no liquid, so the measurement stays put long enough to draw.

## Logbook note

This tumbler came from a mismatched set found at a flea stall on Kolding Street, kept specifically because its wall had an uneven, hand-blown curve — a machine-pressed glass throws a tidier caustic, but a tidier caustic is a less honest one.
