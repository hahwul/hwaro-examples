The simplest plate in the book is also the one visitors ask about most: a single flat pane, no wedge, no facet, just a boundary. A beam entering air at thirty-four degrees leaves the interface bent to twenty-one, and the seven-nanometre gap between where violet and red land on the far wall is the entire subject of this catalogue in miniature.
<!-- more -->

## Reading the plate

The dashed line is the surface normal, not a ray — everything is measured against it, never against the glass edge itself. Snell's law gives the bend directly: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂. With air at 1.00 and this pane's N-BK7 at 1.517, a thirty-four degree entry angle refracts to twenty-one, and that single number is doing all of the visible work in the fan on the right.

## Why the fan spreads at all

A flat, uncut pane barely disperses light — the plate exaggerates the effect for legibility, the same way a map exaggerates a coastline. In a real one-centimetre pane the violet-to-red spread at the far wall would measure well under a tenth of a millimetre; here it is drawn wide enough to count the bands with the eye, because a plate nobody can read is not a record, it is decoration.

## Logbook note

Measured on the bench's smallest sample, salvaged from a broken picture-frame pane and cut square. Runa keeps it clipped to the front of the book as the calibration plate — every new apprentice draws this one first, by hand, before touching the SVG method.
