Cut a hexagonal drop steep enough and light stops leaving politely through the nearest face — it hits the glass-air boundary at an angle beyond the critical angle, reflects entirely back inside, and keeps bouncing until it finds a facet shallow enough to let it out. This drop trapped its beam for four bounces before it escaped, dispersed, from a facet on the far side.
<!-- more -->

## Below the critical angle, nothing escapes

Every boundary a ray meets inside the glass is tested against the same threshold: past 38.3 degrees from the facet's normal, on this sample, the glass-air interface stops transmitting at all and reflects the beam back inside as if the facet were a mirror. Cheap cut glass wastes this — shallow facets leak light out at the first surface and the piece looks dull. A steep, deliberate cut keeps the beam captive, bouncing it deeper into the material, which is the entire reason hand-cut crystal reads as brighter than machine-pressed glass of the same clarity.

## Four bounces, then out

The dotted nodes mark each internal reflection logged for this drop: entry near the top-left facet, then three bounces working down through the body, then a fourth that finally meets a facet shallow enough to transmit. Dispersion only becomes visible at that last exit — every bounce before it reflects the full white beam intact, since total internal reflection does not sort wavelengths the way a refracting exit does.

## Logbook note

This drop came off a chandelier arm missing its fitting, bought for the price of scrap. Runa re-cut two facets by hand to steepen the critical-angle geometry before logging it — the original cut let the beam escape after only two bounces, which made for a duller plate.
