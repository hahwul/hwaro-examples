This is the plate every visitor expects and the one Runa is proudest of, because a clean wedge is harder to cut than it looks. Sixty degrees at the apex, ground from dense flint rather than crown, gives the widest, most evenly ordered fan on the bench — the textbook diagram, reproduced from an actual sample rather than assumed from a formula.
<!-- more -->

## Why flint, and why sixty degrees

Dense flint glass carries a much higher refractive index than the crown pane in Plate 01, and more importantly its index changes faster across the visible band — a property called dispersion, distinct from refraction itself. A crown wedge would spread the same white beam into a fan a third as wide. Sixty degrees at the apex is not sacred; it is simply the angle at which this sample happens to sit near minimum deviation, the point where the exit fan is narrowest and most symmetric for a beam entering close to normal.

## Reading the two internal legs

The pale line inside the glass bends once at entry and once more at the apex-side face before it fans on exit — two refractions, not one, which is the whole difference between a wedge and the flat pane in Plate 01. Everything downstream of that second bend is the same physics as Plate 01, just repeated at a sharper angle and a denser material.
