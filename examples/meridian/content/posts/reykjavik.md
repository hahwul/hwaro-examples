Iceland uses GMT year-round and never observes daylight saving time, making Reykjavik one of the few capitals permanently fixed to UTC+0. Despite sitting at 21 degrees west longitude -- which would geographically place it in UTC-1 or UTC-2 -- the country chose GMT for practical alignment with Europe.

<!-- more -->

## Midnight Sun and Polar Night

At 64 degrees north latitude, Reykjavik experiences extreme variations in daylight. In June, the sun barely sets, hovering just below the horizon for a few hours. In December, daylight lasts only about four hours. Time itself feels different when light no longer marks the boundary between day and night.

## Geographic Anomaly

Iceland is one of the most striking examples of political time differing from solar time. Solar noon in Reykjavik occurs around 13:30 GMT, meaning the city's clocks are perpetually ahead of the sun by roughly 90 minutes.

## Key Timezone Facts

- **Longitude:** 21.9426 W
- **Standard Time:** GMT (UTC+0)
- **Daylight Saving:** Not observed
- **Solar Time Deviation:** Approximately +1.5 hours from solar noon
