Ashfold Kiln is Peter and Rae Nettleton, working from a converted lambing shed where a two-chamber climbing kiln does all the talking. Nothing here is glazed in the usual sense — the surfaces are written by four days of flame and falling ash, and no two pots leave the chamber alike.

<!-- more -->

They fire twice a year, in March and October, burning through roughly three tonnes of seasoned larch and ash offcuts from the surrounding plantations. The front of the kiln runs hot and clean; the back catches the heavy ash and comes out crusted, glassy, almost geological. For Conjunction they are bringing the March firing more or less untouched: tall bottles, lidded jars, and a run of tea bowls that show the flame path as a clear diagonal wash.

## What to look for

- **Front-stack bottles** — quiet, toasted, the clay just blushing orange.
- **Back-stack jars** — heavy natural-ash runs, some pooled thick enough to ring.
- **Guinomi and yunomi** — small cups sold in loose sets, no two matched.

Peter will be at the stand between demonstrations and is happy to talk through where in the kiln any given pot stood. If you ask nicely, he will draw you the stacking plan on the back of your receipt.
