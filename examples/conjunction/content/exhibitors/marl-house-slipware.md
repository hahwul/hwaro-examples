Marl House is the nearest studio to the fair — Tom Ferris works barely a mile down the lane from the brickworks — and the only earthenware maker in the halls. His slipware follows a line of English country pottery that runs back three hundred years: red clay, cream slip trailed on freehand, then a warm honey glaze over the top.

<!-- more -->

The decoration is all done wet, at speed, before the slip can set. Tom trails lines from a slip cup, combs through them with a stick, and feathers them with a single quill drawn across the surface. There is no correcting a wobble; the mark is the mark. It gives the work a looseness that fussier techniques never manage.

## On the stand

- **Harvest jugs** — the big showpiece, combed bands under a honey glaze.
- **Pie dishes and bakers** — slip-trailed rims, made to go in a real oven.
- **Chargers** — wide flat plates, feathered, meant for a dresser or a wall.

Because it is earthenware, the work is a little softer than the stoneware around it — Tom is candid about this and will tell you which pieces are for daily use and which are for looking at. He runs the slip-trailing demonstration at two o'clock each day.
