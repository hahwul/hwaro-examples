Eight point six, and falling roughly a degree a fortnight now — the sound going down its grey ladder one rung at a time, and me going down with it. November is the season of subtraction. Every week the sea takes something back: first the long swims, then the light, then, this morning, the feeling in my little fingers somewhere around minute nine.

<!-- more -->

Which reopens the annual gloves question. The purists at the sailing club say skin or nothing, that gloves are the first plank of a slippery deck that ends in a wetsuit and, presumably, moral collapse. I say the purists have desk jobs and I have a keyboard to type on Monday morning.

The settlement, as every year: bare hands to five degrees, because hands are my thermometer-behind-the-thermometer — the cold arrives in them honestly and on schedule, and I trust their reporting more than my own enthusiasm. Below five, the neoprene mitts come out and the purists can file their objections with the sea.

Twelve minutes today in short chop the colour of old aluminium, swimming the ladder line where Halvard can see me. No revelations, no seals, one cormorant surfacing rudely close. The best November swims are exactly this dull. Drama is for the months with margin in them; this is the month you bank routine against January, one boring rung at a time.
