Sea fog sat on the sound this morning like a lid on a pot, and I swam inside it — five minutes at 3.4 degrees in a world reduced to a radius of perhaps ten metres, everything beyond that a soft white rumor.

<!-- more -->

Fog swims have their own protocol, agreed with Ede over two winters: never past the ladder line, bright cap regardless of who is watching, and Halvard on the slip ringing the little brass bell every thirty seconds. It sounds absurd written down. In the water it is the whole architecture of courage. You cannot see the shore, but the bell keeps striking its small dry note behind you, and so the shore continues to exist.

What surprises me every time is how loud a fog is. Last winter [the cold was the whole story](@/swims/the-door-in-january.md); this morning it was only the second strangest thing in the water. With the eyes given nothing to do, the ears take over the ledger: the bell, the drip of my own arms, a ferry mooing somewhere far up the sound, the fizz of my breath on the surface. At three degrees the cold is total, but fog makes it strangely companionable — the water and the air the same grey, the same patience, the border between them mostly a formality I kept crossing with my mouth.

Five minutes, out at the bell, rung twice for confirmation. On the slip the fog had beaded my towel with droplets fine as sieved flour. Ede says fog swims do not count as views. Ede is wrong; they are the view turned inward.
