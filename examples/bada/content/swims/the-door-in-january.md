At 2.8 degrees the sea stops being a place and becomes a door. You do not swim in water like this, not really; you pass through it, and the whole discipline is in how you open the door — slowly, with your breath already long before your feet leave the slip.

Halvard was on the wall with the flask. Air at minus four, the sound flat as poured glass, a low white sun that gave no heat but a great deal of instruction. The thermometer took its usual thirty seconds and I spent them exhaling, which is the only preparation that has ever worked.

The gasp still came — it always comes below five degrees — but January has taught me to treat it as punctuation rather than emergency. One breath taken by the sea, then the count: out for four, in for two, out for four, until the water goes from assault to fact. I did my four minutes along the ladder line, hands stinging, thinking about nothing at all. That is the January gift. The cold is so loud that the rest of the head finally goes quiet.

Out at the shiver's first knock. Dressed in the strict order — top half first, hat before boots — and drank Halvard's coffee, which was somehow both burnt and weak. The walk home took twelve minutes and I was warm again for the last two. Four minutes in the sea, an afternoon of feeling like the door had opened onto a slightly larger room.
