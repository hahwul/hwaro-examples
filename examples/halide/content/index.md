Halide Press is a two-ink screen-printing operation run out of a converted darkroom, publishing one small poster edition at a time. Every plate in the series depicts a piece of the process that turns exposed film into a fixed, permanent image — the tray, the enlarger, the fixer bath, the bulb overhead, the proof sheet, the reel — redrawn at wall scale using only the two colors a working darkroom actually sees: black, and the deep safelight red that everything else in the room is bathed in while a print comes up.

Nothing is added for the sake of the poster. If a shape looks like screen-printed shorthand for a piece of darkroom equipment, that is because it started as a sketch of the real thing, made on-site, under the same red bulb the fourth print in this series is named for. The one constant across every plate is a punched sprocket strip running down its left edge — a leftover from the reel that loaded the first roll of film this whole series was drawn from, kept as the mark that ties one poster to the next.
