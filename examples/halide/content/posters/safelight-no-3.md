The safelight is the reason this entire poster series exists in two inks. Black-and-white printing paper is sensitive mostly to blue and green light, barely at all to deep red — a gap in the emulsion's sensitivity that darkrooms have exploited since the nineteenth century to let printers see what they are doing without ruining the paper they are doing it to.

<!-- more -->

An OC filter — the industry name for the standard amber-red gel — clipped over a low-wattage bulb and hung four to six feet above the work surface is enough light to sort prints, read a timer, and find the edge of a tray without fogging anything, provided exposure stays reasonably brief. Printers learn its limits the hard way: leave resin-coated paper under even a safelight too long, or hang the fixture too close, and a faint reddish fog creeps into the highlights of every print made that session.

We drew this one as the bulb itself, rays extended past the edge of the poster, because the safelight is the only light source in the room that a photograph is allowed to witness its own making under. Everything else in the series — the tray, the enlarger, the reel — exists inside the glow this bulb casts. It is less an object than the room's one permitted color, which is also why it supplies this whole series' second ink.
