Every darkroom is built around one piece of furniture, and this is it: a light source, a negative carrier, a lens, and a column tall enough to throw that light down onto a baseboard at whatever size the print calls for. Ours is drawn as a plain condenser enlarger — no diffusion head, no color filtration drawer, just the bones of the machine that every printer eventually learns to operate half-blind, by feel, in the dark.

<!-- more -->

The cone in the poster is the actual geometry of the projection: negative at the narrow end, image spreading as it falls, focus sharpening it back down at the baseboard. Move the head up the column and the print grows; stop the lens down and depth of focus forgives a little imprecision in the negative carrier, at the cost of exposure time. Printers talk about "burning" and "dodging" as if they were verbs invented for the darkroom, but they are just this cone, interrupted — a hand or a cutout held in the light path for a few extra seconds to hold back a sky or open up a shadow.

We drew the lamp housing in flat safelight red because it is, functionally, the one part of the machine that must never leak white light while paper is exposed. A pinhole in a housing gasket can fog a whole box of paper before anyone notices; the poster's solid red block is a reminder that the enlarger's job, first and foremost, is containment.
