Before a single negative gets enlarged, it gets proofed. A contact sheet is made by laying an entire strip of developed negatives directly against a sheet of printing paper under glass, exposing the whole stack at once, and developing it as one print — every frame from a roll at true negative size, in one pass, in the order they were shot.

<!-- more -->

It is a working document more than a finished print, and printers treat it that way: grease pencil marks circling the frames worth enlarging, crop lines sketched straight onto the print's surface, whole rows crossed out without ceremony. Nothing on a contact sheet is precious. Its entire purpose is to let a printer see thirty-six exposures at a readable size before committing paper, chemistry, and an evening to the two or three that deserve it.

The poster's grid keeps six of those frames — the geometric shapes standing in for whatever a roll actually contained, since the negatives themselves are long gone. One frame is outlined in red, the way a printer circles a keeper: not because it is objectively the best exposure on the roll, but because contact sheets are where a photograph gets chosen, not just taken, and every roll eventually needs a decision made about it.
