The tray is the least glamorous object in any darkroom and the one every print actually depends on. Ours is drawn at three-quarter scale from a battered eleven-by-fourteen Cesco tray that has sat under the same amber light for eleven years, its bottom scarred with the faint ghost-scratches of ten thousand sheets dragged through by tongs.

<!-- more -->

Print developer is a slow, alkaline reaction — hydroquinone and Phenidone (or Metol, if the formula runs old-school) reducing exposed silver halide crystals to metallic silver while leaving the unexposed grains alone. Time it wrong and the image blocks up or starves; sixty seconds too short and the shadows never open, sixty seconds too long and the highlights start to fog. The whole art of contrast control lives inside that narrow window, which is why every darkroom worth trusting has a timer bolted to the wall at eye level and a printer who watches it instead of the paper.

The poster's central ripple pattern is not decorative. It traces the actual agitation wave a sheet makes when you rock a tray by its short edge — the motion printers are taught before anything else, because uneven agitation is the single most common cause of streaking in an otherwise perfect print. The corner of paper lifting out of the bath is the moment every printer watches for: the first hint of an image surfacing through liquid, still reversible, still just chemistry deciding what to become.
