Nobody frames a fixer poster expecting romance, but sodium thiosulfate — hypo, in every darkroom's shorthand — is the compound that makes photography a lasting medium instead of a fifteen-minute trick of light-sensitive salts. Development builds the image; fixing is what stops the rest of the paper from turning solid black the first time someone opens a drawer in daylight.

<!-- more -->

The chemistry is a straightforward substitution: thiosulfate ions dissolve the unexposed, undeveloped silver halide still sitting in the paper's emulsion, converting it into soluble silver-thiosulfate complexes that wash out in the following rinse. What remains bonded to the paper is only the silver that development already reduced to metal — the image itself, now chemically inert and ready to survive light, air, and several decades of handling.

The poster's diagram is drawn schematically rather than to strict bond geometry — sulfur and oxygen atoms in white, sodium counterions in red — because the point is legibility on a wall, not a chemistry-class citation. Under-fixing leaves a print that yellows and stains within a year as residual silver salts continue reacting; over-fixing bleaches the image itself. Between those two failures sits a window of a few minutes, timed as carefully as development, that is the actual difference between a photograph and a photograph that used to exist.
