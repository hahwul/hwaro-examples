Lacquer is the solo studio of Aoi Katsuragi, working from a converted machiya in Kanazawa's Higashi district, three streets from the leaf-beaters who have supplied the city's gold since the seventeenth century. Aoi trained for eleven years under a maki-e restorer before opening this bench in 2013, and still sources leaf from the same beating house her teacher used — partly out of loyalty, partly because 0.1-micron gold behaves consistently only when it comes from someone who has been beating it the same way for three generations.

The material is *urushi*: sap tapped from the lacquer tree, *Toxicodendron vernicifluum*, refined by slow stirring and gentle heat until it is smooth enough to brush in a film a few microns thick. It does not dry in open air. It cures through an enzyme, laccase, that only works in humidity — roughly 75 to 85 percent, at a warm room temperature — so every coat goes into a *furo*, a damp cedar cabinet, and stays there for a day or two before it can be touched again. Rush the cure and the surface stays tacky forever. There is no shortcut version of this material.

## How a piece is built

A piece leaves this bench somewhere between thirty-eight and forty-six coats deep, depending on its size and what it asks for in decoration. Roughly, the schedule runs:

| Stage | What happens |
|---|---|
| Ground | Base coats over the core — wood, metal, or a hand-built lacquer-and-cloth form — sanded flat between each pass to remove brush marks and open grain. |
| Build | The bulk of the coats: thin, even layers laid one at a time, each cured a full day or two before the next, each sanded back before it goes on. This is where most of the three months lives. |
| Decoration | Gold or shell worked into a still-wet or nearly-cured coat — leaf laid whole, powder sprinkled through a bamboo tube, shell inlaid and later ground level — then sealed under further clear coats. |
| Polish | The final coats are cut back by hand with charcoal, water, and deer-horn powder rather than sanded, finishing in a mirror the sandpaper stages could never reach on their own. |

Every current piece on the bench, and its exact coat count, is logged under [Collections](/collections/).

## Materials and sourcing

Urushi comes from a cooperative of tappers in the mountains north of the city, harvested by scoring the bark in narrow cuts through the growing season — a single tree yields perhaps a small cupful across an entire summer, which is most of why the material costs what it does. Gold leaf comes exclusively from the beating house mentioned above, in 24-karat sheets beaten to roughly 0.1 microns; shell inlay, when a piece calls for it, is abalone sliced by hand to translucency.
