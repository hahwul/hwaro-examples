Plum blossom is the flower that opens while there is still snow on the ground, which is why it shows up on lacquer more than almost any other motif this bench works with — it reads as patience made into an image, which happens to be the whole argument of this workshop anyway. This ring carries one blossom, five petals, cut from a sheet of abalone shell sliced to something close to paper thickness.

<!-- more -->

*Raden* is the technique: shell ground down until it is thin enough to cut cleanly with a fine blade, the blossom shape scored petal by petal, then set into a coat of black lacquer while it is still tacky enough to grip the shell but firm enough not to let it drift. Three further coats went over the inlay to bury it completely — shell this thin disappears under lacquer almost immediately — before the surface was ground back with charcoal and water until the blossom reappeared exactly level with the black ground around it, the same *togidashi* logic used on the cuff elsewhere in this collection, just worked at ring scale instead of cuff scale. The band's outer rim was gilded last, a strip of 24-karat leaf laid with a bamboo brush and burnished by hand until it sat flush rather than raised.

Sized to order before the first ground coat goes on, since a lacquer ring cannot be resized afterward without cutting through months of work — a fact every commission conversation for this piece starts with.
