This cuff has no metal core. It is built the way lacquer Buddhist statuary has been built for over a thousand years — *kanshitsu*, dry lacquer — where layers of hemp cloth soaked in urushi are wrapped around a wooden former until the shape is rigid enough to stand on its own, at which point the former is cut out and discarded, leaving a shell that is lacquer through and through rather than lacquer over something else.

<!-- more -->

Six layers of cloth went around the former before the first sanding, cured a day apart, before the surface was clean enough to take the black ground coats proper. The wave running around the cuff's face is *keshifun* — gold powder fine as dust, sprinkled through a bamboo tube onto a coat still wet enough to hold it, in a line drawn freehand rather than stenciled. Two more clear coats went over the powder to bury it completely, then the surface was ground back with charcoal and water — *togidashi*, "drawn out by polishing" — until the gold reappeared exactly at the surface, flush with the black lacquer on either side of it rather than raised above it.

**Coat log**

| Stage | Weeks | Coats | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kanshitsu core | 1–3 | 6 | Hemp cloth built up around a wooden former in six wraps, former removed once rigid. |
| Ground | 4–6 | 11 | Black kuro-urushi base coats over the cloth shell, sanded flat between each. |
| Wave | 7–9 | 8 | Gold keshifun sprinkled freehand, then buried under clear sealing coats. |
| Togidashi | 10–12 | 12 | Ground back with charcoal and water until the buried gold reappears flush at the surface. |
| Polish | 13–15 | 9 | Final roiro polish by hand — deer-horn powder, camellia oil, and a great deal of the maker's own palm — to the deep black mirror the cuff is named for. |

Forty-six coats is the longest schedule currently on the bench, and the wave is drawn slightly differently from the two prior cuffs built this way, because freehand work on wet lacquer does not repeat itself twice.
