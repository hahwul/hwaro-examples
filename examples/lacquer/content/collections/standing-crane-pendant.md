Forty coats is the number this workshop is named for, more or less by accident — it is simply the count this pendant happened to land on, and it stuck as shorthand for every piece afterward even though the real number runs anywhere from thirty-eight to forty-six depending on what a piece needs. This crane is the one that is actually, literally forty.

<!-- more -->

The base is *kanshitsu*, the same dry-lacquer cloth-and-former method used for the Deep-Water Cuff elsewhere in this collection, scaled down to a pendant the size of a thumbnail. The crane itself is *taka-maki-e* — raised maki-e — built not by carving but by layering: a paste of urushi mixed with fine charcoal powder is worked over the black ground with a small spatula across several sessions, each layer cured before the next, until the bird's body, neck, and folded wing stand a full two millimeters proud of the surrounding lacquer. Once the relief was fully built, the whole crane was dusted in gold powder while a final tacky coat still held it, then sealed and polished so the bird sits slightly raised and unmistakably gold against the black ground rather than flush with it, the way the shell and gold-dust work on other pieces in this collection are.

**Coat log**

| Stage | Weeks | Coats | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kanshitsu base | 1–2 | 5 | Pendant shell built from cloth and urushi over a small former. |
| Ground | 3–5 | 10 | Black kuro-urushi base coats, sanded flat before the relief work begins. |
| Relief | 6–9 | 16 | Crane built up in raised layers of charcoal-lacquer paste, cured between passes. |
| Gold & polish | 10–12 | 9 | Gold powder dusted over the finished relief, sealed, and hand-polished around the raised form. |

Hung on a plain waxed-cord loop rather than a chain, so nothing metal competes with the gold. The next crane, if there is one, will not necessarily land on forty again.
