The cicadas move into the workshop garden every August, loud enough to work around rather than through, and this brooch is the fourth piece built in response to them rather than in spite of them. The wing shape is cut from sterling silver and built up not in the usual opaque black or red but in *suki-urushi* — urushi clarified and filtered until it runs almost clear, so that whatever sits beneath a coat still shows through it, the way real wing membrane shows the veins underneath.

<!-- more -->

The gold is not powder here but *kirigane*: leaf cut into small geometric pieces — rectangles under a millimeter wide, in this case — with a bamboo-handled knife on a lacquer-coated cutting board, then set into a still-tacky coat one piece at a time with a brush barely damp enough to lift them. It is slow, fussy work; a wing's worth of kirigane took four sessions on its own, longer than some entire coats. Each gold piece then disappears under two or three more clear coats, so the final surface reads as one continuous amber field with gold suspended somewhere inside it rather than printed on top.

**Coat log**

| Stage | Weeks | Coats | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ground | 1–3 | 9 | Silver wing cut, keyed, and built up with opaque ground coats before the clear work begins. |
| Kirigane | 4–6 | 6 | Gold leaf cut and set in four sessions, sealed under two clear coats between sessions. |
| Build | 7–10 | 17 | Suki-urushi laid in thin clear passes over the gold, each cured and sanded before the next. |
| Polish | 11–13 | 10 | Final coats cut back by hand with charcoal and deer-horn powder to a soft, deep gloss — kept short of a full mirror so the gold stays visible rather than glaring. |

The pin back is hand-forged sterling, set after the final polish so the finished lacquer is never touched by a clamp. No two kirigane layouts from this bench have matched exactly, and this one will not be repeated.
