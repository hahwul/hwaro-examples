Patience is worked into this atelier the way gold is worked into lacquer: in thin, repeated passes, never all at once. A single brooch begins as raw sap tapped from a lacquer tree in early summer and ends, ten to fifteen weeks later, as thirty-eight to forty-six coats of urushi, each one cured on its own in a damp cedar cabinet, sanded back by hand before the next is laid, before a final polish is allowed to happen and not one day sooner.

Urushi will not be hurried. It does not dry the way paint dries, by losing water to the air — it cures by an enzyme reaction that needs humidity to run, so every coat spends its day or two inside a *furo*, a cabinet kept damp and warm on purpose, and comes out no faster for wanting it to. Ask for a piece by a date and the honest answer is the date the fortieth coat happens to cure, not before.

Nothing leaving this bench is built to meet a season, a fair, or a standing order. What follows is not a catalogue so much as a running log: the pieces currently on the bench or newly off it, each one dated to the week its last coat set.
