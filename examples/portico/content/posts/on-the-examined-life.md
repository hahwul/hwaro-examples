Socrates famously declared that the unexamined life is not worth living. This pronouncement, delivered at his trial as recorded in Plato's Apology, stands as one of the most consequential statements in the history of Western thought. It is not merely a personal confession but a radical claim about the nature of human existence: that self-knowledge is not an intellectual luxury but a moral necessity, and that a life lived without critical reflection on one's beliefs, values, and actions falls short of what it means to be truly human.

The Socratic method of examination proceeds through dialogue. Unlike the Sophists, who delivered polished speeches for payment, Socrates engaged his interlocutors in a process of questioning that exposed contradictions in their assumptions. Through the elenchus -- the method of cross-examination -- he demonstrated that those who claimed to possess knowledge often could not withstand even basic scrutiny. The politician who claimed to know justice, the poet who claimed divine inspiration, the craftsman who extended his expertise beyond its proper domain: each was revealed to possess a confidence unmoored from genuine understanding.

> "I know that I know nothing." -- Socrates, as reported by Plato

What makes the Socratic project enduringly relevant is not its conclusions but its method. The examined life is not a destination but a practice. It demands that we hold our convictions provisionally, subject them to the test of reason and evidence, and remain open to the possibility that we have been mistaken. In an age saturated with information yet starved of wisdom, the Socratic imperative has lost none of its urgency. To examine one's life is to refuse the comfortable sleep of unquestioned assumption and to accept the difficult wakefulness of philosophical inquiry.
