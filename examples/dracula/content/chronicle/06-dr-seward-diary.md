10 September.—I was sets at the railway station when Van Helsing's train arrived. He looked older, his face more heavily lined, but there was the same bright, piercing look in his eyes that I remember from my student days in Amsterdam. He shook my hand warmly and said nothing of the journey, but immediately began to ask of Lucy's case. He is a man of vast knowledge, both in traditional medicine and in those obscure fields of science that modern doctors dismiss as mere superstition. I feel a great sense of relief now that he is here, for the burden of Lucy's mysterious wasting has been heavy on my shoulders.

We drove directly to the house, and he went at once to her room. She was sleeping, but her sleep was restless and her breathing short and shallow. The professor stood over her for a long time, looking at her throat and testing her pulse. When he turned back to me, his expression was grave indeed. He shook his head and whispered that there was something here he did not like—something beyond any ordinary disease of the blood. He has asked for garlic flowers to be placed about the room, and though I do not understand his reasoning, I know him too well to question his judgment.
