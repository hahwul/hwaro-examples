18 July.—Things are getting worse. The men are terrified and refuse to go on watch alone. Yesterday, another man vanished from the deck during the dog-watch. We searched the entire ship, from stem to stern, but found no trace of him. The cargo of wooden boxes filled with earth remains undisturbed, but there is a strange, moldy smell in the hold that I cannot account for. The crew speaks of a tall, thin man who is not one of us, whom they claim to have seen near the hatchway at night. I tell them it is only the fog and their own imagination, but in my heart, I am beginning to fear.

24 July.—A terrible gale has come upon us, and the sea is running mountain high. We are running under bare poles, and the fog is so thick we cannot see a ship's length ahead. Another man is gone, and now only myself, the first mate, and two hands remain. The mate is half-mad with fear, babbling about a demon in the hold. I have chained the wheel, for my hands are trembling so that I can scarcely hold the spokes. If we do not make port soon, we shall all be lost to this shadow. I have tied my hands to the wheel with my rosary, so that whatever happens, I shall die at my post.
