## Abstract

A 34-year-old male ultramarathon runner collapsed at kilometre 68 of a 100-km mountain race with altered mental status and generalised tonic-clonic seizure. Field assessment revealed profound hyponatraemia (serum sodium 118 mmol/L). Despite fluid restriction and initial 3% hypertonic saline bolus, sodium continued to fall to a nadir of 114 mmol/L in the emergency department, accompanied by worsening encephalopathy necessitating endotracheal intubation.

The patient was admitted to the intensive care unit, where a refractory course required continuous hypertonic saline infusion with careful rate-limited correction to avoid osmotic demyelination syndrome. Sodium normalised over 72 hours. The patient was extubated on day 3 and discharged on day 7 with no neurological deficits.

This case illustrates the challenges of managing severe exercise-associated hyponatraemia (EAH) complicated by ongoing free water absorption from the gastrointestinal tract, the critical importance of rate-limited sodium correction, and the need for improved hydration education in endurance sports.
