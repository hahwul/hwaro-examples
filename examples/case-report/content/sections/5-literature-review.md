## Epidemiology of Exercise-Associated Hyponatraemia

Exercise-associated hyponatraemia (EAH) is increasingly recognised as a significant medical risk in endurance sports. A systematic review by Hew-Butler et al. (2015) reported an incidence of symptomatic EAH of 0.1-4% in marathon runners, with higher rates (up to 12%) in ultramarathon events exceeding 100 km.

Severe EAH (Na+ < 120 mmol/L) is rare but potentially fatal. The most comprehensive case series, published by Rosner and Kirven (2007), documented 14 cases of severe EAH requiring ICU admission, with 2 deaths and 3 patients with permanent neurological sequelae.

## Risk Factors

The following risk factors for severe EAH have been identified in the literature:

- **Excessive fluid intake** (> 1.5 L/hour) -- the most consistent predictor
- **Low body mass** (< 70 kg) -- larger dilution effect for a given fluid volume
- **Slow race pace** -- longer exposure time with continued drinking
- **Cool weather** -- reduced sweat losses, leading to greater net positive fluid balance
- **Female sex** -- possibly related to lower total body water and hormonal effects on AVP
- **NSAID use** -- impairs renal free water clearance (not a factor in this case)

Our patient had three of these risk factors: excessive fluid intake (1 L/hour for 8 hours), relatively low body mass (68 kg), and cool weather conditions.

## Current Guidelines

The Wilderness Medical Society (WMS) and the International Marathon Medical Directors Association (IMMDA) both recommend **drinking to thirst** rather than adhering to a fixed fluid intake schedule. This "ad libitum" approach has been shown to significantly reduce the incidence of EAH in marathon and ultramarathon events.

For treatment of severe symptomatic EAH, current consensus recommends:
- Immediate 100 mL bolus of 3% NaCl, repeated up to 3 times at 10-minute intervals
- Correction rate limited to 10-12 mmol/L per 24 hours
- Desmopressin as a "rescue" agent if correction rate is exceeded
- ICU admission for Na+ < 120 mmol/L with neurological symptoms
