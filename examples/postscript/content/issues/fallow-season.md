Six weeks ago, a ceramicist named Ines Corwin sent her last invoice. Since then: two promising leads that went quiet, a grant application still in review, and a bank balance sliding steadily toward the number she built in a slow month two years ago and has not needed until now. She wrote in asking, essentially, what a buffer is actually for once you're spending it — because "save for emergencies" turns out to offer no guidance at all about how to spend once the emergency arrives.

So this issue is a spending plan for the fallow season itself, not just the fund that gets you through it.

<!-- more -->

**Tier your spending before you need to, not during.** Ines built three tiers in the calm month before things went quiet — a habit worth copying whether or not a dry spell is currently visible on the horizon:

- **Tier one, un-cuttable:** rent, insurance, loan minimums, the materials retainer for any commission already in progress. This tier doesn't move regardless of how long the fallow season runs.
- **Tier two, paused first:** software subscriptions with a monthly-cancel option, discretionary materials for speculative work, the framer's invoice that can wait a billing cycle. Cutting this tier is annoying, not damaging.
- **Tier three, cut only past week four:** the portfolio photographer, the studio's second room, anything that exists to grow the practice rather than sustain it this month. Cutting tier three too early can cost more than it saves — it's often the thing that ends the fallow season.

**Watch the buffer's slope, not its balance.** A single number — "I have $780 left" — tells you almost nothing on its own. What matters is the rate: at Ines's current burn of roughly $210 a week against tier-one and tier-two spending, $780 is five more weeks, which is a different conversation than $780 sounding simply low. Recalculate the slope weekly, using the ledger system from [Issue No. 36](/issues/studio-ledger/), and let the number of weeks remaining — not the dollar figure — decide when tier three gets cut.

**The number on this cover is not a crisis.** Zero invoices out for six weeks, with a buffer still covering tier one and two, is exactly the situation the fund in [Issue No. 34](/issues/slow-month-fund/) was built for. The plan isn't to panic when the balance moves; it's to have already decided, in a calmer month, what moves first, what moves last, and what the weekly number needs to say before it's time to worry. Ines's buffer still has eleven weeks left at current burn. She is, by the only metric that matters here, on schedule.
