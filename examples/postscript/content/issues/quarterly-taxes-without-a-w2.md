If you've only ever worked a salaried job, tax season is something that happens to a form your employer already filled out. If you invoice for your income, tax season is something you build all year, four times a year, out of money you have to remember not to spend.

The mechanism is called an estimated quarterly payment[^1], and missing it isn't just inconvenient — the tax authority can charge an underpayment penalty even if you pay the full amount owed by April, because the expectation is that tax gets paid as income is earned, not all at once afterward.

<!-- more -->

**The set-aside habit is simpler than the tax code.** The moment a deposit clears — commission, sale, artist fee, day rate — move 25% of it into a separate account before it touches your operating balance. That figure runs a little high for most artists in the lowest bracket and a little low for anyone with a strong year, but as a default it errs toward "pleasant surprise in April" rather than "scramble in April," which is the direction you want to be wrong in.

**The quarters don't match the calendar.** They fall roughly six weeks after each three-month period ends, not at its close:

| Income earned | Payment due |
|---|---|
| January – March | April 15 |
| April – May | June 15 |
| June – August | September 15 |
| September – December | January 15 (next year) |

Note the second quarter is only two months and the fourth is four — an artifact of how the schedule was set decades ago, not a typo. Mark all four dates in whatever calendar you actually check, not the one you intend to check.

**What the set-aside account should never fund:** a slow month. It is tempting, in the gap between [Issue No. 34](/issues/slow-month-fund/)'s buffer running low and a check finally clearing, to borrow from the tax account "just until Thursday." Don't. A depleted buffer is a bad month; a depleted tax account is a bad month plus a penalty plus interest, arriving together next spring. If you need a cushion for that specific temptation, keep the tax account at a different bank than your checking — friction is a feature here, not an inconvenience.

[^1]: An estimated quarterly payment is a manual tax payment self-employed workers make four times a year, standing in for the withholding a traditional employer would otherwise deduct automatically from every paycheck.
