The email says you got it. The deposit says otherwise — not yet, and maybe not for a while. Across the eleven grant-funded artists who shared their timelines with us this issue, the median gap between award notification and first payment was sixty days. Two waited past ninety. One, a sculptor awarded a materials grant in March, didn't see the check until late June, well after the fabrication window she'd budgeted around had closed.

None of that is a reason to distrust the funder. It's a reason to stop treating a grant like a paycheck, because a paycheck is unrestricted — it's yours to spend on rent, ramen, or a plane ticket the moment it lands. A grant is restricted: it's earmarked, often line-by-line, for the project it funded, and funders can and do ask for documentation, or repayment, if the money drifts.

<!-- more -->

**Keep the ledger separate from day one.** Not a mental separate — an actual separate account, or at minimum a separate column in your bookkeeping software, the moment the award letter arrives. When the deposit lands, log it against the grant's budget categories, not your general income. If the grant funded "materials: $2,000, travel: $600, artist fee: $1,400," your ledger should be able to answer, at any point, how much of each bucket remains — because a funder's final report will ask exactly that.

**The artist-fee line is the part that's genuinely yours.** Most project grants include a fee for your time, separate from materials and expenses. That portion behaves like income: report it, spend it, buffer it. The rest behaves like a pass-through — money moving through your hands on its way to canvas, film, shipping, or a collaborator's rate.

**Two practical habits that save the fall reconciliation:**

- Photograph or scan every grant-funded receipt the day you make the purchase, and note which budget line it belongs to in the file name. "2025-09-materials-gesso.jpg" is worth more in February than your memory will be.
- If a grant runs long — spanning a tax year boundary, as most twelve-month project grants do — split your internal ledger at December 31 even if the funder's fiscal year doesn't. Your accountant will need it split that way regardless of what the grant agreement says.

The sixty-day gap isn't a flaw in the system; it's a review cycle, a board meeting, a finance department's own quarterly close. Budgeting for the wait — rather than for the letter's arrival date — is the difference between a grant that funds the work and a grant that funds three anxious months first.
