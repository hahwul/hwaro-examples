Every general-finance column says the same thing: save three to six months of expenses. It is good advice for someone with a biweekly deposit and a bad one for someone whose income looks like a seismograph. If your last twelve months included a $6,200 mural commission, four months of nothing, a $400 print sale, and a grant that paid out eleven weeks late, "six months of expenses" is not a number you can act on. It is a number that makes you feel behind and tells you nothing about what to actually save.

So we asked a printmaker named Dara Oyelaran, who has kept two years of deposit records in a spreadsheet she calls "the swamp," to help us build a different number: the smallest buffer that would have covered her worst stretch without her touching a credit card.

<!-- more -->

**The method, in three steps.**

1. Pull every deposit for the last 24 months — sale, commission, grant, freelance day rate, all of it — and sort them into calendar months.
2. Find your lowest three consecutive months. Not your average month: your worst run. For Dara, that was February through April 2024, when total income across all three months was $340.
3. Subtract your fixed monthly costs (rent, insurance, materials retainer, the studio's share of utilities) from what you actually earned in that stretch. The shortfall — the amount you would have needed to pull from somewhere else just to stay current — is your buffer target.

For Dara, fixed costs across those three months ran $4,100. She earned $340. The gap was $3,760, but $2,560 of it was covered by a delayed invoice that finally cleared in May — money that was hers, just late. The true shortfall, the part no invoice was ever going to fix, was $1,200.

That is the number on this issue's cover, and it is deliberately not a "feel safe" number. It is a "get through the specific bad month" number, which is smaller, which means you can actually hit it, which means you are more likely to build it instead of feeling perpetually behind a target six times too large.

**Keeping it separate matters more than the amount.** Open a second account — call it the buffer, not "savings," which your brain will raid for a plane ticket. Route 10% of every deposit over $500 into it until it hits target, then stop and let it sit. A buffer you draw from in March and refill in June has done its job. A buffer you never touch was sized wrong.

One caution: this number is not a substitute for a restricted fund[^1] if you're holding grant money earmarked for a specific project. Keep those separate ledgers — [Issue No. 35](/issues/grant-money-is-not-a-paycheck/) is entirely about why.

[^1]: A restricted fund is money a funder or client has designated for a specific purpose — a project grant, a materials advance, a deposit against future work. It is not yours to spend on rent even when it sits in your account, and good bookkeeping keeps it in its own column from the day it lands.
