Ask a working artist what they charge and most will quote a day rate or a per-piece price pulled loosely from what "felt right" the last time, adjusted up a little if the client seemed well-funded. Almost nobody can say what that number reduces to per hour once every unbillable hour is counted — and that number is the one that actually determines whether a full calendar is a living or a treadmill.

We built a floor-rate worksheet with a textile artist named Priya Anand, who does commissioned installation work and, like most working artists, spends more hours per project on things she can't invoice than on things she can.

<!-- more -->

**Start with your annual target income**, after tax set-aside — the number from [Issue No. 37](/issues/quarterly-taxes-without-a-w2/) already assumes a quarter of every dollar leaves before it's spendable, so work from take-home, not gross. Priya's target: $52,000.

**Then count your actual billable hours**, not your working hours. A 40-hour week sounds like 2,000 hours a year, but subtract vacation, illness, the unbillable time spent on quoting, invoicing, materials sourcing, studio upkeep, and the portfolio work that wins the next client but bills no one. Priya's honest count, after tracking three months with a timer: 22 billable hours a week, roughly 1,050 a year.

| Line | Amount |
|---|---|
| Take-home target | $52,000 |
| Fixed studio overhead (rent, insurance, tools) | $9,600 |
| Materials not passed through to clients | $2,400 |
| **Total to recover** | **$64,000** |
| Billable hours per year | 1,050 |
| **Floor rate** | **$60.95/hr** |

Priya's floor landed near $61, well above the $38 on this issue's cover — that figure is the collection average across the eleven respondents who ran their own numbers for us, and it moved a few of them considerably. Two discovered their current day rate, once divided by real billable hours, was under $20. One had been unknowingly profitable for years and used the exercise to justify a rate increase she'd been putting off out of nerves rather than math.

**The floor is a floor, not a price.** It's the number below which a project actively loses money once overhead and unbillable time are honestly counted. Where you price above it — for scarcity, specialization, or a client who can pay more — is a separate, more interesting conversation. But you can't have that conversation credibly until you know where the floor actually sits, and most artists have never calculated it because the arithmetic, while simple, requires admitting how many hours the invoice doesn't cover.
