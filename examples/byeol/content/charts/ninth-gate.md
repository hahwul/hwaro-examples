Two pillars of stars and a bright keystone form the gate the year itself must pass through. The first plate Mun drew, and the one he kept redrawing until the end.
<!-- more -->

## The figure

The islanders counted eight gates in the harbour wall of Keunseom, and said the ninth stood in the sky. Six stars raise its posts and lintel; the keystone is **Munju**, the brightest star of the southern winter — outshone across the whole year only by the lamp of [the Lantern Bearer](@/charts/lantern-bearer.md) — a *keun-byeol* drawn with the four-point prick. In the empty space beneath the arch sits a single faint star the tradition calls **Nageune**, *the traveller* — the one soul permitted to stand in the gateway without paying the toll.

At the turning of the year, boats returning from the long-line grounds would time their approach so that the Ninth Gate stood upright over the harbour mouth. To sail in beneath it was to bring the old year's luck home intact; to arrive after it had tilted past the meridian obliged the crew to circle the breakwater once, stern-first, before landing.

## Named stars

- **Munju** — *the keystone*. Great star at the crown of the arch, the winter steering mark.
- **Nageune** — *the traveller*. A small star inside the gate, visible only on moonless nights; children were sent to find it as a test of eyesight.

## Observing notes

The Gate culminates at local midnight in the week of the winter solstice, standing almost perfectly vertical from the harbour wall. Mun's marginal note reads: *"The pillars do not move. It is we who are ferried through."*
