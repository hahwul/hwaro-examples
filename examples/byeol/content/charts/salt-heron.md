Ten stars trace a heron frozen mid-wade, its long neck folded back over the brightest star of the figure. When the Heron "stooped" toward the western sea, the salt pans were raked for the last time that year.
<!-- more -->

## The figure

The Salt Heron is the tallest figure in the surviving folio, running nearly the full height of its plate. The S-curve of the neck hangs from **Soran**, the great star at the shoulder, before dropping through breast and leg to a foot that Mun drew standing exactly on his lower graticule arc — the bird wades along the altitude line itself.

Darae's outer islands made their winter money in salt, and the pans were governed by this bird. While the Heron stood upright in the evening sky, seawater was still let into the terraces; the night its beak first touched the sea-horizon, the sluices were closed and the rake-days began. A heron standing straight was patience; a heron stooping was appetite. The saying survives: *the pans belong to the bird*.

## Named stars

- **Soran** — *the shoulder*. The plate's *keun-byeol*, and the second-brightest star in the whole tradition. Pan-masters checked its colour at dusk: white meant dry raking weather, yellow meant rain within three tides.

## Observing notes

The Heron rises neck-first in late summer and stands fully upright through autumn evenings. Mun records that children learned the figure by standing on one leg for as long as the beak star stayed above the islet of Hakseom — "which is to say, not long, and with much falling over."
