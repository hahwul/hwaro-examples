Seven stars slant corner to corner across the plate: a sculling oar caught mid-stroke, blade buried in the sea-haze at the horizon. The simplest figure in the folio, and the oldest.
<!-- more -->

## The figure

Four stars make the loom of the oar, running from the grip at the top of the plate down to the throat; three more spread the blade. The blade's tip is **Mulkkot**, *the water-flower* — the flash a blade makes when it breaks the surface — and it is the figure's only great star, burning at the very point where the work happens.

Every Darae skiff was sculled with a single stern oar, and the sky's Oar was the sculler's clock. It stands steepest just after rising, then leans through the night the way an oar leans through a stroke; old boatmen could name the hour from its angle without turning their heads. The figure carried an etiquette, too: an oar is never lent blade-up, never stepped over, and never called broken while at sea — the sky holds one that never splinters, and it was considered enough for everyone.

## Named stars

- **Mulkkot** — *the water-flower*. The great star at the blade's tip. Its low, wet shimmer near the horizon was read for wind: a quiet Mulkkot promised a flat dawn crossing.

## Observing notes

The Oar slants across the northwest after the autumn full moons, its grip star clearing the ridge first. Mun's caption is a single line: *"One oar, and the whole sky to scull."*
