Ten stars build an ox mid-stride, horns lowered toward the west. The islanders held that the spring tides were not pulled by the moon at all, but followed this animal out of habit.
<!-- more -->

## The figure

The Ox is the folio's most complete figure: horns, poll, jaw, a boxy body, and legs planted wide. Its great star is **Pureun**, *the blue one*, set at the poll between the horns — Mun measured its colour against a bottle of harbour water and declared the star cleaner.

Darae's tide-lore was oxen-lore. The animal walks the full width of the sky in a night, and the islanders read the water by its gait: when the Ox rose horns-first, the tides of that fortnight would run hard and high; when it rose rump-first — as it does for half the year — the water would come in shallow and lazy. Clam beds were staked, weirs opened, and mooring lines lengthened on the Ox's schedule, not the almanac's. Mun checked the claim against his tide-pole for three seasons and wrote, with evident irritation, that the beast was *"more often right than my tables."*

## Named stars

- **Pureun** — *the blue one*. The poll star between the horns. Its noticeably cold colour made it the tradition's reference for judging the tint of every other star.

## Observing notes

The Ox crosses the meridian in the small hours of spring. The hind leg dips behind the ridge of Keunseom as the forelegs clear it — the animal is said to step over the island once each night, carefully.
