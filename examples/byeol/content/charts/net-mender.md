A seated figure bends over its work while a zigzag of stars spills from its hand, ending at the bright sinker-stone. Of all the plates, this is the one the islanders called simply "us."
<!-- more -->

## The figure

Head, shoulder, knee, and foot make the mender; from the working hand a line of five stars falls away in the loose zigzag of net gathered on a lap. It ends at **Chumdol**, *the sinker-stone*, the great star that weights the whole figure and keeps it, as one of Mun's informants put it, from blowing away.

Every household in the archipelago mended nets through the southern winter, and the figure returned to the evening sky exactly when that work began. It carried the year's most practical superstition: a tear mended while the Net-Mender was above the horizon would hold; one mended after it set would open again around the same fish it had lost. Menders raced the figure down the sky on winter evenings, and the last knot tied before Chumdol touched the sea was called the *luck knot* and left deliberately imperfect.

## Named stars

- **Chumdol** — *the sinker-stone*. The great star at the net's end. Its steadiness low on the horizon — where other stars flicker — made it the tradition's emblem of finished work.

## Observing notes

The figure sits above the eastern horizon on early summer evenings and works its way west through the season. Mun drew the netting line twice, erased once; his note admits the zigzag *"will not sit still between one island's telling and the next."*
