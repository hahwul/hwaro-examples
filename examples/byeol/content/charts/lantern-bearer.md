The tallest surviving plate: a figure strides down the sky holding a lantern at arm's length, and the lantern is Deungbul, the brightest star the Darae tradition names.
<!-- more -->

## The figure

Ten stars make a walker caught mid-step, one arm trailing, the other extended with a lamp hung from its hand. The lamp is **Deungbul**, the *keun-byeol* of the whole tradition — the one star in the folio that Mun pricked twice, "once for the star and once for what it means."

Darae's storm months close the strait to crossings, but not to emergencies, and night crossings in that season were made toward this star. The Lantern Bearer walks the western sky exactly along the bearing of the safe channel between the reefs of the inner strait; a helmsman who kept Deungbul over the bow, the islanders said, was *walking behind the lantern man*, and would be led the long way but the whole way. The figure is the tradition's only constellation with a job rather than a story — several informants refused to tell Mun any tale about it at all, saying that a lamp explained itself.

## Named stars

- **Deungbul** — *the lamp*. The brightest star of the Darae sky, low in the west during the storm months, reliably visible through thin cloud when every other mark has gone.

## Observing notes

The Bearer stands fully upright at dusk in the middle of the storm season and lies almost flat by dawn — the lamp is set down on the horizon last, as a lamp should be. This was the final surviving plate, and the folio's leaf is scorched along one edge; the lantern corner is untouched.
