**Byeol** — the word simply means *star* — is a digital restoration of the seven surviving plates of the Mun folio, an atlas of folk constellations recorded in the Darae Archipelago, a scattering of thirty-odd islands in the far southern ocean that appears on no modern chart.

## The folio

Mun Se-jin (1841–1909) trained as a calendrical clerk before leaving the mainland observatory service in 1872, reportedly after a dispute over the placement of an intercalary month. He spent the next three decades sailing between the islands of the Darae Strait, keeping accounts for fishing cooperatives in exchange for passage and stories. The stories, it turned out, were mostly about stars.

The islanders steered, fished, and planted by a sky of their own devising. None of the classical lodges appear in their tradition; in their place stand herons, oars, oxen, combs, and gates — figures assembled from the same southern stars but joined along entirely different lines. Mun recorded forty-one such figures in a bound folio of indigo-dyed mulberry paper, pricking each star with a brass point and joining the asterisms in gold ink so fine it disappears unless the page is tilted toward a lamp.

The folio was damaged in the harbour fire of 1911. Seven plates survive — from [the Ninth Gate](@/charts/ninth-gate.md), the first Mun drew, to [the Lantern Bearer](@/charts/lantern-bearer.md), whose leaf is scorched along one edge — held since 1934 in the archive of the Haeun Observatory, which has never once agreed on how to catalogue them.

## Conventions of the plates

Each plate in this restoration follows Mun's own drawing rules:

- **Graded points.** Star size follows the islanders' three spoken magnitudes: *keun-byeol* (great stars, drawn with a four-point prick), *jung-byeol* (middle stars), and *jan-byeol* (small stars).
- **Hairline joins.** Asterism strokes are drawn at the finest weight the ink allows. Tilt a plate toward your lamp — rest a pointer on it — and the strokes catch the light and turn gold, as the originals do.
- **Field stars.** Unjoined stars are kept on the plate wherever Mun kept them. He held that an asterism only makes sense against the sky it swims in.
- **Graticule arcs.** The faint dashed curves reproduce Mun's altitude arcs, sighted from the harbour wall of Keunseom at the winter solstice.

Dates given for each plate are the dates of this restoration's re-drawing, not of Mun's originals, which he stubbornly refused to date at all.

## A note on invention

Every constellation, star name, island, and person in this atlas is invented. The Darae Archipelago cannot be visited, which is precisely why its sky stays clear.
