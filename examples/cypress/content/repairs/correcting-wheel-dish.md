A wheel that rubs the brake pad at the same point every rotation, or pulls to one side under load, is out of true or off-centre — sometimes both. Neither takes more than a spoke wrench and patience to fix, but both reward small, even adjustments over big confident turns.
<!-- more -->
Work slowly. A quarter-turn on the wrong spoke can undo ten minutes of progress, and rim damage from over-tightened spokes doesn't true back out.

## Finding the problem

1. **Mount the wheel and set the gauge.** Put the wheel in a truing stand, or use the frame or fork itself with a zip tie as a reference pointer. Spin the wheel slowly and bring the pointer close enough to just brush the rim at its worst lateral high spot.
2. **Map the wobble.** Spin again and mark every point where the rim touches the pointer with a wax pencil or piece of tape. You're building a picture of which spokes need tightening and which need loosening.

## Truing lateral

3. **Tighten toward the low side.** At each marked high spot, identify the spokes pulling the rim away from true — usually the ones on the opposite side from where the pointer is touching — and tighten them in `¼ turn` increments, alternating sides of a widely wobbled section rather than cranking one spoke.
4. **Recheck and repeat.** Spin the wheel again, tighten the pointer in slightly, and remap. Most wheels need three to five passes before the lateral wobble is under `0.5 mm` at the tightest gauge setting.

## Dish and radial

5. **Check dish.** Flip the wheel in the stand (or hold the same frame reference from the opposite side) and confirm the rim sits centred over the hub within `±0.5 mm`. If it's off to one side, tighten all the spokes on the far side and loosen all the spokes on the near side by the same small amount, all the way around.
6. **True radial.** Check for high and low spots in the rim's roundness — a flat spot usually means the spokes there are all slightly loose. Tighten those spokes evenly rather than one at a time to avoid reintroducing lateral wobble.
7. **Stress relieve.** Grip pairs of spokes on each side and squeeze firmly by hand, working around the wheel. This seats the spoke elbows and nipple threads before they get the chance to do it themselves at speed, on the road. Recheck lateral and radial trueness once more after stressing.

Finish by checking spoke tension by feel or with a tensiometer if you have one — spokes should feel evenly firm, with no single spoke noticeably looser than its neighbours on the same side of the wheel. A wheel that won't hold true after a careful pass often has worn hub bearings underneath it — see [Overhauling Loose-Ball Hub Bearings](/repairs/overhauling-hub-bearings/).
