Disc brake pads are one of the cheapest, most consequence-heavy parts on the bike to ignore. Once the friction compound wears down to the backing plate, you're braking metal-on-metal against the rotor — expensive, and dangerous at the worst possible moment. A five-minute inspection catches this long before it becomes a problem.
<!-- more -->
Check pad thickness through the caliper window whenever you clean the bike. If less than a millimetre of compound remains on either pad, replace the pair — never just the more-worn one.

## Removing the old pads

1. **Remove the wheel.** Pull the wheel to get clear access to the caliper. Give the rotor a visual check for scoring or a blued, overheated look while it's out — either is worth investigating before you reinstall fresh pads against it.
2. **Pull the retention pin or clip.** Most calipers hold the pads in with a small horizontal pin, sometimes secured by a spring clip or a tiny cotter pin. Use needle-nose pliers to draw it out, keeping it somewhere it won't roll away.
3. **Slide out the old pads and spring.** Lift the worn pads and their spring straight out of the caliper, noting which way the spring is oriented — it usually only fits back one way.

## Prepping and fitting new pads

4. **Push the pistons back.** With the old pads removed, use a plastic tire lever or dedicated piston press tool to push both pistons back flush into the caliper bore, giving room for the new, thicker pads.
5. **Clean the caliper bore.** Wipe the exposed piston faces with a rag and a small amount of isopropyl alcohol — never a petroleum-based cleaner, which can swell the piston seals.
6. **Insert the new pads and spring.** Slide the new pad set and spring back into the caliper in the same orientation as the originals, then reinsert the retention pin and clip.
7. **Reinstall the wheel.** Refit the wheel and confirm the caliper bolts are still torqued to `6–8 N·m`. If the rotor was removed for any reason, torque its bolts to `2–4 N·m` in a star pattern before refitting the wheel.

## Bedding in

8. **Bed the new pads.** Ride at a moderate speed and brake firmly to just short of lockup, ten to fifteen times, letting the brake cool between each stop. This transfers an even layer of pad material onto the rotor and is what gives new pads their full stopping power — skipping it leaves you with a soft, inconsistent bite point for weeks.

{{ alert(type="caution", body="Never squeeze a lever with the pads removed and the wheel out. Hydraulic pistons can push out too far to reseat by hand, turning a five-minute pad swap into a full bleed.") }}

If the bite point still feels soft or spongy after bedding in, air may be in the line rather than the pads — see [Bleeding Hydraulic Disc Brakes](/repairs/bleeding-disc-brakes/).
