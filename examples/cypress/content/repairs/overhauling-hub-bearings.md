Loose-ball hubs — the kind with cones, locknuts, and loose bearing balls rather than a sealed cartridge — reward a proper overhaul more than almost any other part of the bike. Done right, the wheel spins with a light, even resistance and holds that feel for a season or more of riding.
<!-- more -->
Work over a tray. Loose balls roll, and a dropped ball is the single most common reason this job takes longer than planned.

## Disassembly

1. **Remove the wheel and skewer.** Pull the wheel from the bike and remove the quick-release or thru-axle skewer so the axle is clear to work on from both ends.
2. **Loosen the locknut against the cone.** Hold the cone still with a cone wrench on the flats closest to the hub shell, and loosen the locknut against it with a standard wrench. The two tools should push against each other, not turn together.
3. **Unthread the cone and catch the balls.** Once the locknut is off, unthread the cone the rest of the way by hand and lift the axle out slowly — the bearing balls on that side are now loose in the race and will want to roll away. Count them as you remove them.
4. **Repeat on the far side.** Remove the opposite cone and locknut the same way, catching that side's balls as well. Set the axle assembly aside, keeping the two sides' hardware separated.

## Cleaning and repacking

5. **Degrease everything.** Wipe old grease from the races, the axle, the cones, and each ball with degreaser and a clean rag until the races show bare metal with no grit visible.
6. **Inspect for pitting.** Run a fingertip around each race, feeling for pits or a gritty texture rather than a smooth curve. Pitted races or discoloured, flat-spotted balls should be replaced rather than repacked — they'll never spin smooth again.
7. **Pack fresh grease and seat the balls.** Lay a thin, even bed of grease in each race, then press the same count of balls back into the grease so they hold in place on their own. Slide the axle back through, threading the first cone on until it just touches the balls.

## Adjustment

8. **Set preload and lock it down.** Thread the cone to hand-tight against the balls, then back it off `⅛ turn` — enough to remove drag without leaving play. Hold the cone in that position with the cone wrench while you thread and snug the locknut against it.
9. **Check for play and drag.** Grip the axle ends and check for any side-to-side play, then spin the wheel and feel for smooth, even resistance with no notchiness. If there's play, tighten the cone a hair; if there's drag, back it off the same small amount and recheck the locknut.

{{ alert(type="note", body="A properly adjusted loose-ball hub has zero perceptible play and spins freely with only light bearing drag — not glassy-smooth freewheel spin. Chasing a completely frictionless feel usually means the preload is too loose.") }}

With fresh bearings installed, it's worth checking the rim while the wheel is out — see [Correcting Wheel Dish and Lateral Trueness](/repairs/correcting-wheel-dish/) if it hasn't been trued recently.
