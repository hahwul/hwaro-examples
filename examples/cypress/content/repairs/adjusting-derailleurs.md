A derailleur that skips under load or hesitates between gears is almost never a broken part — it's a cable and a pair of limit screws that have drifted out of adjustment. This procedure covers both derailleurs in one pass, starting with the rear because it does most of the shifting work.
<!-- more -->
Work with the bike in a stand and the rear wheel able to spin freely. Shift through the full range at least once before you touch anything, just to hear where the noise actually starts.

## Rear derailleur

1. **Set the barrel adjusters to baseline.** Thread the barrel adjuster at the shifter and the one on the derailleur most of the way in, then back each out exactly one full turn. This gives you room to fine-tune in both directions later.
2. **Shift to the smallest cog and check the high limit.** With the chain on the smallest rear cog, look down the chain line from behind. Turn the `H` limit screw until the upper derailleur pulley sits directly under the smallest cog — no farther out, or the chain can shift into the spokes.
3. **Shift to the largest cog and check the low limit.** Shift to the biggest cog and repeat with the `L` screw, stopping the pulley directly under it. Too far in and you'll never reach the easiest gear; too far out and the chain can jump past the cassette into the spokes.
4. **Tension the cable.** Shift back to the smallest cog, loosen the cable pinch bolt, pull the cable taut by hand, and retighten. Torque the pinch bolt to `5–6 N·m` — enough to hold under load without crushing the strands.
5. **Index through the block.** Shift one click at a time through every gear. Hesitation shifting up usually means more cable tension (turn the barrel adjuster counter-clockwise a quarter turn at a time); overshoot or noise in the middle of the block means less.
6. **Set the B-tension screw.** With the chain on the largest cog and smallest chainring, adjust the B-tension screw until the upper pulley sits about 5 mm from the cog — close enough for quick shifts, far enough to avoid chain-to-cog contact.

## Front derailleur

7. **Check cage height and rotation.** The outer cage plate should clear the largest chainring by 1–2 mm at its closest point, and the cage should run parallel to the chainrings, not angled inward or outward. Loosen the clamp bolt to adjust, then retorque to `4–5 N·m`.
8. **Set limits and trim.** Shift to the small ring and smallest cog, set the low limit so the chain doesn't rub the inner cage; shift to the big ring and largest cog, set the high limit the same way. Finish by shifting through the full range and trimming the shifter's micro-adjust if the chain rubs in the extreme gear combinations.

{{ alert(type="note", body="Cross-chaining (small ring with smallest cogs, or big ring with largest cogs) will rub even on a perfectly adjusted drivetrain. That's the drivetrain telling you to shift, not a sign something's wrong.") }}

If shifting still hesitates after a careful adjustment, check chain wear before you chase the cable further — see [Replacing Chain and Cassette Wear](/repairs/replacing-chain-cassette/).
