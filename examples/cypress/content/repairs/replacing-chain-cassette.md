A chain that has stretched past its wear limit accelerates cassette and chainring wear fast — riding on a worn chain for even a few hundred kilometres can turn a fifteen-dollar chain replacement into a full drivetrain replacement. Catching it early with a wear indicator is the cheapest maintenance decision on the bike.
<!-- more -->
Check chain wear every few weeks with a wear indicator tool. Most read in percentage of stretch; replace the chain at 0.5% on narrow 11/12-speed chains, or 0.75% on wider 8/9/10-speed chains, before it starts taking the cassette down with it.

## Measuring and removing

1. **Check wear before you buy parts.** Hook the wear indicator onto the chain per its instructions. If it reads past the replacement threshold for your chain's speed count, plan to replace both chain and cassette — a stretched chain has already reshaped the cassette's teeth to match it.
2. **Remove the wheel and old cassette.** Pull the rear wheel, insert the lockring tool, and hold the cassette body still with the chain whip wrapped around one of the larger cogs while you loosen the lockring counter-clockwise.
3. **Slide the cassette off.** Lift the cogs and any loose spacers off the freehub body, keeping their order — most cassettes have a keyed spline so cogs only go on one way, but spacers between cog groups are easy to lose track of.

## Installing new parts

4. **Clean the freehub body.** Wipe down the freehub splines before installing the new cassette; grit here causes the cassette to seat unevenly and can chew the splines over time.
5. **Install the new cassette.** Slide the cogs and spacers on in their original order, following the keyed spline, then thread the lockring by hand before torquing it to `40–50 N·m` — this is one of the few fasteners on the bike that needs real force to seat correctly.
6. **Size the new chain.** Run the new chain around the largest chainring and largest cassette cog without going through the derailleur, then add two links for a standard rear derailleur cage. Break the chain at that length with the chain tool.
7. **Route and join the chain.** Thread the chain through the derailleur cage and around the smallest chainring, then join it with a quick link or by pressing the supplied pin until it sits flush with no gap on either side of the plate.
8. **Seat the master link.** If your chain uses a reusable master link, seat it fully by backpedalling firmly or squeezing the rear brake and pushing the crank forward hard — you should feel and hear a small click as the link locks into its groove.

Shift through the full range once installed; a new chain and cassette occasionally need one small barrel-adjuster tweak even if the old drivetrain was indexed perfectly before the swap.
