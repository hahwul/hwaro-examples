Marginalia is an ongoing project in which marginal annotations -- the kind a careful reader leaves in the edges of a book -- are extracted from their host pages and presented as standalone artifacts. The work began with a question about authorship: who is the author of a book that has been read closely, the writer or the reader who has filled the margins with response?

Each piece in the series is constructed in three layers. The first is a faithful reproduction of the marginal note, rendered in graphite on archival paper at the original scale. The second is a typeset reconstruction of the surrounding passage, set in the same type as the original book where possible, in a neutral substitute where not. The third is a brief commentary, set apart from the other two layers, locating the annotation in the reader's broader engagement with the text.

The source material has so far come from secondhand books acquired through a network of regional booksellers, with permission obtained from previous owners where they could be traced. Where they could not, the annotations have been included only when their author would be impossible to identify from the content of the note itself.

The project resists the impulse to romanticize annotation. Most marginal notes are not insightful. They are reminders, objections, transcription errors, and arrows. The work is not about elevating these gestures but about treating them as evidence, as something that happened, with the same weight a curator would give to any other artifact left behind by a reader who took their reading seriously.

Twelve pieces are currently complete. The project is expected to run to at least thirty.
