Silent Architecture is a folio of nine ink-and-wash interiors, each rendered without figures, signage, or any other indication of human presence. The omission is deliberate. The work is not about the people who would use these rooms; it is about the rooms themselves, and what they say when there is no one in them to interrupt.

Each interior was drawn from a real reference, then progressively stripped of contextual detail. A library lost its books. A waiting room lost its chairs. A stairwell lost its handrail. What remained, in each case, was the volume itself -- the proportions, the light, the relationship between the floor and the ceiling and the wall that closed the space. These three surfaces, when drawn carefully, will tell the viewer almost everything they need to know about a room.

The ink work uses a single nib width throughout the folio, with tonal variation produced entirely through line spacing and crosshatch density. The discipline is partly aesthetic and partly economic. A consistent line forces the eye to read structure rather than texture, which is the point of the project.

The wash layer was applied sparingly, mostly to indicate the direction of the primary light source. Three of the nine pieces have no wash at all. They are drawings of rooms in which the light is so direct that any modeling would have softened it.

The folio was bound by hand in a single edition of twenty-five copies, each signed and numbered, with an accompanying colophon describing the reference for each interior.
