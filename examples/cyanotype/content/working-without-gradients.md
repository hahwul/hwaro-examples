Designing without gradients is a discipline before it is a style. The temptation, when a layout feels flat, is to reach for a soft transition between two tones and call the surface alive. The Cyanotype approach refuses that move and asks the question one step earlier: what is the surface trying to do?

When the answer is contained — separating a sidebar from a main column, marking a footer, framing a callout — a flat field of color does the work better than any gradient could. The edge becomes part of the design, and edges are where the eye looks first.

When the answer is expressive, the move is usually toward typography rather than color. A heavier weight, a longer line, a wider tracking value at display sizes; each of these can carry the energy a gradient would otherwise smuggle in.

The reward is a site that ages well. Flat fields and solid type do not date the way trending color treatments do, and the resulting pages stay readable across years of devices.
