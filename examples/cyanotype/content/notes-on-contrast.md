title = "Notes on Contrast"

Contrast is the single most reliable tool a typographic design has. In a Cyanotype palette, where the working colors are reduced to a deep indigo and a near-white paper tone, every other decision is shaped by how those two values meet on the page.

The first rule is to treat contrast as structural rather than decorative. Headings should not look louder than body copy because they are bigger; they should look louder because the page reserves space for them. Generous leading above a heading does as much work as the type size itself.

The second rule is to leave the brightest tones for the rarest moments. A single accent rule across an article carries more weight than a dozen colored callouts. Restraint, in this aesthetic, is what gives contrast its meaning.

The result is a page that reads quietly but holds its hierarchy at any zoom level, the way a well-pulled cyanotype print holds its detail through every reproduction.
