A blueprint grid is not a constraint imposed on the page. It is the page reading itself out loud — the structure announcing where the eye is allowed to land. In the Cyanotype tradition, that grid leans toward the strict end: narrow columns, generous gutters, an unbroken baseline that runs from masthead to footer.

Working inside such a grid is faster than it appears. Every block has a known width, every margin has a known multiple, and decisions about composition compress to a small set of yes-or-no questions. Should this image span one column or three? Should this caption sit flush left or centered against the rule above it?

Because the grid is visible — through ruled lines and consistent margins rather than through any ornamental treatment — readers learn it within the first few sections of a long page. After that, every variation is read as deliberate.
