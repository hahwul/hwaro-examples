A book printed on acid-free paper can survive for centuries with minimal intervention. A digital file, by contrast, depends on a chain of functioning hardware, compatible software, and maintained storage infrastructure. Break any link in that chain, and the document becomes inaccessible -- not destroyed, but effectively lost.

This paradox defines the central challenge of digital preservation. We can copy a file a million times without degradation, yet the practical lifespan of any given digital format is measured in decades, not centuries.

## Format Migration

The most widely adopted strategy is format migration: periodically converting files from obsolete formats to current ones. A document created in WordPerfect 5.1 might be migrated to PDF/A, which in turn might someday be migrated to whatever standard succeeds it. Each migration preserves content but risks losing subtle formatting, metadata, or embedded behaviors.

## Emulation

An alternative approach preserves not just the document but the entire computing environment required to render it. Emulation software recreates old hardware and operating systems on modern machines, allowing a researcher to interact with a 1990s HyperCard stack or a 2005 Flash animation exactly as its creator intended.

## Institutional Commitment

Neither strategy works without sustained institutional commitment. Digital preservation is not a problem that can be solved once; it requires ongoing attention, funding, and expertise. The organizations best positioned to meet this challenge are the same ones that have always served as custodians of the written record: libraries, archives, and museums.

The tools have changed, but the mission has not. To preserve is to declare that something matters beyond the moment of its creation -- that future readers, researchers, and citizens will need access to the evidence of the past.
