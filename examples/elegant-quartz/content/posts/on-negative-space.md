title = "On Negative Space"
description = "What the empty parts of the page are doing."
date = "2024-07-22"
+++

Negative space is not absence. It is a working part of the composition, with the same weight and authority as any element rendered in ink. A page that treats the empty regions as filler will feel cramped no matter how spare its content. A page that treats them as material will feel composed even when crowded.

The first move in editing a layout is to identify which spaces are doing work and which are merely leftover. A margin that frames the text block is doing work. A gap between two unrelated panels is doing work. A region that exists because the designer ran out of things to put there is leftover, and it will read as such.

There is a useful rule for thinking about this: every empty region should have a relationship with at least one element on the page. The relationship may be alignment, proximity, or contrast, but it has to exist. When a region has no such relationship, it produces visual noise of a particular kind -- a sense that the page is unfinished, even when nothing is technically wrong with it.

Negative space scales. A single character of letter-spacing in a heading is a small decision; a half-inch of margin around a column is a larger one; the choice to leave a quarter of a page blank is the largest. The principles are the same at every scale. The space is not waiting to be filled. It is already doing its job.

A page that uses negative space well does not feel empty. It feels exact.
