# Cutting the Leaf

<p class="lede">Before a replacement can be tipped in, the offending leaf has to be removed. The cut has to be precise enough to leave a usable stub yet shallow enough to spare the conjugate leaf on the other side of the gathering.</p>

## Where the cut falls

<p>The binder works near the inner margin, sliding a thin blade between the leaf and its neighbor. The blade follows the gutter rather than the printed text block, so a narrow strip of the original paper is left behind. That strip becomes the anchor for the new page.</p>

## What survives

<p>Surviving stubs are useful evidence. They preserve the watermark, the chain lines, and sometimes a sliver of the cancelled text. Bibliographers read these remnants the way an archaeologist reads a foundation: a small trace, but enough to reconstruct what the leaf once carried.</p>
