I did not buy the Aldine No. 4 to be precious about it. A friend's father restored typewriters as a retirement hobby, and this one — a 1961 portable, dove-gray body, seized only in the ribbon-advance mechanism when he found it — came back to life for sixty dollars and a Saturday afternoon of his time. I used it for the zero draft of a novella, forty-one pages, over three weeks. This is what a typewriter is actually like to draft on in 2025, not what it is like to own.

## The specs, for what they are worth

| Spec | Aldine No. 4 |
|---|---|
| Weight | 4.1 kg with case |
| Carriage width | 25.4 cm (fits standard letter) |
| Typeface | Pica, 10 characters per inch |
| Ribbon | Universal spool, two-color |
| Correction | None. Xs and strikeovers only |
| Backspace / delete | Backspace exists. Delete does not |

None of that tells you what it is like to write on. Here is what does: there is no delete key, which sounds like a limitation and functions like a discipline. You cannot go back and smooth a sentence mid-thought, so you finish the thought, on the page, in the only order it is willing to arrive. My zero drafts are usually a mess of half-finished sentences abandoned for better ones two words in. On the Aldine, every sentence gets finished because finishing is cheaper than starting over by hand with correction fluid, which I did not buy and would not recommend.

## Where it actually helps

The percussive rhythm — the physical effort of striking each key, the audible clack, the manual carriage return at the end of every line — turns out to be a genuine pacing mechanism. A laptop lets you write in bursts and pauses that don't feel different from each other; the typewriter's mechanical cadence is roughly constant, which means thirty minutes at the machine produces a more even, more finished-feeling first pass than thirty minutes of stop-start typing on a keyboard where backspace is one careless keystroke away.

It is also, not incidentally, the only writing tool I have used in the last five years that made revising by hand feel natural again. Retyping the forty-one pages into a document, correcting as I went, functioned as an unplanned first revision pass — the kind you get from reading a draft aloud, except slower and more thorough, because your hands are doing the work your eyes usually skim past.

## Where it does not

Anything past a zero draft. Revision on paper with no search function, no way to move a scene without literally cutting and retaping it, becomes punishing past about fifteen thousand words. I retyped the novella into a word processor at forty-one pages and would not attempt a full novel manuscript, at ninety thousand words, by the same method — the retyping alone would cost most of a month that could go toward an actual second draft.

Ribbons are also a genuine supply problem in 2025. Universal spools are findable but not cheap, and a dried-out ribbon mid-chapter is a real interruption in a way "the app crashed" rarely is anymore, because apps autosave and ribbons do not warn you.

## Verdict

A real tool, not a nostalgia object, but a narrow one: excellent for zero drafts under fifty thousand words, unusable for anything requiring structural revision. Buy it for the discipline of finished sentences, not for the whole book.
