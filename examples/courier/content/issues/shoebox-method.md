The shoebox method is not mine. An editor gave it to me after my second manuscript came back from her with "I cannot see the shape of this from inside it" written on the cover page, which is the kind of note that is both devastating and completely correct. It has been my standard revision workflow for four books since, refined across seven full passes of the manuscript I am finishing now.

<!-- more -->

## The setup

One shoebox — mine is an old boot box, sturdier than it needs to be — and a stack of four-by-six index cards in four colors, one per point-of-view character. For every scene in the manuscript, write on one card: the scene's function in one line, the point-of-view character, and the last line of dialogue or action, nothing more. File the cards in manuscript order in the box, colour visible along the top edge like a filing tab.

That is the entire setup. The method is in what you do next.

## The revision pass

1. Pull every card and lay them on a table in manuscript order, colours showing.
2. Read only the colour bands, not the manuscript. A single point-of-view character's cards, laid end to end, should read as a complete arc on its own. If a colour band goes quiet for forty cards and then reappears, that character has disappeared from the book for a stretch nobody planned.
3. Physically move cards to test reordering before touching the manuscript. If moving scene nineteen to before scene twelve makes the colour bands read better, the manuscript probably needs that change too — and you have tested it without opening a document.
4. Pull any card whose one-line function duplicates another card's function. Two cards that both say "reveal secret" are a sign one of them is dead weight.
5. Return the cards to the box in final order and revise the manuscript to match, chapter by chapter.

## Why physical cards over corkboard software

I have used corkboard-style software — the drag-and-reorder scene grid that most drafting apps now include — and it does the same reordering job faster. What it does not do is force you to look at the whole manuscript at once, at a size your eyes can actually take in. A laptop screen shows twelve to twenty scene cards before you have to scroll; my dining table shows all ninety at once, colour bands and all, in a single glance. Software card grids are better for editing a single scene's metadata. Table-sized paper is better for seeing structure, and structure is the actual point of a revision pass — the sentence-level problems get caught in later passes regardless of what tool you used to catch the structural ones.

The colour coding matters more than it should. A four-colour system communicates point-of-view balance faster than any chart I have tried, including Plotmill's tension graph, reviewed two issues ago, which claims to do a version of this and mostly does not — a line graph flattens four characters into one axis; four colours laid end to end on a table keep them legible as four separate threads.

## The real cost

An afternoon per pass, plus the discipline of writing honest one-line summaries — a card that says "important scene" instead of naming the actual function is a card that will not help you later. The boxes themselves also accumulate; I have kept the card set from every manuscript, four boxes now, which is either a research archive or a hoarding problem depending on the week.

## Verdict

Free, low-tech, and the only structural-revision method I have never abandoned partway through a book. Pair it with whatever drafting software you like — the cards are for seeing the shape, not for writing the sentences.
