What separates science from non-science? This is one of the central questions in the philosophy of science, and Karl Popper offered one of the most influential answers.

## Popper's Falsifiability

A theory is scientific if and only if it is **falsifiable** -- that is, it makes predictions that can, in principle, be shown to be false by observation or experiment.

### What Makes a Good Scientific Theory

- It makes **specific, testable predictions**
- It takes **risks** -- it rules things out
- It can be **refuted** by evidence
- It is **precise** enough to be wrong

### Examples

**Falsifiable**: "All swans are white." This can be disproven by finding a single black swan.

**Not falsifiable**: "Everything happens for a reason." No observation could ever contradict this claim.

## The Problem of Induction

David Hume argued that we can never justify inductive reasoning -- just because the sun has risen every morning does not logically guarantee it will rise tomorrow.

Popper's solution was to abandon induction altogether. Science does not prove theories true; it **eliminates false ones**. What remains standing after rigorous testing is our best current knowledge.

## Implications for Practice

1. Design experiments that can **fail**
2. Seek to **disprove** your own hypotheses
3. Treat surviving theories with respect, not certainty
4. Be suspicious of claims that explain everything

*The strength of a theory lies not in what it confirms, but in what it dares to forbid.*
