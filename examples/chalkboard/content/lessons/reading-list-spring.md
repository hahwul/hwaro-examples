A curated list of books and papers that have shaped my thinking this semester. Each entry includes a brief note on why it matters.

## Mathematics

**"Proofs from THE BOOK" by Aigner and Ziegler**
A collection of elegant mathematical proofs, inspired by Erdos's idea that God keeps a book of the most beautiful proofs. Accessible and inspiring.

**"How to Solve It" by George Polya**
The classic guide to mathematical problem-solving. Polya's heuristics -- understand the problem, devise a plan, carry it out, look back -- apply far beyond mathematics.

## Computer Science

**"Introduction to Algorithms" by Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, and Stein**
The standard reference. Dense but comprehensive. Best used as a companion to lecture notes rather than as a first introduction.

**"The Art of Computer Programming" by Donald Knuth**
A monument of technical writing. Read selectively. Volume 1 on fundamental algorithms is worth the investment.

## Philosophy

**"The Logic of Scientific Discovery" by Karl Popper**
Popper's foundational work on falsifiability and the demarcation problem. Demanding but essential for anyone interested in the nature of knowledge.

**"Thinking, Fast and Slow" by Daniel Kahneman**
Not philosophy in the traditional sense, but deeply philosophical in its implications. Understanding cognitive biases changes how you evaluate your own reasoning.

## How to Use This List

Pick one book that challenges you and one that feels comfortable. Alternate between them. Take notes. Argue with the author in the margins. A reading list is not a checklist -- it is a starting point for conversation.
