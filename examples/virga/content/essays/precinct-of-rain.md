*Precinct of Rain* is a beautiful film that does not know what it is sad about,
and after two viewings I am confident that is a fatal condition rather than a
mysterious one. Vaugine has clearly studied the canon. Every surface is correct:
the venetian-blind shadows, the trench coats gone dark at the shoulders, the
rain-glazed precinct windows behind every interrogation. If you paused it at
random you would swear you were watching a classic. It is only in motion that the
hollowness shows.

<!-- more -->

## The surfaces are immaculate

Credit where due. This is one of the best-*looking* noirs in years. The
production design is meticulous and the rain, again always the rain, is handsomely
built &mdash; Vaugine and her team clearly love the genre's weather and lavish
attention on it. There is a rooftop chase in the third act, shot almost entirely
in reflection off standing water, that is as pure a piece of noir craft as
anything in this paper's recent memory.

## The reason is missing

But noir was never about the coats. Underneath the style, the great examples run
on a specific ache: the sense that the system is rotten, that the detective is
compromised, that solving the case will fix nothing. *Precinct of Rain* has all
the iconography of that despair and none of its content. Its cops are corrupt in
the way a mood board is corrupt &mdash; decoratively, without cost. The case
resolves cleanly. The compromised hero is compromised only until the plot needs
him not to be. The film mistakes the *symptoms* of noir for its disease.

Three marks, and they are honest ones. What is on screen is genuinely
accomplished, and a lesser critic could ride the surfaces to a rave. But a
picture this fluent in the language should have something to say in it. Vaugine
can plainly make a noir. Next time I hope she makes one that hurts.
