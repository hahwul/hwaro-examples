Two hundred and eleven minutes. I write the number first because it is the first
thing anyone will ask about, and the last thing that matters. *The Long Wet
Season* runs three and a half hours, most of it spent watching a monsoon
dismantle a farm in a valley Halász never names, and I would not cut a frame of
it. What I would do is prepare you, because the film will not prepare you itself.

The premise, such as it is: a family waits out a flood that is slowly making
their land unfarmable, and does not leave. That is the whole plot. There is a
son who wants to go and a father who will not, and their argument recurs perhaps
four times across the runtime, each time shorter, each time more defeated. Around
this Halász arranges a cinema of pure duration &mdash; a fixed camera, ambient
sound, shots that begin before anything happens and end well after it has
stopped.

## What duration is for

The obvious question is why. Why make an audience sit in real time while a woman
bails water from a doorway for six unbroken minutes? Halász's answer, I think, is
that the waiting *is* the subject. You cannot dramatize patience; you can only
inflict it. By the time the flood crests, you have spent so long inside the
family's stalled hours that the smallest event &mdash; a truck that will not
start, a chicken carried to higher ground &mdash; carries the weight a gunfight
carries in another kind of film.

The rain here is the opposite of Kessler's neon downpour. It is agricultural,
ruinous, entirely without glamour. Halász never once lets it look beautiful, and
that refusal is a kind of respect. This is rain as economics, rain that decides
whether people eat.

## The missing mark

I withhold the fifth ornament for one reason, and it is a real one. The final
half hour reaches for a mythic register &mdash; a dream sequence, a swelling
score where there had been only ambient hiss &mdash; that the preceding three
hours had earned the right to refuse. Halász built a film out of not telling you
how to feel, and then, at the very end, told me. It is a small betrayal of an
enormous achievement. Go anyway. Go rested, go fed, and go without your phone.
Few films this patient are also this alive.
