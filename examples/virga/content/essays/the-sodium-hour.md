There is a color that noir was built on and it is disappearing from the world.
It is the specific amber-orange of low-pressure sodium streetlight &mdash; the
glow that turned every wet road into a river of copper and drained every face to
a mask. For sixty years it was simply what a city looked like after dark. Now it
is being replaced, lamp by lamp, with the flat clinical white of LED, and the
films that come after will have to invent a night that the films before could
just walk outside and find.

## Why orange was the right lie

Sodium light is almost monochromatic. It emits in a narrow band, which is why
under it everything reads as a smear of orange and grey and nothing looks like
its daytime self. For a genre about people who are not what they seem, this was a
gift no production designer could have improved on. The light itself lied. A red
coat went brown; a blue neon sign went muddy gold; skin lost its blood. Noir did
not have to build its moral ambiguity into the frame. The municipal grid did it
for free, every night, on every street.

Slow cinema learned the same trick more quietly. Watch how many contemplative
night films park their camera under a single sodium lamp and simply wait: the
light does the composing, pooling in the center, falling off to nothing at the
edges, so that a static shot acquires a vignette no lens could fake as well.

## What white light takes away

LED night is *legible*. It renders color faithfully, which is exactly the
problem. Under it the red coat is red again, the sign is blue, the face has
blood in it. The city stops lying, and a genre that ran on the city's lie has to
find another engine. Some directors are already color-grading the sodium back in
&mdash; painting the loss in post &mdash; but a recreated glow behaves like a
recreation. It sits on the image instead of coming from inside it.

I do not want to romanticize a technology that was chosen, in the end, for how
cheaply it lit a road. But we should at least mark what is going with it. The
sodium hour was the one time of day when every city agreed to look like a film.
We are turning the lamps to a whiter setting, and the nights are getting easier
to see and harder to feel.
