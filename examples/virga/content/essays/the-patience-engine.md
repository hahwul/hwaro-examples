"Slow cinema" is a bad name for a real thing. It sounds like an accusation, or an
apology, and it flattens a wildly varied body of work into a single complaint
about pacing. But the films it points at do share something, and it is worth
naming precisely: they treat **duration as content**. Time on screen is not a
delivery mechanism for events. It is the event.

<!-- more -->

## What the mode is not

Slow is not the same as boring, and it is not the same as long. Plenty of
three-hour blockbusters are frantic; plenty of slow films are ninety minutes. The
mode is defined not by runtime but by what fills it &mdash; the long take, the
static frame, the refusal to cut on action, the willingness to let a shot outlast
its ostensible purpose. A door closes. The film stays on the door.

## A starter shelf

For readers who want to test their tolerance, here is where this desk usually
points people first &mdash; all invented, all instructive, arranged from gentle
to punishing:

| Film | Director | Runtime | Start here if |
|------|----------|--------:|---------------|
| The Blue Hour Runs Long | Ines Marchetti | 104 min | you like your slow with a plot |
| Sodium | Rennie Vaugine | 141 min | you came from noir |
| The Long Wet Season | Halász Márton | 211 min | you have a free evening and nerve |
| Census of Fog | Dagny Oorloff | 268 min | you have made peace with all of this |

Begin at the top. Nobody's first slow film should be four and a half hours of
fog, however much this writer loves it.

## How to watch it

The single most useful piece of advice is the least cinematic: watch it awake and
watch it big. Slow cinema punishes the second screen more than any other mode
because it has no redundancy &mdash; miss the shift in the light and you have
missed the scene, since the shift in the light *was* the scene. Turn the phone
off. Sit closer than feels comfortable. And when the shot goes long and your
attention starts to itch, resist the reflex to check out. The itch is the film
working. The reward is on the far side of it, and it is real: a quality of
attention you cannot get any other way, and cannot easily give back once the
lights come up.
