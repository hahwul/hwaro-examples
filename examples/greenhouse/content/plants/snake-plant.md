**Dracaena trifasciata**, formerly classified as *Sansevieria trifasciata*, is native to the rocky, dry regions of West Africa from Nigeria east to the Congo. The species produces upright, sword-shaped leaves with a banded pattern that reaches 60 to 90 centimeters in mature specimens. Indoors it tolerates a wide range of conditions and is among the slowest plants to show stress from neglect.

### Light

Snake plants accept everything from a north-facing window to several hours of indirect sun. Direct afternoon light through unfiltered glass can bleach the foliage, so a sheer curtain helps in summer. Growth speeds up noticeably under bright conditions and slows to a stop in deep shade.

### Water

The most common cause of decline is overwatering. The leaves store moisture, so soil should dry completely between waterings. A monthly schedule works in average household humidity; reduce frequency to roughly every six weeks during winter dormancy. Always pour water at the soil line rather than into the central rosette, which can rot if water collects there.

### Soil and Potting

Use a fast-draining cactus or succulent mix amended with extra perlite or pumice. The plant prefers a slightly cramped pot and rarely needs repotting more than every three to four years. Rhizomes will eventually crack thin plastic pots, so terracotta is a sturdier long-term choice.

### Propagation

Division at repotting is the most reliable method. Cut the rhizome between two clusters of leaves with a clean knife and pot each section separately. Leaf cuttings root in water but lose any variegation in the new growth.

### Pests

Mealybugs and spider mites occasionally appear on stressed plants. Wipe the leaves with a damp cloth every few weeks to remove dust and discourage infestations.
