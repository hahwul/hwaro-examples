A raked sand field needs interruption. Without a stone or a moss island, the eye slides across the surface without finding rest. Page layouts behave the same way. A wall of body text without a card, a pull quote, or a divider drifts past the reader and leaves no record.

In Ryoan-ji, fifteen stones are arranged so that no single viewing angle reveals all of them at once. The composition forces the visitor to walk the veranda and rebuild the scene from memory. The rake lines flow around each rock in concentric arcs, marking where the gardener pivoted. The arcs become the visual equivalent of a topographic depression contour, hatched on the inside to denote a basin.

This template applies the same rule. The dark stone components are placed asymmetrically against the contour background. They mark where the reader should stop and absorb a single idea before continuing. Headings, quotes, and code blocks all use the stone treatment, so structural hierarchy emerges from contrast rather than scale alone.

Three guidelines govern the placement.

First, never line stones along a single horizontal axis. The page becomes a railway timetable.

Second, vary the size between adjacent stones. Equal weights cancel each other out and the rhythm collapses.

Third, leave generous sand between stones. The empty space carries as much meaning as the rocks themselves. A reader who finishes a paragraph deserves a moment of unrendered surface before the next anchor appears.

Together, the contour field and the stone marks build a layout that holds attention without demanding it.
