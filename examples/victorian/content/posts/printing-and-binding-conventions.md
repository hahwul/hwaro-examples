Commercial publishing in the 1880s relied on cloth-cased binding produced at scale by steam-driven casing-in machines. The substitution of cloth for full leather, introduced earlier in the century by Pickering and Bohn, had become standard practice across London and Edinburgh imprints by mid-decade.

### Cover Construction

A typical trade volume consisted of three boards cut from rope-fibre millboard, joined by a hollow back and covered in book-cloth dyed with aniline pigments. The cloth was first impregnated with starch sizing to receive die-stamped ornament. Brass dies, cut by pantograph from artists' originals, were heated to roughly two hundred degrees Fahrenheit before being struck into the case under hydraulic pressure. Gold leaf and coloured foils were applied through a glaire of egg albumen.

### Paper Stocks

Book papers of the period drew increasingly on chemical wood pulp, displacing the rag furnishes of earlier decades. Esparto grass, imported from Spain and North Africa, supplied a smooth, opaque sheet favoured for novel printing. Acidic alum-rosin sizing entered widespread use; its long-term consequences for paper stability would not be understood for another half-century.

### Type and Composition

Old Style faces cut by Miller and Richard, and the Caslon revivals issued by Stephenson Blake, dominated literary work. Composition remained largely manual until the introduction of the Linotype machine in 1886. Display matter on title pages was set in Egyptian, Tuscan, or Latin Antique faces, frequently combined within a single panel without regard for what later editors would call typographic restraint.

### Edges and Endpapers

Yellow-edged stock, plain-edged trade volumes, and three-edge gilt presentation copies coexisted within a single print run. Endpapers were commonly marbled or printed in a single ornamental colour drawn from the cover scheme.
