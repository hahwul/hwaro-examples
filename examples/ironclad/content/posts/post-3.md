The first ironclads carried solid wrought-iron plates because rolling mills of the 1860s could not produce anything thicker. As guns grew larger, the plates grew with them, and by 1875 the *HMS Inflexible* carried twenty-four inches of iron at the waterline. That approach hit a wall. Pure wrought iron was tough but soft. A heavy shell could punch through by sheer momentum even when the plate refused to crack.

## The Hard Face

Compound armor solved the problem by welding two metals into one plate. A hard steel face was poured directly onto the back of a softer wrought-iron slab while both were still molten. When the assembly cooled, the steel and iron formed a metallurgical bond rather than a mechanical joint. The face shattered incoming shells; the back absorbed the residual energy without spalling into the crew compartment.

The Wilson process and the Cammell process competed for Admiralty contracts through the 1880s. Both produced plates roughly twice as effective as homogeneous wrought iron of the same thickness, measured by the muzzle energy required to perforate them.

## Trade-Offs

Compound plates were brittle. A near-miss against the unprotected backing, or a hit on a fastener, could crack the steel face across an entire panel. Repair was nearly impossible at sea. Yards stocked spare plates and rebolted them in dry dock.

Compound armor held the line for less than a decade. By the early 1890s, all-steel armor face-hardened by the Harvey process surpassed it on every metric, and the navies that had invested most heavily in compound plate were forced to refit. The lesson is unchanged: armor is a moving target. A ship optimized for the threats of its launch year is already obsolescent at its first refit.
