A tartan is not a picture. It is a *count*. Before there is any cloth, before the warp is even wound, the entire pattern already exists as a short string of colours and numbers — the **thread count**, or *sett*. Get the count right and the cloth will come out identical on any loom, in any country, a century apart. Get it wrong by two threads and you have woven something that is merely *tartan-ish*.

This is the part of tartan that outsiders never see, and it is the part weavers care about most.

## What a Thread Count Looks Like

A sett is written as a sequence of colour codes and thread numbers. A short, real-feeling example:

```
G24 K4 G4 K24 R2 K24 G4 K4 G24 B24
```

Read it left to right: 24 threads of green, 4 of black, 4 of green, 24 of black, 2 of red, and so on. The letters are colour abbreviations — `G` green, `K` black (to avoid clashing with blue), `R` red, `B` blue, `W` white, `Y` yellow. The numbers are literal thread counts. That is the whole language. No drawing required.

The same count governs both directions of the cloth. The threads running lengthwise on the loom are the **warp**; the threads the weaver passes across are the **weft**. In a true tartan the weft repeats the warp exactly, so the pattern is identical horizontally and vertically. That is *why* tartan produces squares rather than mere stripes: every colour crosses every other colour at right angles.

## The Pivot and the Half-Sett

Most tartans are **symmetrical**, which lets the count be written even more briefly. The sequence runs out to a turning point — the **pivot** — then mirrors back. So a count printed as:

```
B24 K4 B4 K24 G48 K24 B4 K4 B24
```

is often recorded as just the half-sett out to each pivot, because the weaver knows to reflect it:

> "Count out to the pivot, reverse, and repeat. The cloth folds the pattern back on itself like a closing hand."

The two pivot points are special. They are the only places where a colour block is *not* mirrored — it simply turns. Skilled weavers can identify a tartan, and spot a counterfeit, by looking only at how the pattern behaves at its pivots.

A minority of tartans are **asymmetric** — they do not mirror, and the full sett must be written out. The Buchanan and a handful of others fall here. Asymmetric setts are harder to set up and easier to weave wrong, which is part of why they are rare.

## Threads, Not Inches

Notice what the count does *not* contain: any measurement. A sett says nothing about how wide the green band is in millimetres. It only says *24 threads*. The physical width depends on the **sett density** — how many threads per inch the cloth is woven at.

This is the elegant part. A fine worsted woven at 100 threads per inch and a heavy district check woven at 40 will show the **same proportions** even though one band is half the width of the other. The count is scale-free. It encodes ratio, not size. A child's scarf and a full kilt length can share an identical sett and read as unmistakably the same tartan.

To turn a count into real cloth, the weaver does the arithmetic:

1. Add every number in the full sett to get total threads per repeat.
2. Divide by the chosen threads-per-inch to get the width of one repeat.
3. Decide how many repeats the finished piece needs, and wind the warp to match.

A repeat of 220 threads at 44 threads per inch gives a five-inch sett — large and bold. The same 220 at 88 gives two and a half inches — tight and formal. Same instruction, two different cloths.

## Why the Count Survives

Paper rots, dyes fade, and looms are scrapped, but a thread count is almost weightless. It can be copied in a margin, memorised, or telegraphed. The great tartan registers of the nineteenth century are, at bottom, just **books of counts**. That is why a pattern first recorded in 1842 can be rewoven today with confidence: nobody is copying a faded sample, they are executing a set of numbers.

So when a weaver speaks of *knowing* a tartan, they do not mean recognising it on sight. They mean holding the count. The cloth is only ever the output. The pattern itself lives in the arithmetic — solid colour crossed with solid colour, exactly so many threads each, mirrored at the pivot and repeated until the warp runs out.
