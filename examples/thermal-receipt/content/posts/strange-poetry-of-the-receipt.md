Nobody sits down to write a receipt. That is exactly what makes it interesting.

Every other printed thing you handle was composed by someone who wanted to be read. A receipt is the opposite. It is the residue of a transaction, spat out by a machine that has no opinion about whether you keep it. And yet, if you slow down and actually *read* one, it reads like a poem written by a stranger about a minute of your life.

---

## A FOUND-TEXT MACHINE

Consider the form. A receipt is a list with a header, a body, and a total. It has a fixed meter — one item per line, quantity on the left, price on the right, a dotted leader stretched between them like a held breath. It rhymes through repetition. It ends, always, on a number.

That is more structure than most free verse ever commits to.

> "BANANAS 0.59 / OAT MILK 4.29 / ONE (1) BIRTHDAY CANDLE 0.99"

Read those three lines aloud and a small story assembles itself without your permission. Somebody is making a quiet breakfast and, almost as an afterthought, marking a day. The receipt did not mean to tell you that. It is found poetry precisely *because* nobody arranged it to move you.

---

## THE TYPOGRAPHY OF NOT CARING

The aesthetic power of the receipt comes from constraint, not taste. A thermal printer has:

- **One typeface**, usually a cramped monospace
- **One color**, a heat-darkened gray we generously call black
- **One column**, about forty characters wide
- **One alignment trick**, the dotted or spaced leader

Designers spend careers chasing the calm that these limits produce by accident. There is no hierarchy to fuss over because there is only one weight. There is no grid to design because the paper is the grid. The all-caps header is not a style choice; it is the only thing the ROM font could do at double height.

The result is honest in a way that designed things rarely manage. You believe a receipt. You would never believe a brochure.

---

## EPHEMERA THAT REFUSES TO BE PRECIOUS

Most things we call ephemera get rescued eventually — ticket stubs in shoeboxes, postcards in drawers. The receipt resists even that. It curls. It fades. It sticks to a gum wrapper at the bottom of a bag and turns into lint.

This is the part I love most. A receipt is *designed* to be forgotten, and that built-in disappearance is what makes the surviving ones feel like relics. The crumpled diner check from a trip you barely remember. The hardware-store slip with `1x HINGE` on it, the only proof a door was ever fixed. These are not kept on purpose. They are kept by accident, which is the only honest way to keep anything.

If you want to see the poetry, try this the next time you are handed one:

```text
THE DAILY MARKET
  1x  COFFEE, MEDIUM .......... 2.75
  1x  NEWSPAPER ............... 1.50
  -----------------------------------
  SUBTOTAL .................... 4.25
  THANK YOU -- CALL AGAIN
```

Do not throw it away immediately. Read it as a stanza. *Coffee, newspaper, four twenty-five, thank you.* That is somebody's whole morning, compressed into a heat-printed haiku, and the machine handed it back without ever knowing it had written anything at all.

We tear these up by the billion. Somewhere in the pile is the most widely circulated and least examined literature on earth. The least we can do is read one slowly before it fades.
