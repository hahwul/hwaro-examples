Pull a receipt out of a coat you have not worn since last winter and you may find it blank. Not faded to gray — *gone*. A clean little tongue of paper with no record of anything. People assume the ink ran out. There was never any ink.

A thermal receipt is not printed. It is *developed*, the way a photograph used to be, and like a photograph left in the sun, it can un-develop itself.

---

## HOW HEAT BECOMES A WORD

Thermal paper is coated with two reactive layers held apart in a powdery film. One is a colorless dye called a **leuco dye**. The other is a mild acid, the **developer**, historically bisphenol A or its cousin bisphenol S. Apart, they do nothing. The paper stays white.

The print head is a row of tiny resistors. When a dot needs to go black, its resistor flicks to roughly 200 degrees Celsius for a fraction of a millisecond. That pinpoint of heat melts the coating, the dye and the acid touch, and the dye changes shape into a form that absorbs light. Black dot. No ribbon, no toner, no liquid ink — just a chemical handshake triggered by warmth.

> "The print head does not deposit anything. It rearranges what is already there. The receipt was always capable of those words; the heat only convinced it to admit them."

The whole page is one molecule waiting to be talked into darkness, one dot at a time, at the speed of a moving roller.

---

## WHY IT REFUSES TO LAST

The same reaction that prints the receipt is the one that erases it. The darkened dye is **metastable** — it would rather, given any excuse, slip back toward colorless. Three things give it that excuse:

1. **Heat.** A hot car, a dryer, a sunny dashboard. The same energy that printed it can re-melt the coating and let the dye relax back to white.
2. **Pressure and friction.** Coins, keys, and other receipts abrade the coating. The dye does not so much fade as get rubbed away.
3. **Solvents and oils.** Hand lotion, alcohol gel, the plasticizer in a vinyl wallet — these dissolve or react with the developer and bleach the print clean.

This is why the receipt you actually need for a return is always the one that has surrendered to a blank rectangle, while the meaningless one for a pack of gum survives a decade in perfect condition. The chemistry plays no favorites. It only obeys whatever the drawer did to it.

---

## DESIGNING FOR DISAPPEARANCE

There is a quiet lesson here for anyone who makes things meant to be kept. Thermal paper is the cheapest, fastest, most convenient way ever invented to put words on a surface, and the price of that convenience is **permanence**. You cannot have all four — cheap, fast, easy, *and* lasting. Something gives. With receipts, it is memory.

If you want a thermal record to survive, the moves are unromantic:

```text
KEEP COOL  .... below 25C, out of sunlight
KEEP DRY   .... no oils, no lotion, no vinyl
KEEP FLAT  .... no folding across the print
OR JUST    .... photograph it the day you get it
```

That last line is the honest one. We have quietly accepted that the paper record is temporary and offloaded permanence to a photo in the cloud. The receipt has become a *first draft* of a memory, expected to fade once a more durable copy exists.

Which is, when you think about it, a strangely human arrangement. We invented a medium that forgets on purpose, then built our whole retail world on top of it, then act surprised when the proof of what we did dissolves in our pockets. The thermal receipt is not a flawed document. It is an honest one. It tells you, in its own slow fade, that nothing printed in heat was ever meant to be kept.
