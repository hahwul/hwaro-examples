Every house we draw begins with the void at its centre. The madang — the packed-earth courtyard of the traditional Korean house — was never leftover space. It was the house's lung: the place where winter sun was gathered and thrown into the rooms, where summer air was pulled through open halls, where kimchi jars, weddings, and threshing all happened under the open sky.

A passive house is usually described in numbers — fifteen kilowatt-hours, point-six air changes, no thermal bridges. We accept every one of those numbers and then ask the older question: where does the light land in February, and which two doors do you open in July? When the courtyard is drawn first, the physics gets easier, not harder. The envelope closes tight around rooms that already know how to breathe. Every house in [our project record](@/projects.md) began this way, and [the method](@/approach.md) has not changed since the first frame.
