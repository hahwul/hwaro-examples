Dear Roster,

The season closes where it opened, more or less — on the ground, moving on foot rather than wing. The Porcupine caribou herd, named for a river most of them never cross, spends its winters scattered through the forests and foothills of northern Yukon and moves each spring toward the coastal plain of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, one of the longest overland migration routes of any land mammal on the continent.

<!-- more -->

The deadline is the whole story. Cows are pregnant through the entire journey and need to reach the coastal plain by early June, when the calving window opens — arrive too late and calves are born en route, in worse terrain, with more predators and less new-growth forage to recover on. So the herd moves fast and mostly together in spring, tens of thousands of animals crossing rivers still running high with snowmelt, climbing passes that in a bad year are still holding late snow themselves. Satellite collars have clocked individual cows covering over thirty miles in a single day when the pressure is on.

The coastal plain itself is the payoff for the timing: exposed, wind-scoured ground that keeps mosquitoes and warble flies down in the first weeks after birth, and a flush of new sedge growth precisely synchronized to when a nursing cow needs the calories most. Calves that are born on schedule and on the plain survive their first weeks at meaningfully higher rates than calves born short of it — which is why the herd's insistence on reaching a specific strip of tundra by a specific week isn't ritual so much as arithmetic, run the same way every year by animals that have no calendar but the ground under them.

That's six letters, six routes, and one season closed. Read the whole run again from [Issue No. 1, the Central Flyway](/issues/central-flyway/), or see [how the roster actually works](/about/). It reopens in January with six new ones.

— W.H., Field Roster
