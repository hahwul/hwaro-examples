Dear Roster,

Of everything this letter has covered this season, this is the crossing that still doesn't sound plausible written down. Bar-tailed godwits leave Alaska's Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta in late September and fly to New Zealand, over open Pacific water, without landing, feeding, or drinking a single time along the way. Satellite-tagged birds have logged flights well past a week in the air — some over eleven days — covering distances longer than the diameter of the continental United States four times over.

<!-- more -->

The bird can't carry fat the way a small plane carries fuel and still expect to fly, so it rebuilds itself instead. In the weeks before departure a godwit roughly doubles its body mass, almost all of it fat, while simultaneously shrinking its gizzard, liver, kidneys, and intestines — organs it will not need in flight and cannot afford to carry as dead weight. The flight muscles and heart, by contrast, enlarge. By the time it lifts off the mudflats of the delta, the bird is, functionally, a fuel tank with wings, and the digestive system it left the ground with will need to regrow almost from scratch after it lands.

There's no route-finding mystery here in the way there is with the monarch — godwits appear to use a combination of magnetic and celestial cues refined by experience, and adult birds that have made the crossing before tend to arrive more precisely than first-year birds flying it cold. What remains genuinely unresolved is the physiology: how a vertebrate sustains aerobic exercise at this intensity for over a week without rest, sleep in the conventional sense, or measurable organ damage, is still an open question in flight biology, godwit-shaped and otherwise.
