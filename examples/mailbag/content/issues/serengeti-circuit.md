Dear Roster,

There is no departure date for this one, which is the whole point of the letter. Roughly one and a half million wildebeest are moving through the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem as you read this, but they are not on a schedule — they are on a rain gauge. The herd tracks the green-up of short grass across the plains, and since the rain doesn't arrive on the same day two years running, neither does the herd.

<!-- more -->

Right now the concentration is on the Ndutu Plains in the southern Serengeti, where the calcium-rich volcanic soil grows grass dense enough to support a synchronized calving season: something close to eighty percent of this year's calves will be born within a three-week window, most of them in the past two weeks. The timing is a defense in itself. A predator can only eat so much in a given month, and a herd that floods the plain with half a million calves at once overwhelms the math of any lion pride or hyena clan in range.

From here the loop runs clockwise, more or less, as the southern grass browns off and the animals push north and west toward the Grumeti and then the Mara River, where the crossings that make this migration famous actually happen — not out of ambition but because there is no way around the water. Crocodiles wait at the same fords every year, and the herd crosses anyway, because the grass on the far bank hasn't been eaten yet and the grass behind them has.

Ecologists increasingly describe this less as a migration than as a moving equilibrium — the herd is, in effect, the mechanism that keeps the Serengeti's grasslands from either overgrazing or reverting to bush. Wildebeest don't follow the rain so much as *maintain* the system that makes the rain worth following.

Next dispatch: an ocean crossing with no coastline in sight.

— W.H.
