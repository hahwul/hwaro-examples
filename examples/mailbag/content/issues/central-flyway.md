Dear Roster,

Half a million sandhill cranes are standing in the Platte River right now, or near enough to it, in water shallow enough to keep a coyote honest and wide enough that nothing can approach unseen. They have been stopping on this eighty-mile stretch of braided channel for longer than anyone has been counting them, refueling on waste corn in the surrounding fields by day and roosting in the open river by night, wings half-spread, waiting out the last cold weeks before the flyway opens north.

<!-- more -->

What they are waiting for is not a date but a thaw line. The Central Flyway runs roughly along the 100th meridian, and the cranes read it the way a sailor reads wind — leaving the Platte in loose skeins of a few hundred, then a few thousand, as soon as the ground far ahead of them softens enough to probe for food. By late April the same birds that clogged the river in March will be spread across four thousand five hundred kilometers of continent, from Nebraska cornfields to the tussock tundra of Alaska's North Slope, where they will nest on the ground in scrapes barely lined with sedge.

The unison call is the thing to listen for if you are ever on the river at dusk: a mated pair throws their heads back and calls together, the male's note dropping under the female's, and the whole roost answers in overlapping waves until it sounds less like birds than like weather. Biologists studying the site have found individual pairs returning to the same half-mile of river for over a decade running. The Platte, in other words, is not a rest stop. It is closer to a family address.
