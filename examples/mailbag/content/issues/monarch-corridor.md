Dear Roster,

This is the one that breaks the usual rule of a migration letter, because no individual monarch actually completes the route this issue describes. The butterflies that leave southern Ontario each August and fly to a few dozen acres of oyamel fir forest in the mountains of Michoacán are not the same butterflies that fly north again the following spring. Those are their great-grandchildren, or close to it — the return trip is finished in relay, over three or four short-lived generations, each one flying a little further north before laying eggs and dying.

<!-- more -->

Only the generation that makes the southbound leg is built for distance. Cued by shortening days and cooling nights in late summer, monarchs emerging from late-season eggs enter reproductive diapause — their ovaries simply don't develop — and redirect that energy into fat storage and a lifespan stretched to eight or nine months instead of the usual few weeks. That "super generation" flies the full four thousand kilometers to a specific band of Mexican mountainside, where the microclimate is cool enough to slow their metabolism to a near-stop but not cold enough to freeze them, and clusters by the millions on oyamel branches until the trees themselves seem to be made of wings.

Navigation on the southbound leg relies on a sun compass corrected by an internal clock seated in the antennae — remove or paint over a monarch's antennae, laboratory work has shown, and its heading goes wrong in a specific, predictable way. What no one has fully explained is how the great-grandchildren, none of which have ever seen Michoacán, find their way there each spring using the same compass and clock, several generations removed from anyone who's made the trip before.

Next dispatch: a nonstop flight long enough to make the brant look conservative.

— W.H.
