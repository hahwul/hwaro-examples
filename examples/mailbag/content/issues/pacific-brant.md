Dear Roster,

Most waterfowl on the Pacific Flyway hug the coast, dropping into estuaries every few hundred miles the way a driver stops for gas. Pacific brant do something stranger. In late autumn they stage by the tens of thousands at Izembek Lagoon on the Alaska Peninsula, gorging on what is likely the largest eelgrass bed on Earth, and then a large share of them simply leave the coast — flying south and slightly west, over open Pacific water, with no intention of landing until Baja California Sur.

<!-- more -->

Call it roughly three thousand miles of ocean with nothing beneath it for most of the trip. There's no reliable place to rest along that line, so the birds don't try; they fuel to a body mass well above their summer weight at Izembek specifically because the eelgrass there is dense and undisturbed enough to let them do it fast, then wait for a weather window — a strong tailwind behind a departing low-pressure system — and go. Flights of thirty-six to sixty hours nonstop have been documented by satellite tag, which for a bird the size of a small goose is a genuinely strange way to travel when the coastline was right there the whole time.

The eelgrass dependency cuts both ways. Izembek's beds are unusually stable, but they're not infinite, and a bad ice year or a disturbance event at the lagoon shows up almost immediately in brant body condition and, a season later, in survival. Wildlife managers now treat the health of one lagoon in the Aleutians as a leading indicator for a wintering population spread across four thousand kilometers of Mexican coast — a reminder that a flyway's weakest link is sometimes a single staging ground, not the distance in between.

Next dispatch: a multigenerational relay that no single traveler completes.

— W.H.
