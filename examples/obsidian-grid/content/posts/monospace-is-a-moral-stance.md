# Monospace Is a Moral Stance

The proportional typeface is a civilizing force. It assigns each letter a width appropriate to its shape and hands the reader a rhythm that feels inevitable. It is the correct choice for a novel, a poem, or a love letter.

It is the wrong choice for a website that takes itself seriously.

## The virtue of uniform columns

In a monospaced face, every letter occupies the same cell. This looks, at first, like constraint. In practice, it is an accounting. Columns line up. Tables are tables without effort. A number hanging in the margin hangs exactly where you expect. The page inherits the truth of the grid it was set on.

A proportional column is an estimate. A monospaced column is a receipt.

## Against the appearance of ease

There is a genre of website that tries very hard to look casual — the hand-drawn sans, the playful wobble, the cheerful serif with a single italic word in the headline, underlined by the user's cursor. These sites are not relaxed. They are performing relaxation. They are exhausting.

A monospaced site admits that a page is a page, that a grid is a grid, and that the reader came here to read. It does not flirt. It does not wink.

## When mono fails

Long passages in a heavy monospaced face tire the eye. Use a light weight for body copy; reserve the bold for headings and inline code. Keep measure under seventy-two characters. Spring for a humanist monospace — Space Mono, JetBrains, IBM Plex — and avoid the Courier family unless you mean it as a statement.

You will mean it as a statement eventually. That is fine.
