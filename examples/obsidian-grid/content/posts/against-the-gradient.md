# Against the Gradient

Every decade, the web rediscovers the gradient and treats the rediscovery as revelation. This is strange, because gradients have always been a confession. They concede that a designer could not choose. They take two colors that disagree and smear them together in the hope the seam will be mistaken for intention.

## What a flat color is for

A flat color has a job. A heading carries it; a rule cuts it; a panel holds it in place. When the color is doing work, the work is visible. When a gradient is doing work, the work is visible only at the endpoints, and everything between is ornament.

In editorial layout, the worst failure is not a boring page. The worst failure is a page that is simultaneously bored and busy — neither committing to silence nor earning its noise. Gradients invite that failure because they are cheap.

## A defense of hairlines

One pixel, one gray, one grid. That is enough to divide a page. A hairline is assertive without shouting. It says: the next line is a different thought. A gradient says: I would like you to feel something here, and I will not say what.

## When to soften

There are places for graded tone: photography, simulated materials, ambient light where a shadow would otherwise have a hard edge. Editorial typography is not one of them. If your body copy needs a halo, the type is too small.

Set it bigger. Set it darker. Let the grid do the rest.
