The Scrivener came in writing like a needle dragged across slate — mechanically sound, cosmetically lovely, and unpleasant on every stroke. Under the loupe the diagnosis was ordinary: decades of a right-handed overwriter had worn the tipping into a flat with two crisp corners, and the corners were doing the scratching.

<!-- more -->

## Reading the tipping first

Smoothing is subtraction, and tipping material does not grow back. So before any abrasive came out, the nib went under the 10x loupe on the setup that never changes: tines checked for alignment against a fingernail, slit checked for even gap, and the wear flat mapped by rolling the pen through its writing angle. If the tines are misaligned, smoothing is malpractice — you will grind a ramp into the high tine and the pen is ruined at a workbench that meant well.

These tines were true. The flat was the whole story.

## The figure-eight budget

Micromesh at 4000 grit, laid over a leather pad for the barest give, and the pen at its owner's writing angle — which I had from a writing sample, the most underrated diagnostic document in this trade. The budget is strict:

```
grit 4000   2 figure-eights   re-inspect under loupe
grit 6000   4 figure-eights   re-inspect, wet test
grit 8000   6 figure-eights   wet test on rag paper
grit 12000  polish only       final writing test
```

Two passes at a coarse grit do more than twenty at a fine one, which is exactly why the coarse allowance is smaller. Between every stage the nib was wiped, inked with a dip, and tested — smoothing dry tells you nothing, because ink is the lubricant the pen will actually live with.

## Result

The corners rounded into a soft oval that keeps the fine line but glides on the down-stroke. Total material removed: invisible without magnification, which is the correct amount. The regret budget was never touched.
