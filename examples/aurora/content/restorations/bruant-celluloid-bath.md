A demonstrator is a pen with nowhere to hide, and this Bruant had been hiding everything anyway: the amber barrel had gone the color of strong tea, opaque with layered ink residue baked on through decades of refills and no rinses. The owner wanted to see the piston travel again. The pen, being celluloid, wanted to be left alone.

## What celluloid remembers

Celluloid is cellulose nitrate plasticized with camphor, and it never stops being a slightly nervous material. Heat it and it warps; wet it too long and it clouds; shock it with solvents and it crazes into a craquelure that no amount of polishing removes. Every cleaning decision starts from the same question — what will this barrel remember in ten years?

So: no ultrasonic (cavitation finds every internal stress), no ammonia at celluloid strength (that is for hard rubber feed channels, briefly), and no soak longer than ten minutes at a stretch, ever.

## The regimen

The residue came off in campaigns rather than battles. Distilled water at room temperature, ten minutes in, thirty minutes resting dry, repeated through an afternoon. A cotton swab wound thin enough to enter the barrel lifted what each soak loosened. Progress was measured against a window: after the fourth cycle, daylight; after the seventh, the piston seal visible as a shadow; after the eleventh, the swab came out clean.

The piston itself was another story — a cork seal, original, compressed to uselessness. It was replaced with cork, not silicone, cut from a wine-grade sheet and soaked in silicone grease before fitting. Original material where possible; honest substitutes where not.

## Plate notes

The barrel will never be water-clear again — a faint amber deepening at the section end is permanent, and the plate records it as such. It reads, to my eye, less like damage than like provenance. The pen fills, the piston glides, and light passes through 1935 on its way to the ink.
