Black hard rubber does not fade so much as it drifts — ultraviolet light and moisture pull the sulfur chemistry apart until a pen that left the factory like polished jet arrives at the bench the color of milky coffee. This Vesper eyedropper had drifted for a century, and its rose-and-thorn chasing, machine-cut in 1912, had drifted with it.

<!-- more -->

## The temptation to sand

The fast fix is well known and ruinous: abrasive polish until fresh black rubber shows. It works, in the sense that amputation cures a hangnail. Chasing on a pen this age is perhaps a tenth of a millimeter deep; aggressive polishing planes the pattern into a ghost of itself, and a chased pen with erased chasing is worth less than an honestly brown one. The bench rule is blunt — the pattern outranks the color.

## What was actually done

The finish was revived in three conservative stages, testing each on the barrel end where the cap covers all sins:

1. A wash in cool water with two drops of mild detergent, dried immediately — hard rubber and standing water are old enemies.
2. Surface oxidation lifted with a felt pad and an ultra-fine polish rated for museum ebonite, worked *along* the chasing lines under a bench lamp raked low so the pattern threw shadows. Raking light is the honest inspector; overhead light flatters and lies.
3. A final hand-rub with microcrystalline wax, buffed with flannel.

The result is a deep umber-black — not showroom, and not meant to be. Every rose and every thorn still catches the raking light.

## Sealing the eyedropper

An eyedropper pen is a bottle with a nib, so the section threads got a whisper of pure silicone grease against seepage. Nothing else was needed: no sac, no bar, no spring. The oldest filling system in the catalog remains the most reliable one in it, which feels like a lesson the plate should record without comment.
