The first pen in the catalog arrived in a tobacco tin, wrapped in a handkerchief that had clearly done this job before. A Corvid Cadet in grey pearl celluloid, the entry-level pen of a maker that never made anything carelessly. The lever flopped without resistance — the surest symptom of a sac gone to dust.

<!-- more -->

## Opening the pen

Section and barrel had not been separated since the war, so the joint got twenty minutes of gentle dry heat from a heat gun on its lowest setting, waved rather than aimed. Celluloid scorches at temperatures that feel merely warm to a fingertip, so the rule at this bench is heat the air, not the pen. The section gave with a quarter-turn creak, and the old sac came out in flakes, like black confetti from a very small, very sad party.

## Fitting the new sac

The Cadet takes a size 16 sac. The petrified remnant was scraped from the section nipple with a rosewood pick — never steel, which gouges the threads that give shellac its grip. A fresh latex sac was trimmed to length against the pressure bar, leaving three millimeters of clearance at the barrel's blind end.

Shellac discipline is the whole trick of this repair. One thin coat around the nipple, one minute of open time, then the sac rolled on and left alone overnight. Impatience here is the leading cause of pens that fill beautifully for a week and then drown their owners.

## The record

Dusted the new sac with pure talc, reseated the pressure bar, and threaded the section home. The pen drew a full reservoir of washed-out blue on the first stroke of the lever, which after eighty-seven years dry felt less like a repair than a resuscitation.

- Condition in: no fill, lever slack, sac petrified
- Work: sac 16, shellac, talc, lever spring cleaned
- Compromise: none; fully reversible
