Every sprung nib tells the same story and the owners always confirm it: the pen rolled off a desk, uncapped, and met the floor point-first. The Scrivener Deluxe arrived with its right tine lifted a full half-millimeter above the left — visible without a loupe, which for tine misalignment is the difference between a sprain and a compound fracture.

<!-- more -->

## What sprung means

Gold is forgiving up to its yield point and treacherous after. A *sprung* tine has been bent past yield, so it no longer returns on its own; bend it back carelessly and the metal work-hardens, and work-hardened gold cracks at the shoulder — a repair that becomes a re-tip, at ten times the cost. The whole craft of unsprung nibs is applying force that never accumulates.

## The slow bend

The nib stayed on the pen; the feed makes the best anvil it will ever have. With the section braced against the bench pad, the lifted tine was pressed down with a thumbnail — not a tool, a thumbnail, because the nail gives before gold does — just past level with its partner, held for three seconds, released, and inspected. Then again. Eleven small corrections over twenty minutes, each one taking the tine a shade further and letting the metal rest between.

Overcorrection is deliberate and tiny: a sprung tine pressed exactly to level springs back proud of it. You aim a hair below level and let the memory of the metal split the difference.

## Final alignment

Under the 10x loupe the tines now meet with their tips level and the slit even from breather hole to tip. Once true, the point took two conservative passes of micromesh under the same figure-eight budget recorded in [Plate No. 02](/restorations/halcyon-nib-smoothing/) — realignment always leaves a whisper of roughness where the tips re-learn to agree. The stub lays down a crisp two-millimeter line with the faint music a good stub makes on cheap paper. The owner has been advised, in the gentlest terms the trade allows, that pens have caps for a reason — and the plate closes the catalog's sixth entry with the tine gap recorded at zero.
