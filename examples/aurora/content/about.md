Aurora is the bench journal of Maren Voss, a fountain pen restorer working out of a two-window workshop above a framer's shop in a harbor town that freezes solid four months of the year. The long dark is good for this work. Nothing about a seventy-year-old lever-filler rewards hurry.

## Why plates

Old auction catalogs and museum registers numbered their photographic illustrations as *plates*, each with a small caption recording maker, date, and condition. This journal borrows the convention. Every restoration that crosses [the bench](/restorations/) receives a plate number in order of arrival, and the write-up is filed under it — what came in, what was wrong, what was done, and what compromises were accepted. The numbering never restarts and never skips. A pen that cannot be saved still gets its plate; failure is part of the record.

## The bench rules

A few principles govern everything catalogued here:

- **Reversibility first.** Shellac, rosin, and heat-set friction can all be undone by the next restorer. Cyanoacrylate and epoxy cannot. If a repair is permanent, the plate says so in plain words.
- **The pen decides the schedule.** Ammonia soaks run as long as they run. Celluloid moves at celluloid's pace. Deadlines belong to courier services, not to nib work.
- **Original parts stay with the pen.** A cracked feed or a mismatched blind cap is bagged, labeled with the plate number, and returned to the owner. History is not mine to discard.
- **No plating, no re-stamping, no fakery.** A restored pen should look honestly old, not implausibly new.

## Correspondence

The catalog does not take commissions through the site, but corrections are welcome — if a plate misidentifies a filling system or dates a clip style wrongly, write to the workshop and the entry will be amended with a note. The record matters more than being right the first time.
