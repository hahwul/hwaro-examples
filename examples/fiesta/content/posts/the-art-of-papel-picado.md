Papel picado, meaning "perforated paper," is one of Mexico's most recognizable and beloved folk arts. These delicate tissue paper banners have adorned streets, plazas, and altars for centuries.

## Origins

The tradition dates back to pre-Hispanic times when the Aztecs used bark paper (*amatl*) for ceremonial decorations. Spanish colonizers later introduced tissue paper, and the art evolved into what we see today.

## The Craft

Artisans stack dozens of tissue paper sheets and use chisels and hammers to cut intricate designs — flowers, birds, skeletons, and geometric patterns. Each banner tells a story:

- **Día de los Muertos** — Skulls and marigolds guide spirits home
- **Weddings** — Doves and hearts celebrate union
- **Fiestas Patrias** — Eagles and flags honor the nation

## A Living Tradition

Today, papel picado remains essential to Mexican celebrations. From small village fiestas to grand national holidays, these colorful banners transform any space into a place of joy and remembrance.

> "Each cut is a prayer, each color a blessing."

The next time you see papel picado dancing in the wind, remember — you're witnessing centuries of artistry in motion.
