The Great Smoky Mountains section of the Appalachian Trail offers some of the most beautiful and challenging hiking in the eastern United States. We tackled a 115-kilometer section through peak fall foliage season.

<!-- more -->

## Trail Overview

This section runs through Great Smoky Mountains National Park, crossing the Tennessee-North Carolina border multiple times. The trail features:

- Continuous elevation changes
- Dense forest canopy
- Historic shelters
- Abundant wildlife
- Spectacular viewpoints

### Route Profile

Starting at Newfound Gap, we hiked northbound to Davenport Gap, covering approximately 115 kilometers over 7 days with an average of 16.5 km per day.

## Daily Breakdown

### Day 1: Newfound Gap to Icewater Spring Shelter (12 km)

The trail immediately climbs to Charlies Bunion, one of the most photographed spots on the entire AT. The exposed rock outcropping provides panoramic views of the surrounding mountains, painted in brilliant reds, oranges, and yellows.

### Day 2: Icewater Spring to Pecks Corner Shelter (16 km)

This section follows the state line along the ridge, with numerous ups and downs. The trail passes through high-elevation spruce-fir forests, a rare ecosystem in the southern Appalachians.

### Day 3: Pecks Corner to Tricorner Knob Shelter (14 km)

Mount Guyot (2,025m), the highest point on the AT in the Smokies, dominates this section. The summit itself is wooded, but nearby viewpoints offer excellent vistas.

### Day 4: Tricorner Knob to Cosby Knob Shelter (18 km)

A long day with significant elevation loss and regain. The trail passes through diverse forest types, from high-elevation conifers to lower hardwood forests.

### Day 5: Cosby Knob to Davenport Gap (15 km)

The final day involves a steep descent from the high country. By afternoon, we reached Davenport Gap, marking the northern boundary of Great Smoky Mountains National Park.

## Wildlife Encounters

We were fortunate to observe:
- Black bears (from a safe distance)
- White-tailed deer
- Wild turkeys
- Various salamander species
- Numerous bird species including ravens and hawks

## Trail Conditions

November conditions were generally good:
- Temperatures: 2°C to 18°C
- Light rain on two days
- No snow above 1,800m
- Well-maintained trail surfaces
- Adequate water sources

## Shelter System

The Smokies have a mandatory shelter system - tenting is only allowed at designated campsites. Shelters fill up quickly, especially during peak seasons. We were strategic about our timing and never had issues securing spots.

## Recommendations

**Best time to hike**: Late September to mid-October for peak colors, or late April to May for spring wildflowers.

**Permit requirements**: Backcountry permits are required and can be reserved up to 30 days in advance.

**Bear precautions**: All shelters have bear cables for food storage. Never sleep with food in your pack.

**Water treatment**: All water sources should be treated. We used a combination of filtration and chemical treatment.

This section of the AT represents quintessential Appalachian hiking - challenging terrain, beautiful forests, and a strong sense of wilderness. Highly recommended for experienced backpackers.
