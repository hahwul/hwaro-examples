I trained as a composer, not an engineer, which is probably why I spent my first two years in games frustrated rather than curious. I would deliver a loop I was proud of, watch it play under a chaotic firefight and a quiet loading screen with equal indifference, and conclude that the problem was the mix. It usually wasn't. The problem was that nothing in the game was telling the music anything.

That realization is the whole practice now. A score that cannot hear the game is a linear track wearing an interactive costume, and players notice — maybe not consciously, but they notice. So I learned Wwise and FMOD properly, not as delivery targets for finished audio but as instruments in their own right, with parameters I compose against the same way I'd compose against a chord progression.

## How I work

Most engagements start in pre-production, before there is anything to score yet, because the music system needs to be designed alongside the systems it will respond to — combat, traversal, dialogue state. I sit with the design team, find the two or three variables that actually carry emotional weight in a given moment, and build the smallest system that can express them well. Bigger is not better; a single well-chosen RTPC beats a twelve-state machine nobody remembers to update.

I write and produce everything myself — no outsourced stems, no template libraries — because implementation decisions and compositional decisions are the same decision made twice, and I'd rather make it once, correctly, with the engine open.

## Outside the sessions

When I'm not in a Wwise project, I run a small modular synth setup at home, mostly for the generative patches that end up informing granular work later. I also mentor two students a year through a regional audio program, on the condition that at least one session happens with the DAW closed.
