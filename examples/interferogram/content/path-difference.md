A interferogram is a record of path difference. Each fringe in the pattern marks a contour where the optical path between two beams differs by exactly one wavelength. The eye reads the fringes as bright and dark bands, but the underlying quantity is geometric: how far one wavefront traveled relative to the other.

## Setting the Scale

Fringe spacing depends on three things: the wavelength of the source, the angle between the interfering wavefronts, and the medium between them. A green laser at 532 nm crossing two beams at one milliradian produces fringes about half a millimeter apart in air. The same setup in water compresses the spacing because the wavelength inside the medium is shorter.

## Reading a Surface

When one of the beams reflects off a polished surface, every dimple or scratch on that surface adds a tiny extra path length. The fringes bend around the defect by an amount proportional to the depth. A half-fringe deflection means the surface drops by a quarter wavelength — sub-100-nm sensitivity from a setup that fits on a desk.

## Coherence Limits

Fringes only form when the two beams remain phase-locked. Lasers with a long coherence length give crisp patterns over meters of path mismatch. Broadband sources, like a halogen lamp through a narrow filter, restrict the visible region to a few microns of equal path. That narrow window is useful: it picks out a single height plane in a transparent sample and ignores everything above and below.
