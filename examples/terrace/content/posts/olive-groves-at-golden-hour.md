There is a particular hour in the Mediterranean afternoon when the sun drops low enough to thread its light between the twisted trunks of old olive trees. The leaves, silvery on one side and deep green on the other, catch the warmth and scatter it in every direction. It is the hour when the groves seem most alive, humming with cicadas and the distant sound of someone working the land.

The olive harvest is one of the oldest agricultural traditions in this part of the world. Families gather beneath the canopy with nets spread wide on the ground, shaking branches or combing through them with wooden rakes. The fruit falls in a quiet patter, dark and glossy, each one carrying the concentrated flavor of months of sun and dry wind. There is a rhythm to the work that has not changed in centuries.

After the picking comes the pressing. Small mills, some of them centuries old and others outfitted with modern steel, crush the olives into a thick paste. The first oil to emerge is extraordinary, bright green and peppery on the tongue, with a bitterness that speaks of the land itself. Tasted on a piece of coarse bread, still warm, it is one of the simplest and most satisfying things the Mediterranean has to offer.

As the sun finally sets behind the hills, the grove settles into blue shadow. The workers gather their tools and head home along dusty paths. The air cools, carrying the scent of crushed leaves and dry earth. Tomorrow they will return to do it all again, as their grandparents did, and their grandparents before them.
