Walk any street in Lisbon and the walls speak. They are covered in azulejos, the painted ceramic tiles that have decorated Portuguese buildings since the fifteenth century. Blue and white are the dominant colors, though you will find tiles in yellow, green, and terracotta as well. The patterns range from simple geometric repeats to elaborate figurative scenes depicting ships, saints, and the daily life of centuries past.

The tradition arrived with the Moors, who brought the technique from North Africa. The word azulejo itself comes from the Arabic al-zulaij, meaning polished stone. Over the centuries, Portuguese artisans made the craft their own, developing a distinctive style that blended Islamic geometry with European narrative art. Churches, palaces, train stations, and ordinary houses all received the same treatment, their facades transformed into luminous surfaces that catch and reflect the Lisbon light.

What makes the azulejo so enduring is its practicality as much as its beauty. The glazed surface protects walls from the damp Atlantic air and keeps interiors cool in summer. A tiled building needs little maintenance and ages gracefully, its colors remaining vivid for centuries. When a tile cracks or falls, it can be replaced without disturbing the whole. This combination of durability and elegance explains why the tradition has survived when so many others have faded.

Today, contemporary artists continue to work with the form, creating new patterns and installations that honor the tradition while pushing it forward. In the Alfama district, old tiles crumble alongside fresh ones, and the effect is of a city that wears its entire history on its surface. To stand in a Lisbon square surrounded by azulejos is to be enclosed in a kind of open-air gallery, where every wall is a canvas and every pattern tells a story that stretches back five hundred years.
