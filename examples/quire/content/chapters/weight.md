Every design system eventually gets asked to make a word "stand out," and the
laziest answer is always a new color. Resist it. A page with one accent color
used sparingly reads as disciplined; a page with red for warnings, orange for
tips, green for success, and blue for links reads as a control panel, and
control panels are exhausting to read as prose.

<!-- more -->

## One color, two jobs

Weight does most of what a second color would have done, at no cost to the
palette. A semibold word inside a regular paragraph pulls focus the same way
italics do, without changing the reader's sense of what document they're in —
it's still black text on paper, just spoken slightly louder. This book keeps
its accent, a red the color of a proofreader's pencil, reserved for exactly
two jobs: links, because a reader needs to know where a click will take them,
and rules — the thin lines that divide a page into sections. Everything else
that needs emphasis gets it from weight, from scale, or from position on the
page, never from a fourth or fifth color creeping into the palette.

Notice that the second box didn't need red to make its point. That's the
whole chapter: emphasis is a job weight can do alone, so give the accent
color a smaller job — one it can do without ever feeling arbitrary about
which word earned it. Carried far enough, this habit becomes a whole design
posture, which is the subject of [Restraint](/chapters/restraint/).
