Bismuth grown from a melt rarely forms a clean hexagonal block. Instead it produces hopper crystals: skeletal, terraced shells that thicken at the edges before the interior fills in. The reason is a steep growth gradient. Heat is lost fastest at the corners and edges of the cooling surface, so atoms preferentially attach there, leaving a hollow stair on each face.

## Reading the Stairs

Each terrace step is a single crystallographic plane, separated from the next by a unit-cell jump. Counting steps under a loupe gives a rough log of how quickly the melt cooled. Closely spaced terraces signal a rapid drop and a thin oxide layer; widely spaced steps indicate a slower bath that allowed each layer to migrate before the next nucleated.

## The Oxide Film

The colors are not pigment. Once the casting hits air, a transparent bismuth oxide film grows on the freshly exposed metal. The thickness varies with how long that surface has been hot — a few nanometers for cool corners, tens of nanometers for the still-glowing valleys. Light reflecting off the top of the film interferes with light reflecting off the metal beneath, and the path difference selects which wavelengths survive.

## Practical Notes

For consistent color, hold the melt at 280C until the surface is mirror-flat, then cool at roughly 1C per second. Faster cooling shortens the terrace spacing and shifts the dominant interference band toward blue. Slower cooling thickens the oxide and pushes the spectrum into yellow and rose. Tilting the mold during the first ten seconds biases the hopper geometry toward one face, which makes the staircase pattern legible from a single viewing angle rather than scattered around the specimen.
