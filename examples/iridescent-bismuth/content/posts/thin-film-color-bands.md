Anyone who has watched an oxide film grow on hot metal has seen the sequence: straw yellow, brown, magenta, indigo, teal, gold, then magenta again. The cycle does not stop. As long as the film keeps thickening, the same colors recur.

## The First Cycle

Constructive interference happens when the round trip through the film equals an integer number of wavelengths in the medium. For visible light, the first match for blue (around 450 nm) lands when the film is roughly 80 nm thick. Green peaks near 100 nm. Red peaks near 130 nm. Between those values, mixtures of suppressed and reinforced wavelengths produce the warm transition shades.

## The Second Cycle

Once the film passes about 250 nm, the same wavelengths begin to satisfy the integer condition again — now with two full wavelengths fitting inside instead of one. The colors return, but they are paler. Each higher-order pass mixes more wavelengths simultaneously, so the spectral selectivity drops and the bands wash toward white.

## Why Bismuth Looks Vivid

Bismuth oxide has a higher refractive index than most native oxides, which compresses the optical path. A thinner physical film produces the same color shift, so the first few cycles arrive quickly during cooling. Crystals pulled from a clean melt usually settle in the second cycle, where the dominant pinks and teals still hold strong saturation.

## Stabilizing the Color

The film keeps reacting until the metal cools below its oxidation threshold, around 100C. Quenching pieces in mineral oil at that point freezes the current thickness. A piece pulled at 90 nm stays magenta for years; the same piece left to cool slowly drifts through teal into a duller gold within minutes.
