Before there were outlines there were counters cut in reverse into a bar of soft steel, filed to a face, hardened, and struck into copper. In this full-day bench you cut one letter — most people choose a lowercase **a** or a period, because ambition is the enemy of a first punch — from blank to strike.

<!-- more -->

We work at the pace the metal allows, which is slower than you expect and exactly as satisfying as you hope.

## What you make

You leave with your cut and hardened punch, a set of smoke proofs pulled through the day, and if the strike goes well, a single justified matrix. Most of all you leave able to *read* a punch: to see, in any metal face, the decisions a hand made two hundred years ago.

## The sequence

1. **Layout in reverse.** Draw the counter mirror-wise and blacken the face with a candle so your graver's line reads.
2. **Cutting the counter.** Remove everything that will not print, checking constantly against a smoke proof.
3. **Filing the face.** Bring the outline down to the line; this is where the letter's weight is decided.
4. **Hardening and tempering.** Heat to cherry, quench, then draw the temper back to straw so the steel is hard but not brittle.
5. **The strike.** Drive the punch into copper to sink a matrix — one clean blow, no second chances.

## Come prepared

Wear closed shoes and tie back long hair; we are grinding steel and burning candles. Gravers, files, blanks, the forge, and loupes are provided. If you own a favourite loupe, bring it. Twelve benches only.
