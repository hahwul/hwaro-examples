A fabric reads as gossamer when light passes through it cleanly enough to reveal the structure of the weave. Achieving that quality is a matter of geometry rather than material. Cotton, silk, polyester, and nylon can all produce sheer cloth if the yarn diameter, thread count, and weave pattern are chosen correctly.

Three variables determine sheerness:

- Yarn count: in textile measurement, a higher count number indicates a finer yarn. Cotton voile typically uses an 80-count single-ply yarn or finer. Below roughly 60 count, the fabric loses transparency regardless of weave density.
- Thread density per inch: open-weave fabrics such as gauze use 30 to 50 threads per inch in each direction. Higher densities fill the gaps between yarns and reduce light transmission.
- Twist level: high-twist yarns hold their cylindrical shape under tension and resist flattening. Low-twist yarns spread under the weight of weaving and close the open spaces in a sheer construction.

Plain weave is the most common structure for sheer fabrics because it produces the most uniform open grid. Each warp thread passes alternately over and under each weft thread, leaving a small square aperture at every intersection. Variants such as leno weave twist pairs of warp threads around each weft pass, locking the open structure in place and preventing slippage that would otherwise close the apertures during finishing.

Finishing treatments matter as much as construction. A sheer fabric pulled too tight on the tenter frame during drying loses its drape and becomes papery. One pulled too loose develops uneven aperture sizes that scatter light unevenly. Production lines for high-end voile and organza include moisture-controlled relaxation steps that allow the cloth to settle before final pressing. The result is a textile that holds its open geometry without crispness, and that hangs in folds soft enough to read as transparent rather than translucent.
