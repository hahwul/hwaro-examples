Spider dragline silk is regularly cited as a benchmark fiber. Its tensile strength reaches roughly 1.1 gigapascals, comparable to high-grade steel by weight, and its toughness — the energy absorbed before failure — exceeds that of Kevlar by a factor of two to three. The numbers are accurate, but they describe a single property under controlled conditions. Working textiles built from silk-like fibers behave differently.

The dragline of *Nephila clavipes* is a composite of two proteins, MaSp1 and MaSp2, organized into crystalline beta-sheets embedded in an amorphous matrix. The crystals provide tensile strength; the matrix provides extensibility. Dragline silk can elongate roughly 27 percent before breaking, an order of magnitude more than most synthetic fibers of similar tensile strength. This combination is why total toughness is so high.

Three properties matter for textile applications and rarely appear in headline comparisons:

- Hydration response: silk fibers contract by up to 50 percent in length when wet and stiffen substantially. A garment woven from raw dragline would distort visibly in humid air.
- Fatigue under repeated low-stress cycles: silk performs well under single high-load events but degrades under thousands of small flexes faster than nylon or polyester.
- UV degradation: ultraviolet exposure breaks the protein backbone within weeks of direct sunlight, reducing tensile strength by 60 percent or more.

Engineered fibers based on recombinant spider silk proteins, produced in yeast or transgenic silkworms, address some of these limitations through cross-linking and post-spinning treatments. Commercial products such as Bolt Threads' Microsilk and Spiber's Brewed Protein have shipped in small runs but have not displaced conventional fibers at scale. Production cost per kilogram remains the binding constraint, not the protein chemistry itself.
