A relief print built from more than one block lives or dies by registration. The first impression sets the coordinates for everything that follows, so the initial pull is treated as a reference rather than a standalone result. Pencil marks on the bench, kento notches at the corner of each block, and a fixed paper stop together hold the work within a quarter-millimeter tolerance.

### Cutting the Reference Block

The key block carries the strongest contour line and the deepest carved areas. It is finished first because every color block will be aligned to it. The carver leaves a thin border of uncut wood around the image so that the kento marks survive the inking process. Without that buffer, repeated baren pressure rounds the corners and the paper begins to drift.

### Building Color Blocks

Each color block is cut from a tracing of the key block, transferred face-down onto fresh shina or cherry. The L-shaped kento on the lower-left and the straight notch on the lower-right correspond to the same positions on the key block. When a sheet is dropped into the kento, the same paper edge meets the same wood edge every time, regardless of how many blocks are pulled.

### Inking and Pulling Order

Lighter colors print first, working toward the deepest tones. This order is not absolute: a translucent yellow laid over a dried blue shifts toward green, while the same yellow under blue stays clean. Some printers reverse the convention to exploit that interaction. The baren pressure also changes by block. Flat areas tolerate broad, even strokes; fine carved detail prefers a tighter circular motion that polishes the paper into the recesses of the wood.

### Drying and Settling

Between layers, prints rest under a flat board with light weight. The dampened paper relaxes and dries slightly without warping. Skipping this step costs more than time, since paper that has stretched even a little will refuse to register on the next pass.
