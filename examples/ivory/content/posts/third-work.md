Relief is subtraction made permanent. The carver begins with a full block and removes everything that is not the figure, working in stages from rough chisel to flat scraper to fine burin. Each stage commits to the previous one. There is no recovery from a slip beyond the depth of the next cut.

## Depths and Planes

A successful relief reads at three distances. From across the room it must register as silhouette. At arm's length the principal planes resolve and the eye can follow a curved surface from raised edge to recessed ground. Up close the tool marks themselves carry meaning, recording the angle and pressure of every pass.

These three readings cannot be optimized independently. A figure cut sharply enough to throw a strong shadow will lose its delicate surface detail under the same shop light. The carver chooses which reading to favor and accepts the cost at the others.

## Material Memory

Bone, ivory, and dense fruitwoods all hold a tool the same way at the surface and quite differently a millimeter below. The grain structure is anisotropic. Cutting with the grain produces a clean shaving and a smooth wall. Cutting across it tears small fibers loose and leaves a furred edge that no amount of polishing will fully recover.

Experienced carvers map the grain before they sketch the design. The composition is laid out so that the most important curves run with the strongest fibers and the negative spaces fall where short grain would otherwise crumble.

## Finishing

A relief is finished when the tool marks read as intended and not before. Sanding flattens the surface evidence of the carver's hand and is generally a sign of work abandoned rather than completed. The final pass is a light cut with a freshly sharpened blade, made dry, leaving a faceted surface that catches light from every direction.
