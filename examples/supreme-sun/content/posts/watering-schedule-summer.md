The standard advice to water deeply but infrequently breaks down on a balcony in July. Container soil dries from all sides, not just the surface, and a terracotta pot in full afternoon sun can lose half its moisture in a single day. Schedules written for in-ground beds are not portable.

A more reliable rule starts from the pot itself. Lift each container in the morning. The weight tells you, without instruments, whether the soil is saturated, damp, or close to dry. After a week of doing this you will recognize the dry weight by feel and stop second-guessing the surface, which lies.

Watering before the sun reaches the plants gives roots a few hours to take up moisture before transpiration peaks. Evening watering works too, but leaves stay wet longer and fungal pressure rises, especially on basil and tomatoes. If only one window is available, morning is the safer choice.

Mulch helps even on a balcony. A two-centimeter layer of fine bark or pebbles slows surface evaporation and keeps the top of the root zone from baking. The trade-off is that mulch hides the soil and makes it harder to read moisture from the top, which is why the lift test stays useful.

Saucers are convenient and dangerous. Standing water suffocates roots within a day or two, even in a hot pot. Empty saucers thirty minutes after watering and accept that some excess will drip onto the floor. That is a smaller problem than rotted roots in the middle of a heat wave.
