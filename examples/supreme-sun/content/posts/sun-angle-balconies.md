A south-facing balcony in a temperate latitude receives between four and seven hours of direct sun each day, depending on the season and the surrounding building line. The number rarely matches the figure printed on a plant tag, because tags assume open ground without taller obstructions nearby.

The cleanest way to measure is to photograph the balcony floor every thirty minutes from eight in the morning until four in the afternoon. Mark each frame with the timestamp and walk the resulting sequence after sunset. The shadow line will reveal where the sun cleared the railing, where it was blocked by a neighboring wall, and where it lingered long enough to matter for a tomato or a young rosemary.

For most apartments the usable patch is narrower than expected. A planter pulled six centimeters away from the railing often gains an extra hour of light because the railing slats stop casting a barred shadow across the soil. That extra hour, repeated through the growing season, is the difference between a leggy seedling and a plant that fruits.

If the balcony faces east or west, the sun arrives or leaves at a low angle, and tall plants behind shorter ones will block the only useful window. Stagger by height and reserve the front edge for the species that flower or fruit. Leafy greens tolerate the back row.

Track the change month by month. The sun moves north in summer and south in winter. A balcony that is generous in July may be entirely shaded by November, and a planting plan should account for that decline rather than assume light is constant.
