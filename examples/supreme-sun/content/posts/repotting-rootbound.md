A plant becomes rootbound when the volume of roots exceeds what the container can hold. Water runs straight through, growth slows, and the surface soil pulls away from the rim. Most houseplants reach this state within two or three years, sometimes sooner if they were potted into a container that was already too small.

Slide the plant out by tipping the pot sideways and supporting the base of the stems. If the root mass holds the shape of the pot when free, the plant is rootbound. The visible signs are circling roots, a dense exterior mat, and a smell of stale water near the bottom.

Cut, do not unwind. A clean knife slicing a shallow vertical line down each side of the root ball, plus one cross at the base, encourages new roots to grow outward instead of continuing the old spiral. Removing more than a third of the mass risks shock, but anything less than a clear cut tends to leave the spiral intact.

Choose a pot one size larger than the original, not three. Roots grow into available soil, and a pot far larger than the plant traps water in zones the roots cannot reach yet. That standing moisture invites rot before the plant has a chance to fill the space.

Water once after potting and then leave the plant in bright indirect light for a week. Skip fertilizer until new growth appears, usually two to four weeks later. The roots are repairing more than they are absorbing, and a stronger feed at this stage burns rather than helps.
