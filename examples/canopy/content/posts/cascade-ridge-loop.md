The trailhead sits at 3,200 feet in a dense corridor of Douglas fir and western red cedar. Within the first mile the canopy breaks and the grade steepens sharply, switchbacking through avalanche chutes still carrying late-season snow patches in mid-September.

## The Ascent

By mile three, the trail emerges above treeline onto a rocky spine running northeast. The exposure here is real -- wind gusts pushed steadily from the west and the path narrows to boot-width in several spots. Trekking poles are not optional on this stretch.

The ridgeline itself is the reward. Looking south, the volcanic peaks line up in succession. To the north, glacial cirques hold remnant ice fields that catch morning light in a way photographs never quite capture.

## Summit Plateau

The high point at 6,820 feet is a broad plateau of fractured granite and alpine heather. We spent forty minutes here, eating lunch behind a wind shelter built by previous hikers from stacked rock. The silence above treeline has a particular quality -- not empty, but full of wind and distant water.

## Descent Through Old Growth

The return leg drops through the western drainage into a stand of old-growth forest that has likely stood for five hundred years. The trunks are enormous and the understory is thick with sword fern and salal. The trail becomes soft with accumulated duff and the temperature drops noticeably under the canopy.

The final two miles follow a creek bed back to the trailhead parking area. Water crossings at this time of year are manageable but require attention to footing on moss-covered rocks.

## Notes

- Snow patches persist on north-facing slopes through mid-September. Microspikes advisable.
- No water sources along the ridgeline. Carry a minimum of three liters.
- The trailhead parking lot fills by 7:30 AM on weekends. Arrive early or plan for a weekday attempt.
