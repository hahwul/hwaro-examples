Andrea del Verrocchio's bottega on the Via Larga produced more than panel paintings. It was a fully equipped foundry, a goldsmith's bench, a carpenter's shop, and a drawing school operating under one roof. A young apprentice taken into such a household learned the entire material vocabulary of the visual arts, from the grinding of azurite to the casting of bronze.

The hierarchy was strict but not rigid. A garzone began by sweeping floors and preparing pigments. After a year or two he might be permitted to gesso panels, then to lay in underdrawing, then to render the secondary figures of a master's composition. The work of the apprentice and the work of the master coexisted within a single canvas, distinguishable only to the trained eye.

The Baptism of Christ, now in the Uffizi, illustrates this practice clearly. The principal figures and the landscape were laid out by Verrocchio, but the kneeling angel on the left is generally attributed to the young Leonardo da Vinci, who entered the workshop around 1466. Vasari recounts that upon seeing Leonardo's contribution, Verrocchio resolved never to take up the brush again. The story is likely apocryphal, but it captures something true about the relationship: the apprentice could surpass the master without diminishing him.

The bottega system collapsed slowly over the following century, replaced by academies that separated drawing from craft and elevated the painter above the gilder, the carver, and the founder. Something was gained in prestige and lost in practical knowledge.
