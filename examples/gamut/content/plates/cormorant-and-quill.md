Cormorant & Quill made objects meant to outlast their makers, and the gamut
shows it. There is not a single bright on this shelf. Every color was mixed to
sit quietly under a hundred years of shelf light, to age with the paper instead
of against it, and to let the one true luxury on the page — gold foil, hand-laid
— do all the shouting.

<!-- more -->

The heart of the plate is Quill Sepia, the text ink for the private editions. It
is not black. The shop argued, correctly, that a reader spends hours with body
text and that a warm brown-black is kinder to the eye across a long evening than
a cold true black. Foxed Umber was mixed to match the tone old rag paper drifts
toward, so that a fresh reprint could be slipped among originals without a
seam showing.

Bindery Olive is the quiet workhorse: a cloth-stamping green deliberately dulled
so that gold foil struck over it read as light. The formula book records the
dulling as a ratio, not a color:

```ink
Bindery Olive — cloth stamping base
  chrome green ......... 5 parts
  Foxed Umber .......... 2 parts   (the dulling)
  binding varnish ...... to tack
strike hot; foil reads only if the green stays matte
```

A whole shelf of browns sounds monotonous until you see it working. Restraint,
here, is the entire point.
