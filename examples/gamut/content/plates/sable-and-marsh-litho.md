Sable & Marsh printed information, not decoration, and the gamut is engineered
for it. A county map has to carry roads, water, contours, township grids, and
six-point legend type all on one sheet without any of it fighting the rest. That
means the colors cannot be loud. Every hue on this shelf was desaturated on
purpose so that black type would always win, and so that four or five inks could
overlap on a dense page without turning to mud.

<!-- more -->

The formula book is precise to a fault.
Survey Green exists in two strengths — a full-ink boundary
version and a 30% tint for woodland fill — because the shop understood that the
same color at two densities does more work than two different colors.

Marsh Teal was the shop's quiet pride. It reads unmistakably as water even in a
black-and-white photostat, which mattered when maps were copied and recopied.
The reconstruction records the tint ladder rather than a single mix:

```ink
Survey Green — tint ladder for county sheets
  boundary line ...... 100%  (full ink)
  woodland fill ......  30%  (tinted screen)
  faint contour ......  15%
one pigment, three jobs; type stays black on all three
```

A gamut this muted looks dull in isolation and sings in service. That was always
the point.
