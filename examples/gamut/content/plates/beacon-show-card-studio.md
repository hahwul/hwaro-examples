Beacon dressed the counters of a big Atlanta department store, and the gamut is
pure optimism. These are the colors of a soda fountain on a Saturday: a pink
that tastes like strawberry syrup, a mint green for prices that never looked
like a bill, a lemon yellow that made a sale feel like a holiday. Even the dark
color is Root Beer, because a true black would have been rude in a room this
happy.

<!-- more -->

The studio's discipline hid inside its cheer. Cherry Pop, the urgent red, was
rationed: one card in ten was allowed to use it, so that "today only" still
meant something by the end of a season. Everything else was tuned to sit
together without clashing, which is harder with brights than with earths. The
trick was warmth. Every color on this shelf leans a few degrees toward warm,
even Mint Counter and Cornflower Sky, so the whole gamut feels lit by the same
afternoon.

The reconstructed card for a spring-sale starburst reads almost like a recipe
for a dessert:

```ink
Spring Starburst — 1961 sale card
  ground ...... Lemon Ticket, flat
  rays ........ Soda Pink over Cornflower Sky, wet-on-dry
  price ....... Mint Counter
  "SALE" ...... Cherry Pop  (rationed — one card in ten)
```

Beacon proves a bright gamut can still be edited. Cheer is not the same as
chaos.
