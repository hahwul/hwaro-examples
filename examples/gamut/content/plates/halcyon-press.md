Halcyon printed the rhythm of a working harbor. Six mornings a week the shop
turned out tide tables for the bar pilots, and every one of them was set in the
same stubborn Harbor Cyan 2B that the founder mixed himself and refused to buy
pre-canned. The pressmen complained it dried slow and skinned in the tin. It
also never once drifted between print runs, which is why the pilots trusted a
Halcyon tide chart over the newspaper's.

<!-- more -->

The rest of the gamut orbits that cyan. Deep Wharf is the same pigment pushed
toward night with a trace of lamp black, used for the heavy rules on regatta
bills. Kelp is the cyan again, warmed with chrome yellow on a cold slab so the
green never went muddy. Only Regatta Red and Cannery Cream came in as bought
inks, and the shop treated them like guests: Red for the one loud line on a
poster, Cream as a tint base that never printed at strength.

The reconstructed formula book lists the house cyan as a weight recipe, scaled
to a single pound of ink:

```ink
Harbor Cyan 2B  — 1 lb batch
  phthalo cyan base ........ 12 oz
  transparent white ........  3 oz
  cobalt drier .............  1 oz  (winter: 1.5 oz)
mix cold; grind three passes; skin forms in ~40 min
```

What makes Halcyon worth a plate is restraint. A whole business ran on six inks,
and five of them were arguments with the first one.
