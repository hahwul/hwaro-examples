Alma lettered the nightlife, and the gamut was tuned for a world lit by colored
bulbs. Colors that look correct in daylight go strange under a marquee, so this
shelf was mixed under the conditions it would live in: warm filament light for
theater work, blue and green neon spill for the clubs. Every hue is deep,
slightly grayed, and built to hold its character when the sun goes down and the
switches come on.

<!-- more -->

Midnight Indigo is the plate's foundation. Struck nearly black, it was the
standard ground for gilt lettering, because gold leaf reads richest against a
cool dark that is not quite black — a true black kills the shimmer. Stage Black
carries a deliberate violet undertone for the same reason. And Absinthe, the
green with a secret, was mixed to go quietly acid under blue neon, so a club sign
that looked tasteful by day turned electric by night.

The formula book treats gilt as a two-layer system, ground first:

```ink
Gilt lettering — marquee standard
  ground ...... Midnight Indigo, struck heavy, let skin
  size ........ slow oil, tacky at 12 hrs
  leaf ........ 23k, or Bourbon Gold if budget says so
  outline ..... Stage Black, one hair's width
never lay gold on true black; the shimmer dies
```

Orchid Smoke, the rarest tin, closes the set: a dusky violet the shop saved for
the fanciest nightclub cards, expensive to mix and never wasted.
