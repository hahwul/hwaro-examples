Gamut is a museum of pigment palettes reconstructed from imagined mid-century
print shops. Every shop here is a fiction. None of the tins were ever opened,
because none of them were ever filled. What is real is the discipline the
exhibits are drawn from: for most of the twentieth century, a print shop was
defined less by what it could imagine than by what it kept on the shelf.

## Why a gamut

A gamut is the finite set of colors a system can actually produce. A painter has
the whole spectrum; a print shop had a row of tins, a slab, a knife, and a
pressman's memory. Everything the shop ever made came out of that row. So the
palette was not decoration — it was the shop's entire vocabulary, its accent,
its limits, and its signature all at once. Two shops with the same six pigments
still printed differently, because a gamut is also a set of habits.

## How the reconstructions work

Each plate is assembled the way a restorer assembles a lost fresco: from the
edges inward. We start from the kinds of jobs a shop took — tide tables, seed
catalogs, nightclub cards — and reason backward to the inks those jobs demanded.
Then we tune each color the way the shop would have: under the light it printed
for, against the paper it printed on, and next to the other inks it lived
beside. The hex values are honest modern matches, sampled under daylight-balanced
light. The mixing notes are written in the voice of a formula book that a working
pressman would recognize.

## On restraint

The collection has a bias, and it is worth naming: it admires shops that did a
lot with a little. A whole business run on six inks, five of them arguments with
the first, is more interesting than a shelf of a hundred colors used once each.
Restraint is the through-line. It is also, not coincidentally, the through-line
of good design in any medium — which is the quiet reason a color museum belongs
in a gallery of websites.
