A logbook turned up last winter in a box of foundry correspondence nobody had catalogued past its folder label: "Misc. — bench notes." It belonged to a nineteen-year-old apprentice named Corliss Reyes, hired in March 1934 to learn the Vanderloo photolettering rig before anyone at the foundry would say out loud that the rig was meant to replace two Linotype operators.

<!-- more -->

Reyes kept the log the way apprentices are told to and rarely do: exposure times, lens settings, which negatives scratched under the enlarger lamp and which held. Six weeks in, the entries stop describing settings and start describing arguments — foremen who wouldn't let Reyes touch the machine in front of the union rep, a floor manager who filed the phototype proofs under "specimens" instead of "production" so the shop committee wouldn't ask what they were for.

The foundry's official 1934 annual report mentions the Vanderloo rig once, in a footnote about "experimental processes under evaluation." Reyes's log puts it running full shifts by May. The gap between the footnote and the log is the story: not that phototype arrived, but that the people running it were told, on the record, that it hadn't.

Reyes left the trade in 1937. The log went into a box. It sat there for eighty-nine years, filed under a label that was, in its own small way, also a kind of lie.
