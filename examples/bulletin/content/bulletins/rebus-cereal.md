In February 1951, a package designer at a Midwest cereal maker submitted a rebus mark for legal review: a picture of a bee, a picture of a wheat sheaf, and the numeral "4," meant to be read aloud as the brand name. The design had already been approved by marketing, tested on a regional test-market box, and scheduled for a national rollout that spring.

<!-- more -->

The rejection memo, four pages long and unusually candid for trademark counsel, still survives in the company's legal files. The attorney's objection wasn't aesthetic. Rebus marks, she wrote, could not be registered as trademarks in most jurisdictions because a mark has to function as a word — something a grocer or a rival could say and mean the same thing every time. A picture-puzzle invited a different reading from every customer who solved it differently, and some, she noted dryly, might not solve it at all.

The cereal shipped six months later under a conventional wordmark. But the memo circulated informally among design and legal departments at three other companies that year, according to a cover note stapled to a second copy found in an unrelated file. Rebus logos, briefly fashionable in postwar packaging, mostly disappeared from American shelves by 1954 — not by trend, but by counsel's memo.
