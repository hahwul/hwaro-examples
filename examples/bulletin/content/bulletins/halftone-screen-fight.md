Halftone screens turn a photograph into dots a press can print, and by the mid-1930s two suppliers were each claiming to own the angle. The dispute — technically a patent interference proceeding rather than a lawsuit, though it functioned like one — ran from 1935 to 1938 and turned on a question that sounds absurd stated plainly: could a company patent the number 45.

<!-- more -->

Both suppliers were producing halftone screens etched at a 45-degree angle relative to the paper's grain, a convention that minimized the visible moiré pattern where overlapping color screens crossed. One supplier held an earlier filing describing the angle in the context of single-color newspaper work; the other had filed a broader claim covering multi-color process printing, which is where the real money was.

The interference record — testimony, screen samples, engineers' affidavits about who had used which angle first and where — runs to several hundred pages and ends, after three years, in a negotiated cross-license rather than a ruling either side could claim as a win. Both companies kept selling 45-degree screens. So does nearly everyone else: the angle that two engraving suppliers spent three years litigating is, with only minor refinements, still the halftone default printers reach for today.
