The payroll ledgers are unglamorous documents — names, hourly rates, department codes — but they are exact in a way trade magazines from the same period never had to be. Between 1941 and 1949, eleven people are listed under "Drawing — Type," the department that produced finished letterforms for the foundry's production alphabets. Seven of the eleven names are women.

<!-- more -->

Cross-referencing those names against the foundry's own trade advertising, and against three independent type-trade magazines covering the same years, turns up exactly zero credits. Specimen sheets from the period name the alphabet, the foundry, and occasionally a "chief designer" — always a man, always someone with an executive title, rarely someone whose weekly hours appear in the drawing department's ledger at all.

This was not unusual practice; it was standard practice, and the ledgers make clear it was also known internally to be inaccurate. One 1946 memo, filed alongside a specimen proof, instructs the drawing department to route "all inquiries about authorship" to the sales office rather than answer them directly — an instruction that only makes sense if inquiries were expected and a different answer than the official one was anticipated.

None of the eleven names appear in any general type-history reference published since. The ledgers give a start: full names, department codes, and — in six cases — a home address, which is how three living descendants have since been located and asked what they remember.
