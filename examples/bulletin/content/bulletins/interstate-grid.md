Before the federal government settled on a single national standard for interstate signage in 1966, at least one state highway design office built and tested its own system — a full family of exit markers, mile posts, and directional panels, worked out on an eight-unit modular grid and fabricated for a closed test mile in 1962.

<!-- more -->

The system survives almost entirely on paper: grid sheets, a fabrication spec, and a set of black-and-white photographs of the test mile taken by the design office's own staff photographer, since the road itself was resurfaced and re-signed in 1969 and no physical example is known to remain. The grid sheets show a numeral set drawn specifically to stay legible from a moving vehicle at 60 miles per hour, tested — according to a field report stapled to the spec — by driving the test mile repeatedly at night with headlights only.

The federal standard adopted in 1966 shares almost nothing with this system beyond the modular logic itself. The design office's own closing memo, dated the same year, describes the program as "superseded" and recommends the fabrication molds be scrapped. Whether they were scrapped is not recorded. The grid sheets were filed, not discarded, which is the only reason this dispatch exists.
