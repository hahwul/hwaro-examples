House circulars — the internal newsletters type distributors mailed to their own sales representatives, not to customers — rarely survive, because nobody thought to keep them. One did: issue 33 of a Swiss-adjacent distributor's circular, dated March 1947, and it contains an unsigned essay arguing, in plain and slightly impatient prose, for what it calls a "cleared grotesque" — a sans serif stripped of the geometric quirks and stylized curves fashionable in the period's display faces.

<!-- more -->

The essay reads, with the benefit of hindsight, like a design brief for Neue Haas Grotesk, which the Haas foundry would not begin drafting until 1956 and would not release until 1957. It calls for even stroke weight, a large x-height, tight spacing "suited to the eye's new speed," and — this is the detail that makes the essay worth citing rather than just noting — a specific rejection of rounded terminals "in favor of the cut, honest ending."

There is no evidence the essay reached anyone at Haas. The distributor and the foundry were unrelated companies, and the circular's own masthead lists a print run of 40 copies, mailed to sales staff only. The essay's author is not named. What survives is the argument, made a decade early, filed in a company newsletter nobody outside the sales department was ever meant to read, and forgotten by everyone who might have acted on it sooner.
