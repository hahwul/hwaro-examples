Ember stone-grinds chile and pepper blends in batches of forty pounds, sold mostly to restaurants that need to know exactly how a blend will behave before they commit a dish to it. Their old labels used adjectives — "bold," "fiery," "for the brave" — which is marketing, not information, and chefs told them so.

<!-- more -->

## The brief

Replace the adjectives with something a line cook can read in two seconds under kitchen lighting: a heat rating, an origin, a grind size, and nothing else competing for attention.

## The system

We built a five-notch heat gauge as a single geometric glyph — a stepped arc, filled to a level, printed in one ink. It sits in the same corner on every tin regardless of blend, so a shelf of twelve Ember tins reads as a sortable chart rather than twelve separate designs. Below it, a fixed three-line spec block: blend name in Syne, origin in small caps, grind size as a numeral with a unit.

The wordmark keeps the geometry honest — a squared-off E built from the same stroke width as the gauge's arc, so the two elements feel cut from one tool rather than assembled from two projects. Tins print in a single spot color per heat level, which meant designing a five-color system that still reads as "one brand" from across a shop.

## The result

Ember's wholesale accounts now reorder by heat number instead of blend name — "send us six of the fours" — which is the kitchen shorthand we were aiming for. The origin card insert, a small provenance note that ships inside every tin, has become the thing customers photograph most for social feeds, entirely unplanned. Good information design travels on its own.
