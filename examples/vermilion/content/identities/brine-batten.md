Brine & Batten hand-packs mackerel, sprat, and smoked herring in oil, in runs small enough that a bad photograph of one catch would end up on tins for a whole season. Photography was the problem: it dated fast, cost more than the cannery could justify at their volume, and never looked consistent from batch to batch.

<!-- more -->

## The brief

Drop photography. Design a mark system that identifies each species clearly enough to shop by, ages well over a multi-year print run, and prints in one or two spot colors on curved tin stock without registration headaches.

## The system

We drew four species marks as reduced, geometric silhouettes — mackerel, sprat, herring, and a smoked variant marked with a second texture — each built on the same body curve so they read as a family from a shelf's width away. The lockup borrows its rhythm from batten siding on the harbor's fish houses: horizontal bands of alternating width, which became the label's structural grid before it became a decoration.

The wordmark sits inside a fixed batten frame that doesn't change size or position across the four species, so a case of mixed tins photographs as one coherent product line even though no two tins carry the same artwork. Printing runs one spot color per species plus a shared black, keeping the cannery's plate costs flat no matter how many varieties they add.

## The result

Brine & Batten added a fifth species — smoked bloater — eighteen months after launch and produced its mark in an afternoon using the system's construction rules, no studio involved. The retail shelf-strip, a small header card explaining the mark system to new customers, has been reprinted by three independent grocers who wanted to feature it at eye level. That's the kind of unplanned adoption a good system earns.
