Quill presses eleven single-varietal ciders a year from a forty-acre orchard, and every one of them used to look like a different company. Hand-lettered labels, three different bottle shapes, a logo that changed depending on which sign painter was free that month. The cidery had grown past the point where charm alone could carry it.

<!-- more -->

## The maker

Quill's founder, a former viticulturist, presses varieties most cideries blend away — Kingston Black, Yarlington Mill, Foxwhelp — and bottles them separately so drinkers can taste an orchard block instead of a formula. That specificity was the whole brand, if we could get out of its way.

## The system

The wordmark comes from a single quill stroke, tapered at both ends and cut at an angle that reads as a pressed apple row when it repeats. We drew it once at full weight and let a shared reduction rule generate every smaller lockup, so the mark never gets redrawn by hand again.

The label grid does the real work. Each varietal gets its own ink on the same die-cut shape: a horizontal band for the varietal name, a fixed coordinate for the orchard block number, and a pressing date printed in a monospaced numeral set that reads like a tasting note rather than a label footnote. Eleven ciders, one grid, zero improvisation.

## The result

Cases now ship with a printed matrix on the inside flap — varietal, block, press date, blend percentage — so retail buyers can build a flight without opening a single bottle. The tasting room repainted its sign to match the wordmark's cut angle within a month of the guidelines going out, unprompted. That's the test we use for whether a system actually took: does the client keep it moving after we leave the table.
