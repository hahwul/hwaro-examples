Cask & Coal smokes its chiles over mesquite before fermenting them, and sells four sauces that climb from a mild table sauce to a genuinely dangerous extract. Their first labels tried to sell the danger with skulls and flame graphics — the kind of hot-sauce cliché that makes every bottle on a shelf look identical to its neighbors.

<!-- more -->

## The brief

Build an identity confident enough to skip the clichés entirely, while still making the heat climb from bottle to bottle unmistakable to a first-time buyer standing at a market stall.

## The system

We put all the heat signaling into a single cap band, widened and printed in one of four flat colors keyed directly to Scoville range — no numbers required to read the order. The wordmark is set once, in heavy Syne, unchanged across all four sauces, so the brand reads as one confident mark rather than four competing label designs.

Body labels carry almost no ornament: sauce name, a one-line flavor note, and the cap-band color repeated as a small tab at the base for anyone restocking a shelf blind. The restraint is the point — next to a shelf of flame-and-skull hot sauce, four plain bottles with a bold wordmark and a color-coded cap read as the most confident thing in the aisle.

## The result

Cask & Coal's farmers-market booth now sells the top heat level, the one with almost no label copy at all, faster than the mild — customers say the plainness itself reads as a dare. The case display box, designed to fan all four caps forward in one glance, became the standard fixture request from every new retail account within its first season.
