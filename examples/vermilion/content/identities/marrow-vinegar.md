Marrow ages vinegar in retired bourbon and sherry barrels for anywhere from three to eight years, and the same barrel can produce four different bottlings as it's drawn down and topped up over time. Their old labels tracked none of that — a customer buying a three-year and an eight-year bottling had no way to tell which was which without asking.

<!-- more -->

## The brief

Design a system that makes barrel age, cask type, and bottling date legible at a glance, borrowing the visual authority of apothecary and chemist labeling without tipping into pastiche.

## The system

The batch number became the organizing device: barrel code, draw date, and age in years, set in a fixed monospace block that sits identically on every bottle regardless of vintage. The wordmark — an M built from two barrel-stave curves meeting at a point — appears once, small, at the label's foot, so the batch data can carry the visual weight instead of a logo shouting over it.

Wax dip caps are color-coded by cask type rather than age: amber for bourbon-finished stock, deep red for sherry casks. That single decision let retail buyers sort a case at a glance without reading a single number, while the batch block still gives connoisseurs everything they'd want to compare vintages.

## The result

Marrow's trade accounts now order by batch number over the phone — "the same amber as last time, next batch" — which cut order errors from mislabeled stock to nearly zero. The barrel tag set, small brass-look tags for the cellar itself, turned into an unplanned tour prop; visitors photograph the tags more than the finished bottles.
