The parking garage under Building 3 stops being a parking garage every second weekend of November. Cars get moved to the overflow lot two blocks over, and in their place come sixty rubber basins, a dozen folding tables, and roughly four hundred kilograms of napa cabbage — the annual kimjang of a single Incheon apartment complex that has, without any official organization or budget, been doing this for eleven years running.

"It started as three families," said Yoon Mi-ran, who keeps the group's group chat and, by informal consensus, keeps everyone roughly on schedule. "One year a fourth family smelled what we were doing and asked if they could join. Now it's whoever wants to. Some years it's twenty households. This year it's forty, and I genuinely don't know how we're going to fit everyone, but we say that every year."

The choreography, once you watch it for an hour, resolves into three stations working in overlapping shifts. At the salting station, halved cabbages sit submerged in brine for the better part of a day, weighted under basins of water so they don't float free. At the paste station — the loudest, most contested station, where recipes are argued over with real intensity — glutinous rice porridge gets mixed with gochugaru, fish sauce, minced garlic, ginger, julienned radish, and, depending entirely on whose family is running the table that hour, either salted shrimp or anchovy sauce or, in one household's controversial case, both. At the packing station, the paste gets worked between every leaf by hand, gloved but still red by the end of the day, before the finished heads go into plastic tubs destined for forty separate refrigerators.

> Every basin in this garage will feed someone through February. Nobody here thinks of that as a metaphor. It's just Tuesday dinner, four months from now, already decided.

Mi-ran's mother, who is technically retired from kimjang duty but shows up anyway every year to "supervise," put it more bluntly when I asked why forty households bother coordinating something each family could easily do alone in an afternoon. "Because kimchi made by one tired person tastes like one tired person," she said, elbow-deep in paste. "Kimchi made by forty people arguing about fish sauce tastes like forty people arguing about fish sauce. It's a better flavor. I can't prove it scientifically. I just know I'm right."

By Sunday evening the garage empties out, cars slowly returning to their spaces still faintly smelling of garlic and brine, and the group chat fills with photos of finished tubs stacked in refrigerators across the building. Someone always asks, every year, whether next November they should maybe scale it back. Nobody has ever said yes.
