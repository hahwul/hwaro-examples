The rooftop belongs to Han Bok-hee, though she'd tell you it really belongs to the jars. Forty onggi — some inherited from her mother-in-law, a few bought secondhand from a potter in Ulsan who is slowly going out of business because, as Bok-hee put it, "nobody wants to wait for anything anymore" — sit in loose rows facing southeast, catching the first sun and losing it early enough to avoid the worst of the afternoon heat.

Each jar is dated in chalk on its shoulder, the way a distillery might mark a cask. The oldest reads 2023.11.  That one holds doenjang that will not be touched until this coming November, when it turns three, and even then Bok-hee says she'll only use half of it and let the rest go another year. "People ask me the recipe," she said, lifting the lid on a younger jar to check the surface. "There isn't one. There's a ratio, and then there's just time. I can't sell you time."

The process starts in winter, when meju — blocks of boiled, mashed soybean — are shaped, hung to dry, and left to mold in a warm room until they're coated in a fur of white and faint yellow that would alarm anyone unfamiliar with the smell. By late January the meju go into brine inside the onggi, weighted under stones, and by March the liquid has darkened enough to separate: the liquid becomes ganjang, drawn off and boiled down; the solids left behind are mashed and salted again to become doenjang, sealed under a scatter of dried red chilies and charcoal that Bok-hee insists does something to the flavor, though she can't explain what beyond "it just does."

Gochujang follows its own math — glutinous rice, malt, red pepper powder, and meju powder, all wearing down slowly into paste under the sun — and demands even more patience, since Bok-hee won't call a batch finished before eight months regardless of how it tastes on day sixty. "Eight months is when it stops tasting sharp and starts tasting deep," she said. "Before that it's just spicy sugar water with ambition."

What struck me most, watching her work the lids on and off in sequence like a technician checking instruments, was how little intervention any of it actually needs. Her real skill is restraint — knowing which jar to leave alone, which one needs an afternoon uncovered to breathe, which one is a week from being ready and should not, under any circumstances, be opened early just to see. "Every jar I've ever ruined," she told me, resealing the last one, "I ruined by checking on it too much."
