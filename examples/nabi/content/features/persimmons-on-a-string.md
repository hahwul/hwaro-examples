By the second week of October, you can no longer see the front of Oh Jeong-hwan's shop. It disappears behind a curtain of persimmons, hundreds of them, each one hand-peeled and hung by its own stem from a string strung wall to wall under the eaves — a technique so specific to this stretch of Cheongdo that the county has trademarked the name gotgam the way Champagne is trademarked in France. Ordinary dried persimmon exists everywhere. This is not that. This is a forty-day process that turns a hard, astringent fruit into something closer to a fig, honeyed and dense, with a faint white bloom of sugar crystallizing on the skin by the time it's ready.

"You can buy persimmons anywhere in Korea," Jeong-hwan said, running a peeling knife around a fruit in one continuous ribbon without looking at his hands. "You cannot buy this wind anywhere else. That's the whole secret and I'm allowed to tell you because knowing it doesn't help you copy it."

The wind he means is a particular dry autumn current that funnels through the valley where Cheongdo sits, low humidity paired with daytime warmth and cold nights — a combination that lets the persimmons dry slowly enough to develop sugar rather than simply rotting, which is the fate of gotgam attempted almost anywhere else in the country. Growers here have tried explaining the science to visitors for decades; most give up and just point at the mountains.

The market lanes themselves become a kind of seasonal architecture project. Every shopfront strings its own curtain, and by late October the whole street reads as a single orange corridor, string after string swaying slightly whenever a truck passes. Buyers walk the length of it comparing color — too orange means not enough time has passed, a dusty amber-brown means it's close, and the ones already dusted white with natural sugar are the ones locals actually take home, usually at a premium the vendors don't apologize for.

> You cannot buy this wind anywhere else. That's the whole secret and I'm allowed to tell you because knowing it doesn't help you copy it.

Jeong-hwan's family has strung persimmons in this same alley for three generations, through a decade in the nineties when persimmon prices collapsed and half the growers switched to apples, and through a recent run of unusually wet Octobers that ruined two consecutive years of curtains before they'd properly set. He replanted nothing and switched nothing. "My grandfather used to say a bad year for gotgam is still gotgam weather next year," he told me, tying off a fresh string. "You don't quit a forty-day fruit because of one bad forty days."
