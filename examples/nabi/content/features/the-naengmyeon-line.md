There is a particular kind of patience reserved for cold noodles in July, and Ojang-dong has been testing it for sixty-one years. The counter seats fourteen. By eleven the line runs past the pharmacy next door, past the shuttered stall that used to sell fabric, and curls around toward the covered arcade where the market's ice vendor has already sold through his morning delivery and is waiting on a second truck.

"People assume it's about the noodles," said Choi Bang-im, who has ladled broth here since her twenties and is, by everyone's estimate including her own, closer to seventy than sixty now. "It's about the broth being cold enough. If the broth isn't cold, nothing else matters." The broth in question is a beef-and-radish stock cut with dongchimi brine, chilled overnight and finished table-side with a scoop of shaved ice that arrives, always, at the exact moment the bowl is set down — not before, or it dilutes; not after, or the customer has already started eating it warm and will complain.

Getting the ice right is its own small industry. The market's ice vendor, Suh Dae-gil, runs three deliveries a day in peak summer from a block-ice supplier in Guro, shaving each block by hand behind the noodle counter rather than trusting the machine shavers that most newer restaurants use. "Machine ice melts even," he said, demonstrating with a practiced scrape of his blade. "Hand-shaved ice melts unevenly, which sounds like a flaw until you realize it means the broth stays a slightly different temperature from bite one to bite forty. That's the whole trick. A bowl that tastes the same at the start and the end is a boring bowl."

The vinegar comes at the end, and only the end — a thin dark bottle set on every table, unlabeled, refilled from a jug kept behind the register. Regulars add it themselves, usually less than a first-timer expects to need, tipping the bottle for exactly one count before the next bite. Choi watches this ritual from behind the counter with something between pride and mild alarm. "New customers always pour too much," she said. "I let them. You only learn the right amount by pouring too much once."

By one in the afternoon the line hasn't shortened so much as replaced itself, one contented customer stepping out for every anxious one stepping in. Choi wipes down her station between orders in movements so economical they look choreographed, and when I asked if the summer crowds ever wear her down, she shrugged in a way that ended the conversation more efficiently than any answer could have. "Sixty-one years," she said. "The noodles get tired before I do."
