Park Yeong-sun has run the same three-meter stretch of tarp outside Dongmyo market for twenty-six years, and she still gets to the corner before the streetlights switch off. By five, she has already separated the *dallae* from the *naengi* — two wild alliums that look nearly identical under sodium light but taste nothing alike once you've had them both — and started building her pyramids: ssukgat in loose green coils, chwinamul bundled like sheaves of wheat, the first thin scallions of the year still wearing clumps of dark soil like a signature.

"People think spring starts when the cherry trees do," she told me, not looking up from a bundle of minari she was trimming with a paring knife worn to a sliver. "Spring starts here. The trees are just slow to hear about it."

I came back four mornings in a row to understand her sequence, because there is one, even if she'd never call it that. Monday it was dallae, sharp and mineral, good raw with soy and sesame oil and nothing else. By Thursday the naengi had turned sweeter, past its first-week bitterness, and she was recommending it blanched into a doenjang soup base rather than eaten fresh. "Every green has maybe ten good days," she said. "You have to know which day you're buying."

Her customers are a mix that tells you something about the market's second life: elderly regulars who've bought from her since her mother ran the stall in the eighties, and a newer crowd — mostly women in their thirties, phones out, photographing the tarp before they buy anything. She doesn't mind the photos. She minds people who squeeze the ssukgat to test freshness. "Smell it," she says, holding a bundle up without being asked. "Don't touch it. Smelling it is free. Touching it costs me the sale to the next person."

> Every green has maybe ten good days. You have to know which day you're buying.

By nine the tarp is half-empty and the tourists have mostly moved through toward the antique stalls further in. Yeong-sun starts a second, smaller pass — this one for herself, setting aside whatever she'll bring home for her own table. It's never the prettiest bundle. "The prettiest one sells itself," she said, dropping a slightly bruised handful of dallae into her own basket. "I want the one that tastes like I picked it myself."

We asked what she does with the greens nobody bought by closing. She laughed, the first real laugh of the morning. "Nothing goes home with me twice. If it's still here at noon, it goes in my soup, and my neighbors eat well tonight."
