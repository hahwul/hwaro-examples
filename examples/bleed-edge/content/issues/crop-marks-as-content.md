Every press sheet &mdash; the rectangular piece of paper that enters and exits the offset press &mdash; carries a set of small crosshairs at each corner. These are the **crop marks**, the guillotine operator's guide to where the trim should fall. They are printed in fluorescent orange (traditionally), in a corner of the sheet that will be cut away in the finishing stage.

The reader never sees crop marks. They exist in the margin of the sheet that will be discarded. They are a message from the pre-press operator to the bindery operator, conducted in ink, visible for the approximately forty-five minutes between press and guillotine.

## Preserving the Guide

Our compromise with this is simple: we print the crop marks *inside* the trim area. They appear on the finished page, as small orange crosshairs at each corner, 8mm from the trim edge. They serve no production function &mdash; the guillotine operator has no need of them, because they sit well within the safe area. They are purely a formal gesture.

The effect is that every page of every issue begins with four small marks of fluorescent ink. They are a reminder of the pre-press process, a signature of the production origin, and &mdash; we maintain &mdash; a genuinely useful navigation aid for the reader. The crop marks anchor the reader's eye at the corners of the page, which are normally ambiguous in a full-bleed layout.

> Without crop marks, the reader does not know where the page begins. With crop marks, the page has a frame. The frame is vestigial, but it is there.

## The Registration Mark

In addition to crop marks, traditional press sheets carry **registration marks** &mdash; small crosshairs inside circles, printed in all four process colours stacked atop one another, used by the press operator to confirm that all four plates are aligned. If the registration marks appear as a single crisp black circle, the plates are aligned. If they appear as a bleed of cyan, magenta, yellow, and black, they are not, and the press run must be stopped.

We print registration marks on the outer margins of every spread. They are slightly oversized &mdash; 28mm diameter, rather than the standard 8mm &mdash; and positioned at the midpoint of the outer trim on each page. They are, again, not functional: our plates are always in register, or we would not ship the issue. The registration marks are a gesture, the visible signature of a production process that is normally invisible to the reader.

### The Consequence

A reader picking up BLEED EDGE encounters a page that appears, at first glance, to be a raw press proof. It has crop marks, registration marks, a bleeding image that runs to the trim, and a folio number set along the outer edge. Only after a moment's inspection does the reader realise: this is not a proof. This is the finished object. The proof is the finished object.

This conflation &mdash; of pre-press, press, and post-press into a single finished artefact &mdash; is the editorial project of the magazine.
