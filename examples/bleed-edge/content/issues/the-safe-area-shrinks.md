Within the trim of every page sits a smaller rectangle: the **safe area**. This is the region of the page in which text is guaranteed to appear on every copy, regardless of trim drift, binding variation, or guillotine wander. Commercial magazines draw the safe area 12mm in from the trim on every side.

## The Contest

Our editorial question, issue after issue: *how small can the safe area become before text is no longer safe?*

We have pushed the safe area inward in each of the last five issues. Issue 003 retained a standard 12mm safe area. Issue 004 reduced it to 10mm. Issue 005 reduced it to 8mm, and we began to see cut text in perhaps 3% of shipped copies. Issue 006 reduced it to 6mm; cut text appeared in 14% of copies. The current issue returns to 10mm, not because we have lost conviction but because our subscribers asked us to.

> A magazine in which 14% of copies have cut text is not a failure of production. It is the declared intent of the layout.

### Where the Cut Text Goes

In the copies where the safe area is violated by trim drift, the cut text falls along the outer edges of the page. It is never cut in the centre. The copy remains readable, but the reader encounters the truncated text as a production event &mdash; a visible failure, or a visible feature, depending on the reader's orientation.

We have begun to encourage this reading by setting footnote text and folio marks in the outer 4mm of every page. These elements are designed to be partially cut. They survive in some copies, are truncated in others, and are cut off entirely in a small minority. The result is that every reader's copy is slightly different; the magazine is an edition, but the edition has variance.

## Against Safety

The safe area is a production convention. It is not a design principle. Every magazine ever printed has had a safe area, because every magazine has been cut by a guillotine whose tolerance is not zero. The safe area is the concession the designer makes to the bindery.

Our editorial position: this concession has become a habit, and the habit has become a rule, and the rule now governs layout decisions that have nothing to do with production tolerance. A design can choose to accept some variance across copies. A design can use trim drift as an expressive tool. The safe area is a floor, not a law.

### The New Safe Area

We define a new safe area, which we call the **editorial safe area**. It is drawn 24mm from the trim &mdash; generously inside the traditional safe area &mdash; and contains only the editorial spine of the article: the headline, the first paragraph, and the author's byline. Everything outside the editorial safe area is at risk of being cut.

Most of the magazine sits outside the editorial safe area.
