Three millimetres. Every bleed specification in every print shop in every country begins and ends with three millimetres. Sometimes 5mm, sometimes 2mm, but the consensus is three. Three millimetres of ink, beyond the trim line, that will never be seen in any finished copy.

## Why Three

The math is simple. A commercial guillotine operates within a tolerance of &plusmn;1.5mm when cutting a 500-sheet stack. To guarantee that no untrimmed white edge appears along any side of any finished copy, the printed area must extend at least 1.5mm past the trim on every side. Doubled, for safety, this becomes 3mm. The industry has agreed to double the tolerance, so that bindery mistakes become invisible.

## What Three Millimetres Hides

When you specify a 3mm bleed, you are acknowledging &mdash; explicitly, in ink &mdash; that your page is not what you think it is. The page is a nominal rectangle: 210mm by 297mm. The printed area is a larger rectangle: 216mm by 303mm. The difference is the bleed, the margin of uncertainty, the acknowledgement that between the press and the finished book there will be a knife, and the knife will not cut where you ask it to.

The 3mm bleed is, in this sense, a form of honesty. It says: *I know that the trim is not fixed. I am printing over where I think the trim will be. When the blade falls, it will fall somewhere within this band of over-ink, and the result will be acceptable.*

> Every professional print job carries its own uncertainty in ink. The bleed is a declaration, not a decoration.

### The 3mm That Is Not Printed

In a traditional magazine, the 3mm outside the trim is discarded as trim waste. It is ink on paper that is, by design, thrown away. Every copy of every publication in every language contains, along its four edges, an invisible border of intentional waste. This waste is what makes the finished page possible.

## Our Departure

At BLEED EDGE, we do two additional things with the bleed area:

1. We print intentional content in the bleed. Page numbers, issue identifiers, registration marks, and occasional typographic fragments are set in the 3mm trim zone. Some are cut through; some survive; none are guaranteed.
2. We sometimes do not trim at all. Special copies of each issue are delivered untrimmed, with a raw deckled edge and all 3mm of the bleed visible. These copies are labelled DECKLE and sold separately.

This is a rhetorical move as much as a production one. We are asserting that the bleed is not waste &mdash; it is the page, and the trim is a choice the finisher makes on our behalf.
