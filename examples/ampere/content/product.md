Ampere ships as a single hardware unit, the module, and a base plate that
holds the inverter, the battery-management electronics, and the network
radio. Everything after the first module is purely mechanical: lift, seat,
lock, done. There is no rewiring event hiding at module three or module five.

## The module

Each module is a sealed lithium iron phosphate pack rated for 5.2 kWh of
usable capacity. LFP chemistry gives up a little energy density compared to
nickel-based cells, and we take that trade gladly — it does not thermally
run away, it tolerates a full state-of-charge sitting in a hot garage, and it
is rated for more cycles than a home battery will see in twenty years of
daily use.

| Spec | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Usable capacity | 5.2 kWh per module |
| Chemistry | Lithium iron phosphate (LFP) |
| Continuous output | 3.8 kW per module |
| Peak surge (10s) | 7.2 kW per module |
| Round-trip efficiency | 96% |
| Cycle life | 6,000 cycles to 80% capacity |
| Dimensions | 52 × 42 × 18 cm |
| Weight | 41 kg |
| Ingress rating | IP65, garage or exterior rated |

## Stack sizes

Most households land on three or four modules. We still sell the extremes,
because a one-module stack behind a small apartment's mini-split is a
perfectly reasonable thing to want, and a six-module stack behind a house
running a heat pump and a home office through every storm is too.

| Stack | Modules | Usable capacity | Continuous output | Typical home |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Starter | 1 | 5.2 kWh | 3.8 kW | Studio or small apartment |
| Household | 3 | 15.6 kWh | 11.4 kW | Three-bed home, evening cooking load |
| Full stack | 4 | 20.8 kWh | 15.2 kW | Solar home targeting evening self-sufficiency |
| Whole-house | 6 | 31.2 kWh | 22.8 kW | Heat pump plus home office, storm backup |

## What is in the base

The base module is where the intelligence lives. It carries the hybrid
inverter, a cellular and Wi-Fi radio for the monitoring app, and the
battery-management board that balances every cell across every module above
it. Because the electronics do not live inside each module, a stack upgrade
never touches wiring you have already commissioned — the new module reports
to the same base the moment it locks into place.

## Noise and heat

There is no fan under normal load. The module dissipates through a finned
rear panel, passively, and the only moving part in the entire stack is a
small compressor-style fan in the base that spins up under sustained peak
output — rare in a residential load profile. Measured at one metre, a
four-module stack under typical evening discharge reads at 30 dB, quieter
than a running refrigerator.
