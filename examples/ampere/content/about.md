Ampere started as a frustration with two products that never seemed to talk
to each other: a home battery sized once at install and never again, and a
utility bill that changed shape every year as rates, appliances, and weather
shifted underneath it. We wanted a battery that could grow the way a house
actually grows — a room at a time.

## The shelving idea

The founding insight was almost embarrassingly literal. Home batteries are
usually a single sealed cabinet, sized for the installer's best guess at your
future needs, bolted to a wall once and left alone for a decade. We looked at
that problem the way you would look at a closet that is always either too
big or too small, and asked why nobody had built a battery you could shelve
like storage — add a unit when the need is real, not when a sales estimate
guessed it might be.

That is the whole mechanical premise of the product: one rail, rated for a
full stack from day one, and modules that lock into place without a
electrician re-touching a single wire that was already commissioned.

## Appliance-grade, on purpose

We borrow the phrase "appliance-grade" deliberately. A refrigerator does not
ask for your attention. It does not have a companion app that pushes weekly
engagement notifications, and its industrial design does not try to look
faster than it is. We hold the stack and the monitoring app to the same
standard: calm, legible numbers, and a design that a home inspector — not
just an early adopter — recognizes instantly as hardware built to be lived
with, not admired.

## Where the team came from

The founding engineers came out of grid-scale battery storage and
residential HVAC manufacturing, two industries that rarely trade notes but
share a hard requirement in common: whatever you build has to survive a
technician who is tired, a customer who is annoyed, and twenty years of
weather. We designed every mechanical connection in the stack — the rail,
the latch, the base plate — to be serviceable by a competent electrician on
the first visit, without a factory callback.
