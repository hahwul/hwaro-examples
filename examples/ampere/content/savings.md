The number on our homepage — 87% of your evening, off the grid — is a
median, not a promise, and we would rather show the model behind it than
ask you to trust a headline.

## What counts as "evening"

We define evening as the window between your solar production falling below
your household's base load and the point where your utility's cheapest
overnight rate begins, typically 5pm to 11pm. It is the window every
time-of-use rate structure prices highest, because it is when grid demand
peaks across every home on your street at once, solar or not.

## The model

For a four-module stack (20.8 kWh usable) behind a 6 kW rooftop array, we
track how much of that evening window's load the stack covers before
falling back to grid import, across a full year of weather. The 87% figure
is the median across our installed base of comparable systems; individual
results move with household load and local sun hours.

| Percentile | Evening coverage |
| --- | --- |
| 25th | 74% |
| Median | 87% |
| 75th | 95% |

## What that looks like on a bill

Using average time-of-use rates from our installed regions, a household
covering 87% of its evening load avoids most peak-rate import for roughly
26 evenings out of 30. Below is a representative monthly comparison for a
four-module stack against the same household's pre-Ampere bill.

| | Before | After |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Peak-rate import (evening) | 142 kWh | 18 kWh |
| Off-peak import (overnight) | 96 kWh | 104 kWh |
| Monthly bill, typical utility | $187 | $61 |

## Payback

At current hardware and installation pricing, a four-module stack paired
with existing solar typically reaches payback in six to eight years,
depending on local rates and any utility or state incentives applied at
purchase. The battery is warrantied for ten years and rated past that in
cycle life, so payback is not the end of the savings — it is roughly the
midpoint.

## Storm and outage days are not in this number

The 87% figure is a normal-weather, grid-connected model. On days the grid
goes down, the stack switches to backup mode automatically and the savings
math stops mattering — the number that matters then is which circuits stay
lit, which is a question the [installation](/installation/) team answers
per household during the site review.
