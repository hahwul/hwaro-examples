# Quantum Phase Transition at Millikelvin Temperatures

**Experiment ID:** SZ-2025-003
**Temperature:** 50 mK (0.050K)
**System:** Rubidium-87 atomic cloud
**Phase:** Bose-Einstein Condensate

## Abstract

We have successfully cooled a cloud of rubidium-87 atoms to 50 millikelvin using a combination of laser cooling and evaporative cooling in a magnetic trap. At this temperature, the atoms undergo a quantum phase transition into a Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC), a state of matter where quantum effects become macroscopically observable.

<!-- more -->

## Cooling Sequence

### Stage 1: Laser Cooling (Doppler Cooling)
Initial temperature: ~300K (room temperature)
Final temperature: ~100 μK
Method: Six-beam optical molasses

The atomic vapor is slowed by counter-propagating laser beams tuned slightly below the atomic resonance frequency. This preferentially absorbs photons from atoms moving toward the laser source, creating a velocity-dependent damping force.

### Stage 2: Evaporative Cooling
Initial temperature: ~100 μK
Final temperature: 50 mK
Method: Magnetic trap with RF knife

The coldest atoms are preferentially removed from the trap, allowing the remaining atoms to rethermalize at progressively lower temperatures. This process is analogous to the cooling of hot coffee through evaporation of the hottest molecules.

## Critical Temperature Observation

At approximately 170 nK, we observed the critical temperature for BEC formation. Below this threshold, a sharp peak in the center of the velocity distribution appears, signaling the onset of macroscopic quantum coherence.

### Visual Signature

The condensate appears as a dense central core in absorption imaging, surrounded by a thermal cloud of normal atoms. The bimodal distribution is a clear signature of the quantum phase transition.

## Quantum Coherence

The condensate exhibits:
- **Phase coherence** over macroscopic distances
- **Matter-wave interference** patterns
- **Collective oscillations** (breathing modes)
- **Zero viscosity** (superfluidity)

## Environmental Conditions

The dilution refrigerator maintained exceptional thermal stability:
- Temperature fluctuation: < 1 mK
- Vibration isolation: Critical for magnetic trap stability
- Vacuum pressure: < 10⁻¹¹ torr
- Frost-free observation windows: Maintained above 4K

## Theoretical Implications

This experiment confirms quantum mechanical predictions at macroscopic scales. The atoms lose their individual identities and behave as a single quantum entity, described by a collective wavefunction.

## Applications

Understanding BEC formation is crucial for:
- Quantum simulation of condensed matter systems
- Precision atomic interferometry
- Development of quantum computers
- Tests of fundamental physics
