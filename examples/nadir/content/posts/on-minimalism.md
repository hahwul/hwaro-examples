The Japanese concept of Ma refers to the space between things. Not empty space -- meaningful space. The pause between notes that gives music its rhythm. The white space on a page that gives text room to breathe. The silence between words that allows understanding to form.

## Subtraction as Method

Most creative processes are additive. We build, we layer, we accumulate. Minimalism inverts this. The question is never "what can I add?" but always "what can I remove?"

A wall painted white is not the absence of color. It is a deliberate choice -- the selection of every wavelength of visible light simultaneously. White is not nothing. White is everything at once.

Black is different. Black is the absorption of all light. Nothing returns to the eye. In a pure black environment, the visual cortex has no data to process. The mind fills in what it can, but mostly it rests.

## The Last Reduction

There is a point beyond which minimalism becomes erasure. The challenge is to find the boundary -- the precise point where one more removal would destroy meaning. That edge is where the most interesting work happens.

A single period on a black page. Is it minimalism or is it nothing? The answer depends entirely on whether someone intended it to be there.

## Practical Darkness

This site is an exercise in that boundary. Pure black backgrounds. Gray text. No images, no decoration, no color. Every element present has earned its place by being irreducible. What you are reading is what remains after everything unnecessary has been taken away.
