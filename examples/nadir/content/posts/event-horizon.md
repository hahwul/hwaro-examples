The event horizon of a black hole is not a surface. There is no wall, no membrane, no edge you could touch. It is a mathematical boundary -- the radius at which the escape velocity equals the speed of light. Cross it, and the geometry of spacetime itself bends so severely that all paths lead inward.

## Falling In

An observer falling through an event horizon would not notice the moment of crossing. There is no sign, no sensation, no discontinuity. Space and time continue smoothly. The laws of physics do not change. From the inside, everything appears normal.

It is only from the outside that the crossing is remarkable. Light from the falling observer redshifts toward infinity. Their image dims, reddens, and eventually fades below the threshold of detection. They do not disappear suddenly. They fade.

## Information Paradox

What happens to information that crosses the event horizon? Quantum mechanics insists that information cannot be destroyed. General relativity insists that nothing escapes a black hole. Both theories are correct within their domains. At the event horizon, they contradict each other.

This contradiction has occupied theoretical physicists for decades. Hawking proposed that black holes emit radiation -- that they slowly evaporate over timescales longer than the current age of the universe. If this is true, the information must be encoded in that radiation somehow. But no one has proven how.

## The View From Inside

If you could survive the tidal forces and look outward from within a black hole, you would see the entire future history of the universe compressed into a bright ring above you. Stars would be born, burn, and die in fast-forward. Galaxies would collide and merge. The cosmic microwave background would blueshift into visibility.

All of time, visible at once, from inside a point of no return.
