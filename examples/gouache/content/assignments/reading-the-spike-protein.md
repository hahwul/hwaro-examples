Vector Quarterly's public-health desk wanted a sidebar that could stand alone from the article — something a reader could understand from the images even if they skipped every word of text, since the piece was going to run alongside a much longer feature on vaccine hesitancy.

<!-- more -->

I broke the mechanism into four beats rather than trying to fit it into one busy diagram: the lipid particle arriving at a cell, the cell's own machinery reading the instructions inside it, a spike protein taking shape on the cell surface, and finally an antibody locking onto that same shape from a training run months later. Each panel got one visual idea and one label, no more.

The hardest note to satisfy was scientific accuracy without a maze of arrows — the reviewing immunologist flagged an early draft for implying the antibody formed instantly rather than after a genuine immune response, so panel four was redrawn as a flashback framing device, set slightly apart from the first three with a dashed border, to signal "later" without another paragraph of caption. The hexagonal spike lattice became the piece's recurring motif, echoed at small scale in the section's running head so a reader flipping past could tell at a glance which explainer they were looking at.
