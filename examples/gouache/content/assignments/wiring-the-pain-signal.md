Cortex Monthly's feature was about chronic pain outliving its original injury, and the neuroscientist consulting on the piece kept returning to one idea in our calls: the signal is a relay, not a straight wire, and every relay point is a place treatment can intervene.

<!-- more -->

I drew the pathway as a single continuous line from a fingertip, up the arm, through the spinal cord, and into the brain, rather than the usual cutaway body diagram with labels scattered around it. The line thickens slightly at each real synapse — the dorsal horn, the thalamus — so the relay points read as deliberate pauses rather than just anatomy. Everything else in the drawing, the arm, the torso, the skull, is reduced to a thin cerulean contour so it recedes and the signal path stays the only heavy mark on the page.

The consulting neuroscientist's main correction was to the spinal cord crossing: an early draft had the signal switching sides too early, which a specialist reader would have caught immediately. Cortex Monthly runs a fact-check pass with outside experts on every science diagram before print, and this was exactly the kind of small structural error that pass exists to catch — worth the extra day it added to the schedule.
