Periapsis runs an eclipse cover every few years and their art director was blunt about the risk: readers have seen the black-disc-with-a-ring image so many times it barely registers as remarkable anymore. The brief asked for something that looked at the event sideways.

<!-- more -->

I spent a week with corona photographs from past totality events, tracing the plasma streamers rather than the disc itself — the fine, uneven filaments that fan out unevenly depending on where the sun is in its activity cycle. Those streamers became the whole cover: no visible horizon, no crowd of onlookers, just the corona rendered in cerulean ink against true black, wrapping from the front around the spine to the back so a reader turning the issue over in their hands keeps finding more of it.

The masthead sits inside a gap in the streamers rather than on a solid band, which took three rounds of hand-lettering adjustments to keep legible without boxing it in. The art director's favorite detail, and the one that made the final cut over a tighter alternate, was a single stray streamer breaking the masthead's baseline — small enough not to hurt legibility, loose enough to keep the whole cover from feeling engineered.
