Substrate's science editor sent over a 4,200-word feature on midwater trawl surveys with a note: "no submarine windows, no diver in a cage — we want the reader to feel like the light itself is rare." That single line did more work than the rest of the brief combined.

<!-- more -->

The reference folder was mostly grainy ROV footage and a handful of preserved specimens photographed under lab light, none of which look anything like the animal in its own element. I built the opener instead from behavior: an anglerfish's lure held out ahead of its body like a lantern on a pole, a scatter of siphonophores trailing below in a loose spiral, marine snow falling in long diagonal streaks to give the black field some sense of depth and current.

Color stayed to a single value of cerulean against near-black ink, with the lure and a thin rim of light along the fish's flank left as the only warm break in the page. The art director's one revision request was to make the lure brighter and everything else darker — a fair note, since the first pass had let ambient blue creep in where the brief wanted true dark. Final art shipped as a bled double spread with the feature's headline set into the black at the gutter, so the type reads like it is floating in the same water as the fish.
