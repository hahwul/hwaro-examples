The reporting behind this piece followed one reef survey site through a bleaching event over eleven weeks, and the writer's first instinct was a before-and-after photo pair. The trouble was that the two site photos, taken months apart under different light, told a weaker story side by side than the field data did.

<!-- more -->

I proposed a sequence instead: the same branching coral drawn three times across the spread, losing color panel by panel rather than jumping straight from full to bleached. The trick was keeping the colony's actual branch structure identical across all three drawings, tracing the first one and reusing the linework so the eye reads it as one organism changing, not three different corals. Only the fill drops out, stage by stage, against the same paper-white ground.

The science editor asked for a fourth panel showing recovery, which the survey data did not actually support at that site — a good reminder that the honest version of a chart is sometimes the version without the ending the reader wants. We agreed on a short caption instead, noting that recovery is possible under some conditions but was not observed here. The restraint of the palette, plain paper against a single flat ink line, ended up doing more to make that caption land than another drawing would have. It ran two issues after [Into the Midnight Zone](@/assignments/into-the-midnight-zone.md), the desk's other water piece that quarter.
