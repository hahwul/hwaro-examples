Gouache is the working name of Noa Lindqvist, an illustrator who has spent the last nine years drawing the parts of science stories that photographs cannot reach: the inside of a cell, the far side of an eclipse, the pressure at the bottom of a trench. Trained as a printmaker and later pulled sideways into editorial work, the studio now takes on four to six commissions a month, mostly for science and health desks that need a single strong image to carry a reader past a headline.

## Process

Every job starts with the art director's brief and, where one exists, the paper the story is based on. I read past the abstract — the interesting detail is usually in the methods section or a throwaway line in the discussion. Roughs go back within two to three days as loose graphite thumbnails; once a direction is approved, final art is built in layered gouache and colored pencil, scanned flat, and cleaned up digitally without smoothing away the brush texture. Color is decided early and held to two or three flat values per piece — restraint reads better at thumbnail size than a full palette does.

## Clients

Recurring desks include *Substrate*, *Periapsis*, *Vector Quarterly*, and *Cortex Monthly*, alongside occasional cover work for university press science trade books. Turnaround is typically two weeks for a spread and four to five days for a single explainer panel, rush fees apply under that. A running sample of the work is kept on the [assignments wall](@/assignments/_index.md).

## Get in touch

Commissions, licensing, and print requests go to the studio inbox linked from each assignment page. I keep a short waitlist during conference and cover-art season (roughly September through November) and I am upfront about it rather than letting a brief go quiet.
