Cinnabar is the hot shop of Marek Ferro, run out of a converted radiator-repair garage on the edge of town. The building came with a concrete floor rated for heavy machinery and a bay door tall enough to move a furnace shell through — both, it turns out, matter more than anything else on a lease application when the thing you are installing burns propane around the clock.

The shop takes its name the way a lot of hot shops take theirs: from the color at the far end of the working range. Cinnabar is the mineral, a red-orange ore of mercury sulfide that miners once ground to make vermilion pigment. It has nothing to do with glass chemistry. It has everything to do with what a fresh gather looks like coming out of the furnace door — that exact shade, for about four seconds, before it starts to cool toward amber.

## The furnace

The day tank holds about 120 kilograms of clear glass at 1100°C and has not been shut down since it was relit after the move, apart from one week in the second year for a replacement crown. A second, smaller furnace — the glory hole — sits beside it purely for reheating: pieces go back into it dozens of times over the course of a build, since worked glass loses its temper to the air in under a minute.

Keeping a furnace at temperature costs money whether or not anyone is blowing that day, which is the practical argument for the log this site is built around. If the gas bill runs the same either way, the pieces might as well be documented properly on the way through.

## How the days run

Mornings start with a walk of the annealer — checking overnight schedules finished clean, pulling anything ready to come out, loading the day's blanks for later. Blowing happens from mid-morning through mid-afternoon, when the light in the shop is easiest to judge color by. Afternoons are cold work: grinding punty marks, sandblasting, packing finished pieces, and writing up whichever entries from the week haven't made it into the log yet.

Visitors are welcome by arrangement — the shop is small and the furnace does not pause for a tour, so it works best to come during an active gather rather than ask for one to be started. The easiest way in is a Saturday open bench, held most weeks when a build is running; check the newest entry in the [vessel log](/vessels/) to see what is currently on the bench.
