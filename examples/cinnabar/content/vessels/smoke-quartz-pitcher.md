The tallest piece to come off this bench in over a year, and the first serious build of the new furnace cycle after the annual crown inspection put the shop a week behind schedule. Worth the wait for the smoke-gray batch, which had been sitting untested in the color rack since a supplier sample arrived back in the autumn.

<!-- more -->

Three separate gathers went into the body, each one added and fully fused before the next, building volume gradually rather than trying to pull it all from a single gather the way the smaller pieces in the log usually go. Between each addition the piece went back to the glory hole for a full reheat and a careful block on the marver to keep the wall thickness consistent — a tall form like this fails most often from an uneven wall rather than from any single mistake in the shaping.

The spout was pulled directly from the rim with a pair of jacks once the final shape was blown and paddled to volume: a firm pinch at one point on the lip, drawn out and slightly upturned before it could set. The handle came from a fourth, smaller gather of the same smoke-gray batch, pulled into a rope on the marver, attached hot at the shoulder, curved down, and joined again at the base — the two-point attachment is what keeps a handle this long from sagging before the glass sets.

Transferred to the punty at the foot, cracked off the blowpipe end, and the opening cleaned up cold on the lathe the next morning before going back for a fire-polish pass. Eighteen hours in the annealer, the longest schedule in the log so far, which the mass and wall thickness of a three-gather piece this size genuinely earns. Came out of the lehr with a base flat enough to stand without a wobble, which on a piece this tall is its own small victory.
