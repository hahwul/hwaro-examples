The furnace had been idling low over the holiday, and the first gather back was noticeably stiffer than usual — it took two extra passes through the glory hole before the glass would move the way it should. That stiffness turned out to work in the tumbler's favor.

<!-- more -->

Gathered a single bubble of amber batch on a preheated blowpipe and marvered it into a true cylinder on the steel plate before the first breath. Rather than blocking it perfectly round, it went straight into the optic mold — a ribbed cast-iron sleeve that presses shallow vertical grooves into the parison without cutting all the way through the wall. One firm blow, one quarter turn, and the ribs were set before the glass had time to relax back to smooth.

From there it was standard tumbler shaping: block, reheat, jack line at the base to define where the foot would separate, reheat again, and a long steady blow on the bench to bring the diameter up to eight centimeters while keeping the wall thickness even. The ribs stayed crisp through all of it, which is the part that does not always survive reheating — too much time in the glory hole and the pattern softens back toward round.

Cracked off clean at the jack line, no chips, straight into the annealer at 516°C. Eleven hours on the standard tumbler schedule: forty-five minutes at temperature, then a ramp down of about 38°C per hour until the lehr reaches room temperature and the door can be opened without shocking the glass. Came out with the ribs still sharp under a thumbnail and a warm amber that reads almost gold when the light comes through it low, the way January light does.
