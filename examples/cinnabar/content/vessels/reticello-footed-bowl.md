Reticello is a cane technique borrowed from the Venetian tradition, and it is the slowest build that happens at this bench by a wide margin — a bowl this size costs most of a working day before it ever touches the annealer, and almost all of that time is preparation rather than blowing.

<!-- more -->

The pattern starts the day before, off the furnace entirely: alternating rods of clear and white cane, pulled thin and cut to length, laid side by side on a graphite plate and fused at low heat into a single flat cane sheet with a striped face. Two sheets get made — one striped left-to-right, one right-to-left — each rolled into an open cup shape on a separate blowpipe.

The two cups are gathered together, one dropped inside the other with the stripes running opposite directions, and reheated as a unit. Where the two layers meet, the crossing stripes trap a fine grid of air at every intersection — that grid is the entire point of reticello, and it only appears if the two cups are exactly the same wall thickness and go into the glory hole at exactly the same time. A few degrees of difference and one layer moves before the other, and the lattice comes out lopsided.

Once fused into one wall, the piece was shaped into a shallow bowl on the bench, opened wide with the jacks and paddled flat at the rim. A separate small gather formed the foot: pulled, flattened, and attached hot underneath before the final reheat. Fifteen hours in the annealer at a slower ramp than usual, since the double wall holds heat differently than a single-gather piece. The lattice held its grid the entire way around — no gaps, no doubled lines, which does not happen on every attempt.
