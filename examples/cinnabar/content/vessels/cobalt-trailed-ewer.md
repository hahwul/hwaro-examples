Trailed decoration looks simple from the audience side of the bench — a thin line of color wound around a clear form — and is one of the least forgiving techniques in the shop, because the trail has to go on at almost exactly the same temperature as the body it is landing on, or it either slides or shatters the surface with thermal shock.

<!-- more -->

First gather was clear, blocked into a rough bulb and set aside on the bench stand while a rod of cobalt cane went into the glory hole to soften. Trailing itself happens fast: the cane tip touches the shoulder of the piece, the blowpipe turns steadily on the bench arms, and the trail winds down the body in a single unbroken spiral, pinched off at the base with tweezers. Too slow a turn and the coils overlap; too fast and they spread too thin to read.

Once the trail was on, the whole piece went back to the glory hole for a full, even reheat — this is the step that either welds the color into the surface as one smooth wall or leaves a ridge that will catch on everything afterward. It came out smooth. From there, standard ewer shaping: second gather to build volume, blocked and shaped into a teardrop body, then a third small gather pulled and flattened at the rim into a pouring spout with the jacks.

The handle is its own short build — a fourth-run gather of matching cobalt, pulled into a thick rope on the marver, attached hot at both ends, and shaped into a loop before it set. Cracked off the punty and into the annealer for sixteen hours; the extra volume in the handle and spout junctions needs the slower ramp to cool without stress cracking at the joins. Both survived clean.
