Sulfur-struck yellows are a nuisance to work with and everyone at this bench keeps making them anyway, because nothing else in the color rack sits at that exact acid-bright end of the amber range. The chemistry is genuinely called striking: the color is not fully developed straight out of the pot and needs a controlled reheat cycle to come up to full saturation.

<!-- more -->

Gathered clear first, then dipped into a crushed sulfur-based color frit on the marver and reheated in the glory hole to fuse the frit into an even casing over the clear core. This first reheat is also the striking step — held in the heat a beat longer than a normal color pickup, watching the surface shift from a dull ochre toward the fuller yellow before pulling it back out. Held too long and the color can strike past yellow into a muddy brown; pulled too soon and it stays pale.

Once the casing struck true, a single narrow band of dark red cane went on in an encalmo join — a technique where two different colored gathers are fused together along one clean seam rather than blended, so the border between them stays sharp instead of graduating. The join has to happen while both sections are near-identical in temperature, or the seam wanders.

Shaped into a simple round ornament on the bench, small enough to finish in one heat cycle after the color work, and finished off the punty with a slightly flared opening rather than a fully closed sphere — easier to hang, and it lets the light move through the casing instead of just reflecting off it. Nine hours in the annealer, the shortest schedule in the log so far, appropriate for a piece this size.
