The ferry from Hellesylt to Geiranger takes one hour. In that hour, the world narrows to a corridor of dark water and vertical stone, and you begin to understand why the Norse believed their gods lived in places like this.

<!-- more -->

Geirangerfjord is fifteen kilometers long and, at its narrowest, barely six hundred meters wide. The cliffs on either side rise to more than a thousand meters. When the ferry enters the fjord, the scale shifts in a way that is difficult to describe -- the sky becomes a strip of light between two walls of rock, and the water beneath the hull turns from blue to black.

## The Seven Sisters

Halfway through the passage, the ferry slows. On the port side, seven streams of water fall from the cliff face in parallel lines. These are the Seven Sisters -- De Syv Sostrene -- and they have been falling since the last ice age, ten thousand years of water finding the same path down the same stone.

The waterfalls are tallest in spring, when snowmelt feeds them from above. By late summer, some of the sisters are reduced to thin threads. But in March, after a winter of accumulation, they fall with a force that sends mist across the deck of the ferry.

> The sound arrives before the sight. A low, continuous roar that seems to come from the stone itself, as though the mountain were breathing.

Across the fjord, a single waterfall called The Suitor faces the Seven Sisters. The old story says he is offering a drink to the sisters, each of whom has rejected him. The bottle shape of the cascade -- wider at the base, narrow at the top -- reinforces the legend. It is a landscape that invites mythology.

## Abandoned Farms

What strikes you most about Geirangerfjord is not the water or the stone but the evidence of human habitation in places that seem uninhabitable. Halfway up the cliff face, on ledges that appear too narrow for anything larger than a goat, you can see the remains of farms.

Skagefla is one of them. The farmstead sits on a shelf of rock three hundred meters above the fjord, accessible only by a path that zigzags up the cliff. Families lived here until the early twentieth century, raising livestock and growing what they could on a few acres of flat ground. Children were tied to the farm buildings with rope to prevent them from falling over the edge.

The farm is abandoned now, preserved as a museum. The buildings are small and sturdy, built from timber and stone, designed to withstand avalanches and storms. Standing on the ledge, looking down at the fjord below, you understand both why people left and why they stayed as long as they did. The beauty is not incidental. It is overwhelming.

## The Water Below

The fjord water is remarkably clear. From the deck of the ferry, you can see the dark shapes of the bottom in the shallower sections, though the fjord reaches depths of more than two hundred meters in places. The temperature is cold year-round -- around six or seven degrees Celsius -- fed by glacial meltwater and snowmelt from the surrounding peaks.

In the early morning, before wind disturbs the surface, the fjord becomes a mirror. The cliffs are reflected so precisely that it is difficult to tell where stone ends and water begins. Photographers wait for these moments, but no photograph captures the silence that accompanies them -- the absolute stillness of air and water and rock.

## Getting There

Geiranger is accessible by road from Alesund (approximately three hours) or by ferry from Hellesylt. The ferry runs multiple times daily in summer; in winter, service is reduced. The road to Geiranger descends in a series of hairpin bends called Ornesvingen -- the Eagle's Bend -- which offers one of the most dramatic viewpoints in Norway.

Bring warm layers regardless of season. The fjord creates its own microclimate, and temperatures on the water are significantly lower than on the surrounding heights. The ferry has both indoor and outdoor seating; choose the deck for the full experience.
