Ferris & Oyelaran was a good consultancy and a bad customer for enterprise software. We did brand strategy for other small studios, which meant our pipeline was six to ten live deals at any time, our invoices were personal enough that a form-letter reminder felt rude, and our "team" was two people who already knew what the other was working on. Every CRM we tried solved problems we didn't have — lead routing for sales teams we didn't employ, custom fields for processes we hadn't standardized, permission tiers for an organization chart that was just the two of us and, some months, a subcontractor.

So the tool we built for ourselves stayed small on purpose, and when we turned it into a product, we made a decision that shaped everything after: no outside money. Not because we're against venture capital in principle, but because we watched what it did to tools we loved. A round comes in, growth becomes the metric that matters, and within two years the product has three pricing tiers, a sales team cold-calling your inbox, and a roadmap driven by what expands the addressable market rather than what the two hundred people already using it actually need. We didn't want to become the company we were mad at.

Staying two people was never a stunt. It's the actual reason the roadmap stays short, the support replies stay personal, and the price has never needed a tier system to keep growing revenue — we grow by being worth the forty-nine dollars to one more consultancy each week, not by convincing existing customers to pay more for the same thing. If that ever stops being true, we'll say so here, on this page, before it shows up in [the price](/pricing/). Until then, if you'd rather hear it from us directly, [just ask](/contact/).
