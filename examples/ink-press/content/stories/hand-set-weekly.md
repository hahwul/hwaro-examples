The mill at Pietralba was built in 1893 to grind wheat for a valley of three thousand, then shuttered in 1971, then bought in 2011 by a former copy editor who wanted a quiet place to print a newspaper.

## The paper

The weekly it produces is called Il Foglio Pietralba. It runs eight pages, hand-set in a single Monotype face, proofed twice, then pulled on an iron press older than the building it sits in. A first edition of five hundred copies is printed Thursday night and ships by 9am Friday.

## The staff

Four writers. Three contribute to the news pages; one writes a weekly science column that has been running for a decade without missing an issue. The editor-in-chief is Marta Olarte, formerly of a national daily in Rome, who taught herself hand-composition in her fifties.

## The discipline

Olarte is unsentimental about what the paper is for. "The internet moves too fast for journalism," she told me, rolling ink across a galley proof. "Not because of the speed of events. Because of the speed of correction. A paper that can be altered continuously cannot be trusted to report anything."

## The stakes

The paper loses money every month. The shortfall is covered by two patrons from the valley and the editor's retirement savings. If you want to know why the paper still exists, this is the answer: she hasn't found a reason to stop.
