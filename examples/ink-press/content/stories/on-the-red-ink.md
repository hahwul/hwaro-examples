The convention of printing corrections in red ink is older than most presses still running. The Fleet Street dailies of the 1880s used it; so did the Viennese feuilletons of the 1920s. The purpose was never decoration. It was to save the reader's eye — a flash of chroma against a sea of black was the fastest way to say, "look here, something has been changed."

## A practical tradition

The tradition is practical. A single red accent, used sparingly, does three things a designer cannot do any other way. It flags a correction. It marks a pull quote worth stopping for. It announces the next issue with an economy of means that no all-caps head can match.

## Why only one

A second accent defeats the purpose. The instant a paper has two colors of alert, the reader has to decide between them, and deciding is slower than reading. One color. Used sparely. Reserved for what matters.

## The masthead

Some papers extend the red to a single mark in the masthead — the ampersand, the period at the end of the flag, a single ornament above the rule. We do this too. The red in our masthead is the same ink that runs our corrections. When we correct, we are using the same mark that names us.
