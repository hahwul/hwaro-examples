The commission arrived without a sender's address, without a seal, and without the usual letter of introduction that accompanies legitimate work. It was accompanied, instead, by a purse containing twice the cartographer's standard fee for a project of the described scope, and a locked iron box, and a note written in a script that Maret had not seen before and spent three days translating with the assistance of every linguistic reference she possessed.

The note read, in sum: *Draw what has been. Draw what is. Draw what waits. You will know which when you arrive.*

The fee was enough to keep her workshop running for two years. The locked box contained a compass whose needle pointed in a direction that was not north.

She packed her instruments and her good parchment and her ink of three weights, and she went.

---

## A Note on the Cartographer's Method

Maret had trained under the great geographical survey institutions of her era, passed their examinations with commendations, and spent six years producing the kind of maps that governments hang in their war rooms and merchants carry folded in their coats. Accurate maps. Maps whose coastlines could be compared against the actual coastlines and found to match within acceptable tolerances. Maps that were useful precisely because they described things that existed.

She had also, in parallel and in secret, been drawing the other maps.

She did not have a name for them that she shared with anyone else. In her private ledgers she called them the *provisional cartographies*, which was a name that described her uncertainty about their status more than it described what they actually were. She had begun drawing them in her second year of professional practice, when she surveyed a stretch of river valley that showed, on all previous maps, as uninhabited meadowland, and found instead the complete stone foundations of a city that had never, according to any historical record she could locate, been built.

The foundations were old. They were also, she noted with the precision of a trained observer, laid out according to a city plan she recognized---the radial arrangement, the central open space that would have been a cathedral square, the spoke-roads radiating outward. It was the plan of a city she had surveyed the previous year, three hundred miles to the south, a city that was very much in existence and had been for six centuries.

She mapped the foundations. She labeled them as accurately as she could: *stones of uncertain age, purpose unknown, origin unknown, relationship to city of Varenne: unexplained.* She did not show this map to the survey institution. She filed it in the back of her personal cabinet, and she began to notice other things.

---

## The Maps She Did Not Publish

Over the following eight years, Maret accumulated twenty-three provisional cartographies.

There was the map of the coast of the northern inlet as it would appear---or as it had appeared, the distinction proved impossible to determine---after some event had reshaped the shoreline so completely that the harbor no longer existed. The fishing village that currently occupied the harbor's edge appeared on this map as a meadow. Maret drew this map in spring. By autumn of the same year, the harbor had silted up to a degree that made it unusable, and the fishing village had relocated inland. The silting, everyone agreed, had been occurring gradually for decades. It was not surprising. It was simply the sea doing what the sea does.

There was the map of a mountain pass that did not appear on any existing survey, that Maret found when she followed the compass's non-northern direction for three days into the high country. The pass itself was unremarkable: a gap between two peaks, reasonably navigable in good weather, with evidence of some ancient road-building that suggested it had once been used regularly. The road on the other side descended to a valley that appeared on no survey she had ever seen, and in the valley there was a structure she did not have sufficient vocabulary to describe precisely, and she drew it as accurately as she was able, and she did not go inside, and she came back the way she had come, and when she returned the following year to verify her measurements the pass was not there.

She searched for two days. The peaks were where they should have been. The gap between them was not. The geography read as though it had always been thus.

There was the map of the city as it would be---as it might be---as it could be---when the years had done sufficient work upon it. She drew this one from her own city, the city she had lived in for twenty years, and the exercise required her to project forward by an amount she estimated at two to four centuries, and when she finished drawing it, she looked at what she had drawn, and she folded it very carefully, and she placed it in the back of the cabinet behind all the others, and she did not look at it again for six months.

When she did look at it again, she made several small corrections, because she had drawn it in haste and with insufficient attention to certain structural details that the intervening months had allowed her to observe more carefully. The corrections were minor. The substance of the map did not change.

---

## The Commission

The compass led her west.

She had expected the compass---whose needle, she had established through careful testing, was not responding to any magnetic property she could measure---to lead her toward something specific. What she had not anticipated was how long the journey would be, or how ordinary the landscape through which she traveled, or how gradually the ordinary would shade into something else.

The transition was not dramatic. There was no moment at which she crossed a threshold and entered a different world. There was instead a slow accumulation of small wrongnesses: a forest that the maps showed as cleared farmland, roads that departed from their documented courses by distances too great to be surveying error, villages that appeared and disappeared as she traveled through regions that should have been uninhabited. She drew all of it. She drew constantly, stopping on hillsides and bridges and at crossroads, adding to the map that was assembling itself in her portfolio, building a picture of a landscape that could not quite decide which of its several versions it wished to be.

She slept in the open when the villages were not there, and in inns when they were, and she noticed that the innkeepers and the village merchants treated her with the wary courtesy extended to someone who has arrived from outside the local understanding of where *outside* begins. She was not precisely unwelcome. She was simply not expected. She was of a category they had no existing protocol for.

On the nineteenth day, the compass led her to a door.

The door was in the middle of a field. It was a large door, of good craftsmanship, mounted in a frame of dark wood that was anchored in the earth with no visible mechanism. There was no building attached to it. The door was simply there, as doors will sometimes be when something requires the formality of a threshold even in the absence of walls.

On the door was a knocker in the shape of a hand holding a compass. The compass in the carving pointed in the same direction as the compass she carried.

She knocked.

---

## What the Commission Required

The door opened onto a room that was not in the field. It was in a building of some kind---stone walls, vaulted ceiling, the perpetual interior dark of a structure that has never prioritized windows. The room contained a table, two chairs, and a person sitting in one of them.

The person looked like someone who had looked like many different things over a very long time and had not fully settled on a single presentation. Their age was indeterminate. Their clothing was of excellent quality but of no fashion Maret recognized. They had very good hands, the kind that have handled instruments precisely for decades.

They did not greet her. They said: "You found the door."

"I followed the compass," Maret said.

"The compass leads where it leads. Not everyone follows it."

She sat in the other chair. She put her portfolio on the table. "You have a commission."

"We have three commissions," the person said. "For the past, the present, and the probable future. Separate maps, separate scales, overlapping territories. We are aware this is unusual work."

"I have unusual work in my portfolio," Maret said.

"We know," they said. "Why do you think you were commissioned?"

---

## The Maps She Made

She was in the room---which was also, she came to understand, in several other places simultaneously, a quality of space that the door facilitated---for what she initially experienced as two weeks and later revised to an interval she declined to quantify in conventional terms.

She drew.

The map of what had been: landscapes as they had existed before the current arrangement of things, when the geography had been distributed differently, when the mountains stood elsewhere and the great rivers ran to different seas. She drew these from materials that the commissioner provided, materials that appeared to be survey records from expeditions that could not have occurred given the dates marked on them. She drew carefully. She verified her measurements against the reference materials repeatedly. The map was accurate. To what, she did not ask.

The map of what is: she drew this from her own direct observation, traveling through the door to locations she could not have named on any conventional map, observing and recording. She drew coastlines of places she could not identify. She drew mountain ranges whose profile she did not recognize. She drew a city that occupied a position where, according to every survey she had ever worked with, there should have been nothing but open ocean. She drew it accurately, because that was what she did, and because the commission required accuracy, and because the city was quite real and deserved to be drawn as such.

The map of what waits: she sat for a long time before beginning this one. The commissioner did not hurry her. They sat in the other chair and did not speak, and the candles on the table burned without perceptibly diminishing.

She began to draw.

She drew from a knowledge she had not known she possessed until her pen touched the parchment. She drew from the accumulated logic of every provisional cartography she had ever made, every small wrongness she had observed and recorded and filed away in the back of the cabinet. She drew from the understanding, arrived at somewhere in the middle of the second map and solidified by the time she began the third, that the landscapes she had been drawing her whole career were not separate phenomena but facets of a single thing that was doing what it had always been doing, that would continue to do what it had always been doing, that she was simply the first person with sufficiently precise instruments to record the process in progress.

When she finished, the commissioner looked at the map for a long time. Then they said: "Yes. That is accurate."

"I know," she said.

She picked up the map of what waits. She looked at it. She looked at the position where her city appeared on it---the city she had lived in for twenty years, the city she had mapped in the provisional cartography she did not look at for six months.

The positions matched.

She rolled the map carefully and placed it with the others and tied the roll with the cord she used for finished work. She stood up and picked up her portfolio. She walked back through the door, which opened into the field, which was as she had left it except that the light had changed in a direction she did not attempt to quantify.

The compass needle, she noted, was pointing north.

---

## Afterward

She returned to her workshop. She filed the three maps in the cabinet. She sat at her desk for a considerable time.

Then she opened her ledger of provisional cartographies and began a new page.

The work continues. It has always been continuing. The map is never finished because the territory is never still. This is not a frustration for a cartographer who has understood what cartography is actually for. It is simply a description of the conditions.

She lights the candle. She picks up her pen. Outside, the city rearranges itself by the usual imperceptible degrees.

She draws it exactly as it is.
