## How I got here

I did not set out to study robots. My undergraduate degree is in mechanical engineering, and my first job out of school was building end-of-arm tooling for a warehouse automation contractor — mostly vacuum grippers and simple two-finger jaws bolted onto industrial arms that had been programmed for boxes of a known size. The boxes were rarely the exact size the programmer had assumed. I spent a lot of that year writing increasingly elaborate force-limit hacks to keep rigid grippers from crushing overfilled cartons, and none of the hacks worked as well as just making the gripper less rigid in the first place. That observation is more or less the seed of everything I have worked on since.

I started a PhD at Aldergrove Institute of Technology to chase that idea properly: instead of controlling a rigid hand more cleverly, what changes if the hand itself is allowed to yield? The Thornfield Manipulation Lab, where I work under Professor Alden Marsh, has spent the last decade building compliant and underactuated hands, so it was an easy fit. My dissertation is on variable-stiffness fingertips — mechanisms that are soft during the first, uncertain moment of contact and can be commanded stiff once a grasp is confirmed, plus the tactile-sensing and control work needed to make that transition happen automatically rather than on a timer.

## Outside the lab

I teach the introductory mechatronics lab most semesters, which I like more than I expected to — undergraduates building their first gripper ask the same "why doesn't this just work" questions I asked in my first industry job, and answering them keeps me honest about which parts of compliant grasping are actually solved versus merely familiar to me. Outside of robotics I climb, badly but consistently, and I maintain a small collection of broken tendon-driven fingers on my desk that I keep meaning to throw away and never do — most of my best design changes have come from staring at exactly why one of them failed.
