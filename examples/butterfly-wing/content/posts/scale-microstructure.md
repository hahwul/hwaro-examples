The brilliant blues, greens, and iridescent shifts on a butterfly wing are not paint. The wing scales are colorless underneath; the color comes from microscopic ridges that diffract light, the way a soap bubble produces color from a film of clear water.

## Two Mechanisms

- **Pigmented color** — the rarer case. The wing scale contains a colorant, usually melanin or pterin. The color is fixed regardless of viewing angle.
- **Structural color** — the more common case. The scale's surface is corrugated at a wavelength scale; specific colors are reinforced through interference, others are canceled.

Most blues and greens on butterflies are structural. So are the metallic flashes that appear and disappear as the wing tilts.

## What This Tells Designers

Color in nature is often a *behavior*, not a *property*. A surface that shifts hue with viewing angle reads as alive in a way a flat color never does. Translating that to interface design is hard — screens emit light rather than reflect it — but the principle holds: subtle hue shifts driven by interaction (cursor position, scroll, time of day) make a static layout feel less static.
