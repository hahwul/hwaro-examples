A butterfly's two wings are not just similar — they are mirror images, often within a tolerance of less than one millimeter. That kind of bilateral precision is rare. A leaf is roughly symmetric. A bird's wing pair is *similar*. A butterfly's wings are *equal*.

## What the Symmetry Does

The eye notices symmetry instantly, before it can articulate why. A symmetric pattern reads as a single unit, even when the pattern itself is complex. A butterfly with twenty distinct color zones on each wing still reads as one organism, because the bilateral symmetry compresses all of those zones into a single signal.

## When Asymmetry Enters

Many species break symmetry deliberately on the underside of the wing. The upper surface is the broadcast — to mates and to rivals. The underside is camouflage, and bilateral symmetry would *attract* attention rather than dissolve it.

Designers can borrow the same logic: use strict symmetry for elements that should announce themselves (logos, hero images, headlines) and asymmetry for elements that should recede (background motifs, supporting illustrations).
