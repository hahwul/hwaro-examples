Malik Renner's duet for two dancers runs eighteen minutes without a blackout, a scene change, or a single cue that removes light already on stage. The lighting design took that constraint as its brief: every one of the piece's twenty-two cues adds a source, changes an angle, or raises a level — nothing ever fades to zero until the curtain call.

<!-- more -->

The rig sits almost entirely on the floor. Ten low-angle units in R080 Primary Blue are staggered across the deck in a loose grid, so that as the dancers move between them their own bodies constantly recompose the shadow pattern on the back wall — the "bloom" of the title is that shadow accumulation, not a lighting effect layered on top of the movement but a direct consequence of where the fixtures physically sit. Two overhead specials in the same blue carry the only vertical light in the show, reserved for a single unison phase near the end.

**Gel schedule**

| Position | Fixture | Gel | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Floor grid, 10x | Par 16, low angle | R080 Primary Blue | Base system, builds across the piece |
| Overhead specials, 2x | Source Four 19° | R080 Primary Blue | Unison phase only, cue 19 onward |
| Booms, 2x | Fresnel 6" | R080 Primary Blue, half CTB | Edge fill from cue 12 |
| Cyc, 2x | Cyc LED | Matched blue, no gel | Depth cue behind the duet, cue 16 onward |

**Sample cue**

| Cue | Trigger | Look | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | House to half | Single floor unit rises to 15% | The only light in the first ninety seconds |
| 12 | Contact phase begins | Booms add, floor grid at 40% overall | First light to leave the floor plane |
| 19 | Unison phase | Overhead specials add on top of full floor grid | Only moment with vertical light in the piece |
| 22 | Final pose | Full rig holds, no fade | House lights bring the curtain call, not a lighting cue |

Twenty-two cues, zero subtractions, one dimmer channel that never moves below where cue 1 leaves it. The plot book's only annotation on the last page: "if you need to cut something, cut a fixture from the hang. Don't cut a cue."
