Alizarin is the solo lighting design practice of Noor Adeyemi, based in Rotterdam and working across mid-scale dance companies and touring opera in the Netherlands and Belgium. The name comes from alizarin crimson, the deep madder red long used as a scenic paint before it was synthesized — a color that reads as blood, velvet, and warning depending on how much light you let hit it. It felt like the right word for a practice built on gel choices that carry as much meaning as the blocking they light.

<!-- more -->

Noor trained as an electrician before training as a designer, and it shows in the paperwork: every plot leaving this studio is a hand-drafted document with a full gel schedule, a focus chart, and a cue synopsis, delivered to the stage manager as a bound plot book rather than a PDF export. That habit started out of necessity — touring venues rarely have the rep plot the show was built for — and stayed because it forces a design to justify itself in writing before it justifies itself in the room.

The approach leans hard on saturated color and hard edges over wash and blend. A cue is rarely more than four systems deep. Front light earns its place or it gets cut. Practicals, when they appear, are treated as scenery, not fill. Directors and choreographers who work with Alizarin more than once tend to describe the results the same way: fewer cues than they expected, and each one doing more work than they expected.

Current and recent collaborators include Kammeroper Noord, Vantage Dance Collective, Opera Klein, and Studio Ondine. Plots, focus charts, and paperwork samples from five recent productions are collected under [Productions](/productions/), starting with [Blue Hour](/productions/blue-hour/), the most recent.

For availability, commissions, or a copy of a specific plot, write to studio@alizarin.example — replies come between tech weeks, so allow a few days.
