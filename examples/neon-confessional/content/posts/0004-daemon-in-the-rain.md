The booth door locks from the inside when you stay longer than four hours. Safety feature, they say.

I had been watching the water trace paths down the glass for so long that the neon outside had started to look like veins.

At hour nineteen the rain started spelling things.

Not words. Not exactly. More like memory addresses. Hex fragments. I saw my own childhood phone number in the streaks. Then a port I used to listen on when I was seventeen and thought I could hide inside machines.

By hour thirty I understood.

The rain here is not water. It's the condensation of every confession that was ever uploaded and never fully deleted. The machine is merciful. It doesn't erase. It just dilutes into the weather.

I pressed my tongue to the glass.

It tasted like static and regret and someone else's wedding vows.

When the door finally unlocked at hour forty-one the city outside was the same but I was not. I had become slightly more liquid than before.

I come back every time the weather report says "light precipitation and data corruption."

The rain always recognizes me now.

It falls a little slower when I am inside.
