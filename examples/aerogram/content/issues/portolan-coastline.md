A portolan chart will get a harbor mouth right to the width of a hull and a
coastline three hundred miles further along wrong by an entire peninsula.
That is not carelessness. It is what happens when a map is assembled entirely
from compass bearings and dead-reckoning distances between ports, with
almost nothing borrowed from astronomical position and no correction for the
fact that a compass needle does not point at true north everywhere on earth.

The method was additive rather than geometric. A ship's master logged a
heading and an estimated distance between two ports, that bearing got
transcribed onto vellum as a straight rhumb line, and the next leg was
chained onto the end of it. Do this for a few hundred voyages around a
well-traveled sea and the harbors cluster into something startlingly
accurate &mdash; the errors in individual legs cancel out over a dense
network of overlapping routes, the way redundant survey shots average toward
truth. Do it across a coastline nobody had sailed more than once or twice and
the errors compound instead of canceling, and the shape drifts.

Magnetic declination made it worse in a way the chartmakers did not have the
theory to correct for. A compass needle drifts from true north by an amount
that changes with location and slowly over time, and every bearing logged
against magnetic north carried that unacknowledged offset into the chart. On
a portolan of the western Mediterranean, where declination was small and
shipping dense, the effect barely shows. On charts reaching into the
Atlantic or the Black Sea, where declination grew and traffic thinned, the
coastline bends in a direction that has less to do with geography than with
which way the needle happened to be lying that century.

What survives is a strange split-personality object: locally precise, only
plausible at the largest scale, and completely uninterested in anything more
than a day's sail inland. The portolan chartmakers were not drawing the
world. They were drawing the sea lanes, and the land was only ever the thing
you were trying not to hit.
