Most atlases that never reached print did not fail because the maps were
bad. They failed for the same reasons most ambitious print projects fail:
the money ran out, the engraver died mid-plate, a war closed the border the
survey data needed to cross, or a co-editor quietly moved on to a better-paid
commission and took the momentum with them.

<!-- more -->

What is left behind in an archive box is often more interesting than the
finished product would have been. A published atlas is a set of decisions
already made &mdash; which projection, which color scheme for elevation,
which disputed border gets drawn which way. A drawing-table atlas preserves
the arguments that produced those decisions: marginal notes disagreeing with
a colleague's choice of scale, a page of trial hachures for hill shading
abandoned halfway down, correspondence about whether a river's course should
follow the surveyor's fieldbook or the more flattering version the funding
patron preferred.

One folio that turns up repeatedly in these unfinished projects is the
specimen sheet: a single test plate, engraved to work out a technique before
committing it to sixty more pages. These sheets are disproportionately
represented in what survives, because they were cheap to keep and nobody
minded losing them, unlike the expensive finished plates that got recycled
for their copper the moment the project was declared dead. Reading a run of
specimen sheets in sequence is closer to reading a cartographer's private
notebook than anything that made it to a bookshop shelf.

The lesson for anyone building a map today is uncomfortable but useful: the
version that ships is rarely the most honest one. It is the version that
survived the budget meeting. The interesting choices, the ones an editor
walked back or a patron vetoed, tend to live only in the drafts nobody
thought were worth finishing.
