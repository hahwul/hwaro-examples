Every bathymetric chart is a portrait of an object nobody involved in
drawing it has ever seen whole. The seafloor does not hold still for a
survey the way a coastline does; it has to be inferred, one sounding at a
time, from a surface vessel that is only ever certain of its own position
and has to trust the instrument to tell it what is underneath.

<!-- more -->

The earliest soundings were a weighted line paid out over the side, read in
fathoms by feel and eyesight, one point at a time, hours apart on a slow
transect. A single deep-ocean sounding could take the better part of an
afternoon, which meant early bathymetric charts of the open ocean were
built from a scattering of individual points thousands of miles apart,
connected by contour lines that were more informed guess than measurement.
Trenches were routinely missed entirely, simply because no ship happened to
drop a line directly over one.

Echo sounding changed the resolution by orders of magnitude and changed the
character of the error along with it. A single-beam echo sounder returns a
continuous depth profile along the ship's track instead of isolated points,
which is a genuine improvement, but it only ever sees the seafloor directly
beneath the hull. Multibeam systems widened that swath into a strip tens of
times the water depth, and modern surveys stitch adjacent swaths into
something close to a full-coverage map &mdash; the first bathymetric charts
in history where the blank spaces mean "not yet surveyed" rather than
"assumed flat."

What has not changed is the ink. Depth contours on a printed chart still use
the same convention a lead-line surveyor would recognize: darker, denser
blue toward the deep water, a graduated wash rather than a hard color
boundary, because the transition on the seafloor itself is never a hard
line either. It is the one part of the cartography that has stayed
honestly analog even after the instruments went fully digital &mdash; ink
standing in for depth, the way it always has, however the depth got
measured.
