## East of the resorts

The Mallorca that most visitors know is a strip of resort hotels running along the southern bay from Palma to Arenal. It is loud and bright and smells of sunscreen and fried food. There is nothing wrong with it, exactly, but it is not the island.

The real Mallorca -- or at least the version of it that keeps drawing me back -- is on the eastern coast, where the limestone cliffs fracture into dozens of small inlets called calas. These coves are rarely more than fifty meters wide. Their walls rise vertically from water so transparent that the sandy bottom is visible at five or six meters depth. The pines grow to the edge of the cliffs and lean out over the drop, and the only sound is the slow pulse of small waves against stone.

## Cala Varques

Getting to Cala Varques requires a twenty-minute walk along a dirt path through dry scrubland. There is no signage. The trailhead is an unmarked gap in a low stone wall along the road between Porto Cristo and Cala Romantica. You follow the path through the brush until the ground drops away and there it is: a perfect crescent of pale sand between two arms of weathered limestone, the water shifting between turquoise and deep blue depending on the depth.

I arrived at eight in the morning and had the cove entirely to myself for two hours. The water was cold enough to be invigorating but not punishing. Along the eastern wall, there are small sea caves that you can swim into, where the light refracts off the sandy bottom and paints the ceiling in shifting patterns of blue and white.

## The cave system

What makes Cala Varques unusual is the cave system that opens at the back of the beach. A narrow passage leads into a series of chambers with stalactites and a small underground lake. The locals know about it, but it does not appear in most guidebooks. I explored the first chamber with a headlamp, but the passages narrow quickly and the footing is uncertain. This is not a place for casual exploration.

## Cala Mitjana

Further south, Cala Mitjana is smaller and harder to reach. The approach is a scramble down a rocky slope through dense vegetation. The reward is a cove perhaps thirty meters across, walled on three sides by cliffs that block the wind completely. The water here is the clearest I have encountered anywhere in the Mediterranean. At midday, when the sun is directly overhead, the bottom is visible at eight meters and the light creates a kind of luminous haze in the water that is difficult to describe and impossible to photograph accurately.

## What to bring

Sturdy shoes for the approach walks. The paths are rocky and often overgrown. Water -- there are no facilities at any of these coves. A snorkel, if you have one. The underwater visibility makes even basic snorkeling extraordinary. And patience. These places reward stillness. Arrive early, stay late, and resist the urge to move on to the next cove before you have properly seen the one you are in.
