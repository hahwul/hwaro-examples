## The road itself

The Amalfi Coast road is fifty kilometers of engineering that should not exist. It was carved into the cliff face in the mid-nineteenth century, and it has been terrifying drivers and delighting passengers ever since. The road is barely two lanes wide, often less, and it twists through tunnels blasted from the rock and around blind curves where buses and cars negotiate the right of way with horns and hand gestures and a faith in providence that I found, as a pedestrian, both admirable and alarming.

But the views from that road are extraordinary. At every turn, the Mediterranean appears below -- not gradually, but suddenly, as though a curtain has been pulled aside. The water shifts from deep navy in the distance to turquoise near the shore, and the towns along the coast cling to the cliffs like clusters of painted barnacles: white and pink and terracotta, their churches and houses stacked vertically in defiance of gravity.

## Positano

Positano descends from the road to the sea in a cascade of stairs and narrow passages. There are no flat streets. Everything is vertical. The houses are built into the slope at angles that make their foundations a matter of speculation, and the bougainvillea that covers their walls is so dense that in places you cannot tell where the architecture ends and the vegetation begins.

I stayed in a small pensione halfway up the hill, in a room with a balcony that looked out over the rooftops to the beach below. In the morning, the fishing boats were pulled up on the grey sand, and the fishermen sat on the seawall mending nets with the same gestures their predecessors must have used for centuries.

## The food

The Amalfi Coast eats well, and it eats simply. Anchovies from Cetara, salted and packed in oil, served on toast with a squeeze of lemon. Pasta with clams -- the small, sweet vongole that live in the shallow waters along the coast. Lemons the size of grapefruit, their rind thick and fragrant, used in everything from salad dressings to the limoncello that appears at the end of every meal whether you ordered it or not.

In Amalfi town, I found a restaurant down a side street where the kitchen was smaller than the table I sat at. The owner cooked scialatielli -- a flat, rough-cut pasta local to the coast -- with seafood in a sauce that was nothing more than tomatoes, garlic, white wine, and whatever had come off the boats that morning. It was one of the best meals I have eaten anywhere, and it cost less than a sandwich at the tourist restaurants along the waterfront.

## Ravello

Ravello sits above the coast, removed from the noise and the traffic. The town is small and quiet, and its two famous gardens -- Villa Rufolo and Villa Cimbrone -- are among the most beautiful places on the Italian peninsula. The terrace at Villa Cimbrone, known as the Terrace of Infinity, looks out over the entire coast from a height that makes the sea appear to extend forever. On a clear day, you can see past the curve of the earth. Or at least, standing there in the afternoon light, you believe you can.

## Getting around

The SITA buses run frequently along the coast road and cost a few euros per ride. They are the most practical way to move between towns. Ferries connect the major towns during the summer months. Walking the ancient paths between villages is the best option if you have the legs for it -- the Sentiero degli Dei, the Path of the Gods, from Agerola to Nocelle is among the finest coastal walks in the world.
