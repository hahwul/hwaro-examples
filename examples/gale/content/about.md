Pengarrow Head is the last piece of land for three thousand miles. Stand at the coastguard rail on its western tip and there is nothing between you and the far side of the Atlantic except weather, and the weather knows it. The headland takes every gale first and takes it whole, which is a misery for the gorse and a gift for anyone who watches seabirds: wind pushes the ocean's traffic inshore, and for a few hours the pelagic world files past a fixed point at telescope range.

Gale is the log I keep from that rail. I am E. Penhallow, warden here since 2019, and the notes are written the same evening as the watch, from a pocket notebook that has been rained on more often than not.

## The method

Every count follows the same protocol, so seasons can be compared honestly:

- Watches run from a fixed position at the rail, scanning a fixed arc from the Carrick buoy to the open horizon.
- Birds are logged by species, direction, and hour. Passage rates are given as birds per hour west, since west is the direction that means something here.
- Optics are a 30x wide-angle telescope and 8x42 binoculars. In a force nine you use the binoculars and brace.
- A bird not identified to species is logged at whatever level is honest — "large shearwater sp." is data too.
- No watch is shorter than ninety minutes. The third hour is where the rare birds live.

## The name

Fishermen's glass-tap wisdom says a falling barometer empties the harbour and fills the sea lane. The journal is named for the weather that makes it worth keeping. Calm days are lovely; they are also, ornithologically speaking, nearly empty. When the shipping forecast promises a gale from the west, I fill the flask.

The wall of notes on the [front page](./) is the season as it happened, newest first. Each card carries the species' Latin binomial up its left edge — the formal name, standing like a spine on a shelf, because field notes are casual but names are not.
