Every seawatcher has a private superstition, and mine is the third hour. The first hour is traffic; the second is rhythm; the third is when the sea, having checked your credentials, occasionally hands you the bird you came for. Today, at 09:12, in the grey channel of light between two rain squalls, it handed me a juvenile Sabine's Gull.

<!-- more -->

The gale had blown itself from a screaming southwesterly into a hard, clean west-northwest overnight — classic delivery weather, the kind that sweeps oceanic birds up against the coast and pours them past fixed points. The bird announced itself the way Sabine's always do, by geometry: that startling tricolour W across the upperwings, brown, white, and black in three clean triangles, unlike anything else the Atlantic can send past this rail.

It came through at middle distance, unhurried in air that had Kittiwakes labouring, with the buoyant, tern-like wingbeat the field guides promise and the real bird exceeds. Twenty, perhaps twenty-five seconds in the telescope's arc. I gave it a running commentary out loud to nobody, which is standard procedure, then wrote the description on the spot with rain fattening the ink: dusky hood-remnant, scaled brown saddle, forked tail with its neat black band.

A bird hatched this summer on tundra somewhere toward Greenland or beyond, a few weeks into a first journey aimed at seas off southern Africa, briefly intersecting a man wedged behind a concrete rail with tea going cold. The rest of the watch was 1,904 Kittiwakes, 61 Manx, and 3 Bonxies, and I would not have traded the twenty-five seconds for any of it.
