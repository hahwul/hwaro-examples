An evening watch in weather this journal is not named for: a flat calm, the sea like poured pewter, and the *Merrose Maid* hauling her gear a mile off the head with a halo of gulls above her and something far better below them.

<!-- more -->

Storm Petrels — thirty-one at the peak count, though the true number was surely higher — were working the slick behind the trawler, and in a calm you finally see them properly. A bird the size of a House Martin, sooty black with a white rump like a struck match, pattering its feet on the surface as if testing whether the sea will bear its weight. It never does; the bird never entirely commits. They fly like bats reconsidering every decision, and they are, gram for gram, the toughest seabirds this coast possesses.

The paradox of the species is that you meet them in calms and they live in storms. These same birds will spend October in weather that keeps ships in harbour, tucked into wave troughs, feeding in the lee of swells the way songbirds feed in the lee of hedges. Tonight they merely dined out. The light went at nine and took them with it, and I walked back up the path in the dark, deeply content with the least dramatic entry of the season.
