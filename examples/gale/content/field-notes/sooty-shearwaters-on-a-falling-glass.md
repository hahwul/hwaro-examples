The barometer lost eleven millibars overnight and was still falling at dawn, and the Sooties knew it before I did. They were moving from the first scan: long-winged, cigar-bodied, dark as wet slate, cutting through the Manx lines like sentences written in a heavier ink.

<!-- more -->

Four hundred and eighty-seven went west before the building sea forced me off the rail at 11:45. These are birds on one of the great commutes on Earth — hatched on islands in the far South Atlantic and Pacific, looping our hemisphere in their winter, which is our summer, and funnelling back south ahead of the autumn lows. A falling glass here means a shove of wind they can ride, and they take it.

In the telescope the difference from Manx is less the colour than the engine. A Sooty carries more inertia into each shear, climbing higher off the crest, holding the arc longer, flexing those narrow wings only when the sea demands it. In a force seven they look less like they are coping with the weather than collecting a debt from it.

Supporting cast: two Great Skuas lumbering west, a juvenile Arctic Tern being blown sideways with great dignity, and the season's first Great Northern Diver in off the sea, high and heavy, like a freight aircraft joining a circuit of gliders. The low is due tonight. Tomorrow could be enormous.
