The Fulmars are back on the ledges, riding the cliff updraught with that straight-armed, no-effort insolence that makes every other bird look like it is trying too hard.

<!-- more -->

This was a census watch rather than a seawatch: telescope on the north face from the wall stile, mapping apparently occupied sites against last March's sketch. The protocol counts a site as occupied when a bird sits tight on a usable ledge through the whole scan, and by that standard the north face held sixty-four sites today, up from fifty-seven a year ago.

Pairs were cackling head-to-head on the wider shelves, all raised tubenoses and clattering bills, and there was constant low-level squabbling over the prime sites under the overhang — the ones that stay dry in a westerly. Non-breeders wheeled below the rim in stacked ellipses, wings locked, letting the headland do the flying for them.

Worth recording: three birds of the darker, blue-grey type among the wheelers, the northern morph we mostly see in hard winters. And one ledge dispute settled in the traditional way, the sitting bird oiling its challenger at close range. The challenger sat on the sea for ten full minutes afterwards, preening and rethinking its ambitions.
