The first bird crossed the arc at 05:52, before the sun had properly cleared Carrick Point, and after that they simply did not stop. Manx Shearwaters in lines of thirty and forty, low to the swell, tilting from black to white and back like a signal being flashed down the coast.

<!-- more -->

By 07:00 the passage rate had settled at just over seven hundred birds an hour, which is the strongest spring movement I have logged here since the journal began. They were close in, too — the veering wind had pinched the sea lane against the head, and through the telescope you could watch individual birds negotiate each crest: a stiff-winged climb up the windward face, a heeled turn at the top, a long glide down into the trough and out of sight.

Counting at that density is done in tens, clicking once per group and correcting on the pauses. The pauses were few. A single Sooty Shearwater ran through the middle of it at 08:14, conspicuous as a crow at a wedding, and two Puffins went west amongst the auk lines almost unnoticed.

The colony these birds belong to is anyone's guess — the nearest big rafts gather off the islands forty miles north — but on mornings like this the head feels less like a viewpoint and more like a turnstile. Total for the watch: 2,318 west, 11 east. I stayed the extra half hour because stopping felt rude.
