There are seawatches you enjoy and seawatches you survive, and this was the second kind. The storm that the shipping forecast had been promising all week arrived overnight, and by first light the sea state was beyond anything in the logbook's vocabulary — whole hillsides of water detonating against the head, spray carrying sixty feet up to the rail, the telescope tripod lashed to the coastguard bench with a spare bootlace. Inside all of it, in numbers I have never recorded here before, were Kittiwakes.

<!-- more -->

They came through the arc continuously for four hours: 3,406 west by the final tally, adults and first-winters together, the young birds' black wing-Ms flickering in and out of the spume. A wreck in the making — birds wintering far offshore, caught by a week of consecutive gales, running out of sea room and pressing along the coast in search of shelter that does not exist.

What stays with me is not the number but the manner of their flying. A Kittiwake in a force ten does not fight the air; it fences with it — quick, shallow strokes in the troughs, then a dead stop of the wings as each gust arrives, letting the storm pass through the gaps in its effort. Gannets bulldoze. Auks whirr and hope. The Kittiwake negotiates.

Grimmer arithmetic followed inland: nine storm-driven birds picked up on the harbour beach by afternoon, three still alive and taken into care. Wrecks give with one hand — the closest views of the year, feather detail at forty yards in a hurricane's margins — and take with the other. I logged the watch, then spent the evening answering the phone to people finding gulls in their gardens, five miles from the sea.
