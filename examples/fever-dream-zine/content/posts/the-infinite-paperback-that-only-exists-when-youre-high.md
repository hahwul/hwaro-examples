The cover was blank except for one sentence in peeling gold foil:

**"THIS BOOK IS READING YOU BACK."**

I opened it anyway.

Page one was the exact moment I decided to walk into that particular used bookstore at 2:47 a.m. on a Tuesday in a city I had never visited before and will never visit again.

Page two was the first time I ever lied to someone I loved and the exact sound their voice made when they pretended to believe me.

By page seventeen I was reading about my own death. It was surprisingly gentle. I was holding someone's hand. The someone was also me from a different branch. We were both laughing about how long it took us to figure out the joke.

The book got thicker the more I read. Or maybe I was getting smaller.

Around what would have been page 284 (the numbers kept changing) I found a chapter titled "The Reader Who Is Currently Holding This Book in a Used Bookstore at 2:51 a.m."

It described the exact itch on the back of my neck. The precise way the fluorescent light was flickering. The fact that I was about to sneeze.

I sneezed.

The book said "bless you" in my own handwriting on the next page.

I started flipping faster.

There were entire sections about people I had never met but who had apparently dreamed about me. There were footnotes that were longer than the chapters they referenced and the footnotes had their own footnotes that were secretly love letters between different versions of the same sentence.

At some point I realized I had stopped turning pages and the pages were turning themselves, showing me every possible life I could have lived if I had made one different choice at age seven, age fourteen, age twenty-nine, age forty-three.

Most of them were beautiful in ways that made my chest hurt.

One of them was me as a very happy dog.

I stayed in that one for a long time.

When I finally closed the book the cover was no longer blank. It now said, in the same peeling gold:

**"THANK YOU FOR BEING A PAGE."**

I put it back on the shelf between a 1974 copy of *The Teachings of Don Juan* and a water-damaged paperback of *Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas*.

The next morning the bookstore was gone. In its place was a parking lot that had always been there according to everyone I asked.

I still find pages from the book sometimes.

They appear in coat pockets I haven't worn in years. They fall out of library books I'm certain I've never checked out. Once one showed up inside a sandwich.

Every page is different.

Every page is true.

Every page ends with the same sentence written in increasingly shaky handwriting:

**"Keep reading. You're almost to the good part."**
