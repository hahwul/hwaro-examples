It started with the kitchen wall at 11:17 a.m. on a Wednesday that had no business being that ordinary.

The yellow — a perfectly respectable butter yellow — took a deep, shuddering breath and then let it out in a long, exhausted sigh that smelled like lemons and regret.

I froze with my coffee halfway to my mouth.

The orange in the fruit bowl began wheezing. Not metaphorically. Actual, raspy, asthmatic wheezing. I patted it gently. It calmed down for a moment, then started hyperventilating again when the fridge light came on.

By noon the entire apartment had developed a respiratory system.

The deep violet curtains were doing slow, meditative box breathing. The electric blue of a poster from a band I no longer like was having some kind of panic attack — every time it exhaled it got a little more turquoise.

I spent the afternoon talking them through it.

**"You're doing great,"** I told a particularly anxious patch of teal near the window. **"Just breathe into the tight spots. You're allowed to take up space."**

The teal vibrated appreciatively and produced a small rainbow bubble that floated into the hallway and popped into the sound of distant wind chimes.

Around 4 p.m. the magenta in the rug started laughing. Not cruel laughter. The kind that happens when you finally understand a joke the universe told you three lifetimes ago. It laughed so hard the floor shook. My downstairs neighbor banged on the ceiling with a broom. The magenta laughed harder.

By evening most of them had settled into a gentle collective sigh, like a room full of people who had just finished crying and were now quietly passing tissues and making eye contact.

The butter yellow on the kitchen wall looked at me (somehow) and said, in a voice like warm honey:

**"Thanks for not freaking out. Most people paint over us when we do this."**

I told it I had considered it.

**"What stopped you?"**

I looked around at all of them — the breathing, the sighing, the occasional hiccup of chartreuse in the corner — and realized I had never seen anything more alive in my life.

**"You finally started talking back,"** I said.

The colors exhaled together.

For the first time in years, the apartment felt like it was on my side.

Later that night I lay on the floor and let the rug magenta wash over me like a tide. It whispered secrets about light and memory and how every color is just a different way of missing the same thing.

I fell asleep inside the color.

When I woke up everything was back to normal.

Except now I know they're only pretending.

And sometimes, very late at night, if I hold very still, I can hear the yellow in the kitchen taking tiny, careful breaths — just to remember what it feels like to be alive.
