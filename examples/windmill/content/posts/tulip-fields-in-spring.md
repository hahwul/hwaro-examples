Every year, as March fades into April, the flat polder landscape of the Netherlands undergoes a transformation that draws visitors from every corner of the world. Vast stretches of farmland between Leiden and Den Helder erupt into ribbons of color -- crimson, violet, gold, and white -- as millions of tulip bulbs push through the dark soil and open to the spring sun. The effect is staggering in its scale, a living tapestry that stretches to the horizon and shifts with the wind.

The Dutch love affair with tulips dates back to the late sixteenth century, when the first bulbs arrived from the Ottoman Empire. Within decades, the flower had become an object of obsessive speculation, culminating in the famous Tulip Mania of 1637 when single bulbs traded for the price of a canal house. While that financial frenzy long ago subsided, the cultural attachment endured. Today the Netherlands produces roughly 1.7 billion tulip bulbs each year, accounting for the vast majority of global supply.

Walking through the fields near Lisse, the home of the celebrated Keukenhof gardens, one notices details that photographs rarely capture: the faint sweetness carried on the breeze, the precise geometry of the planting rows, the soft hum of bees moving from bloom to bloom. Farmers work methodically among the rows, removing any flower heads that show signs of disease to protect the crop. It is careful, painstaking labor that contrasts with the exuberance of the display it produces.

For those who wish to experience the tulip season at its best, mid-April is typically the peak. Renting a bicycle and riding the paths that wind between the bulb fields remains one of the finest ways to take it all in. The flat terrain makes the ride effortless, and the panoramic views are uninterrupted by hedgerows or fences. It is a simple pleasure that captures something essential about the Dutch relationship with their land -- open, practical, and quietly magnificent.
