The earliest daguerreotype plates required exposures measured in tens of minutes under direct sunlight. Daguerre's first published examples included still views of Parisian streets that appear empty because no person stood still long enough to register on the plate. A boulevard scene from 1838 shows only a man having his boots polished, the single human figure to remain in one place for the duration of the exposure.

Improvements in sensitization reduced the exposure substantially within two years. By 1841 the introduction of bromine and chlorine accelerators, combined with faster lenses calculated by Josef Petzval in Vienna, brought sitting times down to roughly thirty seconds in good light. This shift made portraiture commercially viable. Studios opened across Europe and the United States, often on top floors with skylights and roof gardens to gather as much daylight as possible.

The sitter was held still by an iron neck rest, an armature with a padded fork that gripped the back of the head out of the camera's view. The expression typical of early portraits, severe and slightly strained, reflects the demand of holding the face motionless for thirty seconds without blinking. Children were rarely photographed during the first years of practice. Their portraits became feasible only as exposure times shortened further.

Studio operators learned to pose the body in ways that disguised any small movement. Hands rested on a table edge or clasped a book. Heads tilted against a wall or column. Drapery and patterned backdrops absorbed the slight blur of breathing. The posing conventions of the period, often misread today as stiff or somber, were practical responses to the limits of the medium rather than expressions of mood.
