After exposure inside the camera, the silvered plate carried no visible image. The latent image existed only as a chemical alteration of the silver iodide layer, invisible to the eye until subjected to mercury vapor. The development box held a small dish of mercury heated to roughly seventy-five degrees Celsius. The plate was suspended above the dish at an angle, face down, while the operator watched the surface through a small inspection window lit by a candle.

Mercury vapor rose and adhered selectively to the regions of silver iodide that had absorbed the most light. The condensed mercury formed a fine amalgam with the silver, producing the lighter tones of the image. Areas that had received no exposure remained dark and reflective, becoming the shadows of the final picture. The reaction proceeded slowly, and the operator judged completion by eye. Underdevelopment produced a thin and washed appearance. Overdevelopment produced a flat and chalky surface with no shadow detail.

The plate was then removed and immersed in a hyposulfite solution to dissolve any remaining light-sensitive silver compounds. After a brief water rinse and a final treatment with gilding solution, which deposited a thin layer of gold onto the image to harden it, the plate was dried with a flame held at a careful distance. The completed daguerreotype showed a fragile, mirror-bright surface that could be ruined by a single fingerprint or rubbed away by a careless cloth.

The hazards of the process were not understood at the time. Long exposure to mercury vapor caused tremors and neurological damage in many practitioners. The trade carried a recognizable cost, paid in slow increments by the operators who polished the plates and watched the development dishes day after day.
