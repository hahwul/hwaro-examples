# The Science of the Aurora

The aurora borealis is not magic, though it is easy to understand why our ancestors believed it was. It is the visible result of a collision between the solar wind and Earth's magnetic field -- a process that begins 150 million kilometers away on the surface of the sun and ends in the upper atmosphere above the polar regions.

## The Solar Wind

The sun is not a quiet star. Its corona -- the outermost layer of its atmosphere -- is a seething expanse of superheated plasma at temperatures exceeding one million degrees Celsius. At these temperatures, the sun cannot hold its atmosphere in place. Particles escape constantly, streaming outward at speeds between 300 and 800 kilometers per second. This is the solar wind.

The solar wind is composed primarily of electrons and protons, carrying with it a fragment of the sun's magnetic field. It takes roughly two to four days for the solar wind to cross the void between the sun and Earth. Most of it flows around our planet, deflected by the magnetosphere -- the magnetic shield generated by Earth's liquid iron core.

But the shield is not perfect.

## The Magnetosphere

Earth's magnetosphere is shaped like a comet: compressed on the sunward side, stretched into a long tail on the night side. Where the solar wind's magnetic field opposes Earth's -- a configuration called southward interplanetary magnetic field -- the two fields can reconnect. This magnetic reconnection opens a door. Solar wind particles funnel down along Earth's magnetic field lines toward the poles.

This is why the aurora is a polar phenomenon. The magnetic field lines converge at the north and south magnetic poles, creating oval-shaped auroral zones centered roughly around 65 to 72 degrees latitude. Tromso, Fairbanks, Yellowknife, Reykjavik -- these cities sit beneath the auroral oval, and on active nights the sky above them comes alive.

## The Light

The incoming particles -- mostly electrons -- carry enormous energy. When they strike atoms and molecules in the upper atmosphere, typically at altitudes between 100 and 300 kilometers, they excite those atoms. An excited atom is unstable. It returns to its ground state by releasing the excess energy as a photon -- a particle of light.

The color of the aurora depends on which atmospheric gas is struck and at what altitude:

- **Green** -- the most common auroral color -- comes from oxygen atoms at altitudes around 100 to 200 kilometers. The excited oxygen takes about three-quarters of a second to emit its green photon at 557.7 nanometers.
- **Red** -- a rarer, deeper color -- also comes from oxygen, but at higher altitudes above 200 kilometers. Here the atmosphere is so thin that atoms have time to emit at 630 nanometers before being disturbed by collisions.
- **Purple and violet** -- these come from nitrogen molecules. When nitrogen is ionized by particularly energetic particles, it emits blue and violet light that mixes with red oxygen emissions to create the purple hues often seen at the lower edges of auroral curtains.
- **Pink** -- occasionally visible at the lowest edges of intense displays -- results from nitrogen at altitudes below 100 kilometers.

## The Shapes

The aurora takes many forms. The most common is the **arc** -- a smooth, quiet band of light stretching east to west across the sky. When the arc brightens and begins to fold, it becomes a **band** with visible structure. As geomagnetic activity increases, the band develops **rays** -- vertical streaks of light that follow magnetic field lines upward.

The most dramatic form is the **corona** -- not to be confused with the solar corona. An auroral corona appears when the observer stands directly beneath an active display. The rays appear to converge overhead, like the ribs of a vast green and purple cathedral dome. It is one of the most striking phenomena visible from Earth's surface.

During geomagnetic storms, the aurora can descend to much lower latitudes. The great storm of March 1989 produced aurora visible as far south as Florida and Cuba. The Carrington Event of 1859 -- the most powerful geomagnetic storm in recorded history -- turned the night sky red and green across the entire planet.

## The Sound

For centuries, observers in the Arctic have reported hearing the aurora -- a faint crackling or hissing sound coinciding with bright displays. Scientists were skeptical for decades, but recent research from the Finnish Meteorological Institute has confirmed that the phenomenon is real. The sounds appear to originate at approximately 70 meters altitude, far below the visual aurora, possibly caused by electrical discharges in the temperature inversion layer.

The aurora is, in the end, a reminder that we live inside the atmosphere of our star. The same sun that warms our days reaches across the void with its magnetic breath and paints the polar sky. It is physics made visible, and it is extraordinary.
