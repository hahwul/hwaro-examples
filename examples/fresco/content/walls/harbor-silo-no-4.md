Silo No. 4 is the largest wall Fresco has painted and the only one that is not, technically, a wall — it is galvanized steel curved through a full circle, which meant relearning how paint, scaffolding, and even the tracing-paper sketches all behave on a surface with no straight lines.

<!-- more -->

## Community sketch sessions

The Harbor District Association ran four sessions over a hot July, most of them on the pier facing the silo so people could sketch with the actual curve in front of them. Nearly every early sketch tried to fight the cylinder — put a rectangle on it, basically — until a retired harbor pilot suggested tracing the wake pattern boats actually leave rounding the silo at low tide. That became the wall: a single wave line wrapping the full circumference at three different heights, wide enough that it reads from the ferry terminal a half mile off.

## Wall survey and permit

Silo No. 4 was decommissioned in 2019 and sat unpainted through five years of harbor authority budget cycles before this commission cleared. The permit process was the longest of any Fresco wall to date — fourteen months — largely because the silo sits inside an active industrial harbor and every scaffold plan needed sign-off from both the harbor authority and the coast guard liaison office.

## Scaffold and paint days

Standard ladder scaffolding does not wrap a cylinder; the crew worked from a suspended ring rig that rotated around the silo on a track bolted to the roof collar, repainting the same relative section of wall from four different heights as the rig moved. Paint days ran twenty-six days across September, slowed twice by harbor wind advisories that made the suspended rig unsafe to operate.

## Scaffold day

The ring rig came down on September 30, timed to a harbor open house that drew the largest crowd of any Fresco dedication — including, by his own count, the retired pilot whose wake sketch became the final wall, back for the first time since the July sketch session that started it.
