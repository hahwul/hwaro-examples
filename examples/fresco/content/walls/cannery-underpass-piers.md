Six concrete piers hold up the Cannery Line where it crosses Dockside Avenue, and for as long as anyone in the neighborhood could remember they had been the exact gray of unwashed concrete — not tagged, not painted, just present, the way infrastructure usually is until someone actually looks at it.

<!-- more -->

## Community sketch sessions

Cannery Row's sketch sessions ran on the sidewalk directly under the piers, timed around train schedules so people could watch a train pass mid-conversation and sketch what the piers looked like from underneath, moving. That vantage point decided the design: a ladder motif repeated pier to pier, each rung offset slightly from its neighbor, so that a driver passing underneath at speed sees the rungs animate into a single climbing line — an effect nobody could confirm on paper and that Fresco only verified by driving the underpass at 25 miles an hour, twice, with a sketch pinned to the dashboard.

## Wall survey and permit

All six piers are owned by the regional rail authority, which required a track-adjacent work permit in addition to the usual public art sign-off — flaggers on-site any day the scaffold came within four feet of the active track, and a hard stop on all work during freight schedule windows, which shifted twice during the commission.

## Scaffold and paint days

Nineteen days across May, working two piers at a time with the crew rotating to keep four painters within flagger coverage at all times. The concrete took two coats of a masonry primer before the ladder motif's flat color could go down clean — fifty years of exhaust staining does not prime out in one pass.

## Scaffold day

The piers were unveiled the same week as the neighborhood's spring rail fair, which meant the scaffold strike doubled as the fair's opening event — the first Fresco dedication with a marching band, and the first where the client (the rail authority's regional art liaison) cut a ribbon strung between two of the newly painted piers.
