Sirocco is named for the wind, not the country — the hot, dust-laden gust that rolls up from the Sahara and leaves everything it touches a little warmer. That is more or less what a good roast smells like drifting out of a garage on a Saturday morning, and the name stuck before I had a better one.

## The setup

The drum is a 1 kg air-driven roaster bolted to a workbench that used to hold a table saw, wired to a cheap PID and two thermocouples — one at the bean mass, one at the exhaust. Green coffee comes from three importers who sell in 5–10 kg lots, small enough that I can burn through a bag in a season and never roast the same harvest twice.

## What "chasing the perfect crack" means here

Every batch gets the same treatment: a charge temperature chosen from the last three roasts of that origin, a first-crack time I do not fight once it arrives, and a drop decided by development time ratio rather than a fixed color chip. Some roasts land exactly where I planned. Most don't, and the log below is honest about that — a roast that scorched or stalled still gets written up, because the notes only work if they include the failures.

## Reading the log

Each entry lists origin, process, and roast date up top, plus a five-dot meter showing how far the batch went — one dot lit for a cinnamon-light Ethiopian, all five for a Sumatra pushed into second crack. Cupping notes come three to seven days after roasting, once the coffee has degassed enough to taste like itself.

If you roast at home too and want to compare notes, the [roast log](/roasts/) is the whole archive, newest batch first.
