The one bag in the cupboard that always looks wrong before it goes in the drum — wet-hulled green is a deep blue-green and irregular, nothing like the pale, uniform beans from the washed lots. It roasts wrong-looking too, right up until it isn't.

<!-- more -->

**Green:** 240 g, moisture 12.4% — the highest of any lot this year, typical for giling basah processing where the parchment comes off while the bean is still damp.

**Charge:** 208°C, pushed hot on purpose to drive off that extra moisture fast.

**Turning point:** 1:41, the longest stall of the year — high-moisture green always fights the recovery.

**First crack:** 9:30, and I let this one run. Second crack started at 12:40 and I pulled two minutes in, at 13:02, 231°C — Vienna by any chart, and I don't apologize for it with this bean.

**Development time ratio:** 27.8%, by far the longest DTR in the log. Wet-hulled Sumatra does not reward restraint the way the Ethiopian lots do; the earthy, low-acid character this processing is known for only shows up once you've pushed well past first crack.

Cupped at one week, because dark roasts need longer to shed the roasty edge: cedar, wet soil after rain, a whisper of dark chocolate, body like a stout. Almost no brightness left, which is exactly the assignment. This is the bag I reach for on the mornings I want coffee that tastes like weather, not fruit.
