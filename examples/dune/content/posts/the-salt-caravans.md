# The Salt Caravans: Following Ancient Trade Routes Across the Sahara

For over a thousand years, caravans have crossed the Sahara carrying slabs of salt from the mines of Taoudenni to the markets of Timbuktu. The route covers roughly 700 kilometers of some of the most inhospitable terrain on Earth. Against all logic, this trade continues today.

## The History of Saharan Salt

Salt was, for centuries, worth its weight in gold -- sometimes literally. The mines at Taoudenni, located in northern Mali, sit on the bed of an ancient lake that dried up millions of years ago, leaving deposits of high-quality salt beneath a thin layer of sand and rock.

The salt trade predates written records in the region. By the time Arab geographers began documenting West African commerce in the 9th century, the caravans were already ancient. Timbuktu grew wealthy as the meeting point between the salt coming south and the gold, ivory, and enslaved people being traded north.

## The Route

The classic caravan route from Taoudenni to Timbuktu follows a corridor through the Tanezrouft -- a region known as the "land of thirst." It is one of the most barren stretches of the Sahara, with virtually no water sources between wells that are sometimes separated by days of travel.

Caravans traditionally traveled in the cooler months between November and March. A typical caravan might include hundreds of camels, each carrying two slabs of salt weighing roughly 30 kilograms each. The journey took between two and three weeks in each direction.

Navigation relied on stars, wind patterns, and accumulated knowledge passed down through families of guides. A wrong turn in the Tanezrouft could mean death -- the desert offers no second chances with water.

## The Modern Caravan

Today, trucks carry most of the salt. But a diminishing number of traditional caravans still make the journey by camel. These are organized primarily by Tuareg and Arab communities for whom the caravan is not just an economic activity but a cultural tradition that defines their identity.

I joined a modern caravan for a four-day stretch south of Araouane, a nearly abandoned town that once served as a waypoint. The experience was humbling. The camels set the pace -- roughly four kilometers per hour -- and the days stretched into a rhythm governed entirely by sun and thirst.

The caravan leader, a Tuareg man named Ag Mahmoud, spoke little during the march. But in the evenings, over tea and dried dates, he described the route's landmarks with a precision that rivaled any GPS. A slightly darker patch of sand, a particular rock formation, the way wind left distinctive ridges at a certain point -- these were his coordinates.

## The Economics of Salt

A slab of salt purchased at Taoudenni for a few dollars can sell in Timbuktu for ten times as much, and for even more in markets further south. The markup reflects the cost of transport and the danger of the journey.

But the economics are fragile. Truck transport is faster and cheaper. Young men increasingly prefer other work. The traditional caravan may not survive another generation. What will be lost is not just a trade route but a living connection to a way of navigating the world that predates the compass, the map, and the satellite.

## A Route Worth Remembering

Walking even a short section of the caravan route changes your understanding of distance, time, and the human capacity for endurance. These paths were not roads in any modern sense. They were threads of knowledge, maintained across centuries by people who trusted their lives to memory and starlight.
