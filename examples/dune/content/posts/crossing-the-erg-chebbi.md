# Crossing the Erg Chebbi: Three Days in the Moroccan Sahara

The Erg Chebbi rises from the flat hammada like a mirage that refuses to dissolve. From the town of Merzouga, the dunes appear as a golden wall along the eastern horizon -- some reaching over 150 meters high. This is one of Morocco's two true ergs, and crossing it on foot remains one of the most accessible yet genuinely transformative desert experiences available.

## Day One: Into the Sand

We departed at dawn from the edge of Merzouga, following Hassan, a Berber guide whose family has lived at the edge of the erg for generations. The first hour crossed hard-packed reg -- the flat, stony desert that surrounds the sand sea. The footing was easy but the sun was already fierce.

The transition from reg to erg is abrupt. One moment you are walking on gravel. The next, your boots sink into sand that has the texture of fine flour. The rhythm of walking changes immediately. Each step requires more effort, and the pace slows to something the desert dictates rather than the traveler.

By midday, we had climbed the first major ridge. The view from the top was the first of many revelations: dune after dune, stretching east toward Algeria, their crests sharp as knife edges where the wind had sculpted them overnight.

## Day Two: The Interior

The second day was spent entirely within the erg. Without reference points, distance becomes meaningless. A dune that appears ten minutes away takes an hour to reach. The landscape repeats -- ridge, trough, ridge, trough -- but no two formations are identical.

Hassan navigated without instruments, reading the angle of the sand, the direction of ripple patterns, and landmarks invisible to my untrained eye. When I asked how he knew where we were, he said simply, "The sand tells you, if you know how to listen."

We stopped for tea at noon in the shade of a dune face. Hassan boiled water over a small gas burner and poured mint tea with the practiced precision of someone who has performed this ritual ten thousand times. In the silence that followed, the only sound was the wind moving sand grains along the crest above us -- a faint hissing, like static from a radio tuned to an empty frequency.

## Day Three: The Descent

The final morning, we climbed the highest dune we had encountered -- a towering crescent-shaped barchan that stood alone like a monument. From its peak at sunrise, the world divided cleanly: to the east, the amber dunes continued toward the horizon; to the west, the dark hammada stretched back toward Merzouga and the Atlas Mountains beyond.

The descent from the erg felt like a return from somewhere outside ordinary time. The town, when it came into view, seemed impossibly detailed after days of sand and sky.

## What the Desert Teaches

Three days is enough to understand why people return. The Sahara does not offer comfort or entertainment. It offers clarity -- the kind that comes only from being reduced to the essentials. Water, shade, direction, and the slow passage of hours under an unobstructed sky.
