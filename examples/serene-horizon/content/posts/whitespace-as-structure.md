Whitespace is not absence. It is a structural element with the same weight as a column rule or a heading. A page composed entirely of text and margin can communicate hierarchy without a single decorative flourish, provided the spacing is intentional.

The mistake is treating whitespace as the leftover after all the content is placed. Reverse the relationship. Define the gaps first—gutters between columns, padding inside containers, breathing room around section breaks—and let the content take whatever room remains. The result is a layout that feels deliberate even when nothing on the page draws the eye.

Three rules cover most cases. First, whitespace inside an element should be smaller than whitespace between elements; otherwise the boundaries dissolve. Second, related items share tighter spacing than unrelated items, so proximity does the work of grouping without borders or boxes. Third, the largest negative spaces appear at the highest level of structure—section to section—so the reader perceives the page as a few large blocks rather than a continuous stream.

A practical test: print the page in grayscale, blur it slightly, and check whether the structure still reads. If everything blends into a uniform smear, the whitespace is doing too little. If the page breaks into legible blocks, the structure is honest.

Whitespace is the cheapest design tool available and the one most often spent carelessly. Spend it on purpose.
