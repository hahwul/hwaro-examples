## 1.1 Background and rationale

Population-level early screening programs have been proposed across multiple disease areas as a mechanism to reduce late-stage disease incidence, improve quality-adjusted survival, and shift resource use away from high-cost tertiary care. Decision-makers are routinely asked whether such programs represent good value for money at commonly used willingness-to-pay thresholds. The literature contains a wide range of published estimates, often with limited harmonization of parameter assumptions across studies [1, 2, 3].

This study evaluates the cost-effectiveness of biennial screening from age 45, assuming a centrally administered program with standardized test performance and realistic population-level uptake. We conduct the analysis from a healthcare-sector perspective and a lifetime horizon, using a six-state Markov microsimulation calibrated to age-specific incidence and mortality.

## 1.2 Prior literature

Fourteen cost-effectiveness studies of early screening programs with similar disease models were identified through systematic review (Markham et al. 2024). Published ICERs ranged from $8,100 to $78,400 per QALY gained, with heterogeneity primarily attributable to assumptions about test sensitivity (range 0.62 &ndash; 0.94), screening interval (annual vs. biennial vs. triennial), and discount rate. The present analysis addresses a gap in the literature by reporting probabilistic sensitivity intervals alongside traditional deterministic tornado plots, and by making explicit the willingness-to-pay threshold at which acceptability transitions.

## 1.3 Objectives

We evaluate three specific questions:

1. What is the incremental cost-effectiveness ratio (ICER) of biennial early screening from age 45, compared with usual care, over a lifetime horizon?
2. At a $50,000 per QALY willingness-to-pay threshold, what proportion of PSA iterations are cost-effective?
3. Which parameters contribute most to uncertainty in the ICER, and what are the implications for program design?

## 1.4 Decision problem

| ELEMENT | VALUE |
| --- | --- |
| Comparators | Biennial screening from age 45 vs. usual care (no screening) |
| Population | Adults aged 45 &ndash; 85, general risk |
| Time horizon | Lifetime |
| Perspective | Healthcare sector |
| Discount rate | 3.0 pct for costs and effects |
| Outcomes | Life-years, QALYs, late-stage cases averted |
| Decision rule | ICER &lt; willingness-to-pay threshold |
