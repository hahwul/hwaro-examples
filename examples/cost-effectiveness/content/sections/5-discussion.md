## 5.1 Principal findings

At commonly used willingness-to-pay thresholds, biennial early screening from age 45 is cost-effective in the majority of probabilistic iterations. The base-case ICER of $23,944 per QALY falls comfortably below the $50,000 per QALY benchmark frequently cited in the literature, and the 92.4 pct probability of cost-effectiveness at that threshold provides strong statistical support for the conclusion.

Subgroup results (Table 3) demonstrate that earlier initiation produces more favorable ICERs. This is consistent with the mechanistic intuition that incremental QALYs accrue over a longer follow-up window when initiation is earlier.

## 5.2 Consistency with prior literature

The ICER estimate reported here falls within the range of published cost-effectiveness estimates for comparable programs ($8,100 &ndash; $78,400 per QALY) identified in our systematic review. Our estimate is in the middle of that range, consistent with our use of conservative assumptions on test sensitivity (0.86) and uptake (0.68). Studies that used higher uptake assumptions reported lower ICERs, which is consistent with the dominance of uptake as the single most influential parameter in our tornado analysis.

## 5.3 Limitations

Several limitations warrant mention.

1. **Short-horizon observational data.** Progression probabilities (p_prog, p_detect) were estimated from cohort data with a median follow-up of 11 years. Extrapolation to a lifetime horizon introduces structural uncertainty not fully captured by PSA.
2. **Homogeneous uptake assumption.** The model assumes uniform biennial uptake across the cohort. In practice, uptake varies by socioeconomic and demographic factors, and heterogeneous uptake could change both costs and effects in ways not captured here.
3. **Healthcare-sector perspective.** The base-case analysis excludes productivity losses, caregiver costs, and out-of-pocket expenses. The scenario analysis addresses this (Section 4.3) but a full societal-perspective analysis is warranted for decision-making at the national level.
4. **Single-test assumption.** The analysis assumes a single standardized test across the program. Real-world programs often use confirmatory or follow-up tests, which would increase program cost but potentially reduce false positives.

## 5.4 Policy implications

Three implications follow from these findings.

First, **ICER &lt; &lambda; is a necessary but not sufficient condition for program adoption.** The 92.4 pct acceptability probability at $50,000 per QALY provides strong economic support, but implementation requires budget impact analysis (not reported here) and consideration of opportunity cost in the specific healthcare system.

Second, **program design should prioritize uptake.** The tornado analysis (Figure 4) shows that uptake is the single largest driver of ICER uncertainty. Investments in communication, access, and reminder systems that raise uptake from 0.55 to 0.80 shift the ICER from $34,120 to $17,560 per QALY. That is the largest lever available to program planners.

Third, **biennial rather than triennial.** While triennial screening has a marginally lower ICER ($21,100 per QALY), biennial screening produces 57 pct more late-stage cases averted per 1,000 screened. For a public-health program, late-stage averted cases is likely the more relevant outcome.

## 5.5 Conclusions

Biennial population-level early screening from age 45 is cost-effective at a $50,000 per QALY willingness-to-pay threshold with 92.4 pct probability under plausible parameter ranges. Uptake rate is the single dominant driver of uncertainty, and program implementation should prioritize investments that raise uptake over investments that raise test sensitivity.
