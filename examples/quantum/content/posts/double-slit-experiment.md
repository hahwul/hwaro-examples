## The Experiment That Changed Everything

In 1801, Thomas Young demonstrated that light behaves like a wave by passing it through two narrow slits and observing the resulting interference pattern on a screen. Over a century later, quantum mechanics would show that this experiment hides something far more radical: a single particle can interfere with itself.

When electrons are fired one at a time through the double-slit apparatus, they still produce an interference pattern -- as if each individual electron passes through both slits simultaneously. The electron exists in a superposition of states, taking all possible paths at once, until it is detected.

## The Observer Effect

The truly unsettling aspect of the double-slit experiment emerges when we attempt to determine which slit the electron actually passes through. The moment we place a detector at one of the slits, the interference pattern vanishes. The electron behaves as a classical particle, choosing one definite path.

This is not a matter of the detector physically disturbing the electron. The act of gaining information -- of the quantum state becoming correlated with an external system -- is sufficient to collapse the superposition. The wave function collapses not because of physical disturbance, but because of entanglement with a measuring apparatus.

## Wave Function and Probability

The interference pattern is a direct visualization of the wave function squared: the probability density of detecting the particle at each location. The alternating bands of bright and dark fringes correspond to regions of constructive and destructive interference between probability amplitudes.

This is the central mystery. The wave function is not a wave of matter, nor a wave of energy. It is a wave of probability amplitude -- a mathematical object whose absolute square gives us the likelihood of particular measurement outcomes.

## Implications for Reality

The double-slit experiment forces us to confront a fundamental question: what is the nature of quantum reality between measurements? Does the electron truly pass through both slits, or does it take a single definite path that we simply cannot know?

Interpretations of quantum mechanics offer radically different answers. The Copenhagen interpretation holds that quantum systems do not have definite properties prior to measurement. The many-worlds interpretation suggests that both outcomes occur in branching parallel realities. Pilot wave theory proposes a hidden guiding wave that determines the particle's path deterministically.

After more than a century, the question remains open. The double-slit experiment is not merely a historical curiosity -- it is a window into the deepest unresolved questions in all of physics.

## Modern Variations

Contemporary experiments have extended Young's original setup in remarkable ways. Researchers have demonstrated interference with molecules containing hundreds of atoms. Quantum erasure experiments show that the interference pattern can be restored even after which-path information has been obtained, provided that information is subsequently erased before detection.

Delayed-choice experiments, proposed by John Wheeler and later realized experimentally, suggest that the particle appears to retroactively choose its behavior based on a measurement decision made after it has already passed through the apparatus. The universe seems to demand consistency between what was observed and what will be observed, regardless of the temporal order.

The double-slit experiment is not a puzzle with a solution awaiting discovery. It is the puzzle that defines the quantum world.
