# Phosphorescence

Phosphorescence is a slow form of photoluminescence. A material absorbs photons, electrons are promoted into excited singlet states, and then through intersystem crossing they fall into a triplet state. The return to ground is forbidden by ordinary selection rules, so the emission proceeds slowly, sometimes over seconds, sometimes over hours.

The familiar consequence is the afterglow on a watch dial or a safety sign. The light is dim, but it persists long after the room has gone dark.

## Materials

Common phosphors include zinc sulfide doped with copper, strontium aluminate doped with europium and dysprosium, and various rare-earth oxysulfides. The dopants introduce trap states that delay recombination. Without the traps, the material would simply fluoresce and fall silent.

## In the gallery

Each piece in this series uses a different dopant ratio cast into a low-iron glass blank. The viewer is invited to walk through the room with the lights on, then sit while the room is darkened in stages. The work emerges, holds, and fades. The emission curves are roughly exponential, but the perception is not. The eye adapts faster than the glass dims, which extends the visible interval.

The afterglow is a record of the light that was. It is also an argument that the boundary between reflective and emissive surfaces is a matter of timescale.
