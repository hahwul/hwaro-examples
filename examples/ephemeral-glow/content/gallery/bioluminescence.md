# Bioluminescence

Bioluminescence is the emission of light by living organisms. The reaction is enzymatic. A small molecule called a luciferin is oxidized in the presence of an enzyme called a luciferase. The product is left in an excited electronic state, and the relaxation to the ground state releases a photon. Different luciferins produce different colors, ranging from blue and green in marine species to yellow and red in some terrestrial beetles.

Roughly four-fifths of known bioluminescent species live in the open ocean, where ambient light falls off rapidly with depth. Light becomes a scarce signaling channel, and many species have evolved ways to use it.

## Functions

The functions of bioluminescence are diverse. Anglerfish use lures stocked with symbiotic bacteria to attract prey. Some squid produce a counter-illumination glow on their undersides to erase their silhouettes against the surface. Fireflies use flash patterns for mate selection, with timing precise enough to identify species. Dinoflagellates flash when disturbed, which appears to startle predators or attract larger predators that hunt the smaller ones.

## In the gallery

The pieces in this series do not use any living material. They use sealed quartz cells holding the same chemistry that drives the firefly reaction, with regenerating cofactors and a slow oxygen feed. The reaction proceeds at a controlled rate, producing a steady greenish output for the duration of the exhibit. When the cofactors are exhausted, the cells go dark and are removed.

The work is a finite light source. It is also a study of how life arranges chemistry to make a signal carry across darkness.
