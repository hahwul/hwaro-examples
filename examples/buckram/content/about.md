Buckram began as a smeared, dog-eared notebook shared between three cooks who
kept ending up on the same expeditions and kept making the same mistakes.
After one trip where we carried four days of fuel for a six-day route, we
started writing things down properly. This is the fair copy.

## Why a manual, and why cloth-bound

*Buckram* is the stiff, sized cloth used to bind books meant to be used hard —
library ledgers, field manuals, things that live in a pocket and get rained on.
That is the ambition here: a reference you can trust cold, tired, and by
headlamp, where the type is large, the tables are ruled, and nothing depends on
a screen you forgot to charge.

## How to read it

The chapters are ordered like a kit list, lightest first, and each one carries
a weight in grams. That weight is a joke with a straight face: it is the
*printing* weight, roughly what the chapter would add to your pack on 90&nbsp;gsm
paper if you carried it up the hill. You will not, of course. The food weights
inside the chapters are the real ones, and those you should trust to the gram.

- Press **Ctrl&nbsp;K** (or **⌘&nbsp;K**) anywhere to search the whole manual.
- Use the chapter list in the sidebar to jump around; the prev/next links at
  the foot of each chapter walk you through in order.
- The ration tables are the heart of it. If you read one thing before a trip,
  read the ledgers in [Ration Math](/chapters/ration-math/).

## A note on judgement

Every number here is a starting point, not a rule. Mountains are specific:
your stove, your altitude, your appetite, and your weather will all argue with
the tables. Cook the plan a few times at home before you trust it at 5,000&nbsp;m,
and always carry one more day of hot drinks than the spreadsheet says you need.

This edition was written at the Marrow Lake hut and tested from valley
trailheads to a high, cold col where the water never quite boiled.
