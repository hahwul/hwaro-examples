A stove is the one tool on an expedition that turns snow into water and water
into dinner. Everything else can be improvised. This chapter is about picking
a burner that will light when your hands are numb, and running it so it does
not quit at the worst possible hour.

<!-- more -->

## Pick the burner for the temperature

There are three families worth carrying, and the choice is almost entirely
about how cold it will get overnight.

- **Upright canister.** Light, instant, and lazy in the best way — until the
  canister drops below about 0&nbsp;°C and the pressure sags. Good for summer
  and shoulder-season valleys.
- **Remote canister with a preheat tube.** The canister sits beside the burner
  and can be inverted to feed liquid fuel through the tube, which vaporises on
  the way. This is the quiet workhorse of cold three-season trips.
- **Liquid fuel (white gas).** Heavy, loud, and utterly reliable at depth. If
  the forecast has a minus sign and two digits, this is what you bring.

## Priming a liquid-fuel stove

Priming means burning a small pool of fuel to heat the generator tube so the
stove can vaporise the rest. Rushing it is how you get a fireball.

```text
1. Pump 15–20 strokes. Cold fuel needs more pressure.
2. Crack the valve for one second — a teaspoon of fuel in the spirit cup.
3. Close the valve fully. Light the pool. Let it burn down ~30 s.
4. As the flame goes blue and low, open the valve slowly.
5. If it sputters yellow, close and prime again. Never force it.
```

A stove that roars steady and blue is primed. A stove that spits orange is a
stove that has not finished its warm-up — give it another cup and wait. How
much fuel to carry for all this warming-up is its own calculation, covered in
[Reading Fuel](/chapters/reading-fuel/).

## Wind is the real fuel thief

An unshielded burner in a 20&nbsp;km/h breeze can waste more than half its
heat. Carry a foil windscreen, but leave a finger-width gap on the upwind side
so the canister does not overheat. On snow, build a low wall of blocks and let
the kitchen sit in its lee. The flame should be yours, not the weather's.
