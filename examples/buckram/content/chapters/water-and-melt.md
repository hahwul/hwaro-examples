Above the snowline, water stops being something you find and becomes something
you manufacture. It is slow, it burns fuel, and it is the single most common
reason a strong team turns around. Treat the melt pot as seriously as the map.

<!-- more -->

## Never dry-melt a pot

Dropping snow into a bone-dry hot pot scorches the bottom layer, taints every
litre with a burnt smell, and wastes fuel. Always start with a **seed** — a
cup of liquid water held back from the night before.

```text
Melt loop:
  1. Start with ~200 ml seed water in the pot.
  2. Add snow a fistful at a time, keeping liquid over the base.
  3. Pack snow down; airy snow is mostly nothing.
  4. Reserve a cup of finished water as the next seed.
```

Prefer dense, old snow over fresh powder — a pot of powder can be ninety
percent air and will disappoint you twice.

## Treat it anyway

Meltwater looks pure and often is not: wind carries dust, and any snow near a
camp has seen boots. High and remote, a rolling boil is enough — at altitude,
reaching the boil at all has already done the work, so you do not need to hold
it for minutes. Lower and near animal country, add a chemical treatment or a
filter. When in doubt, boil — though as [Altitude Baking](/chapters/altitude-baking/)
explains, "boiling" high up is a cooler affair than it sounds.

## Drink before you are thirsty

Altitude dehydrates you three ways at once: dry air, hard breathing, and a
blunted thirst reflex. Aim for **three to four litres a day** and check the
oldest, bluntest gauge there is.

> Pale straw is on track. Deep amber means you are already behind, and behind
> at altitude is how a headache becomes a bad night.

Melt a big batch each evening into insulated bottles, tuck one in the foot of
the sleeping bag against morning ice, and start the day a litre ahead.
