The last chapter is the one people skip and then regret. A clean kitchen does
not get you up the mountain, but a dirty one gets the whole team sick, and a
broken stove you cannot fix ends the trip. Teardown is a skill, not an
afterthought.

<!-- more -->

## Washing up on a water budget

You will not spare litres of hot water for dishes, and you should not need to.

- **Eat it clean.** Scrape and wipe the pot with bread or a spatula before any
  water touches it. Less residue, less washing.
- **The one-cup method.** A single cup of hot water, a drop of biodegradable
  soap, a cloth. Wash, then use the grey water to pre-rinse tomorrow's pot.
- **Sterilise by heat, not soap.** A quick boil in the pot does more against
  stomach bugs than any amount of cold scrubbing.

Strain food scraps from grey water, pack the solids into your rubbish bag, and
scatter the water wide, well away from camp and any stream.

## When the stove sulks

A field repair kit is three items and five minutes of knowing how.

```text
Symptom            Likely cause          Fix
-----------------  --------------------  ---------------------------
Weak yellow flame  clogged jet           clear with the wire pricker
Fuel leak at pump  dry pump cup          a drop of oil, work it in
Won't hold press.  cracked O-ring        swap from the spares baggie
Roaring, no heat   air in the fuel line  re-prime, bleed the line
```

Carry a jet pricker, a spare pump cup, and two O-rings. That baggie weighs
almost nothing and has saved more summits than any luxury item. If the flame
never came right in the first place, the problem may be upstream in
[Stove Craft](/chapters/stove-craft/) rather than in the stove itself.

## Leave the site lighter than you found it

Do a slow sweep of the kitchen at first light, when low sun catches every
foil corner and twist tie. Pack out all of it — including the scraps that
"will biodegrade," because at altitude nothing does, and an orange peel can sit
frozen and bright for years.

> The best-run camp is the one the next team cannot tell you were there.

Weigh your rubbish bag at the car if you want the last, quiet satisfaction of
the trip: it should be nearly as heavy as the food you carried in, which means
you brought the mountain nothing it has to keep.
