The Fayum mummy portraits, painted in encaustic nearly two thousand years ago, remain vivid today. No other painting medium from antiquity has survived with such startling color fidelity. The beeswax encapsulates pigment so completely that oxidation and moisture cannot reach it.

This permanence is not accidental. It is a direct consequence of the process. When wax is heated and fused, it undergoes a molecular bonding that seals each layer into an impenetrable surface. Unlike oil paint, which dries through chemical oxidation and eventually cracks, encaustic cures through cooling. The result is a medium that is effectively timeless.

### The Lesson for Design

Permanence comes from structure, not decoration. A well-defined border will outlast a thousand drop shadows. A solid color block communicates more confidently than a gradient that fades to nothing. The boldest designs are the ones that commit fully to their choices.

### Materials and Methods

The encaustic painter works with a limited toolkit:

- A heated palette to keep wax molten
- Natural bristle brushes or metal tools
- A heat source for fusing layers
- Pigmented wax cakes in a deliberate, curated palette

This economy of means forces clarity. Every tool has a specific purpose. Every color choice must justify its presence on the palette. There is no room for excess.
