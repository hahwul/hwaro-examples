The earliest known use of encaustic was purely practical. Greek shipbuilders mixed pigment into beeswax and applied it to the hulls of their warships. The wax waterproofed the wood while the pigment identified the fleet. Art emerged from function.

By the first century AD, encaustic had evolved into one of the most sophisticated painting techniques in the ancient world. Pliny the Elder described three distinct methods of application. The most refined involved using a metal stylus called a _cestrum_ to work heated wax into an ivory or wood surface.

### The Fayum Portraits

The most celebrated surviving examples of ancient encaustic are the Fayum mummy portraits from Roman Egypt. These panel paintings, created between the first and third centuries AD, were placed over the faces of mummified remains.

What makes them extraordinary is their realism. These are not idealized representations. They are specific, individual faces rendered with a naturalism that would not be matched in Western art for over a thousand years. The medium made this possible. The molten wax could be blended and textured on the surface with an immediacy that fresco and tempera could not offer.

### The Medieval Silence

After the fall of Rome, encaustic largely disappeared from Western art. The knowledge of the technique was not so much lost as abandoned. Fresco and tempera were simpler, cheaper, and better suited to the large-scale wall paintings that medieval patrons demanded.

It was not until the eighteenth century that European artists rediscovered the technique, inspired by archaeological finds at Herculaneum and Pompeii. Today, encaustic is practiced by a dedicated community of artists who value its unique combination of immediacy, permanence, and luminous depth.
