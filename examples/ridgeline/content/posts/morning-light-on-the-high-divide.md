## Overview

The High Divide Trail in Olympic National Park is one of the finest ridge walks in the Pacific Northwest. Running along the spine of a broad ridge above the Hoh River valley, the trail offers continuous views of Mount Olympus, the Bailey Range, and a chain of alpine lakes that sit in glacially carved basins below the ridgeline.

We walked it in early September, when the summer crowds had thinned and the first hints of autumn color were beginning to show in the huckleberry patches.

## Trailhead

Begin at the Sol Duc Trailhead, reached via Sol Duc Hot Springs Road from Highway 101. A wilderness camping permit is required for overnight stays and must be reserved in advance during peak season. The park service limits the number of permits for the High Divide area, which keeps the trail from feeling overcrowded.

## Day One: Sol Duc to Heart Lake

### Sol Duc Falls

The first two kilometers follow the Sol Duc River through old-growth forest to Sol Duc Falls, a powerful cascade that drops through a narrow basalt gorge. The forest here is dense and green, carpeted with ferns and moss. The air smells of cedar and wet earth.

### Ascent to the Divide

Beyond the falls, the trail begins its long climb toward the ridge. The grade is steady but never punishing, switchbacking through increasingly open forest. As elevation increases, the trees thin and views begin to open to the south.

At around 1,400 meters, the trail breaks out of the tree line entirely and enters the subalpine zone. The meadows here are spectacular in late summer, filled with lupine, aster, and mountain heather. Marmots whistle from their rocky lookouts.

### Heart Lake Camp

Heart Lake sits in a small basin just below the main ridge. The designated campsites are well-situated with views south toward the Hoh valley. Water is available directly from the lake. We set up camp with two hours of daylight remaining, enough time to walk up to the ridge for sunset.

The evening light on Mount Olympus was extraordinary. The glaciers caught the low sun and turned gold, then pink, then a deep blue-grey as the light faded. The silence at that hour was complete.

## Day Two: High Divide and Descent

### The Ridge Walk

The High Divide Trail proper runs for approximately 4 kilometers along the top of the ridge. The walking is easy on a well-maintained trail, but the views demand frequent stops. To the south, Mount Olympus dominates the horizon with its network of glaciers. To the north, the forested valleys of the Sol Duc drainage stretch toward the Strait of Juan de Fuca.

We started walking before sunrise. The pre-dawn light turned the ridge into a silhouette against the eastern sky, and we watched the sun come up over the Bailey Range from a promontory at the trail's high point. The mountains emerged from darkness in layers, each range a slightly lighter shade of blue.

### Bogachiel Peak Side Trip

A short spur trail leads to the summit of Bogachiel Peak at 1,595 meters. The detour adds roughly 2 kilometers to the day but provides a panoramic view that is worth the effort. On a clear morning, you can see from the Pacific Ocean to the interior peaks of the Olympic Range.

### Descent via Deer Lake

The return route drops south from the divide to Deer Lake, another scenic basin with designated camping, before descending through forest back to the Sol Duc valley. The final kilometers follow the river on a gentle grade back to the trailhead.

## Conditions and Timing

The High Divide is typically snow-free from mid-July through October. Earlier visits may require route-finding across snow patches on the north-facing slopes. September offers the best combination of stable weather, thinner crowds, and autumn color.

## Gear Notes

- Backpacking setup for one overnight
- Bear canister (required in Olympic National Park backcountry)
- Water filter
- Layers for exposed ridge walking (wind and temperature drops are common)
- Trekking poles for the long descent on day two
- Camera with a wide-angle lens, because the views deserve it

## Water Sources

Sol Duc River at the trailhead, Heart Lake at camp, and Deer Lake on the descent. Several small streams cross the trail at lower elevations. The ridge itself has no water, so carry at least 2 liters for the exposed section.
