Anglage, sometimes called bevelling, is the act of cutting and polishing a 45-degree facet along the outer edge of a movement component. Bridges, levers, click springs, and even the inner corners of cut-out windows are treated this way. Done well, the bevel runs at a uniform width along the entire perimeter, mirrors the light cleanly, and meets neighbouring surfaces at crisp arrises.

The process begins with a graver. The artisan removes material to roughly establish the angle, paying particular attention to internal angles—the sharp inward points where two bevels meet. Industrial machining cannot produce a true internal angle without a tool radius; only hand work yields that pin-sharp geometry. The width of the bevel is held constant by sight and feel, gauged against the grain of the surface and the line of the edge.

## From Grain to Mirror

After the graver, progressively finer abrasives are applied: emery paper wrapped on hardwood pegs, then diamond paste on tin, zinc, or boxwood lapping tools. Each grade removes the scratches of the previous one until the bevel takes on a black-polish—an optical mirror that, when rotated past a light source, alternates between full reflection and complete darkness.

Black polishing is the more demanding cousin of anglage. It is performed on flats, on screw heads, and on the outer surfaces of certain steel components. The work is judged not on a polishing wheel but under a loupe, under angled light, and against the standard set by a reference piece.

A finished bridge will carry several finishes side by side: a Côtes de Genève top, a perlage underside, brushed flanks, and a mirror bevel. The transitions between them are themselves a measure of the workshop. A bevel that fades, a corner that is rounded by a buffing wheel, an internal angle that has been softened to save labour—these tell a careful eye what kind of movement is being held.
