A movement submitted to the Contrôle Officiel Suisse des Chronomètres is tested in five positions and at three temperatures over fifteen days. Each unit receives a numbered bulletin recording its results against seven criteria. The aggregate verdict is binary—pass or fail—but the individual figures tell a longer story about the calibre and the workshop that prepared it.

The first and most familiar criterion is mean daily rate. Across the testing period, the movement must run within minus four and plus six seconds per day. The width of this window reflects the reality that mechanical oscillators are not symmetric; a small positive bias is preferable to a negative one because winding state during normal use tends to drag the rate downward.

## Beyond the Headline Number

Mean variation in rates measures the consistency between positions. Greatest variation in rates flags the worst position relative to the average. Difference between rates in horizontal and vertical positions captures the asymmetry that arises from balance wheel poising and pivot friction. Greatest variation in rates per day, calculated within a single position, exposes lubricant problems and amplitude instability.

The thermal criterion compares the rate at eight degrees Celsius with the rate at thirty-eight degrees, divided by the temperature interval. A well-compensated balance and hairspring assembly will register a thermal coefficient of less than six-tenths of a second per day per degree.

The final criterion is rate resumption. The movement is tested at the start of the period, then again at the end after returning to standard position and temperature. The difference between these two figures captures long-term drift due to settling, lubricant migration, or changes in mainspring torque profile.

A bulletin is therefore not a quality stamp so much as a signed record. Two movements with identical mean daily rates can produce very different bulletins, and a careful collector reads all seven lines.
