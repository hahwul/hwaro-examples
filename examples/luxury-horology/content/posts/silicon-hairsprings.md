The hairspring sets the rate of a mechanical watch. Its stiffness, mass, and geometry govern how often the balance wheel reverses direction, and any deviation in those quantities translates directly into timekeeping error. For most of the twentieth century, the dominant material was Nivarox, an iron-nickel-chromium alloy with a low thermal coefficient and useful elastic limits. It is a remarkable material, but it is magnetic, it work-hardens during forming, and its terminal curve must be hand-shaped to achieve concentric breathing.

Silicon, etched from a single-crystal wafer using deep reactive-ion etching, sidesteps several of these constraints at once. The hairspring is produced in three-dimensional form already, with whatever inner and outer terminal curves the designer specifies, requiring no manual adjustment of the geometry. It is non-magnetic, which is a meaningful advantage given the density of magnetic fields in modern environments. Its mass is roughly one-quarter that of an equivalent metal spring, reducing the kinetic energy lost to escapement impulse.

## Thermal Compensation in Layers

Pure silicon has a thermal coefficient that is incompatible with chronometric performance. The solution, demonstrated by several Swiss research collaborations during the 2000s, is a controlled silicon dioxide growth on the surface. Oxide and silicon respond to temperature in opposite directions, and the right thickness ratio produces a composite spring whose effective elastic modulus is essentially flat over the operating range of a wristwatch.

The remaining limitations are mechanical. Silicon is brittle. A single sharp shock at the wrong angle can fracture a spring that would have merely deformed in steel, and broken springs cannot be re-bent. The geometry of the spring and its mounting must absorb impact through compliant features rather than yielding. Service intervals also assume the spring will be replaced as a unit, since hand correction of a damaged silicon coil is not a meaningful repair path.
