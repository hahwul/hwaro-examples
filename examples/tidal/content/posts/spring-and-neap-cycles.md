Tidal range is not constant from day to day. Every two weeks it swells to a maximum and then collapses to a minimum, tracing a slow envelope over the daily rise and fall. The two extremes are called spring tides and neap tides, and the schedule is set by the relative positions of the sun and moon.

### Alignment Drives the Range

Spring tides occur near the new and full moon, when the sun and moon lie roughly along the same axis. Their gravitational pulls add, lifting high water higher and dropping low water lower. Neap tides occur near the first and last quarter, when the two bodies pull at right angles. The forces partly cancel, and the range narrows.

### Magnitudes in Practice

In a typical mid-latitude port, spring range may be 1.4 times the mean range, while neap range may fall to 0.6. On a coast with a 4-metre mean range, that is roughly 5.6 metres on springs and 2.4 metres on neaps. The asymmetry matters for harbour engineering, intertidal ecology, and any work that depends on access to the lower foreshore.

### Lag from Theory

Springs do not arrive on the day of the new or full moon. Most coasts show an age of the tide between one and three days, caused by friction and the finite speed at which the tidal bulge propagates. Local tide tables already account for the lag, so plan from the printed values rather than from astronomical dates.

Watch the cycle for a full month and the pattern becomes intuitive. The fortnightly rhythm is older than any harbour; the wood and stone of every working port were sized to fit it.
