Tide tables list the predicted high and low water levels for a given station, usually in twelve-hour blocks. Most charts include four columns: the time of high water, the height in metres, the time of low water, and the corresponding lunitidal interval. The numbers come from harmonic analysis of long-running gauge records, then projected forward using the moon and sun positions.

### How the Numbers Are Built

The tide is the sum of many small periodic terms called constituents. The principal lunar semidiurnal term, M2, accounts for most of the rise and fall on open coasts. Solar terms add or subtract from M2 to give spring and neap cycles. Coastal stations also include shallow-water terms that capture local resonance inside bays and estuaries.

### Reading a Daily Entry

A line such as `06:14  4.8m  /  12:31  0.6m` means high water at 6:14 a.m. reaching 4.8 metres above chart datum, followed by low water at 12:31 p.m. at 0.6 metres. The range that morning is 4.2 metres. Compare this to the listed mean range to decide whether the day is closer to springs or neaps.

### Field Adjustments

Always confirm the time zone printed at the top of the table. Some publications use Universal Time year round; others switch with daylight saving. Wind and atmospheric pressure can shift the predicted height by several decimetres, so add a margin when working close to either extreme.

For small-craft access across a bar, plan your transit for the third hour after low water, when the flood is strongest and the depth is rising fastest. For shore collecting or photography, the hour either side of low water gives the widest exposed flat.
