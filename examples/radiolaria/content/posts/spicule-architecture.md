A radiolarian skeleton is not a hollow sphere. It is a sphere reinforced by spicules, which are needle-shaped projections of opaline silica that radiate from the central capsule outward through the cortex. The spicules carry compressive load, distribute hydrostatic pressure, and provide the mounting points from which the soft cytoplasm extends its pseudopodia into the surrounding water.

The arrangement of spicules is rarely random. In the order Spumellaria the spicules tend to align along axes of icosahedral symmetry, producing the regular twelve-fold or twenty-fold radial patterns that catch the eye under transmitted light. In Nassellaria the spicules form a basal tripod, three struts angled outward from a single point, with secondary projections layered above. The geometry is mechanical first and ornamental only as a consequence.

Each spicule is grown from a single nucleation site within the central capsule and extends through guided deposition of silica along an organic template. Once growth ceases, the spicule is structurally inert. It cannot be repaired or remodeled. The organism therefore commits to the geometry it builds at maturity, and the fossil record preserves that commitment with high fidelity. A spicule from the Cretaceous looks the same in a thin section today as it did when it sank from the photic zone.

For a designer studying these forms, the lesson is that ornament and structure can be the same line. The spicules do not decorate the shell. They are the shell, drawn in their thinnest possible form. Removing one removes both the support and the silhouette at once. The constraint produces the shape, and the shape produces the identity.
