The Baltic basin holds the largest known reserves of amber on the planet. Most pieces traceable to that region originate from a single Eocene-age forest that once stretched across what is now the seabed.

## What the Inclusions Show

Roughly one in a thousand specimens from Baltic deposits contains a recognizable inclusion — a fragment of leaf, a wing, or, more rarely, a complete arthropod. The capture is faithful: hairs, joints, the gloss on a chitin shell remain visible under modern lenses.

These inclusions are still the primary record we have of small forest animals from that era. Bones do not survive at this resolution; resin does.

## A Note on Provenance

Authenticated Baltic amber carries a fluorescence signature under longwave UV that copal — a much younger resin — does not. The two are easy to confuse by appearance and impossible to confuse under proper lighting.
