## The matrix impression

<p class="lede">The flong is the negative of a page. Where type stands raised on the forme, the flong bears an indentation. Where spaces sit low between the letters, the flong rises in flat plateaus. To look at a flong is to see language in reverse -- mirrored, inverted, pressed into soft material like a footprint in wet clay. The flong is not the text; it is the absence of the text, shaped precisely enough to recreate it in molten metal.</p>

## Preparing the flong

The preparation of a flong requires careful layering of materials. The process begins with a sheet of thin tissue paper, dampened and laid over the locked-up type forme. Over this, the maker layers several sheets of blotting paper or specially prepared flong paper -- a soft, absorbent stock that takes impressions readily. The stack is brushed firmly with a stiff-bristled brush, forcing the damp paper into every letter cavity, every rule, every ornament on the forme.

The brushing must be thorough but controlled. Too little pressure produces a shallow, indistinct impression that will cast poorly. Too much pressure can tear the tissue layer or distort the softer areas of the mold. The experienced flong maker develops a feel for the material, sensing through the brush when the paper has fully conformed to the type surface.

## Drying the matrix

Once the impression is complete, the flong must be dried before it can receive molten metal. In newspaper stereotyping, speed was essential -- editions could not wait for molds to air-dry. Hot-press drying, in which the flong was placed between heated plates under pressure, reduced drying time from hours to minutes. The temperature had to be carefully controlled: too cool and the flong remained damp, causing steam bubbles in the casting; too hot and the papier-mache scorched, producing a brittle mold that cracked under the weight of the metal.

The dried flong is a rigid, heat-resistant negative of the original type page. It can be stored, shipped, and reused multiple times. A single flong might cast dozens of stereotype plates before the detail of its impression began to degrade.

## The material of memory

There is something uncanny about the flong. It preserves language in its absence -- not as ink on paper, readable and direct, but as a shaped void, a cavity in the precise form of each letter. The flong does not speak; it holds the shape of speech, waiting to be filled with metal and made legible again. It is a material memory, a mold of meaning.
