## The casting apparatus

<p class="lede">The casting box is a heavy metal frame into which the dried flong is placed face-up. A backing plate closes the assembly, leaving a thin gap between the flong surface and the plate -- the space that the molten metal will fill. The gap determines the thickness of the finished stereotype plate: typically three to six millimeters for flat-bed printing, thicker for plates that must be planed or routed.</p>

## Type metal

The alloy used for stereotype casting is a specific mixture of lead, tin, and antimony. Pure lead is too soft and shrinks excessively on cooling; the addition of tin improves fluidity, and antimony -- uniquely among common metals -- expands slightly as it solidifies, pressing the casting into the finest details of the mold. The standard stereotype alloy contains approximately 80 percent lead, 6 percent tin, and 14 percent antimony, though formulations varied between foundries.

The metal is melted in a pot or furnace at temperatures between 280 and 330 degrees Celsius. The casting must be poured at the correct temperature: too cool and the metal solidifies before filling the mold completely, leaving blank areas on the plate; too hot and the metal may scorch the flong or introduce porosity through outgassing.

## The pour

The casting itself is a brief, violent act. The caster lifts a ladle of molten metal from the furnace and pours it through the opening in the casting box in a single smooth motion. The metal flows across the flong surface, filling every indentation, every letter cavity, every rule and ornament. Within seconds, the metal begins to solidify against the cooler flong surface, and within a minute the plate has set sufficiently to be removed from the box.

The freshly cast plate is a mirror of the flong, which was itself a mirror of the original type. The double reversal restores the text to its readable orientation: the stereotype plate reads correctly, left to right, just as the original type forme did.
