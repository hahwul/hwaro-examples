## The foundry

<p class="lede">The casting of the stereotype plate takes place in the foundry, the hottest and most dangerous room in the printing works. Here, type metal -- an alloy of lead, tin, and antimony -- is melted in iron pots at temperatures around 300 degrees Celsius and poured into the matrix mold. The transformation from papier-mache impression to solid metal plate takes only a few minutes, but the process demands precision, speed, and respect for the molten metal.</p>

## The metal

Type metal is an alloy carefully formulated for the demands of printing. Pure lead is too soft; it would deform under the pressure of the press. The addition of tin (roughly 4 percent) improves the metal's ability to fill fine detail in the mold. Antimony (roughly 12 percent) hardens the alloy and causes it to expand slightly as it solidifies, ensuring that the cast plate fills every detail of the matrix with precision.

The melting pot is maintained at a constant temperature. Too cool, and the metal will solidify before it fills the mold completely, producing a plate with missing letters or pitted surfaces. Too hot, and the metal may scorch the papier-mache matrix or produce a casting with excessive shrinkage as it cools. The foundry worker monitors the temperature by eye, judging the state of the metal by its color and surface behavior.

## The flat cast

For letterpress work, the stereotype plate is cast flat. The dried matrix is placed face-up in a shallow iron frame -- the casting box -- and clamped firmly in position. Molten metal is poured through an opening at one edge, flowing across the matrix and filling every depression. The metal solidifies in a few minutes, and the plate is removed from the box, trimmed to size, and mounted on a wooden or metal base to bring it to type height.

The flat stereotype plate is the simplest form, but it carries a significant limitation: it can only be used on a flatbed press, which prints one sheet at a time. For the high-speed rotary presses that the newspaper industry demanded, a different approach was needed.
