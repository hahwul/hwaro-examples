## The demand for speed

<p class="lede">The flat stereotype plate served well enough for book printing, where editions were measured in hundreds or thousands. But the newspaper industry of the late nineteenth century demanded something faster. A metropolitan daily might print hundreds of thousands of copies in a single press run, and the flat-bed press -- which printed one sheet at a time -- could not keep pace. The rotary press, which printed on a continuous web of paper fed between revolving cylinders, could run at speeds of twenty thousand impressions per hour. But a rotary press required curved printing plates that conformed to the surface of its cylinder.</p>

The solution was elegant: bend the flong. A papier-mache flong, while still slightly damp and flexible, could be formed around a semicylindrical casting box. The molten metal, poured into this curved mold, produced a stereotype plate with a concave printing surface that mounted directly onto the press cylinder. The entire process -- from composed type to curved plate -- could be completed in under fifteen minutes, enabling newspapers to update their plates for successive editions throughout the night.

## The autoplate

The fully mechanized stereotype casting machine, known as the Autoplate, automated the sequence of casting, trimming, and finishing that had previously required manual labor. Introduced in the early twentieth century, the Autoplate accepted a dried flong on a semicylindrical form, clamped it in position, and cast the plate automatically. The machine trimmed the plate to the correct dimensions, routed the non-printing areas to reduce weight, and cooled the finished plate -- all in a continuous operation that took approximately ninety seconds.

## The newspaper foundry

The stereotype foundry was one of the hottest, loudest, most demanding workplaces in the newspaper building. The air was thick with the sweet, metallic smell of molten lead alloy. The casting machines ran continuously through the night, their operators working in shifts to keep pace with the editorial deadlines. The foundry floor was strewn with metal shavings from the routing machines, and the clang of plates being stacked and carried to the press room punctuated every few minutes.

Despite the heat and noise, the stereotype founders took pride in their craft. A well-cast plate reproduced every detail of the original type with fidelity -- the fine serifs, the hairline rules, the delicate gradations of halftone illustrations. A poorly cast plate, by contrast, could ruin an edition: missing letters, filled-in counters, and uneven inking that rendered the text illegible.
