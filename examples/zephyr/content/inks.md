A reviewer's cabinet is a working shelf, not a museum. At any given moment only a
handful of pens are inked — the rest wait, clean and capped — and the rotation
tells you more about my honest favourites than any score could.

## How the swatches are made

Every review opens with a swatch drawn by hand, not printed from a chart. I lay a
broad, wet band with a glass dip pen so you can see the extremes at once: the
saturated pool where the ink sits thick, and the pale edge where it runs thin.
Shading, sheen, and the true dry-down colour all live in that single band, which
is why it earns the top of the page.

The colour you see on screen is calibrated to the dried line on coated paper, not
the wet bottle — bottles always lie a little, always toward the dramatic.

## Building a small collection

If you are starting out, resist the urge to buy a rainbow. A cool blue-black for
work, one warm brown for letters, and a single character ink for pleasure will
carry you for a year. Start with a reliable [blue-black](@/reviews/harbour-fog.md),
add a [violet worth the shelf](@/reviews/iris-nocturne.md), and only then chase
the greens. The best cabinet is the one you actually write your way through.
