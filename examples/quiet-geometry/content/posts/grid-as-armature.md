title = "The Grid as Armature"
date = 2024-06-12
description = "How an invisible scaffold dictates rhythm and rest in a layout."
+++

A layout begins as a set of decisions about where the eye is permitted to land. Long before color or typeface enter the conversation, a grid is drawn. The grid is not the design. It is the armature on which the design is hung, and its presence should be felt as an absence of friction rather than as visible structure.

In quiet layouts the column count is often kept low. Two columns or a single measured column allow the line length to settle near sixty-five characters, which is the width at which most readers stop noticing the act of reading. Wider columns rush the eye. Narrower columns tire it. The middle ground is technical, not stylistic.

The vertical rhythm matters as much as the horizontal. A baseline grid set to the body line height keeps every heading, image, and rule aligned to the same vertical pulse. When a heading sits one line off, the eye does not see the misalignment so much as feel a small unease. The grid is what that unease is reacting against.

Margins are part of the grid. The space around a block of text is not empty; it is the silence that lets the text speak. Reducing margins to save space is the most common mistake in restrained design. A page that breathes can hold more meaning per square inch than a page that is full.

Treat the grid as an instrument tuned before each page is composed. Once it is set, the design proceeds quickly. The decisions that remain are about contrast, weight, and emphasis, and they all rest on the armature.
