The platform opens at 6:30 and the first riders settle into the same positions every day. A man with a leather case takes the bench nearest the third pillar. A woman with running shoes and a folded paper paces the yellow line. The pacing is not impatience. It is a warm-up, the same six steps each direction, completed before the train arrives.

The 6:42 pulls in 40 seconds late. The doors hold for 11 seconds, which is the standard dwell time on this route. The man boards through the second door of the third car, the woman through the first door of the same car. They do not acknowledge each other. They have shared this train for at least the four months I have been counting.

Inside, the lighting is cooler than the platform lighting. The transition is sharp enough to register as a small jolt every morning. Riders take phones from pockets within three seconds of finding a seat. A few keep them down. Two of them, on this morning, are reading a paperback book.

At the second stop, a child boards with a parent. The child is carrying a small backpack with a dinosaur patch sewn over a previous patch. The previous patch has bled a little dye through the new fabric, leaving a faint outline. The parent helps the child find the handhold and the train continues.

This is the texture of the commute. Small repetitions. Tiny variations. A pattern that holds across days and slowly rewrites itself across months.
