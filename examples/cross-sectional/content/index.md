<section class="abstract-box">
  <h2>Abstract</h2>
  <dl>
    <dt>Background</dt>
    <dd>Digital literacy is increasingly recognized as a social determinant of health among older adults, yet cross-national prevalence data using standardized instruments remain scarce. This study captures a snapshot of digital literacy and its demographic correlates across six European cities.</dd>
    <dt>Methods</dt>
    <dd>Cross-sectional population-based survey of 4,218 community-dwelling adults aged 65+ across Geneva, Warsaw, Barcelona, Amsterdam, Helsinki, and Lisbon. Digital literacy assessed using the validated Digital Competence Scale for Older Adults (DCS-OA, 24 items, range 0-96). Stratified random sampling from municipal registers. Data collected September-November 2025.</dd>
    <dt>Results</dt>
    <dd>Mean DCS-OA score was 48.2 (SD = 22.4). Digital literacy was independently associated with younger age (beta = -1.8 per 5-year increase, p &lt; 0.001), higher education (beta = 12.4 for tertiary vs. primary, p &lt; 0.001), and city of residence (range: Helsinki 58.4 to Lisbon 38.6). Women scored 3.2 points lower than men after adjustment (p = 0.004). Living alone was associated with lower scores (beta = -4.8, p = 0.002).</dd>
    <dt>Conclusions</dt>
    <dd>Substantial variation in digital literacy exists among older Europeans, with education and geographic context as the strongest correlates. Policies targeting the digitally excluded should prioritize less-educated, older-old, and socially isolated populations.</dd>
    <dt>Keywords</dt>
    <dd><em>digital literacy; older adults; cross-sectional; population survey; digital divide; ageing; Europe</em></dd>
  </dl>
</section>

## Structure of the Paper

1. **Section 1. Study Design** -- cross-sectional design, single time-point data collection, and study setting
2. **Section 2. Sampling Frame** -- stratified random sampling from municipal registers with sampling diagrams
3. **Section 3. Measures and Data Collection** -- DCS-OA instrument, demographic variables, and data collection procedures
4. **Section 4. Results** -- descriptive statistics, demographic bar charts, and multivariable regression
5. **Section 5. Discussion** -- policy implications, limitations of cross-sectional design, and future directions
