## Key Findings

This cross-sectional survey of 4,218 older adults across six European cities reveals substantial variation in digital literacy, with education and geographic context as the strongest independent correlates. The 19.8-point gap between Helsinki (58.4) and Lisbon (38.6) persisted after adjustment for individual-level factors, suggesting that national and municipal digital inclusion policies play an important role.

## Cross-Sectional Limitations

As a snapshot study, this design captures prevalence at a single moment and cannot establish:

- **Temporal ordering:** whether education led to digital literacy or vice versa
- **Causal effects:** whether living alone reduces digital engagement or whether digitally disengaged individuals are more likely to live alone
- **Secular trends:** whether the digital divide is widening or narrowing over time
- **Cohort effects:** whether age-related differences reflect aging processes or generational differences in technology exposure

Future longitudinal studies following the same participants over time are needed to disentangle these relationships.

## Policy Implications

1. **Education-targeted interventions:** the strongest modifiable correlate is education level, suggesting that digital literacy programs should be tailored to educational backgrounds
2. **Nordic model:** Helsinki and Amsterdam demonstrate that strong national digital infrastructure and public library digital skills programs can achieve high digital literacy among older adults
3. **Social isolation:** the association between living alone and lower digital literacy suggests that community-based interventions (e.g., digital cafes, peer mentoring) may be particularly effective
4. **Gender gap:** the 3.2-point gender gap warrants targeted outreach to older women, particularly in cities with larger gaps

## Strengths

- Large, multi-city sample with standardized instrument
- Population-based sampling from municipal registers (not convenience sample)
- Face-to-face administration reducing non-response bias among digitally excluded
- Validated instrument (DCS-OA) with strong psychometric properties across languages

## Conclusion

Digital literacy among older Europeans varies substantially by education, age, sex, living situation, and city of residence. This snapshot provides a baseline for evaluating digital inclusion policies and designing targeted interventions for the most excluded populations.
