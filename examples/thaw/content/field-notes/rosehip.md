Rosehips are the reward for waiting: the dog rose flowers and is
forgotten for most of the year, then in the last stretch of the
season its fruit turns from hard and green to soft, bright red, and
genuinely worth the trouble of processing.

<!-- more -->

**Field marks**

- Arching, thorny canes with pinnate leaves in five to seven leaflets
- Oval to round hips, bright red to orange-red, roughly the size of a large olive, forming where the pale pink single flower has dropped its petals
- A small dried, star-shaped calyx remnant persists at the tip of each hip
- Canes and hips are common in hedgerows well after leaves have dropped, making the fruit easy to spot against bare branches

**Habitat & range**

Dog rose grows wild in hedgerows, scrubby field margins, and roadside
thickets across a wide range of soils, tolerating poor ground far
better than cultivated roses. It fruits reliably every year and the
canes are conspicuous enough in winter that a patch is easy to find
again the following season.

> Hips that are still hard and green, or that show any sign of mold or
> insect damage, should be left. The fine hairs surrounding the seeds
> inside each hip are a known irritant and must always be strained out
> before eating anything made from the fruit.

**Harvest & use**

Pick hips after the first hard frost, when the cold has converted some
of the starch to sugar and softened the flesh considerably. Simmer
whole hips in water until soft, mash, then strain through a cloth
twice — once for pulp, once more to catch every stray seed hair —
before reducing the strained liquid with sugar into a tart, vitamin-rich
syrup or jelly that keeps the hedgerow's last harvest through the
winter.
