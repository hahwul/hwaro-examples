Nettle is the plant that convinces most people foraging is worth doing at
all: it is everywhere, it is unmistakable once you have been stung by it
once, and the first cutting of the year tastes like nothing from a
supermarket.

<!-- more -->

**Field marks**

- Square, ridged stems with opposite, coarsely toothed leaves shaped like a rough heart
- Fine hollow hairs on stem and leaf undersides that deliver the sting on contact
- Small greenish flower clusters hanging from leaf axils once the plant matures
- Grows in dense stands, often two to four feet tall by early summer

**Habitat & range**

Nettle favors disturbed, nitrogen-rich ground: old barnyards, hedgerow
margins, riverbanks, the edges of compost heaps and fallen fence lines. It
comes up early wherever the soil has been worked or manured, and a good
patch will return to the same square yard of ground for years.

> The sting is the identification. If a plant looks exactly like this and
> does not sting bare skin, treat it as something else and walk away —
> do not eat an unstinging look-alike on the assumption it is a mild
> nettle.

**Harvest & use**

Cut only the top four to six leaf pairs while the plant is under a foot
tall — the sting fades entirely once blanched or dried, but the flavor
turns grassy and coarse after flowering, and older stems develop gritty
calcium oxalate crystals not worth eating. Wear gloves, snip with
scissors into a bag, and either blanch thirty seconds for a spinach
substitute or dry the leaves flat for a mineral-heavy tea through the
rest of the year.
