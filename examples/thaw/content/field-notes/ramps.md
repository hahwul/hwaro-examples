Ramps are the plant that gets foragers into trouble, not because they are
hard to identify but because they are easy to overharvest — a whole
regional culture of ramp suppers has already thinned wild stands badly
enough that some states now regulate the pick.

<!-- more -->

**Field marks**

- Broad, smooth, tulip-like leaves in pairs or trios rising straight from the ground, no true stem showing above soil
- A distinct onion-garlic smell when a leaf is bruised or torn — this is the single most reliable check
- Small white bulb with a pink-to-burgundy blush where it meets the leaf base
- Leaves appear weeks before the small white flower umbel, which shows up after the leaves have already died back

**Habitat & range**

Ramps grow in colonies on the rich, shaded floor of deciduous woodland,
especially near streams and on north-facing slopes with deep leaf litter.
A colony can be decades or centuries old, spreading slowly from seed and
bulb division, which is exactly why it cannot absorb heavy cutting.

> False hellebore shares the woodland floor and early-spring timing but
> has pleated, parallel-veined leaves and no onion smell at all. Smell
> every leaf before you cut — false hellebore is seriously toxic.

**Harvest & use**

Cut the leaf an inch above the bulb with scissors and leave the bulb and
roots in the ground; a colony can sustain light leaf-only harvest
indefinitely, while pulling bulbs can take ten to twenty years to
recover in the same spot. Take leaves from no more than one in ten
plants in any patch. Chopped raw into eggs, wilted into butter for
pasta, or blitzed into a sharp green pesto, the leaf alone delivers the
full flavor without touching the bulb at all.
