Fiddleheads are a genuinely short-season crop — the coiled shoot of the
ostrich fern is only worth picking for a week or two before it unfurls
into an ordinary frond, and identification has to be exact because
several other ferns produce shoots that are unpleasant or unsafe raw.

<!-- more -->

**Field marks**

- Tightly coiled, spiral shoot with a deep U-shaped groove running down the inside of the stem — this groove is the single best check
- Smooth, papery brown scale covering the young shoot, rubbing off easily rather than staying fixed like hair
- Mature ostrich fern grows in a tall, vase-shaped cluster, which helps confirm the parent plant before the fiddleheads show
- No fuzzy wool coating and no white powdery bloom on the stem

**Habitat & range**

Ostrich fern colonizes rich, damp floodplain soil along rivers and
streams, often in dense stands that carpet the bank in early spring
before the tree canopy leafs out and shades the ground.

> Bracken fern also produces an edible-looking coiled shoot but lacks the
> deep U-groove, has a fuzzy rather than papery covering, and carries
> compounds under long-term study for carcinogenicity. Confirm the groove
> and the parent plant before cutting anything.

**Harvest & use**

Snap or cut shoots while still tightly coiled and under two inches tall,
taking no more than a third of the shoots from any one crown so the fern
can still leaf out and feed itself for next year. Rinse off the brown
scale under running water, then always cook fiddleheads thoroughly —
boil or steam at least ten minutes, never eat raw — before sautéing in
butter with a little garlic and a squeeze of lemon.
