Elderberry gives two separate harvests off the same shrub — the white
flower heads in early summer, then the deep purple berry clusters in
late summer — but the note here is for the berries, which carry
real weight for the effort of picking them.

<!-- more -->

**Field marks**

- Compound leaves in five to eleven leaflets, each sharply toothed and lance-shaped
- Flat-topped clusters (umbels) of tiny white flowers in June, ripening into drooping clusters of small, deep purple-black berries by late summer
- Ripe berries hang heavy and the whole cluster droops downward under its own weight
- Stems and unripe green berries have a distinct rank smell when broken

**Habitat & range**

Elderberry thrives at the damp, sunny margins of things: hedgerows,
ditch banks, the edge of a woodland clearing, pond margins. It spreads
into loose thickets and tends to mark exactly the kind of scrubby
in-between ground that gets mowed once a year and otherwise left alone.

> Every part of the plant except the ripe fruit — and even the ripe
> fruit's seeds, stems, and skin in quantity — contains compounds that
> cause nausea when raw. Elderberry must always be cooked; never eat raw
> berries, and always strip fruit from stems before cooking.

**Harvest & use**

Cut whole clusters with shears rather than stripping berries by hand,
then freeze the clusters briefly — the frozen berries come off the
stems with a light rub far more easily than fresh ones do. Simmer the
stripped berries into a syrup with sugar and a little lemon, which
keeps for months in the refrigerator and turns a spoonful of hot water
into something worth having on a cold evening.
