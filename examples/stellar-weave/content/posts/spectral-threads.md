A moving group is not a cluster. The stars no longer share a gravitational well, but they retain a chemical signature from the cloud that bore them. Reading that signature back from intermediate-resolution spectra is a slow, patient task, and it is the only reliable way to confirm membership when astrometry alone leaves the question open.

Iron is the workhorse line. The 6705 angstrom feature pulls clean equivalent widths even in moderately rotating stars, and a curve-of-growth analysis against a synthetic grid yields metallicity to about 0.05 dex. Within a true natal group, the spread should be smaller than the measurement error. When it is not, the group has either swept up an unrelated interloper or formed across a chemically inhomogeneous cloud.

The Pisces moving group, surveyed last winter, showed a 0.12 dex spread. Three stars sat 0.2 dex below the median. Their alpha-element ratios placed them on a different track entirely, and their radial velocities, while consistent with the group at first pass, drifted apart on a second epoch. They were thin-disk passersby, sharing space with the group but not history.

The remaining members showed a tight chemical lock. Lithium abundances followed a clean depletion sequence with effective temperature, anchoring the age near 50 million years. Barium and yttrium ratios matched the regional s-process pattern.

The threads are subtle. They do not glow. They have to be inferred from line strengths and patient bookkeeping.
