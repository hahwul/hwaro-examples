The Perseus OB2 association sits about 320 parsecs from the Sun, a loose gathering of hot, young stars that share a common origin in the same molecular cloud. Charting its members requires careful proper-motion analysis: stars that appear close on the sky may be unrelated, and stars that look scattered often share a kinematic signature.

Gaia DR3 parallaxes resolve the ambiguity. Members cluster in a thin sheet, roughly 50 parsecs wide, oriented at a shallow angle to the Galactic plane. The internal velocity dispersion is small, consistent with the group expanding slowly from a denser progenitor that dispersed within the last 10 to 15 million years.

Patterns emerge when proper motions are projected onto the Galactic frame. The stars trace a faint arc that loops back toward the Per OB1 region, suggesting a triggered formation episode walking outward through a chain of dense filaments. The same arc appears in CO surveys of the residual molecular gas: the gas has thinned but not vanished, and what remains aligns with the stellar tracks.

For the catalog at hand, only stars brighter than G = 14 with five-parameter solutions are retained. Each entry gets a kinematic membership score, a position in the de-projected sheet, and a tentative subgroup label. Outliers receive a flag rather than a deletion. Some of those outliers, on review, are runaways ejected from the cluster core during early N-body interactions.

The lattice is not a perfect grid. It is a record of where star formation paused, restarted, and finally ran out of fuel.
