Buncheong ware emerged in the early Joseon period as a continuation of Goryeo celadon traditions, but with rougher clay bodies and bolder decoration. This vase carries that lineage forward.

The form was coil-built over three sessions to allow each section to firm before adding more weight. The shoulder was paddled gently to compress the coils and to introduce a faceted texture that catches glaze pools during the firing.

Decoration uses the inlay technique known as sanggam. Stamped chrysanthemum patterns were pressed into the leather-hard surface, then filled with white porcelain slip. After the slip set, the surface was scraped back to the level of the dark clay, leaving the pattern in clean relief. A second pass added freehand brushwork along the neck.

A thin transparent glaze made from local wood ash was applied by dipping. The glaze breaks dry on the high points of the texture, deepens to a soft amber where it pools, and reveals the slip patterns with quiet clarity. The piece was fired in light reduction to bring out warmth in the iron-bearing clay.
