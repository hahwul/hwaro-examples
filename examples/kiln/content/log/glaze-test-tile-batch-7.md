Test tiles are the working memory of the studio. Each batch documents a hypothesis: how does a single variable shift the surface, color, or fit of a glaze when everything else stays constant? Batch 7 was a line blend exploring iron oxide content in a feldspathic celadon base.

## The Setup

Ten tiles were prepared from the same stoneware body, all bisque fired to cone 06 in the same load. The base recipe was held constant at 35 percent feldspar, 25 percent silica, 20 percent kaolin, 10 percent whiting, and 10 percent dolomite. Iron oxide was varied from 0.5 percent to 2.5 percent in increments of 0.25.

Each tile was dipped twice with a hold of three seconds per dip. A vertical incised line was cut into each tile to read glaze flow and color shift across thickness changes.

## The Firing

The tiles were placed in the front of the kiln on the second shelf, away from the door and away from the bagwall. Reduction began at cone 010 and was held heavy until cone 8 began to bend. From cone 8 to cone 10 down, the firing returned to neutral. Cooling was free until 1100C, then slowed to 100C per hour through the quartz inversion.

## Observations

The transition from blue-green to olive happens between 1.25 and 1.5 percent iron. Below that, the glaze stays in the celadon family with a notable shift toward grey at higher thicknesses. Above 1.5 percent, the surface darkens into tea-dust territory and loses transparency entirely.

The 1.0 percent tile is the strongest match for the reference samples from the Goryeo study. It will be scaled up for the next batch of bowls. The 2.0 percent tile is unexpectedly attractive on the incised areas and may earn a place in the regular palette under a new name.
