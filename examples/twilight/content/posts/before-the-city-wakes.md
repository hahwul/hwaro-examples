The city at four in the morning is a different city entirely. It shares the same geography, the same buildings and streets and intersections, but it is emptied of the thing that normally defines it. There are no people. Or nearly none. A security guard visible through the glass doors of an office lobby, reading something on his phone. A delivery truck idling at a loading dock, its driver nowhere in sight. These are the only signs that the city is inhabited at all.

I walk down the center of a street that during the day would be impassable. Four lanes of traffic, reduced now to wet asphalt and painted lines. My footsteps echo off the building fronts on either side, a sound that would be inaudible at any other hour but now carries clearly in the stillness. The traffic lights continue their cycle -- green, yellow, red -- directing no one, performing their function out of habit or programming or whatever it is that keeps machines working when their purpose has temporarily evaporated.

> Dawn in a city is not a beginning. It is the interval between two versions of the same place, a pause in which the city forgets itself.

The sky is changing. It started as a uniform dark grey, the color of the underside of a roof. Now, in the east, there is a band of lighter grey, then a thin line of something that might be color. Not yet. The light at this hour is not warm or golden or any of the things that sunrise photography would suggest. It is flat and blue-grey, the light of an unfinished process. Things are visible but not yet illuminated. I can see the facades of the buildings across the street, but they have no shadows, no highlights. They are present without being described.

A cafe on the corner has its lights on, though the door is locked. Through the window I can see someone moving behind the counter, setting up for the morning. The espresso machine is on, producing a faint hiss that I can hear from the sidewalk. This is the first domestic sound of the day. It is more reassuring than the traffic lights, which are mechanical. The espresso machine implies a person, and a person implies intention, and intention implies that the city is beginning to remember what it is for.

The light continues to build. There is now a definite color in the eastern sky, a pale yellow that will eventually become the dawn proper but for now is just a suggestion. The buildings are beginning to acquire dimension. Shadows appear, faint and directionless, as if the light has not yet decided where it is coming from.

A pigeon lands on the ledge of a building across the street and looks down at me with the particular disinterest that pigeons reserve for humans who are not holding food. It is the first living thing I have seen, other than the cafe worker, in forty minutes of walking. The city is beginning to populate itself.

By five-thirty the transformation is underway. A bus passes, mostly empty, its interior lights bright against the still-dim street. A jogger appears at the end of the block, headphones in, moving at a steady pace. A shop owner unlocks a metal gate, the sound of it rolling up sharp and metallic in the quiet air. These are the advance guard of the day, the people who arrive before the city is officially open.

I turn east and walk toward the growing light. The sky is now unmistakably dawn, bands of pale gold and soft blue layered above the roofline. The buildings have shadows now, long ones that stretch westward, and the windows on the upper floors have begun to catch the first direct sunlight. It is still quiet, still sparse, but the quality of the emptiness has changed. It is no longer the emptiness of absence but the emptiness of anticipation. The city is waking up. In an hour it will be unrecognizable as the place I walked through in the dark.
