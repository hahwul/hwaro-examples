There is a moment at the harbor, roughly twenty minutes before the sun disappears, when the water stops reflecting the sky and begins to produce its own light. It is not a scientific observation. The water is not luminous. But the effect is unmistakable: the harbor glows from below, a warm amber tone that seems to rise from the silt and stone beneath the surface, as if the day's accumulated heat has been stored there and is now being quietly released.

The fishing boats are tied up for the evening. Their hulls, painted in blues and reds that look vivid at noon, have darkened into muted versions of themselves. Against the bright water they are only shapes -- curved lines and mast poles, the geometry of rest. A coil of rope on one deck catches the last direct light and turns briefly golden before the angle shifts and it goes dark again.

> The harbor does not perform its beauty. It simply exists in it, the same way it exists in fog or rain or the flat grey light of an overcast afternoon.

I walk along the stone jetty. The surface is uneven, worn smooth in some places by decades of foot traffic and salt spray. At this hour the stone holds warmth from the day. I can feel it through the soles of my shoes, a faint thermal memory that will be gone within the hour.

On the far side of the harbor, the old warehouse district presents a wall of dark brick and small windows. During the day these buildings are purely functional, unremarkable. But now, with the low sun striking them at this angle, every imperfection in the brickwork casts a tiny shadow. The wall becomes textured, dimensional, almost alive. A building I have walked past hundreds of times reveals itself to be beautiful in a way I had never noticed.

The water shifts. What was amber is now closer to rose. The transition happens without announcement. One moment you are looking at one color and then, without having seen the change, you are looking at another. This is the essential character of twilight: it is always moving, always one step ahead of your attention.

A single gull crosses the harbor at low altitude, close enough to the water that its reflection is sharp and clear. For a moment there are two gulls, one real and one made of light on water, and they move together in perfect symmetry until a small wave breaks the reflection into fragments.

The last direct light leaves the harbor. What remains is indirect -- scattered, ambient, the kind of light that has no source you can point to. The sky above is still bright, but the harbor itself has moved into the first stage of evening. The boats are darker now. The water has lost its warmth and taken on a cooler tone, a steel blue that deepens as I watch.

I stay until the first lights come on along the waterfront. They are small and yellow, practical lights mounted on poles and above doorways. They do not replace what has been lost. They are a different kind of light entirely -- human light, electric and steady, without the slow drama of what came before. But they are welcome. They mean the harbor is settling in for the night, and that there will be another evening tomorrow, and another chance to stand here and watch the water hold the last of the day.
