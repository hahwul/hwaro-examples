## Vox Populi, Vox Dei

The Roman Senate Forum Debate is a revival of the finest tradition of the Republic: the open contest of ideas before the full citizenry. Distinguished orators, scholars, and statesmen will take to the Rostra to argue on matters of consequence to the modern world, employing the classical methods of rhetoric perfected by Cicero, Quintilian, and the great speakers of antiquity.

The debate is structured in accordance with Quintilian's *Institutio Oratoria*, the definitive guide to Roman rhetoric. Each speaker has six minutes to present their argument, followed by a period of rebuttal, and finally a concluding statement.

## Topics for Debate

This year's forum will address three great questions:

**I. On Governance and the Common Good**
What obligations does the state owe to its citizens, and what in turn do citizens owe the state? Senators from three provinces present competing visions.

**II. On the Nature of Justice**
Is justice the interest of the stronger, as the Sophists argued, or is there a universal moral law discoverable by reason? A philosophical debate drawing on Stoic and Epicurean traditions.

**III. On the Future of the City**
How should Rome grow and change while preserving the values that made her great? Architects, philosophers, and civic leaders argue their positions.

## The Orators

Each session features three speakers representing distinct philosophical schools. The audience is invited to cast their votes using the traditional marble ballot at the conclusion of each debate.

## Attending the Debate

The Forum Debate is free and open to all citizens. Seats in the lower tiers are reserved for senators and equestrians; the upper tiers are general admission. Standing room is available in the colonnade.

Refreshments in the Roman style — olives, figs, wine, and bread — will be served during the intervals. The full programme runs from the third hour to the ninth, with a midday recess.
