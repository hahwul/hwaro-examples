Aronia and black currant both tolerate wet feet better than almost any other fruiting shrub hardy to zone 3, which makes this guild the answer for the low, poorly-drained corner of the property that nothing else will use. It has been running on a heavy clay swale shoulder since 2020, in ground that stays saturated through most of April.

<!-- more -->

## Site and layout

Set aronia and currant on a slight rise — even 4 to 6 inches of mounding keeps the crowns out of standing water without moving the guild off the wet ground entirely. Space the shrubs 5 feet apart in a staggered double row so the guild reads as one continuous thicket rather than individual bushes; this also blocks wind at knee height, which matters more than canopy-height wind for tender rhubarb crowns emerging in April.

## The guild

- **Aronia** — the anchor shrub, planted first and given two seasons to establish before the rest of the guild goes in around it.
- **Black currant** — alternating with aronia in the row. Both are heavy nitrogen users; neither should be paired with a nitrogen-fixer here, since excess nitrogen delays fruit set on currant.
- **Rhubarb** — planted in the row's shadier gaps. It tolerates the wet ground and its broad leaves suppress the reed canary grass that otherwise invades this kind of site.
- **Garlic** — a border planting on the drier upslope edge. Garlic's sulfur compounds measurably reduce currant's exposure to gooseberry sawfly.
- **Nasturtium** — sown as a living mulch between shrubs in year two onward; it draws aphids off the currant new growth and is itself edible.
- **Bee balm** (Monarda fistulosa) — planted at the guild's sunniest end to pull in the bumblebees that do most of the actual pollination work on aronia, whose flowers are easy for smaller bees to miss.

## Maintenance notes

Do not fertilize this guild beyond an annual inch of compost at the drip line — clay ground already runs nitrogen-rich from decomposing organic matter, and both anchor shrubs will put on soft growth that winterkills if pushed further. Rhubarb needs dividing every five to six years or it stops producing usable stalks; do it in early spring before the guild leafs out, while you can still see the crown clearly.

On better-drained ground, the [apple tree guild](/methods/apple-tree-guild-for-zone-3/) is the better anchor planting and gives a taller canopy layer this guild does not.
