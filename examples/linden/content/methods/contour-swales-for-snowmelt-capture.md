In zone 3 and 4, the single largest water event of the year is not a rainstorm — it is the week the snowpack lets go. A bare slope will shed most of that water downhill in days, still frozen solid a foot down, before a single root can use it. A contour swale intercepts that runoff and holds it long enough to soak in.

## Laying out the contour

Build an A-frame level from three furring strips and a string plumb bob; it costs almost nothing and is accurate enough for a garden-scale swale. Walk the slope in 15 to 20 foot increments, marking each point where the A-frame reads level, and connect the marks with stakes. On a slope over roughly 8 percent, plan swales every 15 to 20 feet of vertical drop; on a gentler slope, space them 30 to 40 feet apart, or the ditches will simply overlap their soak zones and waste digging effort.

## Digging and sizing

A garden-scale swale needs only a shallow ditch, 12 to 18 inches deep and 18 to 24 inches wide, with the excavated soil piled on the downhill lip to form a berm. Keep the ditch bottom level along its entire run — this is the part people get wrong, digging a ditch that drains sideways instead of holding water evenly across its length. Check level every 8 to 10 feet with the A-frame as you dig.

Cap each end of the swale with an earthen plug slightly higher than the berm, or cut a shallow spillway at one end lined with rock, so an unusually heavy melt has somewhere controlled to go rather than blowing out the berm.

## Planting the berm

Plant the downhill berm the same season you dig it, before it settles and before weeds claim it. Deep-rooted nitrogen fixers — caragana, or Siberian pea shrub, does well here — anchor the berm's structure within two seasons. Fruit and nut trees planted directly on the berm crest get the benefit of the stored soil moisture below without sitting in standing water, since the berm itself stays better drained than the ditch bottom.

## Known failure points

A swale dug across a spot where the subsoil is heavy clay with poor infiltration will simply become a stagnant ditch — check infiltration with a shovel test before committing a full run. Berms planted too late, after the first hard rain following digging, are prone to erosion gullies that undo a season of work in one storm.

A [shelterbelt windbreak](/methods/shelterbelt-windbreak-design/) upslope of a swale run changes how evenly snow loads the ground above it, and is worth planning alongside a new swale rather than after it.
