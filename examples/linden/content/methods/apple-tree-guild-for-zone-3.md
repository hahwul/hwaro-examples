A guild built around a single semi-dwarf apple on Antonovka rootstock, planted for zone 3 sites with 200 to 700 chill hours and a frost-free window as short as 100 days. This layout has been running at three sites around the upper Great Lakes since 2021 and has survived two winters below minus 30 Fahrenheit without losing the tree or the understory.

<!-- more -->

## Site and layout

Plant the apple on the south or southwest side of a windbreak, far enough out that the mature canopy will clear the shade line by year eight — roughly 20 feet from a mature spruce row. Mound the planting site 6 inches above grade to keep the graft union above standing snowmelt, and mulch a 6-foot radius with wood chips before adding the understory.

## The guild

- **Comfrey** (Bocking 14, sterile cultivar) — two plants at the drip line, chopped for mulch twice a season. Its taproot pulls potassium up from deeper soil than the apple's fibrous roots reach.
- **Daffodil** — a ring planted at the trunk base. The bulbs are the only reliable vole deterrent we have found that does not also compete with the tree for water; voles girdle young bark under snow cover and daffodil bulbs are toxic to them.
- **Chives** — planted in clumps between the comfrey stations. Chives concentrate sulfur compounds that measurably reduce apple scab pressure on the lower canopy in wet springs.
- **Yarrow** — a low band along the sunny edge, attracting the parasitic wasps that keep aphid colonies down without spraying.
- **Black currant** — one or two bushes at the guild's northern edge, far enough from the trunk to avoid replant disease pressure but close enough to share the mulched zone.

## Maintenance notes

Chop-and-drop the comfrey in June and again in early August; do not let it flower near the trunk, since bees working comfrey blossoms will sometimes ignore the apple bloom two weeks earlier. Divide the daffodil ring every four years — un-divided clumps stop flowering and lose vigor as vole deterrents. Currant needs its own three-year prune to keep air moving through the guild; skipping it invites powdery mildew that will jump to the apple's lower leaves by August.

If the planting site runs wetter than an apple wants, the [aronia and currant berry guild](/methods/aronia-and-currant-berry-guild/) covers the same ground with shrubs that tolerate standing spring water.
