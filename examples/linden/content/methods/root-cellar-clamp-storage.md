Before mechanical refrigeration, every cold-climate farm kept a clamp: a mound of vegetables layered in straw and buried under soil, holding a stable few degrees above freezing all winter using nothing but the ground's own thermal mass. It still works, it costs nothing to run, and it holds more than most people expect from a pile of dirt.

<!-- more -->

## Site and timing

Choose a well-drained spot — never a low point where meltwater collects — and build the clamp only after the first hard frost has already touched the crop; vegetables clamped while still field-warm sweat inside the mound and rot within weeks. Late October through early November is the usual window in zone 3 and 4.

## Building the clamp

Lay a 4 to 6 inch bed of clean straw directly on the ground, then stack the roots — carrots, beets, and parsnips do best; potatoes are better in a separate, cooler clamp since they do not want the same humidity — in a cone shape, largest and least perfect specimens at the base, best keepers toward the top where they are easiest to reach mid-winter. Cover the cone completely with another 4 to 6 inches of straw, patted down snug.

Over the straw, shovel on a layer of soil, starting thin — 2 inches — and building to a final 8 to 10 inches as the weather turns colder through November and December. Leave a wisp of straw poking through the peak as a vent; a fully sealed clamp traps respiration moisture and rots its own contents from the inside.

## Opening the clamp

Work from one side only, reclosing the opening with straw and soil after each visit rather than leaving the clamp open to the air. A clamp opened and resealed carelessly loses its insulating value within a few cold snaps and the exposed face will freeze solid. Plan to empty a clamp within a single season — re-clamping the same vegetables the following year is rarely worth the labor, since whatever survives to spring is already past its best storage life.

## Known failure points

Clamps built on any slope drain poorly on the downhill side and rot first there. Skipping the vent wisp is the single most common cause of a ruined clamp — condensation with nowhere to go will take out the whole mound by January even in a hard freeze.

A [cold frame](/methods/cold-frame-construction-and-siting/) covers the other half of the season, keeping late crops in the ground weeks after the clamp is already built.
