A cold frame is the cheapest season-extension structure that actually earns its keep in zone 3 and 4 gardens: a bottomless box with a transparent, sloped lid, set directly over soil or over a bed of hardening-off seedlings. Built well and sited correctly, it will hold a 15 to 20 Fahrenheit degree advantage over open ground on a clear night.

<!-- more -->

## Materials and dimensions

Build the box from rough-sawn 2-inch lumber, cedar or larch if you have it, since untreated pine rots at the soil line within three seasons. A workable size is 3 feet by 6 feet: large enough to be useful, small enough that one person can lift the lid without a prop stick. Back wall height 18 inches, front wall height 10 inches, giving the lid a slope of roughly 13 degrees — enough for rain to shed and for low winter sun to strike the glazing closer to square.

Glaze the lid with a salvaged storm window if you can find one sized close to the box, or with twin-wall polycarbonate panels, which insulate better than single-pane glass and do not shatter under a snow slide off the greenhouse roof next door. Hinge the lid at the back wall so it opens uphill, away from the plants, and add a notched prop stick cut in three lengths for partial venting on warm days.

## Siting

Face the sloped lid due south, or as close to it as the site allows; a southeast face catches morning sun sooner, which matters more for spring hardening-off than for fall extension. Set the frame against a south-facing wall or fence if one is available — the thermal mass and wind shelter add another 3 to 5 degrees on top of the frame's own effect. Avoid low spots where cold air pools; a frame set in a frost pocket underperforms one on a gentle slope by a wide margin on clear nights.

## Managing the frame

Vent by 9 or 10 a.m. on any sunny day above 40 Fahrenheit outside air temperature — a closed frame in direct sun can exceed 100 Fahrenheit inside within an hour, even with snow on the ground. Close the lid by mid-afternoon to bank the day's heat before sundown. In the coldest stretches, lay an old wool blanket or a few inches of straw over the lid at night and pull it before sunrise; this alone can be the difference between losing and keeping a February planting of spinach.

For the harvest a cold frame cannot hold past midwinter, see [root cellar clamp storage](/methods/root-cellar-clamp-storage/) — the two methods cover opposite ends of the same season-extension problem.
