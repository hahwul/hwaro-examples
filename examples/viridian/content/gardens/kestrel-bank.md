Kestrel Bank runs along the back of a private garden on a former railway cutting — a slope too steep to mow safely and too poor in soil for most border planting to establish without constant feeding. The client had tried, and failed, to keep a wildflower mix going for three years before calling us.

<!-- more -->

We terraced the steepest third with a run of buried gravel-filled gabion baskets, low enough not to need planning consent, and planted the rest as a single continuous matrix of *Salvia yangii* — still sold under its older name, *Perovskia atriplicifolia*, in most nurseries — threaded through with *Nepeta racemosa* for earlier, lower colour and a strong scent that keeps the local rabbit population from treating the bank as a salad bar.

This is our newest documented garden, planted in spring 2023 and hand-watered weekly through that first summer while the root systems worked down through the rubble subsoil. The second summer, 2024, ran with no supplemental water at all, and the bank held its colour from July into September regardless.

The kestrels the bank is named for hunt the verge below most mornings, taking advantage of the field voles that the undisturbed gravel mulch and unmown grass margins now support. That was not part of the brief, but it is the kind of result unwatered, unmown ground tends to produce.

For an older slope on a comparable gradient, see [Harrow Ridge Garden](/gardens/harrow-ridge/), now six years further into its establishment.
