Harrow Ridge sits on eight degrees of chalk slope above a converted barn, ground so thin and free-draining that the client's previous lawn had gone to moss and then to bare earth within three summers. We stripped the topsoil back to subsoil, worked in nothing but the existing rubble, and planted straight into it.

<!-- more -->

The bones of the planting are *Stipa tenuissima* in wide, wind-catching drifts, threaded through with *Euphorbia characias* subsp. *wulfenii* for the acid-green bracts that open in April and hold their shape into June. Behind them, *Cistus × purpureus* takes the hottest, most exposed third of the slope, where its papery pink flowers last barely a day each but arrive by the hundred through May and June.

We watered by hand twice a week through the first summer, tapering to nothing by the following spring. Now the only maintenance is a February cutback of the Stipa to clear last year's growth before the new blades push through, and an annual top-up of the 20mm gravel mulch along the path edges where foot traffic has worn it thin.

What surprised the client most was not the drought tolerance but the winter structure: the seed heads of the Stipa and the woody framework of the Cistus hold their shape through frost, so the slope reads as a finished garden in January as much as in May. Nothing here is cut back until the new growth demands it.

The same *Cistus* genus does heavier lifting at [Quarry Court](/gardens/quarry-court/), where the ground is even thinner than it is here.
