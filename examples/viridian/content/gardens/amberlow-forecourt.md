Amberlow Forecourt was the first garden we documented and remains the one we point new clients to when they ask what a gravel garden looks like after a decade of neglect in the best sense of the word. The brief in 2015 was to remove a struggling front lawn that browned every July and replace it with something that photographed well from the street without a hosepipe in sight.

<!-- more -->

The planting is a late-season matrix: *Hylotelephium* 'Matrona' — better known to most clients by its old name, *Sedum* 'Matrona' — carries the display from August through October with dusky pink flower heads over purple-tinged foliage, backed by fine drifts of *Festuca glauca* for blue-grey texture through the quieter months and scattered spires of *Verbena bonariensis* that seed themselves a little further into the gravel each year.

We stopped watering entirely in the spring of 2016, six months after planting, and the garden has had nothing but rainfall since. The reflected heat off the forecourt paving, which we worried might be a liability, has instead worked in the planting's favour — the sedum and festuca both colour up faster in a hot, dry corner than they do in the shadier beds we have planted since.

Maintenance now runs to a single February cutback and an occasional thinning of self-seeded verbena where it crowds the path edge. Ten years in, Amberlow remains the clearest argument we have for planting dry the first time rather than irrigating a lawn indefinitely.
