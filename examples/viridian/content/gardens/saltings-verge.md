Saltings Verge faces the estuary directly, close enough that spring tides throw spray over the boundary wall in a strong southwesterly. The brief was straightforward: something better than the gravel-and-weeds the previous owners had given up on, that could take salt, wind and drought without complaint.

<!-- more -->

*Eryngium giganteum* — sea holly's steel-blue, thistle-flowered cousin — anchors the planting, its silvered bracts holding colour from June deep into August regardless of what the weather does. We paired it with *Achillea* 'Terracotta' for the flat, rust-coloured flower heads that read well against the grey stone wall, and a low run of *Armeria maritima* along the front edge, more for the salt tolerance than the flower, though the pink blooms in May are a welcome bonus before the Eryngium takes over.

The garden was hand-watered for one summer after planting, largely to establish root systems deep enough to reach the moisture that sits, reliably, about 40cm down in this ground. Since then it has had nothing but what the estuary throws at it — including, on occasion, rather more salt than any inland garden would tolerate.

Eight years on, the only real maintenance is an annual cutback in late February, once the seed heads of the Eryngium have finally gone brown and dropped, and a light raking of the gravel where winter storms have pushed it against the wall.
