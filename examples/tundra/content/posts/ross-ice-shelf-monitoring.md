## 1415 NZST

Reached the monitoring station after a six-hour flight from McMurdo on a ski-equipped Twin Otter. Landing was rough -- the surface had developed a pattern of shallow melt-refreeze undulations since the last visit that made the final approach unpredictable. The pilot set us down three hundred meters east of the station to avoid the worst of it.

## Station Status

The automated systems have been running without interruption for eleven months. Power supply from the wind turbine and battery bank remains adequate despite the loss of one of three turbine blades to an ice strike sometime during the winter darkness. The remaining two blades have kept the batteries above minimum charge.

Data logger shows continuous recording with no gaps exceeding the programmed six-hour transmission window. Temperature records confirm what the climate models have been suggesting: this winter was one point four degrees warmer than the thirty-year average for this location.

## Calving Observation

During the instrument check we heard a sound like sustained thunder from the north. Through binoculars we observed a calving event along the barrier face approximately twelve kilometers from our position. A section of the shelf edge, estimated at eight hundred meters in length, separated and rotated into the sea over a period of roughly four minutes.

The resulting wave reached our position as a barely perceptible vibration in the ice beneath our feet. The GPS station recorded a displacement of six millimeters. This is within normal parameters for a calving event of this magnitude at this distance, but it is a reminder that the surface we stand on is floating, and that it is not as permanent as it appears.

## Return

We completed all instrument servicing, replaced the damaged turbine blade, and uploaded new firmware to the data logger. Total time on station was four hours and twelve minutes. The return flight to McMurdo was uneventful.

The shelf will continue its measurements through the coming winter. It does not require our presence to do its work. That is the purpose of automation -- to watch when we cannot, to record what we would otherwise miss, to hold the data until someone comes to collect it.

We will return in October.
