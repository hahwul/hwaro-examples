The Luberon runs east to west, two long limestone whalebacks with a single
deep cut between them at Lourmarin, and its forests are the only places in
the Vaucluse where you can hide from a mistral for an hour at a time. The
plan, in April, was exactly that: stitch together the sheltered tracks —
cedar forest on the Petit Luberon, holm oak on the north flank — and take
the wind only at the crossings.

<!-- more -->

It half worked, which for a plan involving the mistral is a distinguished
result. The climb out of Venasque was old friends: white gravel, 8%,
switchbacks under oaks that broke the wind into something merely annoying.
Murs and the high plateau to Roussillon were open and paid for in full.
The ochre quarries glowed like embers under a scrubbed blue sky — the
mistral's one unambiguous gift is visibility; on a blowing day you can see
the Alps from a supermarket car park — and the descent into Roussillon's
red streets was worth every watt of the approach.

The cedar forest road on the Petit Luberon crest is the finest gravel
riding in the region and I will not be taking questions. Six kilometres of
smooth white track between trees planted from Algerian seed in the 1860s,
the wind roaring in the crowns forty feet up while the track below sat in
an eerie pocket of calm. Then the gap at Lourmarin, where the whole day's
sheltered credit was repaid at interest: the combe acts as the only door
through the massif, and every cubic metre of air in the northern Vaucluse
was queueing to use it.

I came home through Apt on the old railway line as the light went long
and gold, 164 kilometres up, jacket zipped to the chin, drivetrain full
of ochre dust that I am still finding in the cassette. The long way home
is the whole point of owning the second bike.
