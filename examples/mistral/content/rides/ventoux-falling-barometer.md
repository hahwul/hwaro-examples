There is a barometer in my kitchen, a brass one left by the previous owner
of the house, and on Saturday morning it was falling like it owed somebody
money. The forecast said the mistral would establish itself in the evening.
The barometer disagreed. I chose to believe the forecast, because the
forecast was telling me what I wanted to hear.

The climb from Bédoin is three climbs wearing one name. The first, through
the vineyards to Saint-Estève, is a warm-up with delusions. The second, the
forest, is the honest one: ten kilometres that never drop below 9%, where
the plane trees give way to cedars and the cedars keep the air so still you
can hear your own drivetrain judging your cadence. I rode it well, all
things considered, and came out of the trees at Chalet Reynard forty
minutes before the wind's scheduled arrival.

The wind does not read schedules. The moment the forest ends, the mountain
turns white — bare limestone scree to the summit — and there is nothing
left between you and whatever the Rhône valley has decided to send. What it
had decided to send was the front edge of a proper mistral, six hours ahead
of the courier's estimate. The last six kilometres were ridden at a lean I
usually reserve for cornering. Past the Tom Simpson memorial the gusts were
strong enough that I stopped pedalling squares and started pedalling
trapezoids, and the summit sign, when it came, was less a triumph than an
arrest.

The descent to Sault was the reward: the wind on my right shoulder,
seventy unearned kilometres per hour, lavender fields still a month from
bloom laid out grey-green below. I came home the long way, down the Nesque
gorge with the wind finally, blessedly, behind me. The barometer, when I
got in, had stopped falling. Of course it had. Its work was done.
