The Gorges de la Nesque has a settled reputation: the gentlest great road
in Provence. You climb it from Villes-sur-Auzon at a civilised 4%, carved
balconies and rock arches all the way, coffee in Sault, home with a tailwind.
Every guidebook agrees, and on a windless day every guidebook is right.

<!-- more -->

On the eighth of November there was no windless anything. The mistral had
been running for two days, and a gorge that points north-west is not a road
in a mistral — it is a nozzle. The valley narrows by two-thirds between
Monieux and the Belvédère du Castellaras, and the air, obeying the same
plumbing rules as everything else, accelerates to fit. The weather station
at Carpentras read 60 km/h. The gorge, I am confident, read more.

I climbed it anyway, which is the entire editorial policy of this journal.
From Villes-sur-Auzon the first kilometres were merely rude — a headwind
you could negotiate with, ledge by ledge. Past Monieux's high bridge the
negotiation ended. The wind came down the funnel in slugs, five seconds
apart, each one a soft wall. My speed on a 4% grade fell to numbers I
associate with the Bédoin forest, which is more than twice as steep. At
the belvédère I stopped, wedged the bike against the parapet, and watched
a raven attempt to fly up the gorge, reconsider, and go around over the
plateau instead. Smarter than me by a margin.

Sault, when it came, was warm bread and an empty square. The return leg
over the Col des Abeilles put the wind on my hip, then — after the turn at
Malaucène — finally behind me, and the last twenty kilometres home ran at
a speed that felt fraudulent to sign for.

The wrong way up the Nesque is 26 kilometres of arguing with physics.
I recommend it once. The gorge does not care how you feel about it, and
there is a specific kind of quiet in that.
