Brevet organisers publish elevation profiles, and the profile for this one
was a flat line with a rumour of a bump near Avignon. Riders from hillier
regions saw it and smiled. The locals saw the date, checked the pressure
charts, and quietly fitted smaller front rings. A flat course in the Rhône
delta in March is not a flat course. It is a wind tunnel with checkpoints.

<!-- more -->

We rolled out of Carpentras at six, forty of us, into the kind of cold
dark that makes everyone's dynamo lights seem brighter than they are. The
first two hours, angling south-west to Avignon and down the left bank of
the Rhône, the mistral was a hand on the back. Fifty kilometres passed in
ninety minutes. Nobody was foolish enough to celebrate.

The Camargue in daylight is the strangest place you can take a road bike:
rice paddies, black bulls, pink birds standing one-legged in wind-ruffled
brine. The road to Saintes-Maries runs dead straight and dead exposed, and
the wind came across it at ninety degrees, sustained, all morning. Echelons
formed and shattered. I spent forty kilometres riding at a lean, watching
the rider ahead's rear wheel drift half a metre sideways with each gust,
tasting salt that had been airborne since Aigues-Mortes.

The turn at the sea was the day's hinge. Stamp the card, eat a sausage
roll, look at the Mediterranean for exactly one minute — and then face
north, into it, for a hundred and forty kilometres. There is no tactics
for this. There is a gear you can hold and a wattage you can pay, and the
delta gives you nothing back: no hedge, no cutting, no farmhouse for
kilometres at a time. I rode from control to control on arithmetic and
oat bars, and the arithmetic got very slow after Arles.

I finished in fourteen hours and nine minutes, last light long gone, and
the finish volunteer, stamping my card, said the thing every finisher in
Provence has heard: at least it wasn't blowing today. He appeared to
mean it.
