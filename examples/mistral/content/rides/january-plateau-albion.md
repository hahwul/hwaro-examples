There is a species of ride you do in January not because it will be
pleasant but because it will be true. The Plateau d'Albion in midwinter
is the truest place I know: a thousand-metre-high lavender steppe with
no trees, no walls, and no opinion of you whatsoever.

<!-- more -->

The numbers going up were reasonable. Minus two is a workable temperature
if you dress like a bank robber, and the long drag up the Nesque valley to
Sault is sheltered enough to keep the machine warm. It was on the plateau
itself, past Saint-Christol's silent airfield, that the mistral introduced
the day's real mathematics. Fifty kilometres per hour of moving air takes
minus two and spends it like minus fourteen. I logged the readings at three
points, because writing things down is how I stay calm:

| Point | Altitude | Air temp | Wind | Felt like |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Sault, boulangerie | 765 m | 1 C | 30 km/h | -6 C |
| Saint-Christol crossroads | 850 m | -1 C | 45 km/h | -11 C |
| Plateau high point | 1,101 m | -2 C | 50 km/h | -14 C |

The plateau road runs eleven kilometres north-east, dead straight, dead
into it. Lavender in January is cropped to grey corduroy rows, and the
wind combs along them with a hiss you feel in your fillings. My bidons
froze from the top down, valve first — drink early, a lesson re-learned
annually. At the Col de l'Homme Mort, whose name I chose not to dwell on,
I turned south, and the wind swung from enemy to engine in the width of
one road sign.

The descent back to Sault took eleven minutes for what had cost me
fifty-five going the other way. In the boulangerie the woman behind the
counter looked at the ice on my jacket collar and gave me my pain aux
raisins with the particular tenderness the Vaucluse reserves for its
seasonal idiots. It was, all told, a magnificent day. Ask me again in
July and I will say the same, because by July it will be true.
