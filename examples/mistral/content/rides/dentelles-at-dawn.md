The mistral, whatever else you can say about it, keeps office hours. In
early summer it tends to sleep past sunrise, gathering itself somewhere
north of Valence, and there is a window — four hours, maybe five — when
the windiest corner of France is as still as a photograph. On the longest
day of the year, that window opens at 5:40 in the morning.

<!-- more -->

I was in Beaumes-de-Venise at six, when the Dentelles were doing the thing
they are named for: the limestone crests catching first light like torn
lace, every ridgeline drawn in apricot. The climb to Suzette is the
prettiest six kilometres I know, and in the flat dawn calm I could hear
things the wind normally confiscates — a dog two villages away, my own
tyres on the chip seal, a vineyard sprayer starting up somewhere below
in the Muscat rows.

Over the Col de la Chaîne the road tips down toward Malaucène between
walls of broom in full yellow riot, and I stopped at the top for no
reason a training plan would recognise. Stillness, in the Vaucluse, is
the rarer weather. It seemed wasteful not to stand in it.

The wind clocked in at nine, as forecast, while I was eating in Malaucène.
You feel it arrive as a change in the plane trees first — a stiffening,
a show of silver from the leaves turning their backs — and within twenty
minutes the café awnings were drumming and the day's real regime was
installed. The ride home to Mazan was twenty-five kilometres, crosswind,
earned at full price.

The lesson, learned again each June: the mistral does not forgive rides,
it merely defers them. But four borrowed hours in the Dentelles at dawn
is the best debt in Provence, and I intend to keep running it up.
