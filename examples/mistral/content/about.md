My name is Camille Arnoux. I live in Mazan, a village in the Vaucluse that
sits in the middle of a triangle drawn between Mont Ventoux, the Gorges de la
Nesque, and the Dentelles de Montmirail. It is, by most sane measures, a
wonderful place to ride a bicycle. It is also one of the windiest inhabited
corners of Europe.

The mistral is a katabatic wind that pours down the Rhône valley when a high
settles over the Bay of Biscay and a low digs in over the Gulf of Genoa. It
blows from the north or north-west, it arrives without much warning, it can
run for three, six, or nine days at a stretch, and on the open plateaus it
regularly passes 80 km/h. Local wisdom says you plan your rides around it.
This journal exists because I stopped doing that.

## The rule

Every ride reported here was ridden at least partly into the mistral, on
purpose. Not out of masochism — or not only — but because the wind is the
most honest terrain in Provence. A climb is the same gradient every day. The
wind decides fresh each morning what the road is going to cost.

## The numbers

Each report in [the ride archive](@/rides/_index.md) opens with two figures:
kilometres ridden and metres climbed.
Distance comes from a wired computer on the fork blade, climbing from a
barometric head unit, both rounded to nothing — the number I saw is the
number you get. Wind speeds are quoted from the Mont Ventoux summit
observatory or the Carpentras station, whichever was nearer the day's
high point, and I note gusts rather than averages because gusts are what
put you in the ditch.

What the numbers can't hold — the sound the wind makes in a wheat field,
the particular insult of a crosswind on a bridge — goes in the prose. That
is what the prose is for.

The machinery that carries all of this into the wind is documented on
[the bikes page](@/bikes.md), including the gearing I am regularly mocked
for and regularly right about.
