Two bikes carry everything in this journal. Both were chosen, and then
slowly rebuilt, around a single design constraint: they have to be ridable
in a straight line when the air is moving at 70 km/h and I am not.

## The randonneuse

A lugged steel frame built by a two-man workshop outside Avignon — Atelier
Bruyère, who mostly make touring machines for people crossing continents.
Mine is the opposite: built for going out into the worst forecast of the
week and coming home the same day. Low trail geometry so the front end
doesn't tuck in gusts, a handlebar bag instead of a saddle pack because
weight over the front wheel is stability when the wind is on the nose,
and 650B tyres at pressures that would horrify a time triallist.

The gearing is unfashionable and I defend every tooth of it. A 46/30
up front and an 11–36 out back sounds like touring gears until you have
ridden the Bédoin road at 9% into a headwind that subtracts another
imaginary 4%.

## The gravel bike

The second bike has no romance to it, which is why I trust it. A stock
aluminium frame, bought used in Carpentras, wearing 45 mm tyres and bar
tape older than some of my routes. It exists for the forest tracks of the
Luberon and the white gravel roads of the Albion plateau, where the mistral
has scoured the surface down to washboard and marbles.

## The build sheets

| Component | Randonneuse | Gravel |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Frame | Atelier Bruyère steel, 650B | Aluminium, 700C |
| Tyres | 42 mm slick, 3.2 bar | 45 mm file tread, 2.4 bar |
| Gearing | 46/30 × 11–36 | 40 × 10–44 |
| Brakes | Mechanical disc | Hydraulic disc |
| Lighting | Dynamo hub, always on | USB lamps, usually flat |
| Bags | Front rando bag | Frame bag |

The only shared part is the bell. It has never once been heard over the
wind, but rules are rules.
