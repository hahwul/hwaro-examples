About

Why Dodam exists, who writes it, and what to expect every other Sunday

Dodam started as a note to myself, then it became a letter. I live four floors up, with a balcony that gets direct sun from about ten in the morning until four in the afternoon, and for a long time I treated it like a project to finish rather than a place to be with. Every plant was a task. Every yellow leaf felt like a failure I needed to correct by evening. That is not a sustainable way to garden, and it turned out not to be a sustainable way to do much of anything else either.

The name is Korean — 도담, romanized dodam — a word usually paired with itself, dodam-dodam, used to describe a child growing up steadily, healthily, without being rushed. Gardeners use it too, for the way a good plant grows: not explosively, not showily, just steadily, in proportion to the care and light and time it is actually given. That is the pace this letter tries to keep, both in what it asks of the balcony and in what it asks of you.

What arrives every other Sunday

Each issue is short — usually one clear observation from the two weeks prior, worked through in enough detail to be useful, plus whatever practical note falls out of it. Some issues are about a single plant: a tomato that lagged behind its siblings, a fig cutting that finally broke dormancy. Others are about a habit — overwatering, over-pruning, the general tendency to mistake attention for improvement. None of them assume you have more than a balcony, a few pots, and some patience you are still practicing.

There is no pretense of expertise here beyond what four years of small failures on a concrete ledge can teach a person. Measurements are approximate. Advice is offered the way a neighbor offers it over a shared fence, not the way a manual states it.

Getting in touch

The subscribe form on the homepage is a static demonstration for now — this example site is not wired to a mailing list — but every issue is published in the archive and available over RSS the moment it goes out, so nothing here is actually locked behind a subscription.

If you keep your own balcony, terrace, or windowsill and something in a letter helped or contradicted your own experience, that is exactly the kind of correspondence this project was built to invite, even if the plumbing for replies is not yet built.

Dodam is written from a fourth-floor balcony, in whatever weather it happens to be. New issues arrive every other Sunday — no more often than the plants themselves change.
