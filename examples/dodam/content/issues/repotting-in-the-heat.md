Late May is when the root-bound plants finally announce themselves — water running straight through without the soil absorbing it, growth stalled despite decent light, roots circling the surface. It is also, reliably, when daytime temperatures start climbing past comfortable repotting weather. Those two facts collide every year, and every year I have to relearn the same handful of adjustments to do it safely.

<!-- more -->

## Confirm it is actually root-bound first

Slide the plant out of its pot — supporting the base, not pulling by the stem — before assuming anything. A healthy root system shows white or light tan roots with some visible soil still between them. A root-bound one shows a dense mat of roots circling the inside edge of the pot with little to no soil visible, sometimes pushing the whole root ball into the pot's exact shape. If what you see looks more like the first description, the plant likely needs something else entirely this week — shade, water, patience — not a new pot.

## Size up modestly, not dramatically

The instinct in a heat wave is to give a struggling plant a much bigger home, reasoning that more room solves more problems. It usually does the opposite. A pot much larger than the current root system holds more soil than the roots can use, which stays wet longer than the plant wants, which invites rot right as the plant is already stressed by heat. Two to four centimeters of additional diameter is the standard, reliable step up — enough room for a season of new growth without leaving a waterlogged margin the roots cannot reach.

## Choose the coolest hours available

Repot in the early morning or the evening, never in direct midday sun, and if a heatwave is forecast for the days immediately following, it is worth waiting for a cooler window even if that means living with a slightly cramped pot a little longer. A plant just disturbed at the roots has temporarily reduced capacity to take up water, exactly when hot weather is asking the most of it. Giving it a shaded, sheltered day or two after repotting — even moving it out of direct sun briefly if your setup allows — closes that gap.

## Water it in properly, then leave it

A thorough watering immediately after repotting settles the new soil around the roots and closes air pockets that would otherwise dry roots out unevenly. After that, resist checking on it daily; a repotted plant in early summer heat typically needs about a week of slightly closer attention to watering, not a full return to your normal schedule, and definitely not fertilizer, which can burn a root system that has not finished settling.

None of this is complicated, but all of it is easy to skip when a plant looks distressed and the instinct is to fix it immediately. The fix, again, mostly involves doing less, more carefully.
