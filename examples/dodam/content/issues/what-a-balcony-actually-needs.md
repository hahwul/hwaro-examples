For the first letter, I want to start with the question I get asked most, in one form or another: what do I actually need to begin? The honest answer is less than the gardening aisle wants you to believe. One pot with a real drainage hole, a bag of decent potting mix, a plant suited to the light you actually have, and roughly six weeks of paying attention before you decide anything is wrong. Everything else — the moisture meters, the fourteen kinds of fertilizer, the trellises for plants you have not bought yet — can wait, and mostly should.

## Start with the light, not the plant

The single biggest mistake I made in my first balcony season was choosing plants I liked the idea of, then trying to force my light to accommodate them. It goes the other way. Stand on your balcony at three points in the day — morning, midday, late afternoon — for about a week, and just watch where the sun actually falls and for how long. My balcony faces southeast and gets strong direct light from roughly ten until four; a north-facing balcony one street over gets bright, indirect light all day and almost no direct sun at all. Those are two different gardens, and no amount of enthusiasm changes which plants will thrive in each.

## One pot, done properly

Resist the starter kit. A single five-liter terracotta or glazed ceramic pot, with an actual hole in the bottom — not a decorative cachepot with no drainage — will teach you more in one season than five mismatched pots bought in a single trip. Terracotta breathes and dries faster, which is forgiving for people who tend to overwater; glazed ceramic and plastic hold moisture longer, which suits hotter, windier balconies. Neither is wrong. Match the material to your own habits, not to what looks best.

## Potting mix, not garden soil

Bagged potting mix, not soil dug from anywhere, and not straight compost either. Garden soil compacts hard in a container and drowns roots; a proper mix stays loose and lets air reach them. If you want to economize, a general-purpose mix with a handful of perlite worked in for extra drainage covers the overwhelming majority of balcony plants without needing a different bag for each one.

## Then wait

The hardest instruction in this letter is the one nobody wants: once the plant is in, water it, place it where the light audit told you to, and then leave it alone for a stretch of days rather than checking on it twice daily. A new plant needs time to settle its roots into unfamiliar mix before it can tell you anything true about whether it is happy. Judging it on day three is like judging a conversation by its first sentence.

That is the whole kit. Everything after this is refinement, and refinement is what the rest of these letters are for.
