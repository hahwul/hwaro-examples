More balcony plants die from overwatering than from underwatering, by a wide margin, and I do not think this is really a knowledge problem. Most people who overwater their plants can recite the finger test — push a finger an inch into the soil, water only if it comes out dry — and do it anyway, on the wrong day, because watering is doing something, and doing something feels better than sitting with the discomfort of not knowing whether a plant is okay.

<!-- more -->

## The tell in my own watering can

I noticed the pattern in myself before I could name it: I watered more on the days I felt anxious about something unrelated to the balcony entirely — a deadline, an argument, a piece of bad news — regardless of whether any plant actually needed it. The watering can became a way of managing a feeling that had nothing to do with soil moisture, and the plants absorbed the consequences of a stress that was mine, not theirs. Waterlogged roots cannot take up oxygen, they begin to rot, and a plant that is drowning can look, confusingly, a lot like a plant that is thirsty — wilting leaves, a general droop — which then invites more water, which makes it worse. It is a genuinely vicious cycle, and it starts in the gardener, not the pot.

## Two things that actually broke it for me

The first was switching from a schedule in my head to a physical check every time, no exceptions: lift the pot. A recently watered pot is noticeably heavier than a dry one, once you have felt the difference a few times, and this simple habit stopped more unnecessary watering than any amount of willpower did, because it replaced a feeling with a fact.

The second was accepting that most common balcony plants — tomatoes, basil, chilies, geraniums — genuinely prefer to dry out somewhat between waterings, and that a slightly wilted look in the early evening after a hot day is not an emergency. Plants droop in heat to reduce water loss through their leaves; they often recover fully once temperatures drop overnight, with no watering at all. Learning to wait until morning before deciding whether a plant actually needed water, rather than reacting to the four o'clock droop, cut my watering frequency by close to half.

## What I would ask, before the watering can comes out

Is the top inch or two of soil actually dry? Does the pot feel light? And, the harder question: am I about to water because the plant needs it, or because I need to do something with my hands right now? Only one of those is a reason to water. The other is worth sitting with instead — on the balcony, ideally, where at least the discomfort has decent company.
