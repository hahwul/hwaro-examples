Two Junes ago I rooted a fig cutting in a jar of water on this balcony, more out of curiosity than any real expectation, and moved it into soil once the roots looked substantial. Its first full year in a pot was, by most visible measures, a disappointment: a handful of leaves, no real gain in height, nothing that looked like the vigorous growth fig trees are supposed to be known for. This spring, its second, it broke dormancy with more new growth in three weeks than it had produced in the entire previous year combined, and I think the gap between those two years is the truest thing I can tell you about growing something long-lived in a container.

## What the quiet year was actually for

Cuttings and young woody plants spend their early seasons building a root system proportional to the container they are in, largely out of sight, before they commit real energy to top growth. A fig that looks stalled above the soil line in year one is very often not stalled at all below it — and pulling it out to check, the temptation I mostly resisted, disturbs exactly the structure it needs to finish. I gave it a slightly larger pot at the end of that first year, more out of hope than evidence, top-dressed with fresh compost, and otherwise left it through winter with reduced watering and no fertilizer, which is the standard dormancy care for a fig that has dropped its leaves and is doing nothing you can see.

## Reading dormancy correctly

A dormant fig looks, to an inexperienced eye, exactly like a dead one: bare branches, no leaves, no obvious sign of life. The distinction is in the wood — a light scratch on a young branch with a fingernail should reveal green, moist tissue underneath the bark, not brown and dry. I checked this twice over the winter, mostly for my own reassurance, and both times found green, which was the only real evidence I had that anything was still happening at all. Watering through dormancy stays minimal, just enough to keep the roots from fully drying out, never enough to keep the soil consistently moist the way an actively growing plant would need.

## What this spring looked like

New growth started at the branch tips in early April, small and tentative at first, then genuinely fast through May — new leaves unfurling every few days, the kind of pace that makes a plant look different each morning. I repotted it once more in late May, sizing up modestly the way the last letter described, and by this issue it has more leaf surface than its entire first year produced, with the beginnings of what might, if this second-year pattern holds, be its first fruit next year rather than this one.

## The pace this letter keeps trying to describe

A fig tree does not know that a balcony gardener wants results within one growing season. It grows on a timeline set by its own biology, and the only real choice available to the person tending it is whether to work with that timeline or fight it. This one asked for two quiet years before it did anything worth writing about, and both of those years were, it turns out, required — not wasted, not a sign anything had gone wrong, just root work that had not yet become visible. That is most of what this letter has been trying to say since the first issue, said now by a plant instead of by me: growth at the pace something actually needs is still growth, even in the seasons it does not show.
