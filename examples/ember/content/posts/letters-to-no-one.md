The essay you are reading now began as a letter to no one. I sat down without a topic, without an outline, without even a clear sense of what I wanted to say. I wrote the first sentence because it was the only sentence I had, and then I wrote the next one because the first one demanded it.

This is not a method I would recommend for professional writing. Deadlines and editors require structure, and structure requires planning. But for the kind of writing I do here -- personal, exploratory, written primarily to understand what I think -- the letter to no one is the most honest form I have found.

## The Problem with Audience

When you write with a reader in mind, you make concessions. You explain things that do not need explaining. You soften claims to avoid objections. You perform competence, or vulnerability, or wit, depending on which version of yourself you think the reader wants to see. These concessions are not dishonest, exactly, but they are distortions. The writing bends toward the imagined reader and away from the actual thought.

Removing the audience removes the distortion. When no one is listening, you have no reason to perform. You can say the thing you are actually thinking, even if it is incomplete, unfashionable, or wrong.

## How It Works

The practice is simple:

1. Sit down with a blank page
2. Write as if you are explaining something to yourself
3. Do not worry about structure, tone, or correctness
4. Follow the thought wherever it leads
5. Stop when the thought runs out

What emerges from this process is rarely publishable as-is. The sentences wander. The paragraphs lack transitions. The argument, if there is one, doubles back on itself. But somewhere in the mess, there is usually a core of genuine thinking -- an idea or observation that could not have been reached through outlining alone.

The editing process, when it comes, is not about adding. It is about finding the real essay inside the raw material and removing everything that is not it.

## The Precedent

This approach has a long lineage. Montaigne's essays were, by his own account, attempts to understand himself through writing. The word *essai* means *attempt* -- a trying-out, not a finished product. His essays wander, contradict themselves, and frequently admit ignorance. They are, in the best sense, letters to no one: one mind working through its own material in real time.

> I do not understand myself. I record what I find, without organizing it. Tomorrow I may find something different.

The freedom in this posture is enormous. You do not have to be right. You do not have to be consistent. You only have to be honest about where your thinking has taken you at this particular moment.

## The Value of Not Knowing

The essays I am proudest of are the ones that surprised me. I sat down thinking I believed one thing and discovered, through the act of writing, that I believed something else. This kind of discovery is only possible when you do not know where you are going before you start.

Planning an essay in advance -- choosing a thesis, marshaling evidence, constructing an argument -- produces a different kind of writing. It is useful writing, often persuasive writing, but it is not discovery. The conclusion was determined before the first word was set down. The essay is a performance of thinking, not thinking itself.

Letters to no one are thinking itself. They are slow, inefficient, and frequently unproductive. But when they work, they produce something that no amount of planning could have generated: an encounter with your own mind that changes what you know.
