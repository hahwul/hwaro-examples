A page set in a single, well-chosen typeface tends to outlast pages built from many. The eye does not need novelty to remain engaged; it needs hierarchy, rhythm, and air. Restraint is the easier path only in retrospect. In practice, it asks the designer to refuse most of what the system makes available.

## The Vertical Grid

Line height is the quietest decision on a page and often the most consequential. A measure of 65 to 75 characters paired with leading near 1.6 produces a column that scans without strain. Tightening the leading makes the page feel busy. Loosening it makes paragraphs drift apart and lose their argument.

## Weight as Punctuation

Bold is a tool, not a tone. Reserve it for the rare word that must carry the sentence on its back. Italic handles emphasis with less force and rarely competes with surrounding type. The semi-bold weight, often forgotten, is the right answer for headings that should sit close to body text in scale.

## Numerals and Small Caps

Old-style figures move with the body line and disappear into prose, which is what numerals in running text should do. Lining figures belong to tables and forms. Small caps, when properly drawn rather than scaled, soften acronyms that would otherwise shout. These are the kinds of details a reader never names but always feels.

## Trusting the Reader

The temptation in long-form writing is to underline every turn of thought. A confident page does the opposite. It places the words and steps back. Indents instead of paragraph breaks. A single rule instead of a colored panel. The reader, given room, will follow.
