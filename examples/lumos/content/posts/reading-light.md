The room a reader sits in is part of the typography. Most pages are designed under fluorescent studio light and are read in something darker, warmer, or far more variable. The page that holds together at noon often collapses at dusk, and the page that glows on an OLED at midnight blinds the same reader at a coffee shop window seat.

## Contrast Is a Range

A single contrast ratio will not serve every viewing condition. The WCAG floor of 4.5 to 1 covers legibility in standard light, but readers with tired eyes or matte films overlaid on glossy panels often need closer to 7 to 1 to feel comfortable. Designing to the floor leaves no margin for the room.

## Warm Backgrounds, Cooler Light

Pure white backgrounds reflect ambient color faithfully, which is rarely flattering in practice. A background tinted three to five percent toward warm gray softens the reading surface without muddying the type. The trick is to keep the shift small enough that the page still reads as white when printed or screenshotted.

## Spacing Adjusts for the Eye

In low light, letterforms appear to thicken and counters shrink. Body text that runs comfortably at sixteen pixels in daylight benefits from an extra unit of tracking after sundown. Most readers will never adjust this themselves. Offering a discreet toggle, or simply choosing a typeface drawn with open counters, removes the need to ask.

## The Quiet Default

A theme cannot anticipate every room, but it can avoid making any of them harder. Conservative contrast, tinted whites, generous leading, and a typeface that holds its shape at small sizes will produce a page that survives the lamp on the nightstand and the noon sun on the train.
