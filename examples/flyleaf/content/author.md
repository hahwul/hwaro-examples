Ilse Kovar was born in a river town that has since been renamed twice, an experience she has said taught her everything she needed to know about how paper and memory disagree. She trained as an archivist before she trained as a writer, and spent nine years cataloguing municipal records for a regional library system not unlike the one that appears, considerably altered, in these pages.

[Flyleaf](@/chapters/the-night-ledger.md) is her first novella. She has described it as a piece of furniture built from the true dimensions of a lie: the checkpoints, the timetables, and the shipping manifests are drawn from public transit records of the period; the archivist who carries the names out is invented, along with the country that forces her to.

She currently lives outside a city she prefers not to name in print, works part-time at a conservation studio, and reads flyleaves out of habit before she reads anything else in a secondhand book — a tell, she admits, that gives away exactly which chapter of this novella is closest to her own life.

Correspondence may be left, as is traditional for archivists, with the publisher rather than the author.
