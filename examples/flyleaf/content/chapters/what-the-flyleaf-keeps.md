## I

Eleven years after the last run of the Salt Line, a graduate assistant named Feyza Aslan is cataloguing a shelf of provincial novels donated, according to the accession record, sometime in the previous decade by a regional library that no longer appears under that name in any directory she can find. Dr. Şoim, long retired, still keeps an office two floors up and has told every new assistant, without explanation, to hold every flyleaf to the desk lamp before shelving. Feyza does this out of habit rather than belief, the way one keeps any inherited ritual, until the evening she tilts a green cloth-bound novel toward the bulb and the page resolves, all at once, into a column of names, a marsh, a bell's foundry mark, coordinates for a millpond that has not appeared on any Directorate map for going on two decades.

<!-- more -->

There turn out to be, once the whole donation is checked properly, one hundred and sixty-one such flyleaves spanning eleven years of crates, each one a village, a street, a family plot, a bridge, pressed into paper by a hand that never signed anything. Dr. Şoim, when Feyza brings her the green book, does not seem surprised so much as finally, quietly relieved, the way a person is relieved to set down something they have carried further than they expected to have to.

## II

A researcher two years later will use the marsh quarter's coordinates, cross-referenced against a pre-correction survey held in a different archive entirely, to establish — carefully, in the dry language such things require — that a settlement by that name did, contrary to current official record, exist at that location until a specific and documentable year. It will not be reinstated on any map. That is not, in the end, what the flyleaves were for. What they were for, [Pavol told Anya once, in a kitchen with failed heating](@/chapters/the-flyleaf-trade.md), was smaller and stranger than restoration: simply that somewhere, in ordinary paper that no one had thought worth burning, the true entry would still be legible to anyone patient enough to tilt it to the light. Nothing in the ledger survives a fire. The blank page, it turns out, survives almost anything — because no one has ever thought it worth watching.
