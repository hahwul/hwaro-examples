## I

Pavol Rusen had been Assistant Cataloguer, Third Class for so long that the title had stopped being a joke and become simply a fact about him, like his height or the tremor that had settled into his left hand sometime in his sixties and never left. He trained Anya without ever using the word training. For a year she simply watched him stay late, watched him decline to hurry, and eventually understood that the extra hour was not overtime but the actual job — the one underneath the one on the laminated card.

He told her about his wife only once, on a night the heating had failed and they were both wearing coats indoors. Before Anya was born, an entire river quarter of a town three districts over had been "resettled for hydrological reasons," a phrase that meant, in practice, that a reservoir project had required a village to not have existed in the first place. His wife's family were from that quarter. There is a difference, he said, not looking at her, between losing a place and having a country tell you, patiently, for forty years, that you are misremembering it.

## II

The method itself he demonstrated with a bone-handled burnisher no larger than a pen, the kind bookbinders use to smooth a joint before it dries. Pressed hard enough and evenly enough into cotton-rag paper — the good, pre-war stock the flyleaves of older secondhand books are still sometimes made from — a burnisher leaves no ink, no residue, nothing a chemical swab or an ultraviolet lamp held flat against the page will ever register. It leaves only a compression in the fibers themselves, invisible from directly above and perfectly legible the instant a light is drawn low and sideways across the sheet, the way a groundskeeper's rake reveals the seams in a lawn at evening.

"A censor is trained to look for what was added," Pavol told her, turning the burnisher over in his ruined hand. "Nobody has ever been trained to look for what was merely pressed." At the far end of the line, a librarian named Dr. Marta Şoim receives every crate from Draven personally, and has for eleven years, and knows to run a desk lamp low across each flyleaf before it is shelved — because Pavol wrote to her once, decades ago, in a letter that said nothing about any of this directly, and asked only that she read carefully.
