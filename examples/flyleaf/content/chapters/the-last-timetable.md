## I

The notice arrives the way all the worst ones do, as a footnote to something else: a network rationalization order that consolidates six branch lines into two, effective the first of the following month, with the Salt Line named among those to be "absorbed." No new timetable is published for it. There will simply, after a certain Tuesday, be no train.

Anya reads the order twice before she understands what it actually means beneath the bureaucratic phrasing: not that the route will change, but that the corridor she has used for four years — the one guard she half trusts, the one mail car whose porter stopped asking questions, the one crossing whose floodlight has never been fixed — will not exist in any form after this month. Whatever has not been carried out by the last run will not be carried out. In the drawer at the Registry's temporary annex, in the onionskin she still keeps despite everything, there are eleven names from the marsh quarter she has never had time to press into a flyleaf at all — including, she notices now, the one she carried north folded in her coat lining [the very first night of this book](@/chapters/the-night-ledger.md), and has been meaning, for months, to do properly.

## II

The last run leaves Draven at eleven forty on a Tuesday that looks, from the platform, exactly like every other Tuesday, which is its own kind of cruelty. Costin is not on shift. In his place is a younger guard, new enough that his uniform still creases where the tailor folded it, thorough in the particular anxious way of someone trying to be noticed by a superior. He opens not one book but four, methodically, working from the top of the crate down, and Anya understands with a cold clarity that the eleventh book — the one holding the marsh quarter's true names, pressed in that afternoon with hands that would not entirely stop shaking — is fourteen books from the top, and that his method, unlike Costin's, does not stop at one.

He is holding the ninth book, turning it toward the floodlight, when the station's public address system announces, with no apparent relevance, that the eleven-forty service to the northern terminus is delayed eight minutes for a signal fault at the junction. It is not a signal fault. It is Dr. Şoim's contact in the signals office, a favor Pavol arranged years ago and never told Anya about, spent now on eight minutes that the young guard uses to jog to the signal box and back rather than finish the crate. He waves the rest through unopened, eager to be seen resolving the delay rather than completing the search. The eleventh book rides north with everyone else's, and Anya does not breathe normally again until Marrow Crossing's one floodlight is a small orange coin behind the train, and then nothing at all.
