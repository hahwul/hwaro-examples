## I

Marrow Crossing is four platforms and a single floodlight that has needed replacing since before Anya started riding this line, so the guards work in a wash of orange that makes every face look slightly cooked. Costin is on the night shift again, which she has decided, on balance, to be glad of; he is slow and thorough in a way that at least follows a pattern, and pattern is the one thing a smuggler can plan around.

He opens exactly one book from every crate — his own rule, not the Directorate's — and tonight, out of perhaps four hundred volumes, his hand finds a slim provincial novel with a green cloth cover that Anya worked on eleven nights ago. She keeps her face the way [Pavol taught her](@/chapters/the-flyleaf-trade.md) to keep it: bored, not blank, because blank is what liars perform and bored is what the genuinely uninterested actually look like.

Costin fans the pages the way he always does, back to front, and stops at the flyleaf. He holds it up — not to the floodlight overhead, which is too even, too flat to show a seam, but tilted toward the single bare bulb behind him, and for three full seconds Anya considers, in complete and orderly detail, every version of the next five minutes.

<!-- more -->

He sees nothing, because there is nothing to see straight on. The bulb is behind him, not raking across the page from the side the way lamplight would if he thought to turn it, and a blind-tooled impression pressed into cotton-rag paper without a trace of ink does not announce itself to an overhead glance. He closes the book, restacks it, and marks the crate's manifest number in a ledger of his own — a correction of the mundane kind, three hundred crates seized this quarter against a quota of three hundred and ten.

## II

"Light's cheap tonight," he says, which is what he always says when he lets something through, and Anya has never worked out if it's an apology, a warning, or simply the truth about the floodlight. She thinks, this time, it might be something closer to a confession: that he has done the arithmetic on how much a real search would cost him in hours he doesn't have, against how much a missed contraband flyleaf would cost him in trouble he might never be asked to answer for.

She doesn't test the theory. She thanks him for nothing in particular, the way she always does, and carries the crate back to the mail car with the specific, unhurried gait Pavol drilled into her the first month: the walk of someone who has already been checked and cleared, because nothing invites a second look like the walk of someone trying not to be looked at.
