## I

At the Draven Registry the fluorescent tubes never quite finish warming up, so the reading room holds a permanent, sourceless dusk, and by four in the afternoon Anya Reyk works mostly by the desk lamp and the particular pale light that correction fluid throws back at a face bent low over it. Her title, printed on a laminated card she keeps in a drawer rather than pinned to her collar, is Assistant Cataloguer, Third Class. Her actual job, the one nobody prints on cards, is to process what the Directorate of Public Memory calls corrections: a village folded into the district next door, a bridge reclassified as never having crossed anything, three hundred birth records refiled under a town whose old name no longer appears on any map the Directorate has approved this year.

<!-- more -->

She has learned to read a correction before it is announced, the way farmers read weather. A colleague pulls a whole shelf of gazetteers on a Tuesday, unasked, and stacks them by the incinerator chute instead of the return cart. A supervisor stops answering questions about the eastern marsh with anything but a smile and a change of subject. By the following week, three villages that Anya had visited as a child — she remembers a millpond, a bell that rang for weddings and for nothing else — will have ceased, officially, ever to have existed.

She stays an hour past closing most nights, ostensibly reconciling the day's requests. What she is actually doing, hunched under the one lamp the Registry does not switch off centrally, is copying the true entries onto onionskin before the corrected version goes to the vault: names, coordinates, the bell's foundry mark, the millpond's original depth as measured by a surveyor forty years dead. Pavol Rusen taught her the habit before he retired, though never in so many words. He simply left his own onionskin sheets where she would find them, and let her work out for herself what they were for.

## II

The Salt Line leaves Draven at eleven forty, and Anya is on it twice a month with a crate of secondhand books — textbooks, provincial novels, a shelf's worth of the kind of stock a university library abroad is glad to take as a donation and never troubles to inventory closely. The paperwork is real. The customs stamp is real. Nothing about the shipment, examined on its own terms, would interest anyone.

She likes the mail car at that hour: the smell of glue and coal dust, the porter who has stopped asking what she's carrying because the answer is always the same crate of the same kind of nothing. Tonight there is a name she nearly didn't have time to copy — a marsh village, three letters shorter than it used to be, folded this afternoon into a neighboring district's ledger as though it had always belonged there. She wrote it nowhere. It rides north in the lining of her coat, in her own handwriting, in a place no flyleaf could have held it in time. Ahead, in four hours, is [Marrow Crossing](@/chapters/marrow-crossing.md), where a guard she has met before likes to open exactly one book from every crate, at random, and hold it up to the light.
