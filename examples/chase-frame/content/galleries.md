A galley is a long wooden or metal tray on which type is set before it is locked into a chase. A compositor at the case fills a composing stick with type, empties the stick into the galley, and continues until the galley is full. When the galley is full it is tied up with string, slid onto the stone, and there assembled into a forme.

## The Process of the Stick

<div class="forme">
<span class="forme-label">COMPOSING &middot; STICK &middot; ONE LINE</span>

### Setting a Line

The composing stick is a small metal frame, adjustable, about eight inches long. Set to the desired measure, the stick holds one line of type and four or five leading slugs beneath. The compositor picks type from the case with the left hand, one sort at a time, and places it, face up, reading downward, in the stick with the right hand. The line is built from left to right, which means the letters are picked from right to left as the compositor reads them, and placed in the stick in reverse order.

A line of twenty-six letters takes a skilled compositor about forty seconds to set, including spaces and punctuation. A line of thirty-eight letters, near the upper bound of a comfortable measure, takes about sixty. Our shop's best compositor, M. Brace, can set at ninety seconds per line sustained over a galley, which is about thirty lines per hour, or nine hundred lines in a thirty-hour week. Most of us set at about sixty lines per hour, and the apprentice at forty.

### Spacing

Spacing is done with pieces of type-metal shorter than the letters so that they do not print. The compositor keeps, within the same case, a supply of em, en, thick, thin, and hair spaces, and arranges them between words to justify the line. A line is said to be justified when it is filled to the full measure without the type being loose or tight; a loose line drops letters under the planer, and a tight line cracks the quoin.

Hair spacing is the finest work of the compositor's left hand. A compositor who can space a justified line by feel, without measuring, is said to have the hand. Most of us spend our first two years as apprentices measuring, and our remaining years working to lose the need.

## The Tie-Up

When a galley is full, the type within it must be tied with string so that it does not fall apart on the stone. The tie-up is done with smooth cotton twine, wound around the galley five times, beginning at one corner and finishing at the same corner with a knot tucked between two lines. The knot is tucked rather than tied at the end of the wind because a visible knot can catch on the planer and dislodge type during lock-up.

A galley properly tied will hold if lifted at an angle, though it is not lifted until it is on the stone. The compositor tests the tie by tapping the top of the galley with a soft planer; any letter that falls indicates a slack in the tie-up. A slack galley is re-tied before it leaves the stand.

## When a Forme Is Broken Up

When a print run is complete, the forme is returned to the stone, the quoins are loosened, the furniture is removed and replaced in its rack, and the type is tied up again in its galley. The tied galley is then distributed: each letter is returned to its compartment in its case, one at a time, by a compositor standing at the case.

Distribution is the reverse of setting, and, because it is mechanical rather than creative, it is the slowest and most tedious part of the compositor's day. We typically do not distribute immediately after a print run; we stack tied galleys in a corner and distribute them during slack afternoons when no new setting is required. A shop that distributes promptly is a shop that has no work in hand, and a shop that does not distribute at all is a shop that will, within six months, run out of type.

> A compositor who loves to set but hates to distribute is a child; a compositor who loves both is an adult; a compositor who loves neither is a shop-keeper.
>
> -- Ernst Argent, founder of this shop, 1962.
