Presswork is what happens after the forme is locked and lifted from the stone. It is the making of the actual impression &mdash; ink on paper, one sheet at a time, on one of fourteen hand presses or one of three cylinders. Presswork is the quieter half of the shop and, by tradition, the slower half.

## The Hand Presses

Our fourteen hand presses are a mixture of Albions, Washingtons, and a single Columbian. The Albions, nine of them, are the principal workhorses of fine-book work; they operate by a toggle mechanism and pull a clean impression at modest pressure. The Washingtons, four of them, use a similar mechanism but are larger, and are reserved for forty-by-thirty jobs. The Columbian, the eagle-head press in the corner, is an 1867 model and is pulled only occasionally; it is our ceremonial press, used for the shop's own imprint broadsides.

### The Heidelberg Flatbed Cylinder

Our principal commercial press is a Heidelberg KSBA flatbed cylinder, dating from 1968. It has a bed of twenty-seven by thirty-eight inches, prints up to three thousand impressions per hour at top speed, and is operated by a single pressman standing at its right-hand side. We use it for run lengths above five hundred and for anything that would take more than a day on a hand press.

The Heidelberg is not a subtle machine. Its impression is stronger than a hand press, its ink distribution is done by mechanical rollers rather than by hand, and the pressman has less control over the inking of individual parts of the forme. For this reason, the Heidelberg is used only for formes that will stand up to heavy impression: simple body text, broadsides without halftones, commercial work.

## The Paper

We keep a standing stock of seven papers, in various weights and finishes. Most of our work is on Mohawk Superfine, a smooth-finish laid paper that has been our house standard since 1975. For deckle-edge work we keep Hahnemuehle and, occasionally, St Armand from Montreal. For Japanese-paper work we keep Kozo and Gampi sheets of various weights, which we purchase through a supplier in Kyoto.

## The Ink

We mix our own inks from Van Son base, using Pantone formulas where matching is required and our own custom mixes otherwise. Black is always Van Son 49000 rubber base, which dries slowly, stays open on the roller, and transfers cleanly from type to paper.

We do not use water-based inks. We do not use ultraviolet-curing inks. We do not use soy-based inks, though we acknowledge the environmental argument for them. We use oil-based rubber base, we dispose of waste through a licenced collector, and we clean with Crisco brand vegetable shortening rather than with solvents, a house habit going back to the 1980s.

## Rate of Impression

A trained pressman on a hand press pulls, at sustained pace, about eighty impressions per hour. On a large Washington press the rate is closer to forty, because of the greater lever effort required. On the Heidelberg cylinder, the machine pulls at its own pace; the pressman's role is to feed and watch the impression.

A day's work on a hand press is, therefore, between four hundred and six hundred impressions, depending on the press and the press-man. A day on the Heidelberg is between twelve thousand and fifteen thousand. The two rates are not directly comparable: a hand pull is a different object from a cylinder pull, and our fine-book clients know this and pay accordingly.

## Our House Policy

<blockquote>
<p>We pull to impression rather than to rate. A hand press is a tool of feel, and the pressman is the final judge of a pull. We do not run a press at a pace that compromises the pressman's ability to look at every sheet as it leaves the bed. If you need faster than we pull, you need a different shop.</p>
</blockquote>
