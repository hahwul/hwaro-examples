Every line of type carries a rhythm. The eye does not glide over text; it bounces, lands, and moves again. The shape of that bounce is set by the designer long before the reader notices.

### Cadence Before Color

A heavy display face sets a slow tempo. A condensed sans face sets a quick one. When the two share a page, they argue. The argument is not a problem; it is the point. The contrast between cadences gives the page its pulse.

* Letter spacing is the tempo control. Tighter tracking pushes the eye forward. Looser tracking forces it to rest on each glyph.
* Line height is the breath between phrases. A measured line height lets a heavy face speak with weight rather than crowd.
* Weight changes within a single line act as accents, the way a drummer hits the snare on the off beat.

### Motion as Phrasing

When a marquee runs across the page, it sets a metronome. Words that scroll past at a steady speed become beats in a loop. Words that hesitate, then snap into place, become punctuation. The same letterforms can be a hum or a shout depending on how they move.

> "Type is a beautiful group of letters, not a group of beautiful letters." - Matthew Carter

The lesson holds for kinetic typography as well. The pleasure is not in any one frame; it is in the sequence. A type system in motion succeeds when the rhythm is felt before any specific word is read. The reader leans in, then catches up to the meaning. The design has already done its work.
