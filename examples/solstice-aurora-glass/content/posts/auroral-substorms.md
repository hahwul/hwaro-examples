An auroral substorm unfolds over roughly one to three hours and follows a predictable pattern in three phases: growth, expansion, and recovery. During the growth phase, the dayside magnetopause reconnects with a southward-pointing interplanetary magnetic field, draining flux from the dayside and loading it into the magnetotail. The tail stretches, the inner plasma sheet thins, and the cross-tail current intensifies.

The expansion phase begins when reconnection erupts in the near-Earth tail, around 20 to 30 Earth radii downstream. Fast Earthward flows transport hot plasma toward the inner magnetosphere, where the previously thin current sheet abruptly collapses. Field-aligned currents close through the auroral ionosphere, dumping kilo-electronvolt electrons into the upper atmosphere. The poleward boundary of the auroral oval surges northward and the discrete arcs along its equatorward edge brighten and fragment into rays and folds.

Recovery occurs as the system relaxes back toward a quiescent configuration. The substorm current wedge decays, the auroral oval contracts, and pulsating patches replace the discrete forms. The energy budget for a typical substorm dissipates between five and ten times ten to the fifteenth joules, partitioned between Joule heating in the ionosphere, ring current injection, and plasmoid ejection down the tail.

Ground-based magnetometer chains, all-sky imagers, and incoherent scatter radars provide complementary measurements. Spacecraft constellations such as THEMIS and MMS resolve the reconnection geometry directly. The remaining open question concerns the trigger: whether the onset is set by a current-driven instability in the near-Earth tail or by an inward-propagating rarefaction wave from a more distant reconnection site.
