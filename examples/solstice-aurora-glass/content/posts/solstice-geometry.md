The solstices mark the moments when the Sun's apparent declination reaches its extreme values, plus or minus 23.44 degrees. At these points the Sun appears stationary in declination, hence the Latin sol stitium, "sun stands still." The two effects that govern the geometry are the obliquity of the ecliptic, which tilts the spin axis with respect to the orbital plane, and the eccentricity of Earth's orbit, which makes the orbital speed vary by a few percent across the year.

A common surprise is that the earliest sunset and latest sunrise do not coincide with the December solstice in the northern hemisphere. The asymmetry comes from the equation of time, which combines a sinusoidal contribution from obliquity with one from eccentricity. Near the December solstice, the equation of time is close to zero but changing rapidly, so the solar noon shifts later by roughly thirty seconds per day. Sunset, set by both noon position and the length of the daylight arc, lags the solstice by about ten days at temperate latitudes.

Plotted at a fixed civil time across a full year, the Sun traces an analemma: a slender figure-eight whose long axis runs along the meridian. Its height spans 47 degrees, twice the obliquity. Its width spans about eight degrees and reflects the equation of time. The crossover near the smaller loop falls in late April; the crossover near the larger loop falls in mid-December.

Photographers reproduce the analemma by exposing one frame of film weekly at the same clock time and the same compass bearing for a year. Modern composite techniques use individual JPEGs aligned by horizon reference. The result is a record of orbital eccentricity and axial tilt drawn directly by sunlight on the sky.
