The nearest electric light is miles away, down in the valley where the town sits along the river. Up here on the ridge, the darkness is genuine, the kind that was ordinary to every human being before the last century and is now a rare thing, sought out and traveled to. On a clear night, stepping onto the porch after the lamps are extinguished is like stepping into another world entirely.

It takes several minutes for the eyes to adjust. At first there is only the brightest scatter of stars and the vague pale band of the Milky Way. Then, slowly, the sky deepens and fills. Thousands of stars emerge, then tens of thousands, until the dome overhead is dense with light. Familiar constellations, which in the city are reduced to their brightest members, here reveal themselves in full, surrounded by so many lesser stars that the patterns are almost difficult to pick out from the abundance.

The winter sky is the finest. Orion stands high and sharp, the three stars of his belt unmistakable, the red shoulder of Betelgeuse glowing with a warmth that seems out of place in that cold field of white and blue. The Pleiades are a small bright knot to the west. Sirius blazes near the horizon with a fierce blue-white fire that seems to pulse and shift as the atmosphere bends its light. On the best nights, when the air is still and dry, the seeing is so steady that individual stars appear as true points rather than the usual shimmering spots.

There is a wool blanket kept on the porch bench for this purpose. Wrapped against the cold, with a cup of something warm if the evening demands it, one can sit for an hour or more and simply look. No telescope is needed, though one stands in the corner for nights when the planets are well placed. The naked eye is sufficient for the essential experience, which is not scientific but something closer to devotion: the slow, quiet recognition of how vast the night is, and how small and warm the cabin at one's back.
