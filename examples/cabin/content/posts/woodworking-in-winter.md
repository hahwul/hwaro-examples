The workshop is a lean-to built against the north side of the cabin, sheltered from the worst of the wind but open enough that the sawdust drifts out rather than settling in the lungs. In winter, the cold makes the wood harder to work but also drier and more predictable. There is a clarity to the grain when the moisture has been drawn out by weeks of freezing air.

The current project is a set of shelves for the kitchen wall, cut from a pine that came down in the October storm. The timber has been drying in the shed since then, stacked with spacers to let the air circulate evenly. Now it is ready. The boards are straight and pale, with a faint scent of resin that sharpens when the plane bites into the surface. Each pass of the hand plane reveals a new layer of the grain, tight and even, the record of decades of slow growth on this mountainside.

There is no power in the workshop beyond what the hands and a sharp edge can provide. The joints are cut with a backsaw and pared with a chisel, fitted dry first and then glued with care. Each shelf must be true, not for any abstract principle but because a crooked shelf is a daily irritation, and daily irritations have no place in a well-ordered home. The work is slow, but the winter is long, and there is no reason to rush what deserves to be done well.

By late afternoon the light through the workshop opening has turned golden and thin. The shelves are not finished, but the day's progress is visible in the stack of planed boards and the neat pile of shavings on the ground. Tomorrow the joinery will begin in earnest. For now, the tools are wiped clean and hung on their pegs, the shavings are gathered for fire starter, and the path back to the cabin door is a short walk through cold air that smells of pine and coming snow.
