A fringing reef looks continuous from above but is organized into distinct depth zones. Each zone receives different light, wave energy, and sediment loads, and each supports a recognizable assemblage of corals and associated species. Understanding zonation matters for survey work and for restoration planning, because transplants that work in one band often fail two meters away.

The four standard zones, moving from shore outward:

- Reef flat: shallow, often exposed at low tide, dominated by stress-tolerant species such as *Porites lutea* and encrusting *Montipora*. Wave action is moderate, but desiccation and thermal extremes are the main filters here.
- Reef crest: the highest-energy zone, where waves break. Branching *Pocillopora* and robust *Acropora* species form low, dense thickets that dissipate wave force. Skeletal density is unusually high to resist mechanical fracture.
- Fore-reef: the slope down to roughly 20 meters. Light is still abundant, water flow is steady but not turbulent, and coral diversity peaks. Plate-forming and tabular *Acropora* compete for light by extending laterally.
- Deep slope: below 25 to 30 meters in clear water, dominated by mesophotic species such as *Leptoseris* and platy *Pachyseris* that are adapted to low blue-shifted light.

Boundaries between zones are not sharp. Storm events redistribute coral fragments across the crest and fore-reef, occasionally seeding new colonies in the deep slope where they would not normally recruit. Long-term monitoring stations placed at fixed depths along a transect line are the standard tool for tracking shifts in zonation as sea levels and water temperatures change.
