Most reef-building corals do not produce light from chemical reactions. They fluoresce. The pigments responsible — green fluorescent protein and several red-shifted variants — absorb blue light from the surface and re-emit it at longer wavelengths within the same fraction of a second. The reef does not glow at depth; it converts the small amount of blue that survives the water column into colors the human eye can read at close range.

Fluorescent proteins in corals share a common ancestor with the green fluorescent protein originally isolated from *Aequorea victoria*. The chromophore forms autocatalytically from three amino acids inside a beta-barrel that protects it from quenching by water. Different host species shift the emission peak by altering the residues nearest to the chromophore, producing greens, oranges, and deep reds without changing the underlying machinery.

The functional purpose remains debated. Three hypotheses are most often tested in field studies:

- Photoprotection: the proteins absorb high-energy blue and ultraviolet wavelengths that would otherwise damage symbiotic algae living in the coral tissue.
- Photosynthesis enhancement: re-emitted longer wavelengths penetrate deeper into the tissue and reach algae shaded by the polyp's surface layer.
- Antioxidant activity: certain red-shifted variants quench reactive oxygen species during thermal stress.

Field measurements during bleaching events suggest the third role may matter most. Corals that retain visible fluorescence under thermal stress recover symbiont populations more often than those that lose pigment expression early. The glow, in this reading, is a stress marker as much as a light show.
