The same parent magma can produce three different rocks depending on how it cools. Obsidian, perlite, and pumice are siblings separated by a single variable: quench rate.

## Cooling Regimes

A felsic melt above 800 degrees Celsius is held in a metastable liquid state. As it loses heat, it must either crystallize or freeze into a glass. The transition is governed by the competition between viscous flow and molecular diffusion.

When cooling is fast enough that diffusion cannot keep up, the melt vitrifies. Rhyolitic compositions, with their high silica content and resulting high viscosity, vitrify at modest cooling rates of 1 to 10 degrees per second.

A slower cooling rate, sustained over hours, allows incipient crystallization. The result is a microcrystalline rock such as rhyolite proper, which lacks the conchoidal fracture and luster of obsidian.

## Hydration and Perlite

Obsidian that absorbs water during prolonged residence in moist conditions becomes perlite. The water enters along microfractures and along the surface, expanding the glass and reducing its mechanical strength. Heated perlite expands explosively, which is the basis of its commercial use as a soil amendment and lightweight aggregate.

The transformation from obsidian to perlite is gradual. Specimens recovered from the same outcrop can sit anywhere on the continuum, with corresponding variation in fracture quality and color.

## Pumice and Vesiculation

If the magma is volatile-rich and decompresses rapidly during eruption, the dissolved gases come out of solution before the melt has finished quenching. The result is a foam frozen in place: pumice. The same chemistry that produces obsidian under quiet effusive conditions produces pumice under explosive ones.

A continuous range of intermediate textures exists. Vesiculated obsidian, sometimes called pumiceous glass, retains the fracture properties of obsidian along the dense layers and the buoyancy of pumice in the vesicular zones.
