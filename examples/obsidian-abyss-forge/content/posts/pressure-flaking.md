Pressure flaking is the final stage of biface reduction. A pointed tool of antler, copper, or hardened wood is pressed against a prepared edge until a small flake detaches. The technique allows fine shaping that percussion alone cannot achieve.

## Tool Geometry

The flaker needs a tip radius small enough to localize stress, but large enough to resist crushing on contact. Antler tine works because it deforms slightly under load and grips the edge without slipping. Copper, used in modern reproduction work, holds a sharper point but tends to glance off poorly prepared platforms.

The tool is held in the dominant hand, often braced against the thigh or a leather pad. The blank rests on the supporting hand, also leather-protected, with the target platform exposed at the working edge.

## Platform Preparation

Pressure flaking is unforgiving without correct platform prep. The platform must be:

- Beveled to between 60 and 75 degrees from the intended flake plane
- Abraded with a coarse stone to strengthen the loading point
- Isolated from adjacent platforms so the flake terminates cleanly

A platform that is too steep produces a step fracture. One that is too oblique produces a feathered termination but risks running into the opposite face and splitting the blank. Practiced knappers calibrate the angle by feel.

## Force Application

The flake detaches under approximately 5 to 50 newtons of force, applied over a fraction of a second. The motion combines an inward push with a downward roll that initiates the crack and steers it across the face.

A successful pressure flake leaves a narrow scar with parallel ripples and a clean termination at or near the medial ridge. Long parallel-flaked blades, such as those from late prehistoric sites in the American Great Basin, are the showcase work of this technique.
