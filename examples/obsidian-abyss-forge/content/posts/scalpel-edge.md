A pressure-flaked obsidian blade can hold an edge two orders of magnitude finer than the best stainless-steel scalpel. The advantage shows up under the microscope: incisions made with obsidian close cleaner, with less inflammatory response and reduced scarring during healing.

## Edge Geometry

The cutting edge of a hardened-steel scalpel terminates at roughly 30 nanometers, limited by the grain structure of the metal. An obsidian flake terminates at about 3 nanometers, set only by the molecular dimensions of the silicate network.

The narrower the edge, the smaller the volume of tissue that is crushed during cutting. Crushed tissue triggers a stronger immune response and produces more visible scar tissue during repair.

## Practical Constraints

Obsidian is rarely used in mainstream practice for several reasons:

- The material is brittle and chips on bone
- Sterilization by autoclave produces microfractures from thermal stress
- The blades are not currently FDA-approved for human surgery
- Each blade is hand-knapped, which limits supply and consistency

Some plastic and ophthalmic surgeons use obsidian blades in private practice, particularly for fine incisions where the cosmetic outcome matters and the underlying anatomy avoids hard tissue.

## Manufacturing

Surgical blades are pressure-flaked from selected high-purity obsidian, usually from sources in the western United States or Mexico. The knapper works under magnification, removing flakes a few millimeters wide to refine the edge.

Each blade is mounted in a polymer or stainless handle and individually sterilized with ethylene oxide gas, which avoids the thermal stress of autoclaving. Quality control involves inspecting the edge under scanning electron microscopy and rejecting any blade with visible chips or step fractures.

The result is a single-use instrument that costs more than a steel equivalent but cuts cleaner. For the surgeons who use them, the trade is worth making.
