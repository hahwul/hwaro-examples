Jade carving is among the oldest continuous art forms in human history, stretching back over 7,000 years in China alone. Unlike softer stones, jade cannot be chiseled — it must be slowly ground and polished, demanding extraordinary patience from the artisan.

## The Nature of the Stone

Jade comes in two mineral forms:

1. **Nephrite** — The classical jade of Chinese civilization, ranging from creamy white to deep green
2. **Jadeite** — A harder, more vivid stone prized for its imperial green variety, "fěicuì" (翡翠)

The most valued shade is "imperial green" — a deep, saturated emerald with a vitreous translucency that seems to glow from within.

## The Carver's Philosophy

A jade carver does not impose a design upon the stone. Instead, they study the stone's natural form, color variations, and inclusions, allowing the material to guide the final work.

> "The stone speaks first. The carver merely listens and reveals what was always within."

This philosophy — of working *with* natural qualities rather than against them — produces pieces of breathtaking harmony.

## Techniques

Traditional jade carving employs several key techniques:

- **Relief Carving** — Raising designs from a flat surface
- **Openwork** — Creating intricate lattice patterns by removing material
- **Chain Carving** — Carving interlocking links from a single piece, demonstrating supreme skill
- **Polishing** — The final stage that reveals the stone's hidden translucency

## A Design Metaphor

The jade carver's approach offers a powerful metaphor for design: the best results come not from forcing a vision, but from understanding your medium deeply and working with its strengths. In web design, this means embracing the nature of the screen — its luminosity, its layering capability, its capacity for fluid motion.
