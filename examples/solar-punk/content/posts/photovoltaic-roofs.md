Photovoltaic roofs are no longer optional infrastructure. They are the load-bearing surface of the optimist sci-fi neighborhood, the part of the building that talks to the rest of the block. A residential array no longer means three panels bolted to an asphalt shingle. It means a continuous laminate, a tile, a curved canopy over a kitchen garden, or a translucent awning above a side street that doubles as a play area.

The interesting work happens where the roof meets the grid. A modern photovoltaic surface produces direct current that an inverter resolves into the alternating current the rest of the building expects. From there, the question is local: how much storage, how much export, how much sharing with the neighbor whose array is shaded by an old fig tree. The answers used to live inside utility software. They are now drifting toward the building itself.

## Local First

A microgrid controller, sized for a city block, can balance generation and consumption without leaving the neighborhood. When the central grid is healthy, the block exports its surplus and earns credits. When the central grid is stressed, the block islands itself and keeps the lights on. The transition between these two modes is invisible to anyone reading on a couch.

## Materials and Lifespan

Crystalline silicon panels remain the workhorse, with thirty-year warranties and recyclable aluminum frames. Thin-film modules cover the curves and the awnings, accepting lower efficiency in exchange for shape. End-of-life processing is improving but still imperfect, and any honest plan accounts for the panel that will eventually need to be returned, sorted, and reborn as feedstock.

## What the Resident Sees

Almost nothing. A small dashboard, a quiet fan in the basement, a slightly cooler attic in August. The roof is doing its job. The rest of the city, slowly, is starting to do the same.
