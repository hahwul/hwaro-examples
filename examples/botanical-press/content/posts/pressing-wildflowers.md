Preserving wildflowers is a tradition as old as botany itself. By carefully drying and pressing specimens, we can capture the intricate details of a bloom long after its season has passed.

### Choosing Your Specimens

The best time to collect flowers for pressing is in the late morning, after the dew has evaporated but before the afternoon sun begins to wilt them. Look for specimens that are at their peak and naturally flat, such as:

*   **Pansies and Violas:** Their flat faces make them ideal candidates.
*   **Ferns:** The delicate structure of their fronds presses beautifully.
*   **Queen Anne's Lace:** Adds a lace-like texture to any collection.

### The Pressing Process

1.  **Prepare your press:** Use a dedicated wooden flower press or a heavy vintage book lined with acid-free parchment paper.
2.  **Arrange with care:** Place the flowers facedown on the paper, ensuring no petals are folded unless you desire that specific aesthetic.
3.  **Apply pressure:** Close the press or book and add weight.
4.  **Wait for the magic:** Depending on the thickness of the plant, it can take anywhere from two to four weeks for the specimen to be completely dry and preserved.

The result is a delicate, paper-thin piece of natural history, ready to be mounted in a journal or displayed in a frame.
