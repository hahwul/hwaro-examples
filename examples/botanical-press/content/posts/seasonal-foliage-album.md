A foliage album is a quiet record of place. Where a field journal asks for words, the album asks only for patience and a steady hand at the press.

### Choosing the Specimens

Walk the same path through each season. Pick leaves that lie flat on their own — a maple curling at the edges will fight the press for weeks, while a young birch surrenders in days. Take more than you think you need; a third of any harvest will brown unevenly and have to be set aside.

### The Slow Press

Most pressing failures come from impatience. Layer each leaf between unbleached newsprint, change the paper at day three, then again at day seven. Heavy books work, but a proper screw press gives a more even result and protects the venation on thin specimens.

### Arranging the Year

When the album is bound, resist the urge to sort by color. Order the leaves by the date you gathered them. The result is a kind of slow film — early willow giving way to oak and chestnut, then the spare line of late witch hazel — and the changes between pages do more to convey the season than any caption could.

A finished album is meant to be revisited. Keep it somewhere shaded, and turn the pages once a year, in the same week you began it.
