Most people picture the Bauhaus in primary-colored circles and squares — Josef Albers, Herbert Bayer, the geometric rigor that later defined "Bauhaus style" as shorthand for modernist restraint. The school's actual founding document looked nothing like that. Walter Gropius's 1919 manifesto shipped wrapped in a woodcut by Lyonel Feininger: a crystalline, spired cathedral, cut in the jagged expressionist style of the moment, with three stars rising above its towers.

<!-- more -->

Gropius's text called for the unification of art and craft under one roof — painters, sculptors, and architects "building the future" together, with architecture as the collective's culminating discipline. Feininger's cathedral was the literal illustration of that ambition: a gothic building assembled, guild-style, by many hands working one material. The manifesto's rhetoric was medievalist and quasi-religious, closer to the Arts and Crafts movement's romanticism than to the machine-age functionalism the school would become known for within a few years.

That early expressionist identity didn't survive contact with Weimar-era economics. By 1923, under pressure to prove the school could produce marketable design rather than romantic craft objects, the Bauhaus pivoted hard toward geometric abstraction and industrial production — the visual language most people now call "Bauhaus" was in large part a course correction away from Feininger's cathedral, not a continuation of it.

## In this episode

- What Gropius's 1919 manifesto actually called for, before "form follows function" became the school's shorthand
- Why Feininger's expressionist woodcut fit the founding rhetoric better than any grid would have
- How economic pressure in Weimar Germany pushed the school toward the geometric style it's remembered for
