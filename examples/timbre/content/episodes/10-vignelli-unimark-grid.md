By the mid-1960s the New York City subway had been accumulating signage for sixty years without ever deciding on a system. Hand-lettered tile mosaics sat next to enamel plaques next to whatever a station agent had improvised with a stencil kit. Unimark International won the 1966 commission to fix it, and the fix was not a new set of signs — it was a rulebook.

<!-- more -->

Massimo Vignelli and Bob Noorda's 1970 Graphics Standards Manual reduced the entire system to a closed set of decisions: one typeface (Standard Medium, called Akzidenz-Grotesk's American cousin), a fixed hierarchy of black-on-white directional signs and colored circles for lines, and precise rules for where a sign could and could not go relative to a rider's sightline entering a station. Nothing in the manual was stylistically daring. Its radicalism was procedural — a wayfinding system that a maintenance worker with no design training could apply consistently, station after station, for decades, and have it still cohere.

That consistency is the entire argument of Swiss-descended corporate identity work: that legibility is a discipline you enforce through constraint, not a look you apply once and hope survives contact with the next contractor. The manual outlived Unimark's contract, outlived several rounds of MTA leadership, and — in the signage still readable underground today — outlived most of the individual decisions Vignelli made along the way.

## In this episode

- Why Unimark treated the subway commission as a rulebook problem rather than a signage design problem
- How Standard Medium was chosen for legibility at a hurried glance rather than for its formal qualities
- What survived from the 1970 manual into the system riders still navigate today
