Grapus formed in Paris in 1970, three years after the student and worker strikes of May '68, and its founders — Pierre Bernard, Gérard Paris-Clavel, François Miehe — carried the politics of that year directly into how the studio operated. No individual signatures. No corporate clients. Work made collectively, credited collectively, for unions, municipal theatres, and the French Communist Party.

<!-- more -->

The posters themselves rejected the polish that most agency work of the period aspired to. Grapus printed by hand-cut silkscreen, let brushwork stay visibly brushwork, and borrowed the loose, almost childlike drawing style of protest graphics rather than the tight illustration of commercial studios. The roughness wasn't a limitation of their process — it was the point. A poster that looked too finished, in Grapus's reading, looked like it had already made its peace with the establishment it was supposed to be needling.

That refusal of commercial clients meant the collective survived on municipal and cultural commissions rather than corporate budgets, which kept the work politically legible but also kept it financially precarious. Grapus dissolved in 1990, twenty years after founding, but its combination of hand production and openly political content shaped a generation of French graphic designers who came up expecting posters to argue for something, not just to advertise it.

## In this episode

- Why Grapus refused individual credit on work made by a shared studio
- How silkscreen's hand-cut roughness became a deliberate political signal rather than a budget constraint
- What happened to the collective's members and influence after the 1990 dissolution
