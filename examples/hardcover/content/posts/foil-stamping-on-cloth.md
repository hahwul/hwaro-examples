Hot foil stamping transfers a thin layer of pigment or metallic film from a carrier onto the surface of a bound case under heat and pressure. The process is the standard method for titling cloth-covered hardcovers and remains in use for both trade and edition binding.

## The Die

Magnesium and brass dies are the two common options. Magnesium is inexpensive and acid-etched from artwork files, suitable for short runs. Brass is hand-engraved or CNC-milled and tolerates higher temperatures and longer runs without degradation. Die depth is typically 0.5 to 0.8 millimeters, sufficient to produce a clean impression in cloth without crushing the underlying boards.

## Foil Selection

Pigment foils are available in matte and gloss finishes across the standard ink colours. Metallic foils use a thin vapour-deposited aluminium layer with a coloured lacquer above. Gold and silver foils account for most trade work; specialty foils include holographic, pearlescent, and pigmented metallic blends. Foil is supplied on rolls and selected to match the cloth and the intended visual weight of the title.

## Impression Settings

Three variables govern the result: temperature, pressure, and dwell time. Cloth substrates generally require 110 to 130 degrees Celsius, with pressure adjusted to produce a visible debossment of approximately one third the die depth. Dwell time of half a second to one second allows the foil adhesive to soften and bond to the cloth fibres without scorching.

Test impressions on scrap cloth from the same lot are essential. Different cloth weaves accept foil differently, and a die that performs well on a smooth buckram may strip cleanly off a coarser linen at the same settings. Adjust pressure first, temperature second, and dwell only when the other two are correct.
