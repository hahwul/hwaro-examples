Case binding produces the durable, cloth-covered hardcover format used in trade publishing and library editions. The technique separates the construction of the text block from the construction of the case, then joins the two in a final operation called casing-in.

## Preparing the Text Block

Folded signatures are gathered in sequence and either sewn through the fold with linen thread or, for less demanding work, glued along the spine in a perfect-bound configuration. Sewing produces a stronger, more flexible result and allows the book to lie flat when opened. After sewing, the spine is rounded with a rounding hammer to produce the convex profile that distributes stress along the joint.

Endsheets are tipped to the first and last signatures with a narrow strip of paste. Headbands and a spine lining of mull or super reinforce the text block and provide an attachment surface for the case.

## Constructing the Case

The case consists of two boards and a spine inlay covered with bookcloth, leather, or paper. Boards are cut square with a board shear, leaving a uniform turn-in margin of fifteen to twenty millimeters on all sides. The bookcloth is glued out, the boards positioned with a precise spine gap that allows the joint to flex, and the turn-ins folded over the edges with attention to clean corner mitres.

## Casing-In

The completed text block is positioned inside the case with the spine inlay aligned to the spine of the text block. Adhesive is applied to the endsheets, the case is closed, and the assembly is pressed under weight while the paste cures. Pressing for several hours produces a tight joint and a flat, square book ready for trimming or stamping.
