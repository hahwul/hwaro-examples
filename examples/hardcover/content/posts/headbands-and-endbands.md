The small striped band visible at the head and tail of a hardcover spine is called a headband. Originally a structural element that anchored the text block to the cover, the headband persists in modern trade binding mostly as a decorative detail. Hand binders still sew them by traditional methods when the work warrants the additional time.

## Stuck-On Headbands

Industrial production uses a pre-woven cotton or rayon tape with a coloured bead along one edge. The tape is cut to spine width and adhered with PVA along the spine lining, with the bead exposed at the head and tail. The result is acceptable for most commercial editions and adds no meaningful structural value. It is a finish, not a fastening.

## Sewn Headbands

A sewn headband uses two or more colours of silk or linen thread wound around a core of vellum, leather, or rolled paper. The binder anchors the thread into the kettle stitch at the head of the text block, then weaves successive passes across the core, producing a striped or chevron pattern. Each pass tightens the headband and ties it into the spine at intervals through the signatures.

The technique requires the spine to be rounded and backed before the headband is begun, since the curvature determines the angle at which the thread passes the core. A well-sewn headband shares load with the joint and helps prevent the text block from sagging at the head when the book is shelved upright.

## Material Considerations

Silk produces the cleanest stripe and has been used since at least the medieval period in European binding. Linen is more durable but coarser. The core should be slightly proud of the spine edge to receive the thread without crushing under the cover turn-in.
