Every compost problem we have ever been asked about on this show turns out to be a ratio problem, and almost nobody is taught how to think about the ratio. So we are starting the season by doing the math properly, out loud, with a kitchen scale and a pile that has been sulking at 90°F for two weeks.

<!-- more -->

The target most extension offices give you is 30 parts carbon to 1 part nitrogen by weight — but weight is the hard part to eyeball, so we convert everything to volume, which is what you actually have a bucket for.

Rough carbon-to-nitrogen ratios by material, the ones we use every week:

- Dry fall leaves — about 60:1
- Shredded cardboard — 350:1 (use sparingly, or it slows everything down)
- Straw — 80:1
- Coffee grounds — 20:1
- Fresh grass clippings — 15:1
- Vegetable scraps — 25:1

The volume trick that gets you close to 30:1 without a scale: two buckets of dry brown material (leaves, straw, cardboard) to one bucket of wet green material (grass, kitchen scraps, coffee grounds). It is not exact, but it is close enough that your pile should climb past 130°F within four or five days if the moisture is also right — squeezed pile material should feel like a wrung-out sponge, not a puddle and not dust.

If your pile stalls under 100°F, the first thing to check is not the ratio — it is moisture and pile size. A pile under three feet cubed loses heat faster than it generates it, no matter how well-balanced the ratio is. We walk through a full troubleshooting order in the second half: size, then moisture, then ratio, then oxygen, in that sequence, because checking them out of order is how most people give up on composting entirely.
