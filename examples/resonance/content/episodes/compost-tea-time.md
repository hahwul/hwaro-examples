Jun Baeho spent a full season convinced compost tea was expensive superstition, until a side-by-side bed comparison talked him out of it. He joined us to walk through the brewing method that finally worked — and the one mistake that had made every earlier batch smell like a swamp.

<!-- more -->

The failure mode Jun kept hitting was anaerobic brewing: compost steeped in a bucket of water with no aeration goes oxygen-starved within about eight hours, and the microbial population that takes over from there is the kind that smells terrible and does little for a plant's roots. The fix is almost embarrassingly cheap — a small aquarium air pump and an airstone, running continuously for the full 24 to 36 hour brew, keeps dissolved oxygen high enough that the aerobic, beneficial microbes stay dominant the whole time.

His current method: one part finished compost to five parts dechlorinated water, a tablespoon of unsulfured molasses per gallon to feed the microbial bloom, brewed 24 hours with constant aeration, then strained through cheesecloth and used within four hours — the biology starts dying off fast once the pump comes out. Applied as a foliar spray, diluted again 1:1 with water, every ten days through the growing season.

Does it work? Jun is careful here: the research on compost tea's effect on yield is genuinely mixed, and he will not claim it outperforms a well-managed compost and mulch routine on its own. What convinced him was a single side-by-side row of tomatoes, one sprayed and one not, where the sprayed row showed visibly less early blight by midsummer. He calls it a cheap insurance policy rather than a miracle, which is about as strong an endorsement as this show tends to get.
