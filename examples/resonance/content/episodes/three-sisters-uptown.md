Odalys Reyes teaches the three sisters method to every new grower who joins her community plot, and she has the spacing measured down to the inch because a 4x4 bed does not forgive guesswork the way a full field does.

<!-- more -->

The method itself is old and well documented: corn goes in first, planted in a ring so the stalks can lean on each other in wind; pole beans go in two to three weeks later, once the corn is six inches tall, so the beans climb the stalks instead of smothering the seedlings; squash goes around the outside edge, where its broad leaves shade out weeds and keep the soil from baking.

Odalys plants corn on eight-inch centers in a ring of six to eight stalks — enough for mutual wind support without so much crowding that ears come in undersized. Beans go in at the base of each corn stalk, one or two seeds per stalk, timed to the corn's height rather than the calendar, since a cool spring can push everything back two weeks. Squash gets planted last, one or two vines per bed, trained outward rather than up.

Where it breaks down on a rooftop, by her account, is wind: corn on the eighth floor gets pushed around in ways it never would at ground level, so she adds a loose stake ring around the whole planting for the first six weeks, removed once the root systems have knit together. She also plants her squash a full two weeks later than the ground-level version of this bed would call for, because rooftop soil temperature swings are sharper and an early squash seedling on a rooftop is more likely to stall than thrive.
