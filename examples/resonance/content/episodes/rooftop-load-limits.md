Feride Aksoy has turned down more rooftop farm proposals than she has approved, and she joined us to explain exactly why — with the numbers that make a roof say yes or no, before a single bag of growing medium goes up the freight elevator.

<!-- more -->

The number everyone skips is the live load allowance stamped somewhere in the building's original structural drawings, usually 20 to 30 pounds per square foot for a roof built before 1980, and often much higher for anything reinforced or purpose-built since. That number has to cover snow, mechanical equipment, foot traffic, and growing medium combined — not growing medium alone.

Saturated growing medium is the part that surprises people. A lightweight rooftop soil blend might weigh 40 pounds per cubic foot dry, but fully saturated after a storm it can run 55 to 65 pounds per cubic foot. A four-inch bed of that, saturated, before anything is planted, comes out to roughly 18 to 22 pounds per square foot — which can eat most of an older roof's entire allowance before a single tomato goes in the ground.

Feride's rule for a first pass: get the original structural drawings before you get excited about a rooftop farm, not after. The project she describes in detail — a 4,000-square-foot rooftop farm above a former print shop — got redesigned twice: once when the original beds were found to exceed the roof's rating by 30 percent, and once more when the redesign's drainage layer turned out to pool water in exactly the spot the beam below it was weakest. It opened eighteen months late and is now, by her account, over-engineered in the best possible way.
