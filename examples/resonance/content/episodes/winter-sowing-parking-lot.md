Retta Solheim runs a winter sowing operation out of an unused corner of a strip-mall parking lot, using nothing but recycled milk jugs, a stretch of chain-link fence, and a method that sounds too simple to work until you have seen a jug full of kale seedlings survive a hard freeze.

<!-- more -->

The technique: cut a milk jug most of the way around at the midpoint, leaving a hinge, poke drainage holes in the bottom, fill with four inches of moistened seed-starting mix, sow seeds, tape the jug shut, and leave the cap off for ventilation. Set outside, in full exposure to snow and freezing temperatures, from mid-January through late February depending on the seed.

It works because the jug behaves like a miniature, self-regulating greenhouse: sunlight warms the inside during the day even when the air outside is well below freezing, while the open cap vents excess heat and prevents the seedlings from cooking on an unexpectedly sunny afternoon. Seeds that need cold stratification — many native perennials, poppies, and some brassicas — actually germinate better for the freeze-thaw cycles than they would indoors.

Retta's group runs about sixty jugs a season along the parking lot's fence line, mostly kale, spinach, and a rotating batch of native flowers for the pollinator strip. Her hard-won rule: label everything twice, because a marker left in direct sun for six weeks will fade past reading by March, and a season's worth of unlabeled kale-versus-mystery-brassica jugs is a lesson she has only needed to learn once. By the time outdoor sowing would normally begin, her seedlings already have a six-week head start and root systems strong enough to transplant directly into the beds.
