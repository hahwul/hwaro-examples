In the classical view of the cosmos, a vacuum is defined as absolute emptiness, a state of perfect stillness where all energy has been evacuated. However, quantum mechanics introduces a more complex reality: the zero-point energy.
<!-- more -->
According to the uncertainty principle, a physical system can never possess exactly zero energy, as this would require simultaneous certainty of both position and momentum. Instead, even at absolute zero temperature, quantum fields undergo continuous, minute fluctuations.

These fluctuations mean that what we perceive as a void is actually a quiet sea of virtual particles, briefly flashing into existence and disappearing. The vacuum is not a dead space, but a state of minimal excitation, a background noise that underpins the physical universe. In this view, absolute nothingness is impossible; the void is always vibrating. To study the zero-point state is to recognize that the absolute silence we seek is always relative. The lowest possible state of a quantum harmonic oscillator is not zero, but rather:

```text
E_0 = (1 / 2) * h * nu
```

This equation represents the fundamental limit of stillness in our physical reality.
