In thermodynamics, absolute zero is defined as the lower limit of the thermodynamic temperature scale, a theoretical state corresponding to zero Kelvin. At this point, the fundamental motion of all particles would cease.
<!-- more -->
Yet, according to the laws of physics, absolute zero is an asymptotic limit, a state that can be closely approached but never fully reached.

As a system is cooled toward zero Kelvin, its entropy approaches a constant value. The third law of thermodynamics states that it is impossible for any process, no matter how idealized, to reduce the temperature of a closed system to absolute zero in a finite number of steps. In the laboratory, physicists have cooled atoms to within billionths of a degree of this limit, revealing exotic states of matter such as Bose-Einstein condensates, where individual atoms lose their separate identities and behave as a single macro-quantum wave. Absolute zero remains a silent horizon, a boundary of perfect stillness that we can witness only through its perpetual approximation.
