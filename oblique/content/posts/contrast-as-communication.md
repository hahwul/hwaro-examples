Contrast is the engine of visual communication. Without it, everything is the same. And when everything is the same, nothing is important.

## The forms of contrast

Visual contrast takes many forms, and each communicates differently:

- **Size contrast** establishes hierarchy. The biggest thing is the most important thing.
- **Weight contrast** creates emphasis. Bold text within regular text acts as a signal flare.
- **Color contrast** directs attention. A single red element on a monochrome page is impossible to ignore.
- **Spatial contrast** creates rhythm. Dense text next to open space generates visual breathing.
- **Typographic contrast** separates function. Serif against sans-serif says "these are different kinds of content."

## The minimum effective dose

The temptation is to use all forms of contrast simultaneously. Bigger, bolder, more colorful, more spaced. This is the visual equivalent of shouting every word.

Instead, use one form of contrast at a time. Make a heading bigger *or* bolder, not both. Use color *or* weight for emphasis, not both.

Each additional layer of contrast reduces the impact of all the others.

## Dark on light, light on dark

The most fundamental contrast is tonal. Dark text on a light background for sustained reading. Light text on a dark background for impact.

This site uses both. The body is dark-on-light for readability. The hero and footer are light-on-dark for visual weight. The transition between them, marked by an angled divider, creates the primary rhythm of each page.

## Accents and restraint

A single accent color, used sparingly, does more than a full palette used liberally. This site uses one red: `#c0392b`. It appears in links, in the accent bar above section headings, and in the hover state of navigation.

That is three uses. Not thirty.

---

Make fewer things different, and each difference means more.
