Every strong composition has an invisible diagonal running through it. This line of force, rarely drawn but always felt, is what pulls a viewer through the frame.

## Breaking the horizontal

Horizontal layouts feel stable. Vertical layouts feel tall. But diagonals feel *alive*. They introduce tension, movement, and an implicit narrative direction.

> The oblique line is the line of movement, of life, of drama. The vertical and horizontal are the lines of rest.

Consider the difference between a grid of evenly-spaced elements and one where a single column is tilted three degrees. The tilt is barely perceptible on its own, yet the entire composition shifts. The eye has somewhere to go.

## Practical applications

In interface design, the diagonal principle manifests in several ways:

- **Skewed section dividers** break the monotony of stacked content blocks
- **Angled typography** creates hierarchy without relying on size alone
- **Asymmetric layouts** force the viewer to actively engage rather than passively scan

The key is restraint. A single well-placed angle does more than a page full of rotations. The diagonal should guide, not disorient.

## The three-degree rule

Keep your angles subtle. Between two and four degrees is the sweet spot: enough to feel intentional, not enough to feel chaotic.

```css
.section-divider {
  transform: skewY(-3deg);
}
```

This small transform changes everything. The content above flows into the content below. The page becomes a continuous surface rather than a stack of disconnected boxes.
