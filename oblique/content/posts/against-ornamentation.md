Strip a page down to its essentials. What remains? Text, structure, and the space between them.

## The weight of decoration

Every visual element on a page carries cognitive weight. A border, a shadow, a background pattern: each demands a fraction of the reader's attention. These fractions add up.

The question is never "does this look good?" but "does this help the reader understand what they came here for?"

Most of the time, the answer is no.

## What ornamentation hides

Decoration often masks structural weakness. A page that needs heavy styling to feel complete is a page with unclear hierarchy. Fix the hierarchy and the need for decoration disappears.

Consider these pairs:

- **Drop shadow** vs. **whitespace** for depth
- **Border radius** vs. **alignment** for grouping
- **Color coding** vs. **position** for categorization

The second option in each pair requires more thought and less CSS. That tradeoff is almost always worth making.

## The discipline of removal

Start with everything. Then remove elements one at a time. After each removal, ask: did anything break? If the answer is no, the element was not needed.

This is harder than it sounds. We are drawn to addition. Removing feels like losing. But what you are actually losing is noise.

---

The best designs are not the ones where nothing can be added, but the ones where nothing can be taken away.
