## The computus

<p class="lede">The calculation of the date of Easter -- the computus -- was one of the most important intellectual tasks of the medieval Church. Easter falls on the first Sunday after the first full moon following the vernal equinox, a rule that requires the intersection of the solar calendar (for the equinox), the lunar calendar (for the full moon), and the seven-day week (for Sunday). The computus is a complex calculation, and the volvelle was one of the principal tools for performing it.</p>

The calendar volvelle typically carries three or four rings of data: the days of the year on the outermost ring, the golden numbers (which track the nineteen-year Metonic cycle of the moon) on the next ring, the dominical letters (which determine the day of the week for any date) on the next, and the liturgical seasons or feast days on the innermost ring. By aligning the current year's golden number and dominical letter, the reader could determine the date of Easter and the dates of all the movable feasts that depend on it.

<div class="ring-panel">
<div class="ring-label">The Golden Number</div>
<p>The golden number is a number from 1 to 19 that indicates the position of a given year within the Metonic cycle -- the 19-year period after which the phases of the moon repeat on approximately the same calendar dates. To find the golden number, divide the year by 19 and add 1 to the remainder. The golden number for the year 2025 is 12.</p>

## The dominical letter

The dominical letter is a letter from A to G that identifies which day of the week falls on January 1 in a given year. Since the calendar has 365 days (52 weeks plus one day), the dominical letter shifts backward by one position each ordinary year and by two positions each leap year. A table of dominical letters, combined with the golden number, provides the data needed to compute Easter for any year.

The volvelle mechanizes this table. Instead of looking up values in a grid, the reader rotates the inner disc until the golden number aligns with the dominical letter, and the date of Easter appears in a window or at the pointer. The calculation that would require several steps of arithmetic is reduced to a single rotation.

## Perpetual calendars

Some calendar volvelles are designed as perpetual calendars -- instruments that can determine the day of the week for any date in any year. These typically use four concentric rings: centuries on the outer ring, years within the century on the next, months on the next, and days on the innermost ring. By aligning the four rings to the target date, the reader finds the day of the week indicated by the pointer.

The perpetual calendar volvelle is one of the most elegant applications of the rotating disc format. It compresses a lookup table of thousands of entries into a compact instrument that fits on a single manuscript page, and it can be operated by anyone who can read the labels and turn the disc.
