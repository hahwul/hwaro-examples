## Medicine and the stars

<p class="lede">In medieval medicine, the health of the body was understood to be governed by the four humors -- blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile -- whose balance was influenced by the positions of the sun, moon, and planets. Medical treatment, particularly bloodletting and the administration of purgatives, was timed according to astronomical considerations. The medical volvelle provided the physician with the means to determine the optimal time for treatment based on the patient's condition, the season, and the celestial configuration.</p>

The zodiac man -- a figure showing the human body with each region governed by a sign of the zodiac -- is a familiar image in medieval medical manuscripts. Aries governs the head, Taurus the neck, Gemini the arms, and so on down to Pisces governing the feet. A volvelle combined with the zodiac man allowed the physician to determine which sign of the zodiac was currently ascendant and therefore which part of the body should not be bled (since cutting into a region governed by the ascending sign was considered dangerous).

## The bloodletting volvelle

The bloodletting volvelle typically carries the zodiac signs on the outer ring, the months on the middle ring, and indicators for favorable and unfavorable days on the inner ring. By aligning the current month with the zodiac position, the physician could read from the inner ring whether the day was propitious for bloodletting and which veins should be opened. The system was complex and its theoretical basis -- the influence of planetary positions on bodily humors -- is no longer accepted, but the volvelle itself is a remarkable information design achievement.

## Uroscopy wheels

Another medical application of the volvelle was uroscopy -- diagnosis by the examination of urine. The uroscopy volvelle displayed a spectrum of urine colors on one ring and their diagnostic significance on adjacent rings. By matching the observed color of the patient's urine to the appropriate segment of the color ring, the physician could read the corresponding diagnosis and treatment recommendations from the adjacent rings.

These instruments remind us that the volvelle was not merely a toy or a curiosity. It was a working tool, used by educated practitioners to systematize and apply the knowledge of their discipline. That the knowledge it encoded was, by modern standards, largely wrong does not diminish the sophistication of the instrument itself.
