## From manuscript to print

<p class="lede">The invention of printing in the fifteenth century transformed the volvelle from a unique manuscript artifact into a reproducible instrument. Printed volvelles appeared in astronomical treatises, navigational manuals, cipher books, and educational texts throughout the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. The printer produced the disc components as separate pieces, and the buyer or binder assembled them -- cutting out the discs, piercing the center, and attaching the pivot. Some printed books included detailed instructions for assembly; others assumed the reader's familiarity with the format.</p>

Peter Apian's Astronomicum Caesareum (1540) contains some of the most elaborate printed volvelles ever produced. The book includes multiple layered volvelles with up to six rotating discs each, printed in multiple colors and assembled by hand. Each instrument is a functional astronomical calculator, capable of determining planetary positions, eclipse dates, and other astronomical data. The Astronomicum Caesareum is both a scientific treatise and a masterwork of the printing art.

## The cipher wheel

One of the most enduring applications of the volvelle format is the cipher wheel -- a rotating disc used for encrypting and decrypting messages. The cipher wheel uses two concentric alphabets, one fixed and one rotating. By setting the rotating alphabet to a specified offset, the user can encrypt a message by substituting each letter of the plaintext with the corresponding letter on the rotating ring. The recipient, knowing the offset, reverses the process to decrypt.

Leon Battista Alberti described a cipher disc in his De Cifris (1467), making it one of the earliest applications of the volvelle outside astronomy and calendar computation. Cipher discs remained in military and diplomatic use through the twentieth century, and the basic principle -- concentric rings of substitution values aligned by rotation -- is directly descended from the medieval volvelle.

## The modern volvelle

The volvelle format persisted long after its medieval origins. Twentieth-century examples include the E6B flight computer used by pilots for air navigation calculations, the circular slide rule, and the date wheels distributed by pharmaceutical companies to calculate dosing schedules. These modern volvelles share the essential characteristics of their medieval ancestors: concentric rings of data, a central pivot, and a rotational interface that aligns related values.

In the digital age, the volvelle's function has been absorbed by software -- by calculators, spreadsheets, and apps. But the volvelle's design principle -- the presentation of related data in concentric rings that can be aligned by rotation -- remains influential in information design. Circular charts, radial menus, and rotating interface elements echo the volvelle's spatial logic, organizing information along the arc of a circle rather than the grid of a table.

The volvelle reminds us that information is not naturally flat. It has structure, dimensionality, and relationship. The medieval manuscript makers understood this, and they built instruments that made these relationships visible and manipulable. The rotating wheel is among the oldest and most elegant of their solutions.
