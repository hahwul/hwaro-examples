## Construction

<p class="lede">The volvelle is built from simple materials -- paper or parchment, a pin or thread, ink and compass -- but its construction requires precision. The discs must be perfectly circular and precisely centered. The data rings must be spaced and lettered with care. The pivot must allow free rotation without looseness. A poorly made volvelle gives wrong answers; a well-made one can serve for centuries.</p>

## The discs

Each disc is cut from parchment or heavy paper using a compass and knife. The outer disc, which is glued or sewn to the manuscript page, carries the fixed data -- typically the outermost ring of information, such as month names, zodiac signs, or degree markings. The inner disc (or discs -- some volvelles have three or four rotating layers) carries the movable data, and is attached to the page only at the center point.

The data on each ring must be positioned with care. The divisions must be equal, the lettering must be legible, and the relationship between the data on adjacent rings must be correct when the discs are aligned. A volvelle with twelve zodiac divisions on one ring and thirteen month divisions on the adjacent ring would produce nonsensical results at certain alignments.

## The pivot

The central pivot is the mechanical heart of the volvelle. In manuscript volvelles, the pivot is typically a thread passed through all the layers and knotted or waxed on the reverse of the page. In printed volvelles of the sixteenth century and later, a brass rivet or eyelet sometimes replaced the thread. The pivot must hold the discs firmly enough to maintain alignment but loosely enough to allow smooth rotation -- a balance that the manuscript maker achieved through experience.

Letter the data rings before attaching the discs to the page. Test the rotation before fixing the pivot permanently.</p>

## Windows and pointers

Some volvelles include windows -- small openings cut in the upper disc through which data on the lower disc is visible. As the upper disc rotates, different data appears in the window, creating a dynamic display. Other volvelles use a pointer -- a narrow arm attached to the pivot that extends beyond the outermost ring, indicating the aligned position on the fixed scale.

The choice between windows and pointers depends on the type of calculation. A window is suited to showing a single datum aligned with a position on the outer ring. A pointer is suited to indicating a position on a continuous scale. Some complex volvelles use both, combining windows in the inner discs with a pointer extending from the center.
