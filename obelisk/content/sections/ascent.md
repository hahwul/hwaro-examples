# The Ascent

With the foundation established, the monument begins its climb. Each course of stone is placed with reference to the one below and the one yet to come. The builders work upward, and the world below grows smaller.

## The Logistics of Elevation

Moving multi-ton blocks of stone to increasing heights is one of the great engineering challenges of the ancient world. The methods remain debated, but the results are not: obelisks weighing hundreds of tons were quarried, transported, and erected with precision that modern equipment struggles to match.

The key insight was incremental progress. No single day saw dramatic change. But each day, the monument grew by one course. Over months and years, those courses accumulated into something that would outlast the builders by millennia.

## Course by Course

The metaphor extends to any ambitious undertaking:

- **First ten courses** -- The work feels slow. The monument barely rises above the surrounding walls. Doubt is natural here.
- **Middle courses** -- Momentum builds. The structure begins to assert itself against the skyline. The pattern of work becomes familiar.
- **Upper courses** -- The air thins. Each stone requires more effort to place. But the view expands with every meter gained.

## Material Selection

Not all stone is equal to the task. The builders selected granite for obelisks -- hard enough to take a polish, dense enough to resist erosion, and crystalline in structure. The stone chosen for the ascent must be worthy of the destination.

## The Narrowing

An obelisk tapers as it rises. The base is wide, the summit narrow. This is not merely aesthetic. It distributes weight efficiently and creates the visual impression of infinite height. Each successive course is slightly smaller than the one below, focusing the eye upward.
