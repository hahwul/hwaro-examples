Lava flows exhibit distinct morphological patterns determined by their composition, temperature, effusion rate, and the terrain over which they travel. Understanding these patterns is essential for predicting volcanic hazards and interpreting the geological record.

## Primary Flow Types

### Pahoehoe

Pahoehoe flows are characterized by smooth, undulating, or ropy surfaces. They form from low-viscosity basaltic lava at temperatures typically exceeding 1100 degrees Celsius. The smooth surface develops as the fluid lava beneath a thin, flexible crust continues to move, folding the skin into rope-like textures.

Pahoehoe flows advance by budding -- new lobes break through the cooled crust at the flow front and spread laterally before solidifying. This produces a complex, overlapping structure of individual flow units called **toes**.

### Aa

Aa flows have rough, jagged, clinkery surfaces composed of broken lava fragments called **clinker**. They typically form from the same basaltic compositions as pahoehoe but at lower temperatures or higher effusion rates. A single eruption can produce both types, with pahoehoe transitioning to aa as the lava cools and degasses.

The transition from pahoehoe to aa is irreversible under normal conditions and is governed primarily by viscosity and strain rate.

### Block Lava

Block lava flows are associated with more silicic compositions -- andesite, dacite, and rhyolite. Their surfaces consist of large, angular blocks with relatively smooth faces, in contrast to the irregular clinker of aa flows. These are the flows most likely to produce obsidian at their margins.

## Flow Dynamics

The internal structure of a lava flow typically consists of three zones:

1. **Upper crust** -- Cooled, brittle surface layer
2. **Core** -- Fluid, actively flowing interior
3. **Basal zone** -- Lower boundary showing shear effects from ground contact

```
Flow velocity estimation (Jeffreys equation):
  v = (rho * g * sin(theta) * h^2) / (3 * eta)

  where:
    rho   = density (kg/m3)
    g     = gravitational acceleration (m/s2)
    theta = slope angle
    h     = flow thickness (m)
    eta   = dynamic viscosity (Pa*s)
```

## Glass Formation in Flows

Volcanic glass forms preferentially at the margins of flows where cooling is most rapid. The outer surfaces of lava flows cool quickly through radiation and conduction to the atmosphere, while the interiors cool much more slowly and tend to crystallize.

In silicic flows, this rapid marginal cooling can produce thick obsidian carapaces overlying a crystalline or partially crystalline interior. The Big Obsidian Flow at Newberry Volcano in Oregon is an excellent example, with glassy margins surrounding a microcrystalline rhyolite core.
