Obsidian is an igneous rock that forms as an extrusive volcanic glass. It is produced when felsic lava extruded from a volcano cools rapidly with minimal crystal growth.

## Formation Process

The key factor in obsidian formation is the rate of cooling. When silica-rich magma reaches the surface and loses heat quickly, the atoms within the melt do not have sufficient time to arrange themselves into a crystalline lattice. The result is an amorphous solid -- glass rather than crystal.

The silica content of the parent magma is critical. Obsidian typically forms from lava with 65-80% SiO2 by weight. This high silica content increases the viscosity of the melt, which further inhibits crystal nucleation and growth.

## Varieties

Different trace elements and formation conditions produce distinct varieties:

- **Black obsidian** -- The most common form, colored by magnetite nanoparticles
- **Mahogany obsidian** -- Reddish-brown banding from iron oxide inclusions
- **Snowflake obsidian** -- Contains cristobalite inclusions that form radial clusters
- **Rainbow obsidian** -- Displays iridescence from aligned magnetite nanocrystals
- **Fire obsidian** -- Thin-film interference from layered magnetite produces spectral colors

## Geological Context

Obsidian is most commonly found at the margins of rhyolitic lava flows, known as obsidian flows. Major deposits exist in regions of recent volcanic activity including the western Americas, Japan, Iceland, Italy, and parts of East Africa.

The glass is metastable and will slowly crystallize over geological time. As a result, obsidian older than a few million years is rare, and no specimens from before the Cretaceous period are known to exist.
