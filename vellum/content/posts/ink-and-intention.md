Black ink seems like the simplest thing in the world. But there is no single black. There is the warm, velvety black of carbon ink. The blue-black of iron gall, which darkens over hours as it oxidizes on the page. The flat, matte black of India ink. The glossy, saturated black of modern offset inks.

## Carbon and Soot

The oldest inks are made from soot. Lampblack -- the carbon deposited by burning oil -- is mixed with a binding agent and water to create an ink that is stable, permanent, and deeply black. Chinese and Japanese calligraphers have used carbon-based inks for millennia, grinding solid ink sticks on stone with water to produce a liquid of precise consistency.

Carbon ink does not fade. Manuscripts written with it two thousand years ago remain legible. The pigment particles sit on the surface of the paper rather than penetrating it, which gives carbon ink its characteristic density and surface texture.

## Iron Gall

European scribes preferred iron gall ink, made from oak galls, iron sulfate, and gum arabic. When first applied, it appears pale gray. Over minutes and hours, it darkens to a rich purple-black as the iron compounds react with the air. It is an ink that reveals itself slowly, rewarding patience.

The drawback of iron gall ink is its acidity. Over centuries, it can eat through paper, leaving lace-like holes where the densest strokes once lay. Many medieval manuscripts bear these scars -- the ink has consumed the very surface it was meant to preserve.

## Modern Formulations

Today's inks are engineered for consistency and performance. Fountain pen inks must flow smoothly through narrow channels without clogging. Printing inks must transfer cleanly from plate to blanket to paper at high speed. Screen printing inks must pass through mesh without bleeding.

Yet even with all this engineering, the fundamental question remains the same one that faced the first scribes: what mark do you want to leave, and how long do you want it to last?
