The medina of Marrakech is organized around a labyrinth of covered souks that radiate outward from the great square of Jemaa el-Fnaa. Each souk is traditionally dedicated to a particular trade: the souk of the dyers, the souk of the leather workers, the souk of the coppersmiths, the souk of the spice merchants. This organization by craft dates to the medieval period, when guilds controlled production and trade within the city walls, and it persists today even as the goods on offer have diversified to meet the interests of a global clientele.

Walking through the souks is an experience that engages every sense. The air carries the scent of cedarwood shavings from a carpenter's workshop, overlaid with cumin and saffron from the spice stalls a few steps away. The sound environment is equally layered: the rhythmic tapping of metalworkers hammering trays into shape, the calls of vendors, the murmur of negotiation conducted in Arabic, French, and Berber. Light enters through gaps in the reed and timber roofing, casting patterns on the packed-earth floor that shift throughout the day as the sun moves overhead.

The economic structure of the souks is built on personal relationships. A merchant's reputation is his most valuable asset, accumulated over years or even generations of fair dealing. Regular customers are greeted by name, offered tea, and given prices that reflect loyalty. Bargaining is not merely a commercial transaction but a social ritual, an exchange of respect and wit that establishes the terms of an ongoing relationship. The tourist who pays the first price offered has not been cheated, exactly, but has missed the point of the encounter entirely.

Beyond the commercial souks, the Jemaa el-Fnaa itself transforms as the day progresses. In the morning, it is a quiet open space with a few juice sellers and snake charmers. By afternoon, storytellers and musicians have claimed their customary spots. At dusk, dozens of food stalls assemble with remarkable speed, their operators carrying tables, benches, gas burners, and provisions on handcarts, creating an open-air restaurant that seats thousands and disappears again before dawn. This daily cycle of assembly and dispersal is itself a kind of commerce, one that trades not in goods but in shared experience and communal memory.
