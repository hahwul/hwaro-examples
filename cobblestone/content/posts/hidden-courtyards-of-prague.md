Prague is a city that reveals itself in layers. The casual visitor sees the astronomical clock, the Charles Bridge, the castle on the hill. But the most intimate spaces of the Czech capital are hidden behind unassuming wooden doors along narrow streets in Mala Strana and Stare Mesto. Step through one of these entrances and you find yourself in a courtyard that has remained essentially unchanged for three or four hundred years, surrounded by Renaissance arcades and quiet stone fountains.

These courtyards served as the social hearts of their respective buildings. Merchants conducted business here, children played beneath drying laundry strung between iron balconies, and neighbors gathered on summer evenings to share news from the wider city. Many of the courtyards are connected by passageways called pasaze, forming a hidden pedestrian network that allowed residents to move across entire city blocks without ever stepping onto a public street. During periods of political repression, these passages became routes of quiet resistance and clandestine meeting places.

The architecture of these spaces is remarkably varied. Some courtyards display the sgraffito decoration typical of Bohemian Renaissance style, with geometric patterns scratched into layers of colored plaster. Others are simpler, their beauty residing in the proportions of the arched colonnades and the texture of centuries-old stone. In the Ungelt courtyard near Tyn Church, you can still trace the outlines of the medieval customs house where foreign merchants once stored their goods and paid duties to the crown.

Today, a handful of these courtyards have been opened to the public as cafes or galleries, but many remain private residential spaces. The best way to discover them is simply to try every door that looks old enough to have a story. More often than not, it will be unlocked, and the courtyard beyond will be yours alone for a few quiet minutes, a private audience with the hidden memory of the city.
