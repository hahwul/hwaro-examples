The Stari Most, or Old Bridge, of Mostar was completed in 1566 by the Ottoman architect Mimar Hayruddin, a student of the great Sinan. For over four centuries it arched twenty-four meters above the emerald waters of the Neretva River, connecting the two halves of a city that had grown up on opposite banks. The bridge was built from local tenelija stone, a pale limestone that absorbs light and seems to glow in the late afternoon sun. Its single arch was, at the time of construction, the widest stone arch in the world, and the engineering required to span such a distance without intermediate supports was considered so audacious that Hayruddin, according to legend, prepared his own funeral before the scaffolding was removed.

The bridge was more than a crossing point. It was the civic symbol of Mostar, the image on postcards and stamps, the gathering place where young men performed the traditional diving competition, leaping from the parapet into the river far below as a test of courage. The neighborhoods on either side of the bridge, with their Ottoman-era stone houses, bazaars, and mosques, formed a single cultural ecosystem linked by the daily foot traffic across the arch. The bridge made the city legible: you understood Mostar by understanding the relationship between its two banks.

On November 9, 1993, during the Bosnian War, Croatian forces destroyed the Stari Most with concentrated tank fire. The collapse of the bridge into the river was filmed and broadcast worldwide, becoming one of the defining images of the conflict and a symbol of cultural destruction during wartime. The loss was felt not only in Mostar but across the region, as communities recognized that the bridge represented something larger than any single city's heritage.

Reconstruction began in 1999 under the supervision of UNESCO and a team of international engineers and historians. Using stones recovered from the riverbed and newly quarried tenelija cut to match the originals, the builders followed Hayruddin's methods as closely as modern knowledge allowed. The reconstructed bridge was opened in July 2004. It is not the same bridge, and the city it serves is not the same city, but the act of rebuilding was itself a statement: that connection is worth the effort of restoration, and that a bridge, even a rebuilt one, can still carry the weight of meaning across a divide.
