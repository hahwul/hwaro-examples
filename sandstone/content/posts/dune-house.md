## Concept

Dune House began with a question: what happens when a building accepts sand as a collaborator rather than an adversary? The site --- a windswept parcel on the Atlantic coast south of Essaouira --- receives persistent northeast trade winds that reshape the surrounding dunes month by month.

Rather than resist this movement, the architects designed a building whose form channels sand over and around it. The roofline is a continuous slope that matches the angle of repose of the local sand, allowing wind-blown grains to accumulate on the leeward side and drain away on the windward face.

## Form

The house reads as a series of parallel concrete walls oriented perpendicular to the prevailing wind. These walls, cast in board-formed concrete with deep grain impressions, extend beyond the building envelope to act as wind baffles. Sand collects in the gaps between them, forming natural berms that insulate the interior and soften the building's profile against the dune landscape.

In plan, the house is a simple bar divided into three zones: living spaces at the center, bedrooms to the south, and a sheltered courtyard to the north. The courtyard is the heart of the plan --- a walled outdoor room protected from wind on all sides, open to the sky.

## Materials

The concrete mix incorporates crushed local sandstone as aggregate, giving the walls a warm ochre tone that harmonizes with the surrounding sand. Board-forming was done with rough-sawn cedar planks, their grain pattern transferred permanently to the concrete surface.

Interior walls are rammed earth, using a blend of coastal sand and clay from a quarry twenty kilometers inland. These walls are left exposed, their layered texture providing visual warmth and acoustic absorption.

Fixtures --- door handles, towel rails, light switch plates --- are all fabricated from solid brass, chosen for its resistance to the salt-laden coastal air. Over time, these elements will develop a green patina that the architects consider part of the design.

Cedar appears throughout: window shutters, ceiling lining in the bedrooms, and a long dining table built into the courtyard wall. The wood is left untreated, silvering naturally under ultraviolet exposure.

## Living With Sand

Sand enters the courtyard. This is expected. A shallow channel along the courtyard's perimeter collects wind-deposited sand and directs it to a collection pit, from which it is periodically removed by hand. The architects describe this maintenance ritual as intentional --- a daily engagement with the landscape that prevents the building from feeling hermetically sealed.

The main living space features a floor of polished concrete set flush with the courtyard threshold. When the sliding cedar screens are fully open, the boundary between interior and courtyard dissolves. The floor's surface has been finished with a fine aggregate that catches afternoon light, producing a warm glow that fills the room without direct sun exposure.
