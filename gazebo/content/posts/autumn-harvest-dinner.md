When the leaves begin to turn and the air carries the first hint of wood smoke, we gather under the gazebo for the harvest dinner. This year, a long farmhouse table ran the full length of the structure, draped in rust-coloured linen and set with stoneware plates, brass candlesticks, and garlands of dried hops and bittersweet vine. Pumpkins and gourds of every shape lined the steps, and hurricane lanterns hung from the rafters overhead.

The meal was built around the garden's final offerings of the season. A roasted beetroot and walnut salad opened the evening, followed by a slow-braised lamb shoulder with rosemary from the border beds, roasted root vegetables glazed with honey from our own hives, and a crusty sourdough loaf baked that morning. Cider pressed from the orchard apples filled every glass, its tartness a perfect match for the rich, earthy flavours on the table.

Dessert was a warm apple and blackberry crumble served with thick cream, eaten as the temperature dropped and wool blankets were passed around the table. The conversation turned, as it always does at harvest time, to the year behind and the quiet season ahead. The candles burned low, the stars appeared above the bare branches, and we lingered until the last ember in the fire pit faded to ash.
