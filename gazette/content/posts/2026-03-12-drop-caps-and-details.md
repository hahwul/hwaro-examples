Open any illuminated manuscript from the medieval period and your eye will be drawn to the same element: a single, oversized letter at the beginning of the text, richly decorated, often gilded, commanding attention before a single word is read. This is the drop cap — one of the oldest and most enduring conventions in the history of written language.

The drop cap survived the transition from manuscript to movable type, from broadsheet to tabloid, from print to web. Its longevity is not mere tradition. The drop cap serves a genuine function: it signals the beginning of a new section, creates a visual anchor, and adds a touch of ceremony to the act of reading.

## The Psychology of the First Letter

Cognitive research suggests that readers process the beginning of a text differently from the middle. The first few words carry disproportionate weight in shaping expectations and engagement. A drop cap leverages this by making the opening visually distinctive — it tells the reader, "Something begins here. Pay attention."

In newspaper design, drop caps were used selectively, typically reserved for the lead story or the opening of a feature article. This selectivity was itself a signal of importance: not every story merited the honor of a decorated initial.

## Small Caps, Rules, and Ornaments

The drop cap is just one of many typographic details that collectively define the character of a publication. Small capitals — used for bylines, datelines, and section headers — convey a sense of formality and tradition. Horizontal rules separate stories without the heaviness of full borders. And ornaments — those small decorative elements that mark the end of an article — provide a graceful conclusion.

These details may seem minor in isolation, but together they create a coherent visual language. A reader who encounters small caps in a byline, a drop cap at the opening, and an ornamental rule at the close understands instinctively that they are reading something crafted with care.

## Bringing Detail to the Web

CSS has made many of these typographic niceties available to web designers. The `::first-letter` pseudo-element enables drop caps. `font-variant: small-caps` provides small capitals. Borders and pseudo-elements can simulate the rules and ornaments of print. The tools are there; the challenge is knowing when and how to use them with restraint.
