Wild edible plants grow in parks, along sidewalks, and in vacant lots. Urban foraging reconnects us with our food sources and reduces reliance on industrial agriculture, even if only in small ways.

## Before You Start

Safety is paramount. Follow these rules without exception:

- **Never eat anything you cannot identify with absolute certainty.** When in doubt, leave it.
- **Avoid areas sprayed with pesticides or herbicides.** Roadsides and manicured lawns are often treated.
- **Check local regulations.** Some parks prohibit foraging; others permit it.
- **Take only what you need.** Leave enough for wildlife and regeneration.

## Common Urban Edibles

### Dandelion

Every part is edible. Young leaves are excellent in salads. The roots can be roasted as a coffee substitute. Flowers make a mild tea.

### Plantain (Plantago major)

Not the banana relative. Broadleaf plantain grows in sidewalk cracks and lawns worldwide. Young leaves can be eaten raw; older leaves are better cooked. It has a mild, slightly bitter flavor.

### Clover

Both red and white clover are edible. Flowers can be dried for tea or eaten fresh. Leaves work in salads when young and tender.

### Elderflower

Elder trees produce clusters of tiny white flowers in late spring. These can be made into cordials, syrups, or fritters. Note: other parts of the elder tree require proper preparation.

## Responsible Foraging

Sustainable foraging follows a simple principle: **take less than you find, and never deplete a stand.** Harvest no more than one-third of any plant population in a given area. Spread your foraging across multiple sites to allow recovery.

Urban foraging isn't about replacing grocery shopping. It's about noticing the abundance that already exists around us and developing a closer relationship with the landscape we inhabit daily.
