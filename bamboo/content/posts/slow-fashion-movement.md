The fashion industry is the second-largest polluter in the world, behind only oil. Every year, 92 million tonnes of textile waste end up in landfills. The slow fashion movement challenges this cycle.

## What Is Slow Fashion

Slow fashion is a response to the disposable culture of fast fashion. It emphasizes:

- **Quality over quantity** -- fewer, better-made garments
- **Ethical production** -- fair wages and safe working conditions
- **Natural materials** -- organic cotton, linen, hemp, wool
- **Longevity** -- designs that outlast trends
- **Transparency** -- knowing where and how clothes are made

## The True Cost of Cheap Clothing

A five-dollar t-shirt has hidden costs that never appear on the price tag. Water pollution from dyeing processes, pesticide-laden cotton fields, and exploitative labor practices are subsidized by communities and ecosystems far from the point of sale.

## Practical Steps

You don't need to overhaul your wardrobe overnight. Small changes compound:

1. **Buy less.** Ask yourself if you truly need the item before purchasing.
2. **Choose natural fibers.** They biodegrade more readily than synthetics.
3. **Repair before replacing.** Learn basic mending skills.
4. **Support ethical brands.** Research companies that publish their supply chain data.
5. **Shop secondhand.** Thrift stores, consignment shops, and online resale platforms extend the life of existing garments.

## The Capsule Wardrobe

A capsule wardrobe is a curated collection of versatile, timeless pieces that can be mixed and matched. Typically 25-40 items including shoes and outerwear, it reduces decision fatigue and consumption simultaneously.

The goal isn't perfection. It's intention. Every garment kept in use for an extra nine months reduces its carbon, water, and waste footprint by approximately 20-30%.
