Residential buildings account for roughly 20% of energy-related carbon emissions globally. While systemic change requires policy and infrastructure, individual households can make meaningful contributions.

## Solar Panels

Rooftop solar has become dramatically more affordable. In many regions, the payback period is now under ten years, after which the energy is essentially free.

### Key Considerations

- **Roof orientation.** South-facing roofs (in the northern hemisphere) receive the most sunlight.
- **Shading.** Even partial shading from trees or neighboring buildings can significantly reduce output.
- **Grid-tie vs. off-grid.** Most residential systems connect to the grid, selling excess power back to the utility.
- **Battery storage.** Systems like home batteries allow you to store solar energy for evening use, reducing grid dependence.

## Beyond Solar

Not everyone can install panels. Other impactful measures include:

**Insulation and weatherization.** Reducing heat loss is often the most cost-effective energy improvement. Seal drafts, insulate attics, and consider double-glazed windows.

**Heat pumps.** Modern heat pumps are 2-4 times more efficient than traditional heating systems. They work by moving heat rather than generating it.

**LED lighting.** LEDs use 75% less energy than incandescent bulbs and last 25 times longer.

**Smart thermostats.** Programmable temperature control prevents heating or cooling empty rooms.

## Measuring Progress

Track your energy consumption monthly. Most utilities provide detailed usage data. Compare year-over-year to see the impact of changes you've made.

```
Month    | kWh Used | vs. Last Year
---------|----------|-------------
January  | 420      | -18%
February | 385      | -22%
March    | 310      | -15%
```

Small reductions compound. A 15% decrease in household energy consumption, multiplied across millions of homes, represents a substantial shift.
