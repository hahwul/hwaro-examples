## About This Portfolio

Totem is a portfolio dedicated to Pacific Northwest tribal art -- a tradition stretching back thousands of years along the misty coastlines from northern California to southeast Alaska. This collection showcases contemporary works that honor ancestral techniques while exploring new forms of expression.

### The Formline Tradition

The art of the Northwest Coast is defined by the **formline** design system: a visual language of ovoid shapes, U-forms, S-curves, and split representations. Every line has weight and intention. Primary formlines in bold black define the major shapes. Secondary formlines in red fill the interior spaces. Tertiary areas of blue-green provide depth and contrast.

This system is not merely decorative. It is a way of seeing the world -- where every being contains inner beings, where transformation between human and animal is fluid, and where the boundary between the physical and spiritual dissolves in the act of creation.

### Materials and Methods

The works in this portfolio use traditional materials wherever possible:

- **Western Red Cedar** -- the tree of life, soft enough to carve yet resistant to decay
- **Alder Wood** -- preferred for masks, with fine grain that holds detail
- **Natural Pigments** -- earth red ochre, charcoal black, copper-based turquoise
- **Rawhide and Sinew** -- for lashing, binding, and drum construction
- **Abalone Shell** -- inlaid for eyes and accents, catching the light like living water

### Tools of the Carver

Traditional carving employs a focused set of tools, each shaped by generations of use:

- **D-Adze** -- for rough shaping and removing large amounts of wood
- **Elbow Adze** -- for hollowing and concave surfaces
- **Straight Knife** -- the primary detail tool, always sharp as obsidian
- **Bent Knife** -- curved blade for reaching into hollowed areas
- **V-Tool** -- for incising fine formline details

### The Vertical Narrative

A totem pole is not a hierarchy. It is a vertical narrative -- a stack of interconnected stories. The bottom figure is often the most important, as it is closest to the viewer and bears the weight of all above. Each figure connects to the next, sharing a continuous flow of carved cedar from base to crown.

This portfolio mirrors that structure. Each project is a piece of a larger story, stacked one upon another, building toward something greater than any single work.
