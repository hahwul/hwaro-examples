## Upon the Manner of This Publication

We print in octavo: a single sheet, folded three times, yielding eight leaves &#8212; sixteen pages of text. Four such sheets, nested one within the other, form one **gathered signature**. A book is a sequence of signatures, gathered in order by the small letters at the foot of the first page of each: A, B, C, and so on through the alphabet, doubled (Aa, Bb) when the work runs long.

The articles you will find in the register of signatures that follows are each, in their own way, concerned with that construction. Some concern the folding itself &#8212; the sequence of cross-folds, the accuracy of the register marks. Some concern the sewing frame &#8212; the stations marked with a bodkin along the fold, the passage of the linen thread around the raised bands. Others concern the way a gathered book opens; the way a well-sewn quire lies flat; the small joys of a signature well made.

### A Note on Signatures

The first signature of any book is nearly always A, unreckoned and unmarked on the title page itself (for the compositor sets those leaves last, once the weight of the work is known). The second bears the letter A at the foot of its first recto. Thereafter each signature is marked with a letter, and each of its four rectos with a progressive number: A, A2, A3, A4. The remaining rectos of the quire go unmarked &#8212; they are identified only by their place within the folded sheet.

> The gathering mark is a whisper from compositor to binder: *I placed this leaf here. Put it there.*

We retain these marks in the register at right, for those who wish to read the quires out of order, or to reconstruct the sheet from the folded book.
