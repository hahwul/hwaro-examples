This publication is set in **EB Garamond** for the display and **Cardo** for the body, both derived from the old-style faces cut by Claude Garamond and Robert Granjon in the sixteenth century. Our ornament is the pilcrow (&#182;), our rule is the double, and our signatures are lettered from A through H as the work requires.

## Upon the Workshop

The compositor works at a case of type, the upper holding the majuscules (thus *upper case*), the lower the minuscules. A single folio of eight pages is set in reverse &#8212; mirror-imaged, in four chases &#8212; so that when the printed sheet is folded, the pages fall into their correct order. A single mistake in the imposition &#8212; a single page set in the wrong chase &#8212; cannot be corrected once the sheet is printed.

The binder receives the printed sheets and folds them. Three folds, each perpendicular to the last, yield the octavo. The fold is creased first with a bone folder, then sharpened by pressing the gathered signature beneath weights overnight. A poorly folded signature will never sew flat; a well-folded signature holds its shape across centuries.

## Upon the Sewing

We sew on three raised bands, with linen thread waxed against the tooth of the paper. The sewing frame holds the signatures upright, one atop another, while the sewer passes the needle through each of three stations marked along the fold. The thread loops around the raised bands and returns; at the head and tail, a kettle stitch anchors each signature to its neighbor below.

> A book sewn on bands will break along the cords before it breaks along the text. It is the oldest structural compromise in bookbinding: *sacrifice the binding before you sacrifice the page.*
