## The reinforced concrete revolution

<p class="lede">Concrete, like stone, is a material of great compressive strength and negligible tensile capacity. Unreinforced, it can form arches and gravity structures but not beams or slabs of useful span. The embedding of steel reinforcement within concrete -- a technique developed in France in the 1850s and systematized by Francois Hennebique in the 1890s -- created a composite material that combined the compressive strength of concrete with the tensile strength of steel. Reinforced concrete transformed bridge design by enabling forms that neither material could achieve alone.</p>

The earliest reinforced concrete bridges were modest in span but revolutionary in concept. The Alvord Lake Bridge in San Francisco (1889), designed by Ernest Ransome, used twisted iron bars embedded in concrete to create an arch of approximately six meters span. Joseph Monier in France and G. A. Wayss in Germany developed parallel systems, each protected by patents that limited the spread of the technology until the early twentieth century.

## The concrete arch

Robert Maillart, a Swiss engineer working in the first decades of the twentieth century, demonstrated that reinforced concrete could produce bridge forms of extraordinary elegance. His three-hinged arches -- notably the Salginatobel Bridge (1930) -- exploited the material's ability to form continuous curved surfaces, eliminating the individual members of the truss and creating structures in which form and force were inseparable. Maillart's bridges were lighter, cheaper, and more beautiful than conventional alternatives, yet they were designed using only the simplest calculations and a profound intuitive understanding of structural behavior.<sup>1</sup>

## Prestressed concrete

The most significant advance in concrete bridge technology came with the development of prestressing. Eugene Freyssinet, a French engineer, recognized in the 1920s that if high-strength steel tendons were tensioned and anchored within a concrete member before it was loaded, the resulting compressive pre-stress would offset the tensile stresses that external loads would later produce. The concrete, always in compression, would never crack, and the steel tendons, always in tension, would work at high efficiency.

Prestressed concrete allowed the construction of long-span beams and box girders that would have been impossible with conventional reinforcement. The technique was particularly suited to bridge construction because the members could be prefabricated in a factory, transported to the site, and assembled with minimal formwork. The segmental construction method -- in which short box-girder segments are cast, post-tensioned together, and cantilevered outward from the piers -- enabled the construction of bridges over deep valleys and wide rivers without the need for temporary support from below.

## The box girder

The hollow box girder became the standard structural section for medium and long-span concrete bridges in the second half of the twentieth century. Its closed cross-section provides excellent torsional stiffness -- essential for resisting the eccentric loads of highway traffic -- and its interior can be inspected and maintained. The thin walls use material efficiently, and the top and bottom flanges provide the large moment of inertia needed to span significant distances.
