## Overview

The moon jellyfish is one of the most widely distributed marine invertebrates on Earth. Its translucent bell, marked by four horseshoe-shaped gonads visible through the tissue, makes it instantly recognizable in aquariums and coastal waters alike.

Despite their fragile appearance, moon jellyfish are remarkably resilient organisms. They tolerate a wide range of salinity and temperature conditions, which partly explains their near-global distribution. Populations have been documented from the frigid waters of the North Atlantic to tropical lagoons in Southeast Asia.

## Anatomy and Locomotion

The bell of *Aurelia aurita* contracts rhythmically, creating a vortex that pulls water and prey toward the oral arms hanging beneath. This pulsing motion is not primarily for directional swimming but for feeding. Moon jellyfish are largely at the mercy of currents, which is why they frequently wash ashore in large aggregations.

The margin of the bell is fringed with hundreds of fine tentacles coated in cnidocytes --- the stinging cells characteristic of all cnidarians. In moon jellyfish, these cells deliver a sting too mild for most humans to notice, though it is highly effective against the copepods and larvae that constitute their diet.

## Ecological Role

Moon jellyfish occupy a paradoxical position in marine food webs. They are simultaneously predators of zooplankton and prey for a surprisingly diverse array of organisms. Ocean sunfish, leatherback sea turtles, and several species of nudibranch feed heavily on *Aurelia*. In some ecosystems, jellyfish biomass rivals or exceeds that of fish.

Their population blooms, increasingly common in warmer and nutrient-enriched waters, can have cascading effects. Dense aggregations reduce zooplankton populations, alter larval fish survival rates, and clog industrial water intake systems. Whether these blooms represent a natural cycle or a symptom of broader ecological disruption remains an active area of research.

## Life Cycle

Like all scyphozoans, the moon jellyfish alternates between a sessile polyp stage and a free-swimming medusa stage. Polyps attach to hard substrates --- rocks, pier pilings, shells --- and reproduce asexually by strobilation, releasing tiny ephyrae that grow into the familiar bell-shaped adults.

This dual life history gives *Aurelia* a strategic advantage. Even if medusa populations collapse in a given year, the polyps persist, ready to seed the next generation when conditions improve.
