## Overview

The blue whale is the largest animal known to have existed. Adults routinely exceed 25 meters in length, and the heaviest recorded individual weighed an estimated 180 tonnes. Their hearts are the size of small automobiles. Their tongues alone can weigh as much as an elephant.

Despite this enormity, blue whales feed near the bottom of the food chain. Their diet consists almost entirely of krill, small crustaceans that they filter from seawater using plates of baleen. A single blue whale can consume four tonnes of krill per day during peak feeding season.

## Anatomy of Scale

Everything about blue whale biology operates at extremes. The aorta is large enough for a human to crawl through. The heartbeat, audible through hydrophones, drops to as low as two beats per minute during deep dives. Calves are born at seven meters in length and gain approximately 90 kilograms per day during the nursing period, fueled by milk that contains nearly 50 percent fat.

The baleen plates, numbering 260 to 400 on each side of the upper jaw, are made of keratin and function as a filtration system. The whale takes in a massive volume of water and krill, then uses its tongue to force the water out through the baleen, trapping the krill inside. This method of feeding, called lunge feeding, involves explosive acceleration and enormous energy expenditure, but the caloric return from a successful lunge is substantial.

## Acoustics

Blue whales produce the loudest sustained sounds of any animal. Their low-frequency vocalizations, centered around 10 to 40 Hz, can travel thousands of kilometers through the deep sound channel of the ocean. These calls serve functions related to long-distance communication, mating, and possibly navigation.

Research has documented a global trend of declining call frequency over several decades. The cause remains debated. Hypotheses include increasing ocean noise pollution forcing whales to adjust their calls, changes in population density as stocks recover, and shifts in body size distribution within populations.

## Conservation

Commercial whaling in the twentieth century reduced blue whale populations by an estimated 99 percent. The species was formally protected in 1966, but recovery has been slow. Current global population estimates range from 10,000 to 25,000 individuals, a fraction of pre-whaling numbers.

Modern threats include ship strikes, entanglement in fishing gear, ocean noise pollution, and climate-driven changes in krill abundance. The Southern Ocean population, which depends on Antarctic krill, is particularly vulnerable to the effects of warming waters and sea ice loss on krill recruitment.

Recovery trajectories vary by population. Some stocks in the eastern North Pacific show encouraging growth. Others, particularly in the Antarctic, remain critically depleted with no clear sign of rebound.
