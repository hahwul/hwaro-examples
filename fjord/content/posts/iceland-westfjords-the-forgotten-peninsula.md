The Westfjords are the part of Iceland that most visitors skip. They are remote, difficult to reach, and sparsely populated. The roads are narrow, often unpaved, and frequently closed by weather. There is no ring road connection. You must choose to go to the Westfjords; you cannot pass through them on the way to somewhere else.

This remoteness is precisely the point.

## The Road to Dynjandi

The approach to Dynjandi waterfall follows Route 60 along the southern coast of the Westfjords. The road is a single lane of gravel that winds along cliff edges above the fjord, with passing places every few hundred meters. Driving requires attention and patience. The views require stopping, which the road does not easily permit.

After two hours of careful driving from Isafjordur, the waterfall appears across the fjord -- a wide, white cascade tumbling down a stepped cliff face in a shape that widens as it falls, like a bridal veil or a fan. Dynjandi is thirty meters wide at the top and sixty meters at the base, and it falls through a total height of one hundred meters across a series of ledges.

The name means "thundering," and on a calm day the sound carries across the fjord long before you arrive. A hiking path leads from the parking area to the base of the falls, passing through six smaller waterfalls on the way up. Each has its own name and character. The path is steep and wet, the rocks slippery with spray.

At the base of the main cascade, the air is thick with mist. The water hits the rocks with a force you can feel in your chest. You stand there, cold and wet, and you understand why this waterfall has been a landmark for sailors and travelers for a thousand years.

## Latrabjarg

The westernmost point of Iceland -- and of Europe, if you exclude the Azores -- is Latrabjarg, a cliff that stretches for fourteen kilometers along the coast and rises to four hundred and forty meters at its highest point. The cliff face is home to millions of seabirds: puffins, razorbills, guillemots, and fulmars, stacked in colonies along every available ledge.

The puffins at Latrabjarg are remarkably tame. They have nested here for so long, with so little human disturbance, that they show almost no fear. You can sit on the grass a meter from a burrow entrance and watch a puffin return from the sea, beak full of sand eels, and disappear underground without a glance in your direction.

> The cliff edge at Latrabjarg is unfenced and undercut. The grass extends to the lip of a four-hundred-meter drop, and the ground beneath the grass is soft turf over loose rock. Step carefully, or not at all.

The drive to Latrabjarg from Patreksfjordur takes about an hour on a gravel road that deteriorates as it approaches the coast. The last section crosses a high, windswept plateau with no shelter and no other traffic. On a clear day, the view from the cliff extends to the Greenland ice sheet, a white line on the northwestern horizon.

## Isafjordur

The largest town in the Westfjords, Isafjordur, has a population of approximately twenty-six hundred. It sits on a sand spit in a fjord within a fjord -- Skutulsfjordur within Isafjardardjup -- surrounded on three sides by mountains that rise directly from the water.

The town is compact and functional, built around the fishing industry that sustains it. The harbor is active with trawlers and smaller boats. The commercial street has a bakery, a bookshop, a few restaurants, and a supermarket. There is a museum in a cluster of eighteenth-century timber buildings on the waterfront, the oldest in Iceland.

What Isafjordur lacks in size it compensates for in setting. The mountains above the town carry snow well into summer, and their reflections fill the fjord on calm mornings. The light at this latitude -- sixty-six degrees north -- is long and horizontal, turning the painted houses amber and the snow pink. Evening walks along the harbor become meditative exercises in color and stillness.

## Hornstrandir

North of Isafjordur, accessible only by boat, lies Hornstrandir -- the northernmost peninsula of the Westfjords, uninhabited since the 1950s. The nature reserve has no roads, no services, and no permanent residents. Arctic foxes, the only native land mammal in Iceland, roam freely here.

The hiking in Hornstrandir is among the most remote in Europe. Trails follow old paths between abandoned farms, crossing mountain passes and descending to empty beaches. The landscape is raw -- sea cliffs, scree slopes, snowfields that persist through summer, and meadows of wildflowers that bloom in brief, intense profusion during July.

Boat services from Isafjordur run to several landing points in Hornstrandir during summer. Most hikers arrange pickup at a different point from their drop-off, allowing a traverse of the peninsula over three to five days. There are no huts; you must carry a tent and all supplies.

## Getting There

The Westfjords are accessible by car from Reykjavik (approximately five to six hours to Isafjordur via Route 1 and Route 61) or by domestic flight from Reykjavik to Isafjordur (forty minutes). In summer, a ferry connects Stykkisholmur on the Snaefellsnes peninsula to Brjanslaekur in the southern Westfjords.

A four-wheel-drive vehicle is strongly recommended. Many roads are unpaved, and river crossings are possible on interior routes. Fuel stations are infrequent; fill up whenever you see one. Mobile phone coverage is intermittent outside Isafjordur.

The best time to visit is June through August. Outside this window, many roads and services are closed. Weather can change rapidly at any time -- carry layers, waterproofs, and the expectation that plans may need to bend.
