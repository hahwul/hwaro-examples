The Faroe Islands exist in a state of permanent negotiation between land and atmosphere. The islands are small -- the entire archipelago could fit inside a mid-sized European city -- but the weather moves across them with a scale and velocity that makes the landscape feel vast.

<!-- more -->

You can stand on a hilltop in full sunlight, watch a wall of rain approach from the west, feel it arrive, feel it pass, and find yourself in sunlight again within ten minutes. This is not unusual weather for the Faroes. This is all weather for the Faroes, happening simultaneously.

## Gasadalur

The village of Gasadalur sits on the western coast of Vagar, tucked into a valley between two mountains. Until 2004, the only way to reach the village was by foot, over a steep mountain pass. A tunnel changed that. Now you drive through the mountain and emerge on the other side to find a handful of houses, a church, and one of the most photographed waterfalls in the North Atlantic.

Mulafossur falls directly from the cliff edge into the ocean, a drop of roughly thirty meters. The waterfall is narrow -- more a stream than a cascade -- but its position is extraordinary. The cliff is sheer, the sea below is dark and restless, and the water falls through open air before meeting the waves. Behind the waterfall, the cliff face is covered in grass so green it looks synthetic.

The village itself has fewer than twenty residents. The houses are traditional Faroese construction: stone walls, timber frames, and grass roofs. The grass roofs are not decorative. They are functional insulation, a layer of living turf that moderates temperature and sheds rain. They have been used in the Faroes for centuries.

## The Sheep

There are more sheep than people in the Faroe Islands. Approximately eighty thousand sheep to fifty-four thousand humans. The sheep are everywhere -- on the hillsides, on the roads, on the cliff edges, in places where no animal should be able to stand. They are a native breed, small and hardy, with thick wool that ranges from white to black through every shade of brown and grey.

The Faroese relationship with sheep is ancient and central to the culture. The name "Faroe" derives from the Old Norse for -- sheep -- and oyar -- islands. These are, literally, the Sheep Islands. The annual sheep roundup -- the grindagota -- is a communal event in which the entire population of a village turns out to gather the sheep from the mountains for shearing, sorting, and slaughter.

The landscape of the Faroes has been shaped by sheep as much as by wind and rain. The hillsides are cropped short, a uniform green that gives the islands their distinctive appearance -- bare, rounded mountains without trees, like enormous green sculptures set in the sea.

## Mykines

The westernmost island, Mykines, is reached by ferry or helicopter from Vagar. The crossing takes forty-five minutes and is often cancelled due to weather. When you do arrive, you step onto a rocky landing and climb a steep path to the village above -- a collection of colorful houses and a white church, home to roughly ten permanent residents.

The real draw of Mykines is the birdlife. The island's cliffs host one of the largest Atlantic puffin colonies in the Faroes. In summer, thousands of puffins nest in burrows along the cliff tops, their orange beaks and clumsy flight patterns a counterpoint to the drama of the landscape. A footpath leads to the Mykines Holmur lighthouse, crossing a narrow bridge over a dizzying gap. The bridge sways in the wind. Below, the sea crashes against the rocks. Puffins orbit overhead like small, confused aircraft.

## Torshavn

The capital, Torshavn, is one of the smallest capitals in the world. The old town -- Tinganes -- occupies a narrow peninsula in the harbor and consists of a few dozen red-painted wooden buildings with grass roofs. The peninsula has been the seat of the Faroese parliament since the Viking age, making it one of the oldest parliamentary meeting places in the world.

Torshavn is quiet. There is no rush, no crowd, no urgency. People walk at a pace that suggests they have nowhere particular to be and are content with that. The harbor is full of fishing boats. The shops close early. In the evening, the restaurants serve lamb and seafood and the fermented mutton dish called skerpikjot, which tastes exactly as challenging as its reputation suggests.

## Getting There

Atlantic Airways operates flights from Copenhagen, Reykjavik, Edinburgh, and Bergen to Vagar Airport. The flight from Copenhagen takes approximately two hours.

Inter-island travel is by ferry, subsea tunnel, or helicopter. The helicopter service is subsidized by the government and costs roughly the same as a bus ticket. Driving is straightforward -- the islands are connected by tunnels and bridges, and the roads are well-maintained. Weather can close roads and cancel ferries without notice; plan flexibility into your itinerary.

The best months to visit are May through August, when the puffins are present and daylight is nearly continuous. Bring waterproof clothing regardless of season. The Faroes average three hundred days of rain per year.
