<section class="abstract-box">
  <h2>Abstract</h2>
  <dl>
    <dt>Background</dt>
    <dd>Population-level early screening has been proposed as a strategy to reduce late-stage disease burden, but cost-effectiveness under realistic uptake and test-performance assumptions is unclear.</dd>
    <dt>Methods</dt>
    <dd>We developed a six-state Markov microsimulation of a hypothetical cohort of 1,000,000 adults screened biennially from age 45, comparing the program to usual care over a lifetime horizon from a healthcare-sector perspective. Probabilistic sensitivity analysis ran 10,000 iterations.</dd>
    <dt>Results</dt>
    <dd>The screening strategy produced an additional 0.18 QALYs per person at an incremental cost of $4,310 (2025 USD), yielding an ICER of <strong>$23,944 per QALY gained</strong>. At a willingness-to-pay threshold of $50,000 per QALY, the screening strategy had a 92.4 pct probability of being cost-effective.</dd>
    <dt>Conclusion</dt>
    <dd>Under plausible assumptions, biennial population-level early screening starting at age 45 is cost-effective at commonly used decision thresholds. Sensitivity analyses identify uptake rate and test sensitivity as the dominant drivers of variation.</dd>
    <dt>Keywords</dt>
    <dd><em>cost-effectiveness analysis; Markov microsimulation; QALYs; willingness-to-pay; probabilistic sensitivity analysis; early screening</em></dd>
  </dl>
</section>
