## Mechanical Honesty

There is an honesty to the xerographic process that digital reproduction cannot replicate. Each pass through the machine introduces its own artifacts — the slightly skewed alignment, the toner buildup at edges, the occasional streak across the page. These are not flaws. They are signatures.

The copy machine does not pretend to create originals. It announces its nature through every imperfection, and in doing so achieves something the original never could: a visible record of transmission.

## The Nth Generation

Copy a copy often enough and you arrive at something unexpected. Details fall away. Contrast increases. The image reduces itself to its most essential elements — pure black and pure white, with nothing in between.

This is not degradation. It is distillation.

Photographers spend careers trying to achieve the tonal separation that a photocopier produces by accident on its third pass. The machine strips away nuance and leaves behind only structure.

## Toner as Medium

Consider the physicality of toner: microscopic particles of plastic and pigment, fused to paper by heat and pressure. Unlike ink, which soaks into fibers, toner sits on the surface. Run your finger across a fresh copy and you can feel the text raised slightly above the page.

This tactile quality is unique to xerography. The words are literally built up from the surface, layer by microscopic layer. Each letter is a tiny sculpture, invisible to the eye but present to the touch.
