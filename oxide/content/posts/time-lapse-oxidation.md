# Time-Lapse: Watching Metal Oxidize

For this project, we placed fresh steel plates in an outdoor industrial site and documented their oxidation over twelve months.

## The Experiment

Materials:
- Clean steel plates (mild steel, no protective coating)
- Exposed to weather (rain, humidity, temperature cycles)
- Photographed weekly in identical lighting conditions

## Oxidation Timeline

### Week 1-2: Flash Rust
First signs of oxidation appear after the first rain. Thin orange layer, powdery texture.

### Month 1-3: Surface Rust Development
Orange rust spreads across the surface. Color intensifies. Light powdery layer becomes more stable.

### Month 4-6: Deep Rust Formation
Rust penetrates deeper. Color shifts from bright orange to red-brown. Scaling begins—rust forms in layers that eventually flake off.

### Month 7-12: Stable Corrosion
Rust layer reaches a relatively stable thickness. Heavy rain causes some flaking, exposing fresh metal underneath, which quickly re-oxidizes. Color variations develop based on local drainage patterns.

> "Oxidation is never really 'finished'—it just reaches equilibrium states where the rate of new rust formation equals the rate of old rust flaking away."

## Visual Evolution

The most interesting phase was months 4-6, when the rust transitioned from a uniform coating to a complex, textured surface with depth and variation. This is the aesthetic we see in long-abandoned industrial sites—multiple rust generations layered on top of each other.

## Accelerated Oxidation

In areas with standing water, oxidation happened 3-4x faster. In dry, sheltered areas, the process was much slower. This explains why abandoned buildings show such variation—each surface has its own microclimate.
