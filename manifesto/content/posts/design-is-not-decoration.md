There is a persistent myth in engineering culture that design is what happens after the real work is done. That it is a coat of paint applied to functioning machinery. That "making it pretty" is a separate, lesser concern from "making it work."

**This is wrong, and it costs us more than we realize.**

<!-- more -->

## Typography Is Thinking

When you choose a typeface, you are not choosing a decoration. You are choosing a voice. A 16px sans-serif at 1.4 line-height says something different from a 20px serif at 1.8 line-height. The first says "efficient, modern, scan me quickly." The second says "sit down, take your time, this is worth reading."

Neither is better. But one is appropriate for your content and the other is not. Choosing arbitrarily -- or worse, choosing the default -- is not neutrality. It is negligence.

Consider what this site does:

- **Oversized headings** break the page into clear territories
- **Wide line spacing** gives each sentence room to breathe
- **A single accent color** tells you where to look when everything else is monochrome
- **Serif typefaces** slow the eye down, demanding attention rather than skimming

Every one of those choices is an argument about how this content should be consumed. They are not decorations. They are instructions.

## Whitespace Is Meaning

The most powerful design element is the one most engineers want to eliminate: empty space. Every pixel of whitespace is a pixel that "could" contain information. This thinking produces dashboards that look like air traffic control panels and documentation that reads like legal filings.

But whitespace is not the absence of content. It is the **presence of structure**. It tells the eye where one idea ends and another begins. It creates hierarchy without headings, emphasis without bold text, pacing without punctuation.

A page with generous margins says: *this content is confident enough to stand alone*. A page crammed edge to edge says: *we are afraid you will leave before we finish talking*.

## Color Is Commitment

Using one color is harder than using ten. When you have only black, white, and a single red, every instance of that red carries weight. It becomes a signal rather than noise.

This is why the best opinion writing has always favored restraint. A broadsheet newspaper does not need a rainbow palette to convey authority. A well-set book does not need colored headings to create hierarchy.

The temptation to add color is the temptation to compensate. If the design needs more color to "work," the design does not work. Fix the structure, not the surface.

## The Engineering Parallel

Good design and good engineering share the same principle: **every element should justify its existence**. A function that does nothing useful is a bug. A visual element that communicates nothing is clutter.

The next time you build an interface, treat every pixel like a line of code. Ask what it does. Ask what happens if you remove it. If the answer is "nothing," remove it.

What remains will be honest, clear, and worth looking at.
