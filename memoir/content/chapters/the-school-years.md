<span class="chapter-label">Chapter II</span>

<blockquote class="epigraph">
<p>Education is not the filling of a pail, but the lighting of a fire.</p>
<cite>-- W. B. Yeats</cite>
</blockquote>

The schoolhouse was a mile and a half from our house, a walk I made every morning for eight years along a road that changed with the seasons but never with the times. In spring the ditches ran with snowmelt and the air smelled of wet earth and possibility. In summer -- though school was out -- I would walk the same road to the library, which occupied a room in the back of the schoolhouse, open to anyone who wanted it. In autumn the maples along the road turned colors that no painter could honestly reproduce, and in winter the walk was an exercise in determination, the cold pressing against my face like a hand trying to push me back home.

I was not a remarkable student. My grades were adequate but uninspired, the product of sufficient effort applied to subjects that held my attention unevenly. Mathematics I found satisfying in the way that puzzles are satisfying -- each problem a small locked box that could be opened with the right combination of logic and patience. History interested me when it was told as story and bored me when it was presented as a list of dates and names to be memorized. Science I loved without reservation, because science asked questions about the world I could see and touch and smell, and because the answers, when they came, had a solidity to them that felt trustworthy.

## Mrs. Alderman

But the subject that mattered most was English, and the teacher who mattered most was Mrs. Alderman. She was a tall woman with sharp features and sharper eyes, the kind of teacher who could silence a room with a look and inspire it with a sentence. She had been teaching at the school for longer than anyone could remember, and there was a rumor among the students that she had been there since the building itself was constructed, which was not true but felt true in the way that all good myths do.

Mrs. Alderman believed in reading the way some people believe in religion -- with a fervor that admitted no doubt and tolerated no indifference. Her classroom had more books in it than the library, stacked on shelves that lined every wall, piled on the windowsills, tucked into corners. She lent them freely and without judgment, handing a shy twelve-year-old a copy of Dickens with the same casual confidence she might have used to offer a glass of water.

She was the first person who told me I could write. Not that I was good at writing, which would have been a compliment I could have deflected, but that I could write, which was a statement of capability that carried with it an implied obligation. If you can do a thing, her tone suggested, then you probably should.

### The Assignment

The assignment that changed everything was deceptively simple. "Write about a place you know well," she said, and then added, as if it were an afterthought but was certainly not, "and tell me why it matters." I wrote about the porch. I described the light and the lemonade and the sound of the rocking chairs and the way Mr. Harmon leaned against the railing, and when I was finished I had written more pages than I had ever written for any assignment, and I had not once consulted the clock to see how much time was left.

Mrs. Alderman returned the essay with a single comment written in her small, precise handwriting at the bottom of the last page: "You see things clearly. Keep looking." It was not effusive praise. It was not the kind of encouragement that makes you feel good about yourself for a day and then fades. It was something more lasting -- a recognition of a capacity that I had not known I possessed, offered by someone whose opinion I trusted without question.

## The Library

I became a regular at the library after that, spending afternoons among the shelves the way other children spent them in the fields or by the river. The librarian was a woman named Miss Patterson, who wore her hair in a bun and her glasses on a chain around her neck and who treated every book as if it were a living thing deserving of respect. She never told me what to read. She simply made sure that whatever I was looking for, I could find, and that whatever I found, I could borrow.

I read without method or discipline, following my curiosity wherever it led. One week I might be deep in a novel about the sea, the next lost in a book about the lives of ants. I read biographies of people I had never heard of and poetry I did not fully understand and histories of countries I could not find on a map. The reading was not systematic, but it was sincere, and over time it built in me a foundation of knowledge and empathy that no curriculum could have provided.

> Books taught me that the world was larger than River Street, larger even than the town, larger than I could imagine. And yet they also taught me that the small things -- a porch, a garden, a kitchen, a family -- were not small at all.

## Friends

I should say something about friends, because no account of school years is complete without them, and because the friends I made during those years shaped me in ways I am still discovering.

There was Thomas, who lived on the farm at the edge of town and who had a talent for mechanical things that bordered on the miraculous. He could fix anything -- a broken bicycle chain, a stalled engine, a clock that had stopped keeping time -- with a patience and precision that seemed inherited from some ancestral line of craftsmen. Thomas did not read much and had no interest in writing, but he understood systems in a way that was its own kind of intelligence, and our conversations, which ranged from the practical to the philosophical, were among the most stimulating of my young life.

There was Eleanor, who drew constantly, filling notebooks with sketches of everything she saw. She drew the schoolhouse, the trees, the river, the faces of our classmates, with a line that was both confident and gentle. She saw beauty in things I would have overlooked -- the pattern of cracks in a sidewalk, the way a shadow fell across a fence, the geometry of a bird in flight. Being her friend taught me to look more carefully at the world, to notice what I had been merely seeing.

And there was Samuel, who was quiet in a way that made other people uncomfortable but which I found restful. Samuel listened more than he spoke, and when he did speak, his words had a weight that came from having been considered before they were offered. He was the friend I went to when I needed to think something through, because his silence gave my thoughts room to move, and his occasional questions -- always precise, always penetrating -- helped me see angles I had missed.

## The Last Day

I remember the last day of school with a clarity that surprises me, given how many other days have blurred together in the years since. It was June, warm but not yet hot, and the classroom was filled with the restless energy of children who could feel the summer waiting for them just beyond the windows.

Mrs. Alderman stood at the front of the room and said something I have never forgotten. "You are leaving this room," she said, "but you are not leaving what happened here. Everything you learned, everything you read, every thought you had in this room -- you take all of that with you. It is yours now. Use it well."

I walked home that afternoon with Thomas and Eleanor and Samuel, the four of us following the road we had walked together so many times, and when we reached the point where our paths diverged -- Thomas turning toward the farm, Eleanor toward the center of town, Samuel toward the hill where his family's house stood -- we paused for a moment, as if sensing that something was ending, though we could not have said exactly what.

We said goodbye the way children do, casually, as if we would see each other tomorrow, which we probably would. But something had shifted. The road ahead was longer than the road behind, and we all knew it, even if none of us said so. I continued on to River Street alone, listening to the sound of my footsteps on the gravel, and when I reached the house I sat on the porch and looked out at the yard and the garden and the river beyond, and I thought about Mrs. Alderman's words, and I felt, for the first time, the particular sadness that comes with understanding that a chapter of your life has closed.
