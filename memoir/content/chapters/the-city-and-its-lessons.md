<span class="chapter-label">Chapter III</span>

<blockquote class="epigraph">
<p>One's destination is never a place, but a new way of seeing things.</p>
<cite>-- Henry Miller</cite>
</blockquote>

I left for the city on a Tuesday morning in September, carrying a suitcase that held everything I thought I would need and nothing I would actually want. My father drove me to the train station in the old pickup truck, the same one he used to haul compost and fence posts and, once, a wounded deer he had found by the side of the road. We did not talk much during the drive. My father was not a man who filled silence with words, and I was too full of competing emotions to trust my voice with any of them.

At the station he helped me with my suitcase, shook my hand, and then pulled me into a hug that lasted longer than any he had given me before. "You will do fine," he said, and the way he said it -- not as a hope or a wish but as a simple statement of fact -- gave me more confidence than any speech could have.

The train journey took four hours. I watched the landscape change through the window, the fields giving way to suburbs giving way to the dense, vertical geometry of the city. I had been to the city before, on school trips and family visits, but I had always been a tourist, someone passing through. This time I was arriving, and the difference between visiting a place and living in it is the difference between reading about the ocean and learning to swim.

## The Apartment

The apartment was on the fourth floor of a building that had once been a warehouse and had been converted, with more ambition than skill, into residential units. The hallway smelled of paint and someone's cooking -- garlic and something spiced that I could not identify. My room was small: a bed, a desk, a chair, a window that looked out onto a narrow courtyard where a single tree grew with the stubborn determination of all city trees, reaching upward through the concrete toward whatever light it could find.

I stood at that window on my first night and listened to the sounds of the city. After eighteen years of crickets and river water and wind in the trees, the urban soundtrack was overwhelming. Cars, voices, music from somewhere nearby, the distant wail of a siren, the rumble of something underground that I would later learn was the subway. It was not unpleasant, exactly, but it was relentless, a constant reminder that I was no longer in a place where silence was the default condition of the world.

## Learning to Be Alone

The hardest part of those first weeks was not the classes or the assignments or the unfamiliar rhythms of city life. It was the loneliness. I had never been truly alone before. At home there was always someone nearby -- my mother in the kitchen, my father in the garden, my grandmother on the porch. Even solitude, at home, was a solitude populated by the presence of people who loved me. In the city, my solitude was genuine, and I discovered that there is a vast difference between being alone by choice and being alone by circumstance.

I filled the loneliness with walking. Every evening after classes I would set out without a destination, following whatever street caught my attention, turning corners at random, letting the city reveal itself to me in its own time and on its own terms. I walked through neighborhoods that seemed to belong to different countries -- blocks where the signs were in languages I could not read, where the smells from the restaurants were foreign and intoxicating, where the rhythms of daily life followed patterns I did not yet understand.

> The city taught me that the world is not one thing but many things, layered and overlapping, and that my experience of it was only one thread in an unimaginably complex weave.

These walks became my education in a way that the formal curriculum could not match. I learned geography by getting lost and finding my way back. I learned sociology by watching how people occupied public space -- who sat where in the parks, who gathered on which corners, how the same street could feel entirely different at noon and at midnight.

## The Bookshop

It was on one of these walks that I found the bookshop. It occupied the ground floor of a narrow building on a side street that I had not explored before, its windows so full of books that they blocked most of the light from outside, giving the interior a perpetual twilight that felt less like dimness and more like privacy.

The owner was a man named Hasan, who had come to the city from somewhere he rarely talked about and who had, over the course of thirty years, built a collection that was less a commercial enterprise than a personal archive. He stocked what interested him, which was everything. Literature and philosophy shared shelves with books on astronomy, cooking, mathematics, and the history of cartography. There was no organizing principle that I could discern, which meant that browsing was an act of discovery, each shelf offering unexpected juxtapositions that sparked connections I would never have made on my own.

Hasan and I became friends slowly, in the way that friendships between reserved people tend to develop. He would recommend books by placing them on the counter when he saw me come in, saying nothing about them, letting the books speak for themselves. I would read them and return them, and we would discuss them in fragments, a sentence here, a question there, building a conversation that stretched over weeks and months.

### A Conversation About Memory

One afternoon, during a lull in customers, Hasan asked me why I had come to the city. I told him I was studying literature. He nodded and said, "And what do you want to do with it?"

I said I wanted to write.

"About what?" he asked.

"About home," I said, surprising myself with the honesty of it. "About where I came from."

He considered this for a moment. "That is a good subject," he said. "But be careful. Memory is not a photograph. It is a painting, and every time you look at it, you add a brushstroke."

I have thought about that warning many times in the years since, and I have come to believe that it is both true and insufficient. Yes, memory changes. Yes, every act of remembering is also an act of creation. But the brushstrokes are not arbitrary. They are guided by something -- call it truth, call it feeling, call it the shape of what actually happened, however imperfectly we perceive it. The painting may not be a photograph, but it is not a fantasy either. It is something in between, and it is in that in-between space that memoir lives.

## The Seasons Turn

I stayed in the city for years, long enough to watch the tree in the courtyard lose its leaves and grow them back several times, long enough to learn the shortcuts and the rhythms and the particular quality of light that the city offered in each season. The loneliness faded, replaced by a network of connections -- classmates, neighbors, the regulars at the bookshop, the woman at the corner bakery who always gave me an extra roll because, she said, I looked like I needed feeding.

But the city never fully became home. Home, I was learning, is not just a place. It is a time, a set of relationships, a feeling in the body that cannot be replicated by changing your address. I carried River Street with me everywhere I went, not as a burden but as a compass, a fixed point by which I could orient myself no matter how far I traveled or how lost I became.

And I did get lost, many times, in ways that had nothing to do with geography. I made mistakes. I trusted people I should not have trusted and doubted people who deserved my faith. I spent money I did not have on things I did not need and neglected to spend time on the things that mattered most. These are the ordinary failures of youth, and I catalog them not because they were dramatic but because they were instructive, each one teaching me something about who I was and who I wanted to become.

The city was patient with me. Cities always are. They absorb your mistakes and your triumphs with the same indifference, offering you anonymity when you need it and community when you seek it. I grew up there, in the way that people grow up when they are finally responsible for themselves, and when I eventually left, I took with me not just memories but a kind of knowledge that can only be acquired by being young and uncertain in a large and complicated place.
