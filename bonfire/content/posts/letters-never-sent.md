I found the letter in a coat pocket. It had been there since October, folded once and then again, the creases so deep that the paper had started to separate along the lines. I had written it at a rest stop outside Duluth, sitting in the car while rain sheeted across the windshield, and I had never sent it.

The letter was to my father. It said the things I had been carrying for years --- not accusations, but observations. That he had taught me to notice weather. That the way he stacked firewood was the most orderly thing about him. That I understood, now, why he went fishing alone and came back quieter than when he left.

I think most of us carry letters like this. Not on paper, necessarily, but in the part of the mind that rehearses conversations we will never have. We compose them in the shower, on long drives, in the minutes before sleep. They are addressed to parents, to old friends, to people we loved badly.

There is a particular cruelty in this. The letters improve with time. Each revision adds nuance, removes pettiness, finds the precise metaphor that the moment of feeling could not produce. By the time we have perfected what we want to say, the moment for saying it has passed. The person has moved. The relationship has shifted.

Perhaps that is the function of the unsent letter. It is not really communication. It is clarification. You write it not so the other person will understand you, but so you will understand yourself. The act of putting the words down forces a precision that thought alone cannot achieve. You discover what you actually feel by watching what your hand writes.

I put the letter back in the coat pocket. I will probably find it again next October, more worn and more true, and I will not send it then either. The letter has become its own thing now --- separate from the conversation it was meant to begin.

Some words are not meant to arrive. They are meant to be carried.
