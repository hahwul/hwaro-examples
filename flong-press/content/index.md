## The problem of duplication

<p class="lede">In the early centuries of printing, every page of type was composed by hand, letter by letter, from individual pieces of metal type. Once the edition was printed, the type was distributed -- broken apart and returned to its cases for reuse. If the publisher wished to reprint, the entire page had to be recomposed from scratch: the same labor, the same cost, the same risk of error. The stereotype process solved this problem by creating a permanent metal duplicate of the composed page, freeing the original type for reuse while preserving the ability to reprint at will.</p>

## What is a flong?

The flong is a papier-mache mold taken from a page of composed type. The word derives from the French flan, meaning a flat cake or mold. To make a flong, the printer lays sheets of damp tissue and blotting paper over the locked-up type forme, brushes them into every cavity of the type face, and beats the assembly with a stiff brush until the paper conforms precisely to every letter, space, and rule. The dried flong is a perfect negative impression of the type page -- every letter reversed, every line mirrored, every space a raised plateau.

## Chapters

This publication examines the stereotype process in five chapters:

1. **The Flong** -- The papier-mache matrix mold: its preparation, impression, and drying.

2. **The Casting Box** -- The metal frame and furnace used to cast molten type metal into the flong mold.

3. **The Rotary Stereotype** -- The adaptation of flat stereotyping to curved plates for rotary newspaper presses.

4. **The End of the Foundry** -- How phototypesetting and offset lithography rendered the stereotype obsolete.

5. **The Language of Type Metal** -- The material vocabulary of the foundry: antimony, tin, lead, and the alloys that made industrial printing possible.
