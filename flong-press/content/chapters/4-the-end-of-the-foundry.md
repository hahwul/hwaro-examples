## The photographic revolution

<p class="lede">The stereotype process survived for nearly two centuries, from its invention in the early eighteenth century through its dominance of newspaper production in the twentieth. Its decline was swift and total. Within two decades -- from approximately 1960 to 1980 -- phototypesetting and offset lithography replaced hot-metal composition and letterpress printing in nearly every newspaper and commercial printing operation in the industrialized world. The foundry fell silent.</p>

Phototypesetting replaced the physical type forme with a photographic negative. Text was composed by projecting images of individual characters onto photographic paper or film, producing a paste-up that could be photographed and transferred to an offset printing plate. The offset plate -- a thin sheet of aluminum bearing a photochemical image -- mounted directly on the press cylinder. No flong was needed. No molten metal was poured. No stereotype plate was cast.

The advantages were immediate and decisive. Phototypesetting was faster than hot-metal composition. Offset plates were lighter, cheaper, and easier to produce than stereotype plates. The photographic process could reproduce fine detail -- particularly the tonal gradations of photographs -- with a fidelity that the stereotype process could never match. And the elimination of molten lead from the production process removed a significant occupational health hazard.

## The last presses

The transition did not happen overnight. Some newspapers continued to use stereotyping into the 1980s, particularly in smaller markets where the capital investment in offset equipment was difficult to justify. The New York Daily News cast its last stereotype plate in 1991. A handful of specialty printers retained letterpress equipment for fine art printing and limited editions, but the industrial stereotype process -- the mass-production system that had fed newspapers to millions of readers every morning -- was finished.

## What was lost

The end of the stereotype process was, by any rational measure, an improvement. Offset printing was faster, cheaper, cleaner, and more versatile. But something was lost in the transition. The stereotype plate was a physical object -- a slab of metal that bore the impression of language in its surface, that could be held and weighed and felt. The offset plate is a thin film of chemistry on aluminum, its image invisible to the naked eye. The stereotype process was industrial craft: skilled labor, physical materials, fire and metal. Offset printing is industrial chemistry: automated, precise, and abstract.
