## The impression

<p class="lede">The matrix is the negative impression left in the flong after it has been pressed against the type forme. Every letter, space, rule, and ornament on the page is reproduced in reverse and in intaglio -- the raised surfaces of the type creating depressions in the soft papier-mache. This reversed, sunken image is the mold from which the stereotype plate will be cast. The quality of the final plate depends entirely on the precision of this impression.</p>

## The beating process

In the early technique, the flong was beaten into the type by hand. The stereotype maker placed the damp flong over the locked type forme and worked it into the type face using a stiff brush, beating the papier-mache into every groove and corner of the letterforms. This was skilled, physical work: too little pressure left a shallow, indistinct impression; too much pressure could tear the tissue layers or distort the type.

The hydraulic press, introduced in the 1850s, replaced hand beating for most commercial work. The flong was placed over the type, a heavy felt blanket was laid on top, and the press applied uniform pressure across the entire page. The hydraulic press produced a more consistent impression than hand beating and allowed larger formes to be stereotyped reliably.

## Reading in reverse

The matrix reads in reverse -- mirror-image text, sunken where the type was raised, raised where the type had spaces. This reversal is fundamental to the entire printing process. The original type is cut in reverse so that it prints correctly on paper. The flong impression of reversed type is therefore right-reading, but sunken. The stereotype plate cast from the flong reverses the image again, producing raised, reversed type that prints correctly -- just like the original. Each step in the chain reverses the previous one, and the final product reads as intended.

This chain of reversals -- type (reversed) to flong (right-reading, sunken) to plate (reversed, raised) to paper (right-reading) -- is the logical engine of stereotype printing. Understanding it requires holding four states of the image in mind simultaneously, a mental exercise that the workers of the stereotype foundry performed automatically through years of practice.
