## The alloy

<p class="lede">Type metal is not a single substance but a family of alloys, each formulated for a specific purpose. All share the same three principal ingredients -- lead, tin, and antimony -- but the proportions vary according to the demands of the application. Foundry type, which must withstand the repeated pressure of the printing press, requires a harder alloy than stereotype plates, which print from a single surface and are melted down after use. The metallurgy of printing is a practical science, developed over centuries by craftsmen who understood the behavior of their materials through experience rather than theory.</p>

## Lead

Lead is the base metal of all type alloys, comprising between 54 and 93 percent of the mixture. It is cheap, abundant, and melts at a low temperature (327 degrees Celsius for pure lead, lower in alloy). Its softness, which would be a liability in a structural material, is an advantage in printing: lead type can be cast with fine detail and trimmed easily with hand tools. The weight of lead -- it is among the densest common metals -- gives the type forme and the stereotype plate a satisfying heft that contributes to the even impression required for quality printing.

## Tin

Tin improves the fluidity of the molten alloy, allowing it to flow into fine details and thin sections that pure lead would not fill. It also increases the hardness of the solidified metal and gives the surface a brighter, more silvery appearance. High-tin alloys resist oxidation better than high-lead alloys, which is why foundry type (which must be stored and reused) contains more tin than stereotype plates (which are melted down after a single use).

## Antimony

Antimony is the critical ingredient that makes type metal unique among casting alloys. Most metals and alloys shrink as they solidify, pulling away from the mold walls and losing detail. Antimony expands on solidification, a property known as anti-shrinkage. In a type metal alloy, the antimony expansion compensates for the lead shrinkage, producing a casting that faithfully reproduces the dimensions and detail of the mold. Without antimony, stereotype casting would be impractical: the plates would be undersize, the letters indistinct, the fine details lost.
