Heirloom tomatoes are open-pollinated varieties that have been passed down through generations, selected not for shipping durability or uniform appearance but for flavor. Growing them is one of the great pleasures of the summer garden.

<!-- more -->

## What Makes a Tomato an Heirloom

The term generally refers to varieties that are at least fifty years old, though some define the cutoff at pre-1945 or pre-1950. What matters more than the exact date is the lineage: heirlooms are open-pollinated, meaning they reproduce true to type from saved seed. They were selected by gardeners, not corporations, and their continued existence reflects real merit.

## Varieties Worth Growing

### Brandywine

The standard-bearer of the heirloom world. Brandywine produces large, slightly flattened pink fruit with an exceptionally rich, complex flavor -- sweet with a balancing acidity and a lingering finish. The plants are vigorous and indeterminate, requiring sturdy support. Expect fruit in 80 to 90 days. The potato-leaf foliage is distinctive.

### Cherokee Purple

A dusky, dark-shouldered tomato with deep reddish-purple skin and green-tinged flesh. The flavor is rich, smoky, and sweet -- one of the most complex of any tomato variety. Fruits are medium to large and somewhat prone to cracking, so harvest promptly when ripe.

### Green Zebra

A smaller tomato, lime-green with darker green stripes even when fully ripe. The flavor is bright, tangy, and refreshing. Excellent for salads and for adding visual interest to a tomato platter. Matures in about 75 days.

### San Marzano

The definitive paste tomato. Long, narrow, and thick-walled with relatively few seeds. The flavor is concentrated and sweet with low acidity. Indispensable for sauce-making. While commercial San Marzano seeds of questionable provenance abound, seek out a reputable source for authentic genetics.

### Mortgage Lifter

An enormous pink beefsteak developed in the 1930s by a West Virginia gardener who supposedly paid off his mortgage selling the plants. Individual fruits can exceed two pounds. The flavor is mild, sweet, and meaty with very little acidity.

## Growing Tips

Heirloom tomatoes generally require more attention than modern hybrids. They are often less disease-resistant and more prone to cracking and blossom-end rot. Space plants generously, stake or cage them early, and mulch deeply to maintain even soil moisture. Water at the base of the plant rather than overhead, and avoid wetting the foliage.

Start seeds indoors six to eight weeks before your last frost date. Transplant after all danger of frost has passed and night temperatures stay reliably above fifty degrees.

## The Reward

What heirlooms lack in convenience, they repay abundantly in flavor. A ripe Brandywine, still warm from the sun, sliced thick and eaten with nothing more than salt and good olive oil, is one of the finest things a garden can produce.
