<header class="paper-header">
  <p class="paper-eyebrow">NSF CBET &middot; Full Proposal &middot; April 2026</p>
  <h1 class="paper-title">Resilient Urban Water Infrastructure Under Climate Uncertainty: A Multi-Scale Modelling and Decision Framework</h1>
  <p class="paper-subtitle">Developing adaptive management strategies for municipal water systems facing compound climate risks through integrated simulation, optimization, and stakeholder engagement.</p>
  <div class="paper-meta">
    <strong>Principal Investigator:</strong> Dr. Anika Patel, Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering, MIT<br>
    <strong>Co-PIs:</strong> Dr. Marcus Chen (Stanford), Dr. Fatima Al-Rashidi (UT Austin), Dr. James Okafor (Georgia Tech), Dr. Yuki Tanaka (U. Michigan)<br>
    <strong>Program:</strong> NSF CBET -- Environmental Sustainability &middot; <strong>Duration:</strong> 36 months &middot; <strong>Total Budget:</strong> $2,847,500
  </div>
</header>

<div class="proposal-summary">
  <dl>
    <dt>Project Overview</dt>
    <dd>This proposal addresses the urgent need for resilient urban water infrastructure in the face of accelerating climate change. We propose a multi-scale computational framework that integrates hydraulic network modelling, climate downscaling, economic optimization, and participatory decision-making to develop adaptive management strategies for municipal water systems.</dd>
    <dt>Intellectual Merit</dt>
    <dd>The proposed framework advances the state of the art in three ways: (1) coupling physics-based hydraulic models with machine-learning-enhanced climate projections at unprecedented spatial resolution; (2) developing a novel multi-objective optimization approach that balances infrastructure cost, service reliability, and environmental equity; and (3) creating a stakeholder-informed scenario analysis methodology that bridges computational modelling with community priorities.</dd>
    <dt>Broader Impacts</dt>
    <dd>The project will directly benefit three partner municipalities (Boston, Houston, Phoenix) representing distinct climate vulnerability profiles. Research outcomes will be translated into open-source decision-support tools for water utility managers. The project will train 4 PhD students and 6 master's students, with a dedicated recruitment pipeline for underrepresented minorities in environmental engineering.</dd>
    <dt>Keywords</dt>
    <dd><em>urban water infrastructure; climate resilience; multi-scale modelling; decision support; hydraulic networks; environmental equity</em></dd>
  </dl>
</div>
