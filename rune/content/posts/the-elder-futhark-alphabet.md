The Elder Futhark is the oldest known runic alphabet. It was used across the Germanic-speaking world from roughly the second to the eighth century, carved into stone, bone, metal, and wood by peoples who understood that writing was not merely communication but an act of power.

## The Name

The word "Futhark" is not arbitrary. It is formed from the sounds of the first six runes in the sequence: F, U, Th, A, R, K -- or in their runic names, Fehu, Uruz, Thurisaz, Ansuz, Raidho, Kenaz. This is analogous to calling our own writing system the "alphabet" after the Greek alpha and beta.

The Elder Futhark is distinguished from its descendants -- the Younger Futhark and the Anglo-Saxon Futhorc -- by its completeness. It contains twenty-four runes, divided into three groups of eight called aettir (singular: aett). This division is not accidental. It reflects a deliberate organizational principle, though scholars continue to debate its precise significance.

## The First Aett: Freyr's Eight

The first aett is traditionally associated with the god Freyr and concerns itself with the material world -- wealth, strength, and the forces of nature.

**Fehu**, the first rune, means "cattle" -- and by extension, movable wealth. In a society where a person's standing was measured in livestock, Fehu represented not abstract riches but tangible, living value. It speaks to abundance, but also to the responsibility that comes with it. Wealth must be managed. Cattle must be fed.

**Uruz** is the aurochs, the wild ox that once roamed the forests of northern Europe. It stood as tall as a person at the shoulder and was hunted to extinction by the seventeenth century. As a rune, it represents untamed strength, physical health, and the raw energy of the natural world before it is shaped by human intention.

**Thurisaz** is the thorn or the giant -- a force of chaos and protection simultaneously. It is the boundary between the ordered world and the wild. The thorn bush that guards the homestead. The giant that tests the gods.

## The Second Aett: Heimdall's Eight

The second aett addresses the human experience -- hardship, harvest, the constraints of nature, and the forces that shape a life.

These eight runes move from the destructive power of hail to the slow warmth of the sun, tracing a path through difficulty toward resolution. They acknowledge that the world is not gentle, that ice and need and constraint are as fundamental to existence as joy and abundance.

The rune **Hagalaz** (hail) opens this aett with destruction -- the sudden storm that flattens a crop. But destruction in the Norse worldview is not final. It is transformative. The hail melts. The water feeds the soil. The next harvest grows from the ruins of the last.

**Isa** (ice) is stillness itself. It represents the frozen state where nothing moves, nothing grows, nothing changes. It is not evil. It is necessary. Without the stillness of winter, there is no spring. Without the pause of ice, there is no movement of water.

## The Third Aett: Tyr's Eight

The third aett belongs to Tyr, the god of law and justice who sacrificed his hand to bind the great wolf Fenrir. These runes concern themselves with the higher order -- cosmic forces, human destiny, and the structures that govern existence.

Here we find **Tiwaz**, Tyr's own rune, shaped like an arrow pointing upward. It represents justice, sacrifice, and the willingness to lose something personal for the sake of something greater. When Tyr placed his hand in Fenrir's mouth, he knew it would be bitten off. He did it anyway, because the wolf had to be bound and someone had to pay the price.

**Berkano** (birch) is the rune of new beginnings, of the birch tree that is always the first to grow in cleared ground. It speaks to birth, renewal, and the quiet persistence of life in the face of destruction.

The final rune, **Othala**, means "ancestral property" or "homeland." It closes the Futhark with a statement about belonging -- that identity is rooted in place, in lineage, in the accumulated wisdom of those who came before.

## Reading the Stones

The Elder Futhark survives on hundreds of inscriptions scattered across Scandinavia and beyond. The Kylver Stone from Gotland, dating to roughly 400 CE, bears the complete Futhark sequence and remains one of the most important artifacts in runic studies.

These inscriptions were not casual. Carving runes into stone required skill, time, and intention. Each mark was deliberate. The errors that occasionally appear -- reversed runes, omitted characters -- tell us that the carvers were human, but the overall consistency of the system across vast distances tells us that runic knowledge was transmitted with remarkable fidelity.

The Elder Futhark eventually gave way to the Younger Futhark around the eighth century, as the Scandinavian languages evolved and the older system became unnecessarily complex for the sounds that remained. But the twenty-four runes of the Elder system persist in memory, in scholarship, and in the stones that still stand in fields and churchyards across the North.

They are waiting to be read.
