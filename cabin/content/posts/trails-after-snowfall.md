A fresh snowfall transforms the mountain in ways that are difficult to convey to anyone who has not witnessed it. Every branch, every rock, every rut in the trail is softened and made new. The familiar path to the upper ridge becomes a different country, white and silent, where the only marks are the delicate tracks of birds and the heavier prints of deer moving single file through the deeper drifts.

The hiking is harder after a storm. Snowshoes are necessary once the trail climbs above the tree line, and even with them the going is slow and deliberate. Each step must be placed with care where the snow conceals the uneven ground beneath. But the effort is repaid many times over by the stillness. There is a quality of silence after snowfall that is unlike any other, a deep and absolute quiet broken only by the creak of the snowshoes and the occasional soft collapse of snow sliding from a pine bough.

At the overlook above the valley, the view is immense. The peaks to the north are sharply white against a sky so clear it appears almost dark. The valley below is a patchwork of white fields and dark stands of spruce, with the thin thread of the river visible only where the current has kept the ice from forming. On a day like this, the scale of the landscape is humbling. One stands very small against all that stone and snow and sky.

The descent is quicker, following the tracks made on the way up. By the time the cabin comes into view, the light is failing and the air has taken on the particular bite that promises another cold night. Boots are stamped clean on the porch, the door opens to warmth and the smell of the stew that has been simmering on the stove since morning. There is no better ending to a winter walk than this.
