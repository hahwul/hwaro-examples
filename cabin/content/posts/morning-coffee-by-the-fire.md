There is no alarm clock in this cabin. The day begins when the cold seeps through the quilt and the gray light at the window is just bright enough to see the outline of the stove across the room. The first task is always the same: pull on wool socks, cross the cold floor, and coax the embers back to life with a handful of kindling and a steady breath.

The coffee ritual is unhurried. Water drawn from the hand pump, heated in the same blackened kettle that has served this purpose for years. The grounds are coarse, measured by instinct rather than any careful ratio. While the water climbs toward a boil, the cabin begins to warm, and the particular smell of wood smoke and roasting coffee fills the single room like a kind of greeting.

The first cup is taken in the chair by the window, the one with the view down the slope to where the creek runs beneath a shelf of ice. The coffee is strong and dark, almost bitter, and there is nothing to improve it. No news to read, no messages to answer. Only the sound of the fire ticking in the stove and the slow brightening of a winter morning across the ridge.

By the time the cup is empty, the cabin is warm enough to shed the heavy coat. The second cup is poured with more attention, savored more slowly. Plans for the day begin to form, not as obligations but as natural responses to what the mountain and the season require. There is wood to split, a path to clear, bread dough that has been rising through the night. But none of it begins until the coffee is finished and the fire is well established. That much, at least, is sacred.
