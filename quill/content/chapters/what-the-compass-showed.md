The Vane River was wider than Seren remembered. Six years ago, in midsummer, she had crossed it at a ford where the water barely reached her knees. Now, swollen with autumn rain, the river stretched thirty yards across, its current fast enough to pull a person off their feet.

They found a crossing point two leagues upstream, where a fallen pine formed a natural bridge. The trunk was slick with moss and spray, but it held their weight. Edric went first, his long arms spread for balance, and Seren followed with her pack held high against her chest to keep the notebooks dry.

On the far bank, she took out both compasses.

The Survey Office instrument pointed north. Tova's compass, the one with the bone needle, pointed northeast.

---

"That's new," Edric said, looking over her shoulder.

Seren rotated slowly, watching the bone needle. It held its bearing, unwavering, even as the iron compass swung to track magnetic north. Two instruments, two norths. In her twelve years of cartographic work, she had seen compass anomalies caused by iron deposits, by certain rock formations, by the proximity of lightning strikes. She had never seen a compass that appeared to be pointing at something specific.

"Northeast," she said. "That's consistent with the coordinates on the map."

"So we follow it?"

Seren thought of Tova's words: *When your instruments disagree with your eyes, trust your eyes.* But her eyes showed only pine forest, stretching in every direction with the monotony of a landscape that had never been asked to be interesting.

"We follow it," she said.

---

The first sign appeared on the second day past the river.

It was a stone, half-buried in pine needles, with a flat face that bore a mark. Not a natural formation. Someone had taken a chisel to this stone and carved a symbol: a circle with a vertical line through its center, like a simplified compass rose. The carving was weathered but not ancient. Seren estimated it at perhaps twenty years old, maybe thirty.

She sketched it in her notebook and they moved on.

The second sign was a tree, blazed with the same symbol, cut into the bark at eye height. Then another stone. Then a series of blazed trees that formed a clear line through the forest, spaced at regular intervals of roughly fifty paces.

"A path," Edric said.

"A surveyed path," Seren corrected. "Someone measured these intervals. This is deliberate." She checked the bone compass. Its needle pointed along the line of markers, unwavering.

They followed the blazed trail for the rest of the day. The forest changed gradually around them. The pines grew taller, their trunks wider, their canopy denser. Undergrowth thinned until the forest floor was bare earth covered with a thick layer of needles. The light took on a particular quality: diffuse, amber, as though filtered through something other than branches and leaves.

By late afternoon, they found the wall.

---

It was not a ruin. That was the first thing Seren noticed, and it altered everything she had assumed about this expedition. She had expected, if she found anything at all, to find remnants: foundations, perhaps, or the outline of structures long since reclaimed by the forest.

This wall was intact. It rose twelve feet from the forest floor, built of fitted stone without mortar, in a style that Seren recognized from the oldest buildings in the capital. The stones were precisely cut, their surfaces smooth, their joints so tight that she could not fit a knife blade between them.

She pressed her palm against the wall. The stone was warm.

"This is impossible," she said, not to Edric but to herself, to the professional certainty she had spent twelve years constructing. "I surveyed this area. There was nothing here."

Edric ran his hand along the wall, walking its length. "It continues," he called. "I can't see where it ends. This is massive."

Seren opened the notebook to her copy of the phantom map. The defensive walls she had noted, drawn in careful cartographic notation, corresponded precisely to what she was touching. The map had shown the truth. Six years ago, when she had walked through this same forest and recorded nothing but trees, either the wall had not been here, or she had been unable to see it.

Neither explanation was acceptable. Both demanded the same response.

She took out her instruments, her measuring chains, her protractor. She did what cartographers do when the world refuses to make sense: she began to measure it.

---

The measurements were consistent. The wall was real, solid, and exactly where the map said it should be. She recorded its dimensions, its bearing, the composition of its stone. She documented the symbol carved into the keystone of what appeared to be a gate: the same circle-and-line motif they had been following through the forest, rendered here with greater precision and surrounded by smaller symbols she could not yet interpret.

The gate was closed. Not locked, not barred, simply closed. Two stone doors, each taller than a person, set into an archway of fitted blocks.

Seren stood before the gate and felt something she had not felt since her first expedition, when she was twenty-two and the world was still full of unmapped spaces: the precise, terrible thrill of standing at the edge of what was known.

"We could go back," Edric said. It was not a suggestion. It was an observation, offered in the same neutral tone he might use to note the weather.

"We could," Seren agreed. She pressed her palm against the gate. The stone was warmer here, almost feverish, as though something on the other side was generating heat. Through the fine joints between the doors, she thought she could hear something. Not voices, not machinery. Something more fundamental. The sound a place makes when it is alive and waiting.

She pushed the gate, and it opened.
