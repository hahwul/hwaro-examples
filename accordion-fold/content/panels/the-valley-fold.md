## Trough

A valley fold is a fold where the crease recedes from the reader. Seen from the side, the
paper resembles a V: two panels meeting at a base. The valley fold is the negative half of
the concertina; it is the fold which permits the object to compress.

## What the Valley Conceals

When a concertina is partially closed, the valley panels are the ones the reader cannot
see. They are held against the adjacent mountain panels, flush, and their content is
withheld until the accordion is opened further.

This property can be used deliberately. A valley panel may carry a footnote, an
illustration, or a shift of voice that the editor wishes to offer only as a kind of
afterthought &#8212; something the reader encounters only by committing to the full
extension of the strip.

## Pairing

Every valley is paired with two mountains &#8212; the one behind and the one ahead. The
three together form a basic unit of the accordion, and many editors plan content in these
units of three rather than in single panels.

## The Depth of the Valley

The angular measure of a valley fold is the same as that of a mountain fold, simply
inverted. A concertina made entirely of shallow valleys and sharp mountains (or the
reverse) will not lie flat when extended; the panels will rise and fall like hills.
