## Topography of a Book

A concertina extended against a wall occupies a certain topography. The reader does not
sit with it but stands in front of it, walking its length, turning the head as if looking
down a hillside. This is an unusual posture for reading, and the form rewards editors who
acknowledge it.

> The concertina is the only book that is also, secretly, a landscape.

## Planning by Panel

The editor of a concertina must make decisions most book editors do not. They must decide:

1. Where the reader will pause (usually at the ridge of a mountain fold).
2. Where the reader will be invited to flip forward.
3. Which panels will be seen first, from a distance.
4. Which panels will reveal themselves only on close inspection.
5. Whether the concertina is better read in panel mode or spread mode for this content.

## Scale and Scope

A four-panel concertina fits comfortably in the hand and is read in panel mode, like any
small book. A sixteen-panel concertina is typically read in spread mode, opened against a
table or wall. Beyond sixteen panels the object becomes a kind of mural; beyond thirty-two
it ceases, practically speaking, to be a book.

## Continuous Panels

Some concertinas exploit the continuous strip of paper to carry a single image, line, or
diagram across every panel. This is most effective when the subject is itself continuous
&#8212; a river, a coastline, a chromatic scale, a timeline. Attempting this trick with a
non-continuous subject tends to produce a concertina that reads, strangely, like a broken
chord.
