## The Choice of Bird

Three birds provided the great bulk of historical quill pens: the goose, the swan, and
the crow. The goose was the common quill, used for ordinary document work. The swan was
larger and heavier, preferred for formal book-hands and display scripts. The crow was
small and fine, used almost exclusively for the finest rubrications and for lettering at
very small sizes.

No distinction of merit is intended; each bird produced a feather suited to a particular
task, and the careful scriptorium kept a small supply of all three.

## The Cutting

A quill is not written with raw. The end of the feather must be tempered (heated in hot
sand to harden the barrel), scraped (to remove the outer membrane), and cut to a nib.
The nib is cut in two operations: a long angled slice across the side, to produce the
writing edge, and a short perpendicular slit along the centre, to allow the ink to flow.

The angle of the side-slice determines the slant of the resulting letters. A steeper
angle produces a more upright hand; a shallower angle produces a more oblique one.
Carolingian minuscule typically requires a moderate angle, perhaps thirty-five degrees
from perpendicular.

## The Care

A good quill, properly cared for, will serve for many days of writing. It must be kept
moist (but not wet) when not in use, and re-cut every few hours of active writing.
A quill that has been used hard for a full day will produce noticeably softer letter-forms
in the final hour than in the first; experienced scribes plan their day accordingly and
save the decorative work for a fresh quill in the morning.
