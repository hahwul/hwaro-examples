## The Problem of the Infinite Line

Insular interlace is, formally, a puzzle. The ornament consists of ribbons which enter a
field, weave over and under one another without interruption, and exit the field again.
The visual effect is of a pattern that has no beginning and no end; the practical effect
is that the maker must plan every crossing in advance, because a single mistake
compromises the whole.

> One cannot tie the knot in the middle.

## The Unit of Three

Insular interlace almost always resolves, on close inspection, into units of three
strands. The artist rarely works with two or four; three is the natural unit, being the
smallest number of strands that can weave over and under in a repeating pattern without
producing a trivial crossing. The three-strand unit also carries a Trinitarian
significance that was not lost on the insular scriptoria.

## The Grid

The interlace is drawn over a grid of small equilateral triangles, which ensures that the
strands maintain a consistent width and that the crossings land at predictable points.
The grid is laid down in hard-point, then the ribbons are traced in pencil, then the
crossings are assigned (over or under), then finally the ribbons are inked and coloured.

The apprentice who cannot hold a steady width of ribbon across a full interlace field is
not yet ready to attempt a full-page carpet panel.

## A Simple Figure

A basic three-strand knot may be described as a continuous path that crosses itself six
times: once at each of the three &#8220;knots,&#8221; twice in the &#8220;body&#8221;
between the knots. The six crossings alternate over-under, over-under, over-under; any
other assignment yields a flat or ambiguous figure. This simple knot is the starting
exercise for every illuminator&#39;s apprentice in the Celtic tradition.
