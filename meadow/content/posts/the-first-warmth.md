You can feel it before you step outside. Something in the air changes overnight -- not temperature alone, but a softness, a loosening. The windows that have been sealed shut for months suddenly want to be opened. The house, which has been a refuge, begins to feel like a cage.

## Recognition

The first warm day of the year is not merely pleasant. It is an event. The body recognises it before the mind does. Your shoulders drop. Your breathing slows. There is a physical unclenching that happens when the air finally carries warmth instead of resistance.

I have noticed that people behave differently on this day. Neighbours who have been invisible since November appear in their gardens. Children stay out past dusk. There is a communal exhale, as though the entire village has been holding its breath.

## What Stirs

In the garden, the change is already visible to those who look closely. The soil, which has been grey and compacted, begins to darken as moisture rises. Early shoots -- snowdrops, crocuses, the first brave tips of daffodil leaves -- seem to grow visibly between morning and afternoon.

The birds know before we do. The dawn chorus, which has been thin and tentative through February, suddenly swells. New voices join -- migrants returning, residents staking territory with renewed urgency. The sound is not merely louder; it is richer, more layered.

## A Caution

There is a temptation, on such a day, to rush. To plant too early, to strip back mulch, to declare winter finished. But the experienced gardener knows: this is a promise, not a guarantee. There will be cold nights yet. Frost may return. The wise response to the first warmth is gratitude, not haste.

Enjoy the day for what it is. Sit outside. Let the sun reach your face. But keep the fleece covers close at hand. Spring arrives in its own time, and it does not consult our calendars.
