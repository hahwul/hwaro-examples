You smell it before you see it. Walking through damp woodland in early March, there is a moment when the air shifts from the neutral scent of leaf litter to something sharp and green and unmistakable. Wild garlic. The woodland floor is carpeted with it -- broad, bright leaves pushing up through the brown of last autumn, and soon, clusters of white star-shaped flowers.

## Finding It

Wild garlic -- known also as ramsons, or bear's garlic -- favours damp, shaded ground. Look for it along streams, in the understorey of deciduous woodland, and on north-facing banks where the soil stays moist. It grows in colonies, sometimes vast ones, and once you know a good spot, it will reward you year after year.

Identification is straightforward. The leaves are broad, pointed, and emerge singly from the base. Crush a leaf between your fingers: if it smells strongly of garlic, you have the right plant. Be cautious, though. The leaves bear a passing resemblance to lily of the valley and autumn crocus, both of which are toxic. The smell test is definitive.

## Harvesting with Care

Take only what you need, and never strip a patch bare. A few handfuls of leaves from a large colony is sustainable. Avoid pulling the bulbs -- the leaves are the prize, and the plant needs its roots to return next year.

The flowers are edible too, and make a beautiful garnish. The buds, picked just before they open, can be pickled like capers.

## In the Kitchen

Wild garlic is best used fresh. Its flavour is bright and peppery, less pungent than cultivated garlic, with a green quality that disappears when cooked too long.

**Wild garlic pesto.** Blend a generous handful of leaves with pine nuts, hard cheese, olive oil, and a squeeze of lemon. Use it on pasta, spread it on toast, or stir it into soup just before serving.

**Wilted as a side.** Treat it like spinach. A quick turn in a hot pan with butter and salt. It collapses in seconds and pairs beautifully with eggs, fish, or potatoes.

**In butter.** Finely chop the leaves and work them into softened butter with a pinch of sea salt. Roll in parchment and refrigerate. Slice rounds onto grilled bread or melting potatoes.

## The Brief Window

Wild garlic season lasts perhaps six weeks. By mid-April, the flowers will have faded and the leaves will begin to yellow and retreat. There is no way to extend this. No greenhouse trick, no preservation method that fully captures the freshness of the living leaf.

This brevity is part of its value. It teaches you to pay attention to what is available now, to use it fully while it lasts, and to let it go without regret when the season turns.
