Standard full-colour printing uses four plates: cyan, magenta, yellow, black. These are the process colours. Together they can approximate most, but not all, of the visible spectrum. In particular, they cannot reach the most saturated fluorescent hues &mdash; the orange, the pink, the green that register physically brighter than the paper they are printed on.

## The Fifth Plate

To reach these colours, a printer must add a fifth plate: a **spot colour**, mixed from pigmented ink before the run begins. A spot plate costs money. A spot plate adds a second pass through the press. A spot plate doubles the cost of registration and triples the opportunity for error.

We print every issue with Pantone 021 U &mdash; a fluorescent orange, the colour of a road sign, the colour of warning &mdash; as a fifth plate. It prints as a solid or as a 20% tint, but never as a 50% or 80% tint, because fluorescent ink does not halftone well.

> A spot colour is an editorial declaration. It says: *this one thing, in this one place, must be this exact colour &mdash; not a colour-that-approximates-this-colour, but this colour itself.*

### Where It Lands

On every page of every issue, the fluorescent spot prints in exactly three places:

1. **The crop marks.** At the four corners, printed 8mm inside the trim as discussed in issue 003.
2. **The registration marks.** At the outer midpoint of every page, as discussed in the same issue.
3. **The page rules.** A single horizontal line along the top of every page, 14mm from the trim, 0.25 points thick.

These are the orientations on every page. Beyond this, the spot colour is used sparingly &mdash; headlines in every fifth issue, bleeding backgrounds in every tenth issue, and the spine of every issue in the same fluorescent stock. The spot is scarce, and its scarcity is managed.

## What It Costs

A run of 2,400 copies with a fifth plate costs us approximately 18% more than a four-colour run of the same size. Across the year, across four issues, this is a substantial sum. Our subscribers pay for it; our advertising pays for it; and, to some extent, we absorb it.

We could drop the fifth plate. We could approximate the orange with an aggressive C/M mix. We have tried this in test runs, and the result is an orange that is warmer, less bright, and perceptibly duller. Side by side, the four-colour approximation is obviously a compromise. The fifth plate is not a compromise.

This is, in the end, the editorial argument for the fifth plate: *if the colour matters, it matters specifically and by name.*
