The colophon is the last thing written on a scroll and the first thing sought by the paleographer. It names the scribe, the date, the place, and sometimes the patron or the occasion. The colophon is, in effect, the certificate of production.

<div class="colophon-block">

Scripta est haec volumen Romae, in domo Metelli Pii, mense Maio, anno ab urbe condita DCCLXXX, per manum Gai Cassi Scribae, qui et explicit et emendavit. Si quis error irrepsit, noli calumnari; potius emenda. Liber incipit feliciter, et feliciter finitur.

</div>

## DE SCRIBA

Of the scribe.

<div class="volumen-columns">

Gaius Cassius, the scribe of this roll, worked in the house of Metellus Pius on the Esquiline Hill. He was a freedman, originally from Corinth, and he had been trained in the Greek script before taking up Latin. This dual training shows in his letters: he sometimes gives a Latin C the straight back of a Greek gamma, and his Latin E has the angular top stroke of the Greek epsilon.

Cassius worked at the standard rate of about twenty-four lines per hour. A complete scroll of four hundred lines required roughly sixteen working hours, or two full days at eight hours a day, though in practice he worked only in the mornings, in natural light, and took four days per scroll.

His ink was iron gall, made from oak apples, iron sulphate, and gum arabic, mixed in a small horn cup. He kept a second cup of water and a linen cloth to wipe his pen between lines. His pen was a split reed, sharpened with a bronze knife, and he kept a third reed in reserve for the days when the first one split or the second wore dull.

He worked at a standing desk, the scroll laid flat before him, and he unrolled only as much as he could write in one sitting. At the end of the day he rolled the scroll back up, laid it in a cedar box, and walked home. The next morning he began again, unrolling the scroll to the first empty column.

</div>

## DE MATERIA

Of the materials.

The papyrus of this scroll came from the lower Nile delta, purchased in bulk from a trader named Clodius who maintained a warehouse near the Aventine. The sheets are of the second grade, called hieratica, suitable for literary work but not for official documents. They are roughly eight by ten inches, with the horizontal grain on the side facing the reader.

The ink, as noted above, is iron gall, made locally. The glue at the kollemata is flour paste, strengthened with a small quantity of cedar oil. The umbilicus is of cypress wood, selected for straightness, turned on a small lathe kept in the scribe's workshop. The end knobs are of olive wood, darker, so that reader and scribe can tell which way the scroll was meant to be held. The sittybos is of parchment, cut from a larger sheet, the title written on both faces.

The linen sleeve was woven by a woman named Porcia, who lived on the slope of the Caelian and worked for a number of scribes. Her sleeves were known for the tightness of their weave and the smoothness of their edges, which prevented the papyrus from fraying against the cloth.

## DE TEMPORE

Of the time.

<div class="marginalia">
The scribe's dating uses the Roman system: ab urbe condita, from the founding of the city, traditionally 753 BC. The eightieth year of the seventh century AUC falls in the Roman year 27 CE, the reign of Tiberius.
</div>

The scroll was completed in May of the year DCCLXXX ab urbe condita, which in the Julian calendar falls in the reign of Tiberius. The completion was recorded in the scribe's workbook, and a copy of the workbook entry is preserved in the colophon above. The scroll itself was presented to Metellus Pius on the twenty-first of May, the feast of the Agonalia.

Metellus Pius kept the scroll in his private library, in a cedar box in the triclinium, for twenty-three years. At his death, in the year DCCCIII AUC, the library was inherited by his nephew, who added the scroll to the family library at the Tusculan villa. It remained there for more than a century, and from there it passed into the monastic library at Montecassino, where it was copied, corrected, and eventually translated into the modern edition now in your hands.

## AD FUTUROS SCRIBAS

To future scribes.

> If you are copying this scroll, copy it carefully. Do not guess at damaged letters. Do not correct without marking the correction. Do not add your own thoughts in the margin without signing them. Do not remove the colophon. Do not alter the order of the libri. Do not add your own prooemium or explicit. Leave the kollemata where they are, even if they inconvenience you. The scroll is a witness, and a scribe is a witness to the witness.
>
> -- A standard injunction, found in many late-antique scrolls.
