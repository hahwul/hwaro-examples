## LIBER PRIMUS &mdash; DE FABRICATIONE

<div class="volumen-columns">

The first liber concerns the making of the roll itself. The papyrus plant grows in the marshes of the lower Nile. It is cut with a bronze knife, split along its length, and laid in crossed strips on a wet board. Pressure and the natural sap bind the strips into a single sheet, which is then beaten flat, sun-dried, and trimmed.

A single sheet, called a charta, measures roughly eight by ten inches. A finished scroll is assembled from twenty such sheets, glued edge to edge along a horizontal seam. The glue was flour paste, sometimes strengthened with cedar oil to discourage mice. The finished roll was wound onto a wooden dowel, or umbilicus, and wrapped in a linen sleeve for storage.

The inking of the first column was a ceremonial act. The scribe was expected to write the first letter more carefully than all those that followed, and to draw the first line with a ruler rather than by eye. Many surviving scrolls show this care: the first column is upright and spaced, and subsequent columns lean slightly, as the scribe's discipline wore thin.

The naming of the volume was done on the outside, on a small parchment tag called a sittybos, attached to the end of the roll by a thread. The sittybos contained the title of the work, the name of the author, and sometimes the number of lines. When the scroll was shelved, the sittybos hung down from the shelf like a label, and the librarian could find any title by glancing along the row.

## LIBER SECUNDUS &mdash; DE LECTIONE

<div class="volumen-columns">

The second liber concerns the reading. A scroll was read aloud, even when read alone. The posture was upright, the roll held at chest height, the right hand unrolling and the left hand taking up. The rate of unrolling was governed by the rate of reading, and the rate of reading was governed by the natural pace of speech.

A trained reader did not need to look ahead or back. He or she kept the place in memory, and the roll advanced steadily from right to left. When a long passage was to be memorized, the reader paused at the end of a column, looked away, recited from memory, and only then unrolled the next column to check. This practice is called anamnetic reading, and it was the basis of classical education.

The posture of the reader was known to every sculptor. It appears in Roman and Greek relief -- a standing or seated figure, the arms held out, the roll spread between the hands. The sculptor sometimes indicated the column by incising vertical lines on the marble. Occasionally the sculptor added the sittybos, dangling from the bottom of the roll, naming the work being read.

At the end of the reading, the scroll had to be rewound. This was not the reader's courtesy to himself but to the next reader, and in libraries it was enforced by the librarian, who kept a wooden comb to help feed the roll back onto the umbilicus. A scroll left unwound was a small scandal, and the librarian's wooden comb was called, by some, the discipline of the careless.

## LIBER TERTIUS &mdash; DE BIBLIOTHECIS

The library of Alexandria was not a room but a complex of rooms, and the rooms were not filled with books in the modern sense but with cylindrical wooden pigeonholes, one for each roll. A single pigeonhole, called a nidus, held one scroll. A wall of niduses held several hundred. A complete library, counted in niduses, ran to four hundred thousand.

<div class="marginalia">
A scroll was never stood on end. It was always laid flat in its nidus, sittybos dangling. To stand a scroll on end was to damage the umbilicus and, over years, to bend the roll permanently.
</div>

The catalogue was kept on further scrolls, divided by subject and then by author. To find a work, one consulted the catalogue-scroll; the catalogue-scroll gave the subject, the author, the title, and the number of the nidus; one then walked to the nidus, withdrew the scroll, unrolled it, read, and returned it. The whole process was, in practice, not very different from the practice of a modern library, except that everything took longer and everything was heavier.

Libraries were not always stable. The library at Alexandria was burned, re-founded, burned again, re-founded again, and finally allowed to decay under pressure of religious and political change. The library at Pergamum was given away, whole, to Cleopatra, as a diplomatic gift. The library at Rome of Marcus Aurelius was sold off at his death to pay soldiers. A scroll is durable as a physical object but fragile as an institution, and the history of classical literature is largely the history of what libraries happened to survive.
