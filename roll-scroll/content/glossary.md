A short glossary of the technical vocabulary of scroll-making and scroll-reading. Terms are given in Latin or Greek, with the English gloss alongside. Terms marked with an asterisk are still in use among modern paleographers.

<div class="glossary">

<dl>

<dt>VOLUMEN</dt>
<dd>Latin. The standard word for a roll, from volvere, to turn or to roll. The thing is called a volumen because it is rolled; the rolling and the thing are one and the same. The modern word volume derives from this, though in English a volume is a bound codex, not a roll.</dd>

<dt>CHARTA</dt>
<dd>Latin, from the Greek chartes. A single sheet of papyrus, before it is glued into a scroll. A standard charta measured about eight by ten inches. The modern words chart, charter, and card descend from this; our English carta is its direct cousin.</dd>

<dt>KOLLEMA*</dt>
<dd>Greek. The junction between two sheets of papyrus, glued edge to edge. Plural: kollemata. An experienced scribe counted kollemata to locate his position in the roll. The term survives in modern codicology to refer to any seam between the sheets of a multi-sheet roll.</dd>

<dt>UMBILICUS</dt>
<dd>Latin, navel. The wooden dowel at the centre of the roll, onto which the scroll was wound. The knob on each end of the umbilicus was sometimes called a cornu, a horn. The roll was unwound from the right hand and rewound onto the umbilicus from the left, restoring the scroll to its stored state.</dd>

<dt>SITTYBOS*</dt>
<dd>Greek. The small parchment tag attached to the outside of a scroll, bearing the title and author. When the scroll was laid in its nidus, the sittybos hung down like a shelf-label, and the librarian could identify the work without unrolling. The word is still used for the paper or parchment labels on ancient rolls held in modern collections.</dd>

<dt>NIDUS</dt>
<dd>Latin, nest. A cylindrical pigeonhole in the library wall, sized to hold a single scroll. Scrolls were laid flat in their niduses with the sittybos hanging down. A large library could hold hundreds of thousands of niduses, arranged by subject and author.</dd>

<dt>ANAGIGNOSKEIN</dt>
<dd>Greek. To read, but in the specific sense of to recognize again. Ancient reading was understood as the re-encounter of what had been written, rather than as the first apprehension of meaning. The reader anagigno:skei, recognizes, what the scribe has put down.</dd>

<dt>LACUNA</dt>
<dd>Latin, gap. A break in the text, usually caused by physical damage to the scroll -- worm, water, mouse, fire, or the slow decay of the glue at a kollema. Editors indicate a lacuna with square brackets or an ellipsis. A scroll with many lacunae is said to be lacunose.</dd>

<dt>PROOEMIUM</dt>
<dd>Latin, from the Greek prooimion. The opening of a work, before the main body begins. In a scroll, the prooemium stands in the first column, before any kollema. It often dedicates the work to a patron and names the author. A scroll without a prooemium is said to be acephalic, headless.</dd>

<dt>EXPLICIT</dt>
<dd>Latin, it is unrolled. The closing formula of a work, appearing at the end of the last column. It often repeats the title and author and adds the scribe's name and the date. When a modern scholar reads the explicit of a scroll, he is reading the last words the scribe wrote; he is, in a small way, the next person in line.</dd>

<dt>EMENDATIO</dt>
<dd>Latin. The correction of a corrupt text. When a scroll was copied, errors crept in; when the copy was compared to the exemplar, the errors were marked; when the errors were corrected, the scroll was said to have been emended. A scroll that has been emended by a careful editor is said to be emendatus.</dd>

<dt>COLOPHON</dt>
<dd>Greek, the finishing touch. A note at the end of a scroll, written by the scribe, giving his name, the date, the place of copying, and sometimes a prayer or curse. The colophon is the scribe's signature and the scroll's certification. It is the last thing read and the first thing sought by the paleographer.</dd>

</dl>
