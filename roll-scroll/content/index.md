## DE RATIONE VOLUMINIS

On the method of the scroll.

<div class="column-marks">
  <span>&middot; COL &middot; I &middot;</span>
  <span>&middot; COL &middot; II &middot;</span>
</div>

<div class="volumen-columns">

The scroll, or volumen, was the standard format of publication across the Mediterranean for more than a thousand years. A single work occupied a single roll, or, if too long, was divided into several rolls, each of which was called a liber. From this we inherit our modern word book, though the book as we know it is the codex, a different animal entirely.

A well-made scroll was assembled from rectangular sheets of papyrus, glued edge to edge along a horizontal seam. The junction between two sheets was called a kollema, and an experienced scribe could count the kollemata to know how far into the roll he had written. On the best scrolls, the seams were so well made that ink flowed over them without interruption.

The reader held the roll in two hands. The right hand unrolled; the left hand took up what had been read. As the reading advanced, so the bulk of the roll transferred from right to left. At the end, the scroll had to be rewound, a courtesy owed to the next reader and enforced by the librarian.

Columns were narrow, typically holding between eighteen and twenty-five letters per line. The reader encountered them in sequence, never as facing pages. There were no page numbers, because there were no pages, and navigation was by the roll rather than by the opening. To cite a passage, one named the work and the line, not the folio.

</div>

## DE TYPORUM INSCRIPTIONIBUS

A note on the display type.

Headings in this volume are set in inscriptional capitals after the model of the Roman monumental letter -- the same letter cut into marble along the architrave of the Pantheon and the Trajanic column. The proportions are wide, the strokes contrasted, the counters generous. They are meant not to be read quickly but to be encountered slowly, as one encounters a threshold.

The body face is a humanist garamond, narrow enough to set in twin columns but with enough open counter to read well at small sizes. Italic is reserved for emphasis and for the names of other works, not for quotation -- which takes indentation and a rule -- and not for the occasional Greek word -- which takes its own alphabet.

<div class="marginalia">
The narrower the column, the fewer decisions the eye must make per line. Four inches is generous; three and a half inches is ideal for reading aloud; three inches is for inscription.
</div>

Line height is set at roughly 1.75 times the x-height of the body, loose enough to permit marginal notation and to open the gray value of the block. The overall impression is not of a web page but of a single, patient column of text, such as one might meet on the wall of a temple or on the long unrolled papyrus held between two hands.

## EPIGRAMMATA

A handful of short verses from the collection.

<p class="verse-title">AD LECTOREM</p>
<p class="verse-attr">Posidippus &middot; III BC</p>
<div class="verse">
Unroll me, stranger, but unroll me kindly,<br>
for I am old, and my seams are tender.<br>
The letters I carry were kind to their scribe,<br>
and the scribe, in turn, was kind to his teacher,<br>
and so the good word was passed down to you.<br>
Pass it down further. That is all I ask.
</div>

<p class="verse-title">DE MARGINE</p>
<p class="verse-attr">Anonymous &middot; II AD</p>
<div class="verse">
The margin is not empty, but attentive.<br>
It waits for the reader to add what was left out,<br>
or, failing that, to add what had not yet been thought.<br>
A scroll without marginalia<br>
is a scroll that has not yet been read.
</div>

## AD LECTOREM

A closing note to the reader.

> The scroll is a format that asks for attention. It does not open flat. It does not sit on a desk. It must be held, one hand unrolling, one hand receiving, and it must be rewound. A book can be put down; a scroll cannot, not quite, not without losing one's place. This is a feature of the form.
>
> -- From the editor's preface.
