<h2>On the Weight of the Curtain</h2>
<p>I began writing <em>The Caldera House</em> because of a door. The door was in a house on the north coast of my mother's island, and it had been closed for forty years by the time I saw it. No one had opened it because, the neighbours said, there was a reason it was closed. The reason was never given, and I did not ask.</p>
<p>The play is not about that door. The play is about the weight of pretending a door is only a door.</p>
<blockquote>We make tragedies for the body, not the mind. When the curtain falls, it falls through the air above you. The body knows.</blockquote>
<p>I asked our lighting designer to abandon every electric fixture he had. Oil and gas only, on stage. I asked Calum's fly crew to weight the main curtain until a single person could not open it. I asked the chamber orchestra to mark the movement of the lava with the bow held a full beat longer than the score permits.</p>
<p>All of this is to say: the curtain is not a decoration. The curtain is the last wall. When it falls, the house is sealed. When it rises, you have agreed to walk in.</p>
