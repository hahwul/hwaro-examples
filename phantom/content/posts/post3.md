In a city that rains more often than not, there is a shop that sells mirrors with no reflections. The owner calls them "windows to where you used to be." She has been collecting them for forty years.

## The Shop

The storefront has no sign. Its window displays a single mirror -- an oval piece in a tarnished silver frame -- that shows the street behind you but never you. Passersby often stop, confused by the wrongness they feel but cannot name. Most walk on. A few enter.

Inside, the shop is narrow and deep, lined floor to ceiling with mirrors of every shape and era. None of them reflect the person standing before them. Some show empty rooms. Others display landscapes -- forests in permanent twilight, oceans under grey skies, corridors that recede into darkness. A handful show nothing at all, their surfaces a flat, impenetrable black.

## The Collector

The owner is a woman of indeterminate age with pale eyes and steady hands. She speaks softly and never gives her name. She explains that each mirror lost its reflection at a specific moment -- the instant someone looked into it and saw something they could not accept. The reflection, she says, departed. It went elsewhere.

She does not say where.

When asked how she acquires the mirrors, she says they find her. She wakes some mornings to find one leaning against the shop door. Others arrive in packages with no return address. Once, she claims, a mirror simply appeared on the wall of the shop overnight, already hung, already empty.

## The Reflections

Occasionally, customers report seeing movement in the mirrors -- not their own, but something else. A figure crossing a room. A hand reaching for a doorknob. A face turning toward them and then away. These moments are brief and unrepeatable. The collector nods when told of them but offers no explanation.

She has one rule: nothing is for sale. The mirrors are to be looked at, considered, and left where they are. She permits no photographs. She asks that visitors not touch the glass.

One mirror in the back of the shop is covered with a cloth. When asked about it, the collector's composure changes -- a slight tightening around the eyes, a pause before she speaks. She says only that this mirror still has its reflection. But the reflection no longer matches anything in the room.

She does not remove the cloth. She does not explain whose reflection it holds. She changes the subject with the practiced ease of someone who has been deflecting this question for a very long time.
