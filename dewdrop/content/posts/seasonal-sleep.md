We expect our sleep to remain constant year-round, but our bodies were never designed for that. Before electric light, humans slept differently in winter than in summer -- longer, darker, deeper in the cold months, and lighter, shorter as the days stretched.

## Listening to the Light

Your circadian rhythm is governed primarily by light exposure. In winter, when daylight is scarce, your body produces melatonin earlier and for longer. In summer, the extended light delays that signal. Fighting this pattern -- staying up late in winter, forcing early sleep in summer -- creates a subtle but persistent friction.

## Winter Adjustments

During the darker months, consider:

- **Earlier wind-down**: Begin dimming lights and reducing screens by 8 PM
- **Longer sleep window**: Allow yourself eight to nine hours rather than the usual seven
- **Warmth rituals**: A warm bath or herbal tea before bed signals safety to the nervous system
- **Morning light**: Get outside within thirty minutes of waking, even on overcast days

## Summer Adjustments

As the days lengthen:

- **Later bedtime**: Let your natural rhythm shift slightly later
- **Dawn exposure**: Sleep with curtains partially open to let morning light wake you naturally
- **Lighter covers**: Temperature regulation is essential; your body needs to cool to sleep deeply
- **Evening walks**: Use the long twilight for gentle movement that prepares the body for rest

## The Permission to Rest

Perhaps the most important shift is psychological. We have been taught that consistent productivity is a virtue, but nature operates in cycles. There are seasons for growth and seasons for dormancy. Your sleep can follow the same wisdom.
