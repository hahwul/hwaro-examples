There is a difference between making breakfast and preparing a morning meal. The first is a task. The second is a practice.

## Slow Mornings Begin in the Kitchen

Before reaching for your phone, reach for the kettle. The act of boiling water is one of the oldest rituals in human history. Let it be yours. Listen to the sound of it. Feel the steam rise. These small sensory details anchor you in the present moment before the day pulls you elsewhere.

## Three Principles of Morning Nourishment

### 1. Simplicity

A good morning meal does not require complexity. Oats with seasonal fruit. Toast with good butter and salt. A soft-boiled egg. The fewer decisions you make before 8 AM, the more mental energy you preserve for the hours that follow.

### 2. Warmth

Traditional medicine across many cultures emphasizes warm foods in the morning -- congee in China, porridge in Scotland, miso in Japan. Warm food is easier on the digestive system and creates a sense of internal comfort that cold alternatives often lack.

### 3. Presence

Eat without a screen. Sit down. Notice the textures, the temperatures, the way flavors change as they cool. This is not about being precious with your food. It is about refusing to let the first meal of the day become invisible.

## A Weekly Rhythm

Consider rotating your morning meals through the week:

- **Monday / Wednesday / Friday**: Oats with nuts and seasonal fruit
- **Tuesday / Thursday**: Eggs with vegetables and sourdough
- **Saturday**: Something new -- a recipe you have been curious about
- **Sunday**: Whatever feels right, made slowly

The structure frees you from decision fatigue. The flexibility keeps it alive.
