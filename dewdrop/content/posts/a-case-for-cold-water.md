I did not come to cold water willingly. It began as an accident -- a broken water heater in January -- and became something I now look forward to each morning.

## The First Thirty Seconds

The initial shock is real. Your breath catches. Your skin tightens. Every part of you wants to step out. But if you stay -- just for thirty seconds -- something shifts. The panic gives way to a strange clarity. Your mind, which moments ago was tangled in yesterday's worries, goes perfectly quiet.

## What the Research Says

Cold water exposure has been linked to improved circulation, reduced inflammation, and enhanced mood through the release of norepinephrine. But the numbers tell only part of the story. The deeper benefit is psychological: you learn, each morning, that discomfort is survivable. That you can choose to stay.

## A Gentle Approach

You do not need an ice bath. You do not need to suffer. Start at the end of your normal shower:

1. Turn the water to cool -- not freezing -- for the last fifteen seconds
2. Focus on your breath rather than the temperature
3. Gradually extend the duration as it becomes familiar

The point is not toughness. The point is teaching your nervous system that it can meet a challenge and return to calm.

## Beyond the Shower

What I have noticed most is how this practice bleeds into the rest of the day. Small frustrations feel more manageable. The gap between stimulus and response grows a little wider. You carry that morning steadiness with you, like warmth stored in stone.
