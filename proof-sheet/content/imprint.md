## Whiteside &amp; Co., Typesetters

Whiteside &amp; Co. has set type at fourteen Shoe Lane in the City of London since 1864. We are a small firm: three compositors, one editor, one apprentice. We do not advertise. We accept commissions by introduction only.

We set books of between forty and four hundred pages, principally literary fiction, poetry, essays, and small works of history and translation. We do not set textbooks, manuals, or trade catalogues. We do not set in languages we cannot read, though we maintain a standing arrangement with a firm in Edinburgh for Gaelic and a firm in Paris for modern French.

</div>

## Faces We Hold

Our font of working type consists principally of the following faces, in the sizes given. New casts are ordered as needed from Stephenson Blake, Sheffield, at roughly thirty pounds per case.

| Face | Sizes Held | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Baskerville | 9, 10, 11, 12, 14, 18 pt | Principal body, most titles |
| Caslon | 10, 11, 12, 14, 24 pt | Traditional titling, chapter heads |
| Bembo | 10, 11, 12 pt | Italian reprints, poetry |
| Plantin | 11, 12, 14 pt | Heavy body for cheap paper |
| Times | 9, 10, 11, 12 pt | Second-run reprints |
| Perpetua Titling | 14, 18, 24 pt | Titling and half-titles only |

We do not stock any sans-serif face, and we will politely decline work that requires one. We have no plan to acquire a sans-serif case.

## How We Work

<div class="proof-margin">
A commission starts with a conversation, not a quotation. Please do not send manuscripts unsolicited; we cannot read them fast enough to be fair.
</div>

A commission begins with a letter, and a letter is followed by a conversation, usually in person, at our office, over a cup of tea. We assess the manuscript, we estimate the page count and the time, and we name a price. The price is never negotiated downward; if you cannot afford it, we will propose a smaller format instead, or a thinner paper, or a shorter print run.

We set in hand. We do not use digital typesetting for any part of the work, though we will, if pressed, prepare a digital copy of the finished text for archival purposes. Our compositors stand at the cases throughout the working day; we have no seated compositors and no tablet-setters.

Our proofs are delivered by hand or by registered post. Corrected proofs are returned by the same means. We do not send proofs by electronic mail. If you cannot send or receive paper, we regret we cannot take your commission.

## The People

**M. Whiteside**, compositor and principal. Third generation. Trained in the shop, never anywhere else. Sets on average three books per year.

**S. Penrith**, editor. Joined the firm in 2009 from a university press. Reads a line of proof faster than a machine can scan it.

**T. Brodrick**, assistant compositor. Our apprentice-made-compositor. Trained since 2018. Specialises in poetry setting and in footnote layout.

**H. Anwar**, apprentice, second year. Will be a compositor by 2028 if all goes well.

**The Night Porter.** The porter has been at fourteen Shoe Lane longer than any of us. He does not set type; he lets in the authors who arrive after hours and he watches over the shop when we are not there. We are not allowed to name him, at his own request.

## A Final Word

We are aware that the hand-set book is a decaying craft. We do not expect to be the last shop that sets this way, but we are aware we may be. We have an apprentice and a succession plan. We have a lease on the building that runs to 2042. We have ordered a fresh case of Baskerville fourteen-point that will last us, at the present rate of commission, into the 2030s.

We thank our authors for their patience with our pace, our editors for their work behind the scenes, our binders and our paper merchants for holding their prices where they can, and our readers for continuing to prefer books that have been set by hand to books that have not.

> Setting type is one of the few trades left in which a mistake is permanent, quiet, and final. Make it, own it, fix it in the next proof, and move on.
>
> -- M. Whiteside, in a letter to the apprentices.
