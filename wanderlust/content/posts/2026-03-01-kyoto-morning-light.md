There is a particular quality of light in Kyoto that exists only between six and seven in the morning. It is pale and silver, filtered through cedar branches, and it makes the wooden temples look as though they have been freshly carved.

<!-- more -->

I left the ryokan before breakfast. The streets of Higashiyama were empty except for a woman sweeping the stone path in front of a tea house. She did not look up. The broom made a soft, rhythmic sound against the flagstones, and for a moment that sound was the only thing in the world.

## The Path to Kiyomizu-dera

The approach to Kiyomizu-dera is a steep climb through narrow lanes lined with shuttered shops. At this hour, their wooden facades are still closed, and the lane feels like a corridor in an old house. The paving stones are dark with moisture. Somewhere above, a crow calls once and falls silent.

The temple itself, when you reach it, is almost disappointing in its familiarity. You have seen it in a thousand photographs. But photographs cannot capture the smell of old wood and incense, or the way the veranda extends out over the hillside like the prow of a ship sailing into a sea of maple trees.

## Philosopher's Path

Later, I walked the Philosopher's Path along the canal. The cherry trees were bare --- it was too early in the season --- but the moss on the stone walls was vivid green, almost electric against the grey morning. A heron stood in the shallow water, perfectly still, as though it had been placed there by a set designer.

The path takes its name from Nishida Kitaro, a philosopher who walked it daily on his way to Kyoto University. I like to think he walked slowly, the way I was walking, stopping to watch the water move over the stones in the canal bed.

## Notes

The best time to visit Kyoto's eastern temples is before 7:00 AM. The gates open early, and for a brief window you will have the grounds nearly to yourself. Bring a jacket --- the mornings are cool even in early spring. The convenience stores sell excellent onigiri if you want to skip the formal breakfast at your accommodation.

Coordinates for the start of the Philosopher's Path: the northern end begins near Ginkaku-ji. Walk south toward Nanzen-ji. The entire path is roughly two kilometers and takes about forty minutes at a contemplative pace.
