The number 28 tram is one of those rare things: a piece of public infrastructure that is also a work of art. It climbs through Lisbon's oldest neighborhoods with the determination of a mountain goat, leaning into curves so sharp that you can reach out the window and touch the walls of the buildings on either side.

## Alfama

The tram enters Alfama from the west, and immediately the city changes. The streets narrow to the width of a single vehicle. Laundry hangs between buildings like signal flags. The facades are covered in azulejo tiles --- blue and white geometric patterns that have been telling the same story for three hundred years.

Alfama survived the great earthquake of 1755 because it sits on solid bedrock. Walking its streets, you can feel that solidity beneath your feet. These are not streets that were planned by an urban designer. They grew organically, following the contours of the hill, and they have the logic of a living thing rather than a grid.

## The Miradouros

Lisbon is a city of viewpoints. The miradouros are scattered across the hilltops like observation posts, and each one offers a different composition of terracotta roofs, the Tagus river, and the sky.

Miradouro da Graca is my favorite. It faces south and west, and in the late afternoon the light turns the city into a study in warm ochre and deep shadow. The 25 de Abril Bridge hangs in the distance, a red thread connecting the city to the south bank.

I sat on the low wall with a coffee from the kiosk and watched the light change for an hour. Nobody hurried me. In Lisbon, sitting and watching is considered a perfectly respectable way to spend an afternoon.

## Pasteis de Nata

No account of Lisbon is complete without mentioning the custard tarts. They are everywhere, and they are almost always good, but the ones at Manteigaria on Rua do Loreto are extraordinary --- the pastry shatters at the first bite, and the custard is barely set, still warm from the oven.

I ate three in quick succession and felt no guilt at all.

## Practical Notes

Tram 28 runs from Martim Moniz to Campo Ourique. Board at the Martim Moniz terminus for the best chance of getting a seat. The ride takes about forty minutes end to end. A single ticket costs a few euros; the Viva Viagem card is cheaper if you plan to use public transport frequently.

The tram runs every ten to fifteen minutes during the day. Avoid the midday hours when it fills with tourists. Early morning or late afternoon offers a more authentic experience --- and better light for photographs.
