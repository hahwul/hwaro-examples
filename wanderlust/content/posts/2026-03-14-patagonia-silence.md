El Chalten sits at the base of Mount Fitz Roy like a sentence that trails off into an ellipsis. The town is small --- a few streets of hostels, gear shops, and restaurants --- and beyond it there is nothing but steppe, mountains, and wind.

<!-- more -->

The wind is the first thing you notice. It is constant, a low roar that comes from the west and pushes everything --- clouds, dust, your body --- eastward across the plain. After a day you stop noticing it. After two days you start to miss it when it briefly stops.

## The Trek to Laguna de los Tres

The trail to Laguna de los Tres begins at the northern edge of town and climbs through lenga forest for two hours before emerging onto the exposed moraine. The forest is beautiful in an understated way --- twisted trunks, moss-covered rocks, and the occasional flash of a Magellanic woodpecker's red crest.

The final ascent is brutal: a steep scramble up loose rock that gains four hundred meters in less than a kilometer. Your legs burn. Your lungs burn. You stop every fifty steps to rest and to look back at the valley expanding below you.

And then you reach the top, and you stop thinking about your legs.

Laguna de los Tres is a glacial lake the color of powdered turquoise. Behind it, Fitz Roy rises in a vertical wall of granite, its peak wreathed in a banner of cloud. The scale is impossible to comprehend. Your brain tries to process it as a photograph, a painting, a backdrop. It is none of these things. It is simply there, indifferent to your presence, and that indifference is what makes it magnificent.

## Glacier Country

South of El Chalten, the landscape becomes even more extreme. The Perito Moreno Glacier is a wall of ice five kilometers wide and sixty meters tall, and it is one of the few glaciers in the world that is still advancing. Standing on the viewing platforms, you can hear it: deep groans and sharp cracks as the ice moves forward at a rate of two meters per day.

Every few minutes, a piece of the face calves off and crashes into the lake below with a sound like distant thunder. The splash sends waves across the surface. Then silence returns --- that particular Patagonian silence that is not really silence at all, but the sound of a landscape so vast that human noise cannot fill it.

## Practical Information

El Chalten is accessible by bus from El Calafate, roughly a three-hour drive. The bus follows Route 40 through the steppe, and the views of Lago Viedma are worth sitting on the left side of the bus.

The trekking season runs from November to March. January is warmest but busiest. March offers autumn colors in the lenga forest and thinner crowds. Bring layers --- the weather changes rapidly, and a sunny morning can become a hailstorm by noon.

No permits are required for day hikes. Carry water, sun protection, and enough food for the day. The trails are well-marked but there are no facilities once you leave town.
