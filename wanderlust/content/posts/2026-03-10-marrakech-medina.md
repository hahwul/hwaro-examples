The medina of Marrakech is not a place you navigate. It is a place that navigates you. The narrow alleys branch and reconnect like the vessels of a circulatory system, and no matter how carefully you study your map, you will end up somewhere you did not intend to go.

<!-- more -->

This is not a flaw. This is the point.

## The Souks

The souks are organized by trade, a system that dates back centuries. The leather workers occupy one quarter, the metalworkers another, the spice merchants another still. But the boundaries are porous, and you will often find a carpet seller next to a pharmacy next to a man repairing antique clocks.

The spice souk is the most overwhelming. Pyramids of turmeric, cumin, and paprika are arranged with architectural precision, their colors so saturated they look artificial. The air is thick with scent --- saffron, dried roses, black pepper. A shopkeeper offers you a handful of argan oil almonds and begins explaining the medicinal properties of every spice on his shelf. You came in to buy cinnamon. You leave with a bag of herbs you cannot identify and the feeling that you have just attended a lecture at a very informal university.

## Jemaa el-Fnaa

The great square is different at every hour. In the morning it is nearly empty, occupied only by orange juice sellers setting up their stalls. By noon it fills with food vendors and henna artists. By evening it becomes a theater --- storytellers, musicians, acrobats, snake charmers, and an audience of thousands standing in loose circles, moving from act to act like guests at an enormous party.

I found a rooftop terrace overlooking the square and ordered mint tea. Below, the square pulsed with light and sound and smoke from the food stalls. It looked like a painting by someone who had decided that realism was overrated.

## The Riads

The genius of Marrakech architecture is the riad --- a house built around a central courtyard, its exterior walls blank and unremarkable, its interior a paradise of tilework, fountains, and orange trees. The concept is privacy as luxury: the most beautiful spaces are hidden from the street.

Our riad was down an alley so narrow I could touch both walls with outstretched arms. The door was plain wood, studded with iron. Behind it: a courtyard with a marble fountain, walls covered in zellige tilework, and a plunge pool the color of jade.

## Getting There

Fly into Marrakech Menara Airport. The medina is a fifteen-minute taxi ride from the terminal. Agree on the fare before getting in --- thirty to forty dirhams is reasonable. Better yet, arrange pickup through your riad.

For the souks, leave your map at the hotel. Wander. When you are lost, ask a shopkeeper for directions to Jemaa el-Fnaa --- everyone knows the way, and the walk back will show you things the guidebook missed.
