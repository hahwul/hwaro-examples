In Copenhagen, the bicycle is not alternative transport. It is transport. More than half the city's residents cycle to work or school, and the infrastructure reflects this: wide, separated bike lanes on every major road, dedicated traffic signals, and bridges built exclusively for cyclists and pedestrians.

<!-- more -->

## The First Ride

I rented a bicycle from a shop near Norreport Station --- a heavy, upright Dutch-style bike with a basket and a bell. The shop owner gave me a single instruction: "Stay in the bike lane and signal your turns." Then he waved me into the morning traffic.

The first five minutes were terrifying. Copenhagen cyclists move with the purposeful speed of a river current, and joining the flow requires commitment. You push off, you merge, and suddenly you are part of it --- a stream of riders moving together through the city with an efficiency that would make an urban planner weep with joy.

By the second intersection, the terror had become exhilaration.

## Nyhavn to Christiania

The route from Nyhavn to Christiania takes you along the harbor, past the Opera House and through the old naval base at Holmen. The bike lanes are smooth asphalt, separated from car traffic by a curb and a row of parked bicycles. Pedestrians have their own lane. Everyone stays in their designated space with a discipline that feels almost choreographed.

Nyhavn's colored facades are as vivid in person as they are in photographs, but from a bicycle they are a fleeting impression --- yellow, red, blue, orange --- rather than a static image. This is how the city is meant to be seen: in motion, at human speed.

Christiania is different from the rest of Copenhagen. The freetown is a self-governing community established in 1971 on the site of a former military barracks. Cars are not allowed. The buildings are a mix of converted barracks, self-built houses, and structures that defy architectural classification. There is a lake, a concert hall, and a sense that the rules here are different.

## The Bridges

Copenhagen's bicycle bridges are masterpieces of civic design. The Cykelslangen --- the Bicycle Snake --- is an elevated orange ramp that spirals down from the Brygge Bridge to street level, eliminating a dangerous intersection. Riding it feels like flying, or like being inside an enormous piece of playground equipment designed for adults.

The Inderhavnsbroen, the Inner Harbour Bridge, opens like a drawbridge to let boats pass. When it closes, a light turns green and hundreds of cyclists stream across the water in both directions. It is a small, beautiful piece of urban theater that plays out dozens of times a day.

## What I Learned

After three days on a bicycle in Copenhagen, driving a car felt absurd. Not morally absurd --- just practically absurd, like using a sledgehammer to hang a picture frame. The bicycle is the right tool for a city this size, and Copenhagen has spent decades proving it.

The lesson is not that every city should copy Copenhagen. The lesson is that infrastructure shapes behavior, and that when you design a city for people rather than vehicles, people respond by being happier, healthier, and more connected to the place they live.

## Getting Around

Most hotels can arrange bicycle rental, or try Donkey Republic --- an app-based rental system with pickup and drop-off points across the city. Day rates are reasonable. Helmets are not required by law, but bringing your own is sensible.

The city is almost perfectly flat. If you can ride a bicycle, you can ride in Copenhagen. Follow the bike lanes, signal left turns with your arm, and ring your bell when passing. The locals will do the rest.
